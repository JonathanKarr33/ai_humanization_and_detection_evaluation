This essay brings together theological reflection, conceptual frameworks, empirical observations, and pastoral proposals to examine religious environmentalism as a mode of care that bridges social justice and ecological concern. Focusing on the environmental practices of an Episcopal congregation in Central Texas, it illustrates how faith-based ecological engagement can embody a vision of relational justice that includes the more-than-human world. The article argues that such ecologically attentive practices offer an important normative resource for pastoral theology. By situating ecological commitments within their theological and social contexts, the essay makes a case for pastoral theological engagement with environmental issues as a necessary response to interlocking systems of harm that undermine the well-being of both human communities and the earth.
