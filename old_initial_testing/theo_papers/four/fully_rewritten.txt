The United States, with the highest incarceration rate in the world, exemplifies a prison society shaped by intersecting social, economic, and cultural forces. This article examines how systemic oppression—particularly racial and class-based inequalities—distorted notions of safety and justice, and psychosocial processes such as othering and scapegoating, sustain mass incarceration. Drawing on these analyses, the paper offers a pastoral theological framework that critically engages the racist and imperial foundations of the U.S. justice system, highlighting pathways for ethical and transformative reflection.
