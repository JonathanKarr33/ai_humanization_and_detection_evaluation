This article examines the crisis of depression in postmodern America through the lens of the African American experience. Considering the persistent societal impacts of race, class, and gender oppression, it traces the roots of depression to the historical trauma of the trans-Atlantic slave trade and its enduring effects on Africans and African Americans. The article also addresses the cultural stigmatization of depression as a significant challenge within the African American community today. Drawing on a communal–contextual model of Black pastoral theology, the author highlights the roles of Black pastors and the broader congregational ‘extended family’ as essential resources for addressing and mitigating the impact of this stigma.
