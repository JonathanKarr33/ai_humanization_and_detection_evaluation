This article investigates the crisis of depression among African Americans within postmodern America, situating it in the historical and cultural context of the trans-Atlantic slave trade and its enduring legacy of racial, class, and gender oppression. It explores how intergenerational trauma, cultural stigmatization, and societal marginalization contribute to the persistence of depression, highlighting the complex interplay of socioeconomic and psychosocial factors. Employing a communal–contextual model of Black pastoral theology, the article emphasizes the critical roles of the Black pastor, extended family networks, and African American congregations in addressing stigma, fostering psychological resilience, and promoting holistic well-being. By integrating historical memory, cultural awareness, and pastoral care, the study underscores pathways for ethically responsible and culturally informed interventions within African American communities.
