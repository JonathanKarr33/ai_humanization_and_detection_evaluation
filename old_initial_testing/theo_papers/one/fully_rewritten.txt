Public pastoral theology increasingly takes place within contexts of religious plurality, where multiple traditions shape communal life and public concerns. While pastoral theologians often emphasize mutuality, accountability, and contextual engagement, public theological work rooted in Christianity remains vulnerable to reinforcing Christian privilege. This article asks how public pastoral theology can engage social conflict and communal well-being in religiously diverse settings without marginalizing or colonizing other traditions. Drawing on a public theological response to a community conflict in Little Saigon, Orange County, the article critically assesses the limits of prevailing communal–contextual and intercultural paradigms when applied to contexts marked by overlapping religious identities. In response, it proposes a comparative theological approach—described as “caring across traditions”—that intentionally engages theological resources from multiple faith traditions. This paradigm offers a constructive way for pastoral theology to address public issues while honoring religious difference and fostering genuine interreligious accountability.
