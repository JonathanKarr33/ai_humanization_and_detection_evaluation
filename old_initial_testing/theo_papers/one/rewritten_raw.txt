Public pastoral theology in multifaith and interreligious settings seeks to draw on the depth of diverse religious traditions, remain accountable to the communities it serves, and practice genuine reciprocity. Yet such engagement carries a persistent danger: the unexamined continuation of Christian dominance. This raises a critical question—how can pastoral theology respond to public concerns in ways that serve the common good without reinscribing Christian privilege or appropriating the traditions of others? Reflecting on my public theological engagement with a community dispute in Little Saigon, Orange County, I was prompted to reexamine the adequacy of existing pastoral theological frameworks for contexts marked by religious plurality and overlapping religious commitments. In response, I propose a comparative theological approach—what I call “caring across traditions”—that intentionally engages theological resources from multiple faiths, offering a constructive complement to communal–contextual and intercultural paradigms.
