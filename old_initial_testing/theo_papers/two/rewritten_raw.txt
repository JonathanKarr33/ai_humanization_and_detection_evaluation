The article contends that over the past thirty years pastoral theology, like many humanities disciplines, has largely taken capitalism for granted as a normative social framework. As a result, capitalism itself has rarely been subjected to sustained theological critique. This omission has become increasingly troubling as late capitalism—often expressed through neoliberal economic and cultural logics—has achieved global dominance and contributed to widespread social, relational, and psychological harm. Functioning as an organizing paradigm, neoliberalism not only generates new forms of suffering but also reshapes existing forms in complex and intensified ways. The author argues that if pastoral theology fails to attend to these structural dynamics in its understanding of suffering and its approaches to care, it risks losing moral and scholarly credibility. The article concludes by outlining five key features of a pastoral theology that critically engages, rather than normalizes, capitalist assumptions.
