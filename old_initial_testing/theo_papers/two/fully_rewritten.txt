This article contends that pastoral theology has largely taken capitalism for granted as a normative social framework, mirroring a broader pattern within the humanities. As a result, capitalism—particularly in its contemporary neoliberal form—has escaped sustained theological critique. Drawing on interdisciplinary research, the author argues that neoliberalism now functions as a global hegemonic paradigm that profoundly reshapes social, relational, and psychological life, generating new forms of suffering while reconfiguring older ones. When pastoral theology interprets suffering primarily through individual or intrapsychic lenses, it risks misdiagnosing its sources and diminishing its own credibility. In response, the article calls for a reorientation toward a post-capitalist pastoral theology that critically interrogates economic power, attends to class and inequality, and resists the naturalization of market logic. The essay concludes by outlining five key features of a pastoral theology committed to exposing and challenging capitalism’s formative influence on human suffering and to fostering practices of care oriented toward justice, solidarity, and the flourishing of soul beyond market values.
