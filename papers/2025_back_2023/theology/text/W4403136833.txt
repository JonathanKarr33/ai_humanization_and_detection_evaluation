Abstract 
This study examines the contributions and challenges of theological education within The 
Apostolic Church Nigeria (TACN) from its establishment in 1931 to the present. The objectives 
are to explore the historical development of TACN's theological institutions, assess their impact 
on doctrinal teaching and ministerial effectiveness, and identify the challenges in sustaining 
these educational programmes. Employing a historical and qualitative research methodology, 
the study utilises document analysis, interviews with key stakeholders – including faculty, alumni, 
and current students – and thematic analysis to gather insights into the evolving landscape of 
theological education in TACN. The findings reveal that TACN's commitment to theological 
education has significantly enhanced its leaders’ intellectual and spiritual capacities, contributing 
to the church’s doctrinal consistency and overall growth. Institutions such as The Apostolic 
Church Theological Seminary in Amumara and The Apostolic Church LAWNA Theological 
Seminary in Jos have structured ministerial training. However, challenges such as limited financial 
resources, cultural resistance to formal education, and the need to balance spiritual 
empowerment with academic rigour persist. The study concludes that for TACN to continue 
advancing its theological education programmes, it must invest strategically in infrastructure, 
technology, and faculty development while contextualising theological training to meet 
contemporary needs. The recommendations emphasise fostering a holistic approach integrating 
academic rigour with spiritual formation. This study contributes to understanding TACN's 
educational framework and offers practical insights for enhancing theological education within 
other Pentecostal movements facing similar challenges. 
Keywords: Theological Education, The Apostolic Church Nigeria, Ministerial Training, Doctrinal 
Teaching, Theological Seminaries. 
 
 
Introduction 
The Apostolic Church Nigeria (TACN) has been a significant force within Nigeria's Christian 
landscape since its formal establishment in 1931. Throughout its history, TACN has played 
a crucial role in Nigeria’s spiritual, social, and educational development (Worsfold, 1991). A 
cornerstone of the church's influence has been its unwavering commitment to theological

education, aimed at nurturing both the intellectual and spiritual capacities of its leaders. 
Through the establishment of numerous seminaries and colleges, TACN has ensured 
doctrinal fidelity and pastoral excellence by training pastors, evangelists, and church 
officials across Nigeria and beyond. 
Theological education in Nigeria traces its roots to early missionary activities, which 
focused on preparing indigenous clergy for leadership roles within the church (Fafunwa, 
1974; Sanneh, 1983). Initially, Pentecostal churches like TACN were sceptical of formal 
theological education, favouring direct spiritual empowerment and divine calling over 
academic training (Kalu, 2008). However, as the church grew, it recognised the necessity of 
structured theological education to ensure doctrinal soundness and ministerial 
effectiveness. This marked a shift in the church’s approach, as theological institutions 
became essential to equipping church leaders for the complexities of contemporary 
ministry. 
TACN’s commitment to theological education is evident in the establishment of key 
institutions such as The Apostolic Church Theological Seminary in Amumara, The Apostolic 
Church Bible College and Seminary in Calabar, and The Apostolic Church LAWNA 
Theological Seminary in Jos, among others. These institutions offer programmes designed 
to provide comprehensive training in theology, pastoral care, and leadership, ensuring that 
TACN's ministers are well-prepared for both the spiritual and practical demands of ministry. 
Despite these strides, TACN has faced challenges in advancing theological education. 
Cultural resistance to formal education was a significant barrier in the early years, with 
some Pentecostal contexts viewing academic training as secondary to spiritual 
empowerment and divine experience. Additionally, resource constraints have limited the 
ability of these institutions to expand, affecting both the quality of education and the 
recruitment of qualified faculty. Furthermore, Pentecostalism’s emphasis on experiential 
spirituality, particularly gifts of the Spirit such as healing, prophecy, and speaking in 
tongues, often led to ambivalence toward formal theological training (Anderson, 2004; 
Kalu, 2008). Nevertheless, TACN has made significant progress in balancing spiritual 
empowerment with academic training, emphasising the importance of both in developing 
effective church leadership. 
 
Problem Statement and Objectives 
However, significant challenges remain in advancing and sustaining TACN’s theological 
education programmes. One key issue is the ongoing tension within Pentecostal 
communities between spiritual experiences and formal academic training. Many within 
TACN have historically preferred direct divine calling and spiritual empowerment over 
structured theological education, which has hindered the widespread acceptance of formal 
training. This ambivalence, especially in contexts where charismatic gifts like prophecy and 
healing are highly valued, continues to affect the church's ability to promote theological 
education as essential to ministerial effectiveness.

In addition, TACN’s theological institutions face substantial resource constraints that limit

Conceptual Framework for Pentecostal Theological Education 
Pentecostal theological education has evolved significantly since the early 20th century, 
reflecting the dynamic and diverse nature of the Pentecostal movement. This mode of 
education aims to equip individuals for ministry and deepen their understanding of 
Pentecostal theology, spirituality, and practice. In the world over, Pentecostal theological 
education began with a focus on practical ministry training rather than formal academic 
study. Early Pentecostals were often sceptical of higher education, fearing it might lead to 
a loss of spiritual fervour (Frestadius, 2021). However, as the movement's need for 
structured theological education became apparent, Bible schools and training institutes 
were established to provide foundational biblical and theological knowledge on spiritual 
formation and practical ministry skills (Topf, 2021). This conceptual framework combines 
key elements that define Pentecostal theological training: spiritual empowerment, 
academic rigour, mission-oriented training, and contextualised theology. These 
components reflect the unique characteristics of Pentecostalism, particularly its emphasis 
on the active role of the Holy Spirit in ministry, and the need for practical, mission-driven 
leadership in a diverse and global context. 
 
Spiritual Empowerment 
At the heart of Pentecostal theological education is the belief in spiritual empowerment, 
which emphasises the direct and ongoing influence of the Holy Spirit in shaping ministers 
for effective service (Knoetze, 2022). Pentecostals view the Holy Spirit as essential for 
personal transformation, ministry effectiveness, and congregational leadership (Kgatle,

academic study but also practices that foster spiritual growth, such as prayer, fasting, 
prophecy, and other charismatic experiences. Spiritual empowerment is seen as 
foundational for preparing ministers to lead vibrant, Spirit-filled congregations (Anderson, 
2004). Recent scholarship emphasises the role of experiential spirituality in Pentecostal 
education, underscoring how spiritual formation must go hand-in-hand with theological 
training to cultivate leaders who are sensitive to the Spirit’s guidance (Cartledge & 
Swoboda, 2016). 
 
Academic Rigour 
While Pentecostalism historically emphasised spiritual experience over formal education 
(see Dayton, 1987; Cox, 1995; Alvarez, 2000), contemporary Pentecostal theological 
institutions increasingly recognise the importance of academic rigour (Quampah & Naidoo, 
2000). These institutions now focus on providing in-depth biblical and theological 
knowledge, aiming to produce leaders who are Spirit-filled and intellectually equipped to 
handle complex theological and pastoral issues (Whitt, 2013; Aryeh, 2018). Academic 
training in exegesis, hermeneutics, and doctrinal theology ensures that Pentecostal 
ministers can maintain doctrinal integrity while engaging with contemporary social and 
theological debates (Castelo, 2015). By integrating academic rigour with spiritual 
formation, Pentecostal theological education helps develop leaders capable of navigating 
the challenges of modern ministry while remaining grounded in Pentecostal distinctive 
(Yong, 2010). 
 
Mission-Oriented Training 
Pentecostalism is inherently mission-oriented, and this focus is reflected in its theological 
education programmes (Ott, 2001). Pentecostal institutions prioritise the development of 
practical ministry skills, such as preaching, church planting, and evangelism, aligning with 
the movement’s emphasis on spreading the gospel and fulfilling the Great Commission 
(Akanbi & Beyers, 2017). Students are trained not only in theology but also in leadership, 
pastoral care, and cross-cultural ministry, preparing them for both local and global mission 
work. This mission-driven approach is essential to the Pentecostal worldview, which views 
every believer as a potential minister called to actively participate in advancing the kingdom 
of God (Quampah & Naidoo, 2000; Awojobi, 2020). The training provided in Pentecostal 
seminaries equips leaders to engage in dynamic, Spirit-led ministry that addresses both 
spiritual and practical needs within diverse communities (Nguyen, 2008). 
 
Contextualisation of Theology 
Pentecostal theological education recognises the need for contextualised theology, which 
adapts theological training to the unique social, cultural, and religious contexts of different 
regions (Nguyen, 2008). As Pentecostalism continues to grow globally, particularly in the 
Global South, the need for context-specific theological education becomes more urgent

(Kgatle, 2018). Pentecostal seminaries are increasingly addressing local issues, such as 
indigenous spiritualities, social justice, and community-based approaches to evangelism, 
ensuring that their theological frameworks remain relevant to the cultures in which they 
minister (Kgatle, 2018). For example, in African contexts, Pentecostal theological education 
often engages with African traditional religions, offering a contextualised theology that 
speaks directly to local spiritual and social realities (Asamoah-Gyadu, 2005). By embracing 
contextualisation, Pentecostal institutions ensure that their theology is not abstract or 
disconnected from the lived experiences of their congregations (Gathogo, 2022). 
 
Balancing Spiritual Empowerment and Academic Rigour 
A critical aspect of Pentecostal theological education is balancing spiritual empowerment 
with academic rigour. Historically, Pentecostals viewed formal education with suspicion, 
fearing it could undermine reliance on the Holy Spirit (Banda, 2019). However, as 
Pentecostalism has matured, theological institutions have sought to integrate both 
spiritual formation and academic study, recognising that effective ministry requires a 
combination of spiritual sensitivity and theological competence (Feller & Lombaard, 2018). 
This balance is reflected in curricula that include both academic coursework and 
opportunities for personal spiritual development, such as worship services, retreats, and 
mentorship programmes. Integrating these two elements ensures that Pentecostal leaders 
are Spirit-led and intellectually equipped to engage with theological, social, and cultural 
challenges in their ministry (Quampah & Naidoo, 2000; O'Brien, 2019). 
 
Institutional Support and Global Networks 
The global nature of Pentecostalism has given rise to international networks and 
partnerships that support theological education (Deininger, 2013; Miller, 2013). These 
networks provide resources, facilitate faculty exchanges, and foster collaboration between 
Pentecostal institutions across different regions (Deininger, 2013). For example, institutions 
like the Pentecostal Theological Seminary in the United States of America and the 
Pentecostal Theological College in Africa collaborate through global partnerships to 
enhance theological education standards and share best practices (de Beer, 2020; 
Anderson, 2004). These connections allow Pentecostal institutions to address challenges 
such as limited resources and faculty shortages while promoting ecumenical dialogue with 
other Christian traditions. By fostering international collaboration, Pentecostal theological 
education continues to grow in its ability to produce competent leaders for the global 
church (Nel, 2018).

Several theoretical frameworks undergird Pentecostal theological education. Experiential 
theology, as articulated by John Wesley, emphasises the importance of lived spiritual 
experience in shaping theological understanding. This aligns with Pentecostalism’s focus

theology emphasises the gifts of the Spirit, as explored by theologians like Simon Chan, and 
underscores the importance of maintaining a charismatic focus in theological education. 
Finally, contextual theology, as developed by scholars like Robert Schreiter, highlights the

for Pentecostal institutions operating in diverse regions (Schreiter, 1985). 
 
Methodology 
This study employs a historical and qualitative research methodology to explore the 
theological education’s development, contributions, and challenges within TACN. A 
historical approach was used to trace the evolution of theological education in TACN from 
1931 to the present. Primary and secondary sources, including church records, archival 
materials, and publications were analysed to understand the church's early resistance and 
gradual embrace of formal theological education. Also, semi-structured interviews were 
conducted with faculty, alumni, and current students from TACN’s theological institutions. 
These interviews provided insights into the impact of theological education on ministerial 
effectiveness and doctrinal teaching, as well as the challenges faced by the institutions. 
Thematic analysis was used to identify key patterns from the data. Church reports, 
seminary publications, and institutional documents were reviewed to assess enrolment 
trends, programme offerings, and institutional challenges, providing a comprehensive view 
of TACN’s theological education framework. Informed consent was obtained from all 
interview participants, and confidentiality was ensured by anonymising data. The study 
adhered to ethical standards in collecting and storing information. 
 
Historical Development of Theological Education in Nigeria 
In Nigeria, the development of theological education can be traced back to early missionary 
efforts, to train indigenous clergy to support the missionary agenda. Scholars such as 
Fafunwa (1974) and Sanneh (1983) emphasise that early theological institutions were vital 
in transferring ecclesiastical authority to local leadership, which was essential for the 
sustainability of Christianity in the country. While these early missionary efforts laid the 
groundwork for theological education, they were mainly directed toward mainline 
denominations. 
For Pentecostal churches like TACN, however, the journey toward embracing formal 
theological education has been more complex. Initially, Pentecostals, including TACN, were 
sceptical of academic training, preferring spiritual empowerment and divine calling over 
structured education (Kalu, 2008). This sentiment is echoed by Anderson (2004), who notes 
that Pentecostalism’s emphasis on experiential spirituality often led to resistance toward 
theological formalism. While this perspective helped Pentecostals prioritise spiritual 
authenticity, it also delayed the development of formal educational structures within 
churches like TACN.

The shift in TACN’s stance towards theological education is documented by Worsfold 
(1991), who highlights how the church’s expansion led to the realisation that formal training 
was essential for doctrinal integrity and pastoral effectiveness. This transition allowed 
TACN to align with broader educational movements within Christianity while maintaining 
its Pentecostal distinctives. As a result, TACN began establishing seminaries and Bible 
colleges to train its leaders formally, addressing the challenges of sustaining doctrinal 
consistency across a rapidly growing denomination. 
Theological education plays a pivotal role in shaping church leadership, particularly in 
Pentecostal settings where leadership often emerges through spiritual charisma. Formal 
theological education has significantly contributed to leaders’ intellectual and spiritual 
growth (Knoetze, 2022). It ensures that ministers are spiritually equipped intellectually and 
grounded in biblical interpretation, pastoral care, and leadership strategies. 
Kalu (2008) notes that the balance between spiritual empowerment and academic training 
has been a recurring theme in a broader Pentecostal context. Pentecostal leaders trained in 
theological institutions are often better positioned to lead their congregations through the 
complexities of modern ministry, such as social change, doctrinal challenges, and interfaith 
dialogue. The impact of formal theological education is also seen in its role in shaping 
doctrinal fidelity, a point reinforced by Anderson (2004), who argues that structured 
theological training helps maintain doctrinal consistency in large, growing denominations. 
However, there is ongoing tension within Pentecostal communities, including TACN, 
between experiential spirituality and the perceived rigidity of formal theological training. 
Some argue that academic education could stifle spiritual spontaneity, which has 
historically been a defining feature of Pentecostalism (Ukah, 2008). This tension highlights 
the challenge of integrating formal education with the charismatic aspects of Pentecostal 
leadership. 
 
A Historical Overview of Theological Education within TACN: The Founding of Key 
Seminaries 
Before going into the founding of key seminaries of the TACN, it would be good first to give 
a succinct overview of the antecedent to the founding of theological institutions in the 
church. Also, it is noteworthy to discuss the apprenticeship mode (in-service training) and 
the inception of theological training in the church. 
 
Antecedent to the Founding of Theological Institutions in TACN 
At the inception of The Apostolic Church in Nigeria (TACN) in 1931, theological education 
was largely informal, with a significant portion of indigenous ministers being illiterate 
farmers, artisans, and traders. Apart from European missionaries, only a few leaders like 
Pastors D. O. Odubanjo, S. G. Adegboyega, and J. A. Babatope had any formal education. 
Babatope was the only trained Anglican catechist among them. This educational gap led to 
the development of various forms of ministerial training in TACN, including the

www.afropolitanjournals.com 
apprenticeship model, Sunday school, and eventually formal theological institutions 
(Worsfold, 1991:262). 
While apprenticeship and Sunday school offered semi-formal education, theological 
institutions and religious departments in secular institutions eventually became the primary 
means for formal theological education in TACN. This was crucial for the church’s life and 
mission, as theological education ensured that ministers could lead worship and interpret 
biblical texts in a meaningful way. Historically, the Church had developed schools for lay 
and clergy teachers, organising catechetical systems that provided basic theological 
education to baptismal candidates. Over time, however, theological education became 
more associated with clergy and church professionals, often lacking focus and direction in 
many parts of the global Christian community, including TACN. 
In Nigeria, and particularly among Pentecostals, there has been resistance to formal 
theological education. According to Dada (2007:41), three factors contribute to this 
aversion: first, the belief that a calling into ministry only requires spiritual direction from the 
Holy Spirit, often mediated through prayer, fasting, and spiritual exercises; second, the 
concern that theological education fosters pride and arrogance; and third, the fear that it 
could lead people astray from the faith. These beliefs led to a perception that theological 
education was unnecessary, especially in the early days of TACN. Education, in general, was 
seen by some as antithetical to spirituality, which prompted reliance on informal modes of 
training such as in-service or apprenticeship methods (Interview with Awotanmi, 2012). 
Thus, while theological education is recognised as vital for church growth and leadership, 
early resistance within TACN mirrored broader Pentecostal concerns about its necessity. 
However, over time, theological institutions were established to meet the growing need for 
well-trained ministers capable of both spiritual and intellectual leadership. 
 
Apprenticeship Mode (In-Service Training) 
In the early days of TACN, the apprenticeship mode, or in-service training, played a critical 
role in the development of church leaders. Ministers who claimed to have received a divine 
calling were typically put on probation and placed under the mentorship of experienced, 
often elderly ministers. These senior ministers acted as guardians, guiding the less 
experienced ministers in their pastoral journey. This form of ministerial development was a 
key feature of the church’s early approach to leadership training. 
One of the prominent leaders developed through this system was Pastor I. G. Sakpo, who 
began his ministerial career as a helper under Pastors Esinsinade and D. O. Odubanjo. In 
1931, Pastor J. B. Esinsinade, a national leader of the church, took Sakpo into the Mission 
House at 24 Abasi Street, Ijebu-Ode, where Sakpo learned under his direct supervision 
(Adegboyega, 1978:40). This form of mentorship was critical for Sakpo’s growth in ministry. 
In 1933, he became the first ordained prophet within TACN (TACN, 1993:35). Despite not 
having any formal theological education, Pastor Sakpo ascended to significant leadership 
roles, serving as an international prophet, Apostle, and later as the first vice president and

Similarly, Pastor A. A. Ogundoro followed the apprenticeship mode when he joined TACN’s

Pastor Ogundoro’s leadership was marked by significant church growth and development, 
earning him the reputation of being an exemplary Area Superintendent (Adeleye, 2022). 
The apprenticeship mode or in-service training was effective in shaping TACN leaders, 
despite their lack of formal theological education. It provided practical, on-the-ground 
training that emphasised spiritual growth, leadership, and direct mentorship. Many of these 
leaders would go on to shape the direction of the church, contributing significantly to its 
expansion and development. 
 
The Inception of Theological Training in TACN 
The inception of formal theological training within The Apostolic Church Nigeria (TACN) 
can be traced back to Pastor J. A. Babatope, who was the first among the church’s founding 
fathers to recognise the urgent need for structured ministerial education. Pastor 
Babatope's vision for a Bible college was aimed at providing adequate theological training 
for the church’s ministerial staff. In 1933, he took the first step toward realising this vision 
by starting a rigorous Bible class at a semi-formal Bible school. His initial set of students 
included Pastors Anjorin, Fadahunsi, Odeyemi, and Olatonade. By 1937, more students like 
Pastors Ogundele, S. O. Akindiya, Oluyomi, and Ataba had joined the programme, with 
Babatope serving as both principal and teacher (TACN, 2003:19). 
Understanding the need for additional resources to formalise the school, Babatope sought 
assistance from the authorities of The Apostolic Church in Great Britain during the 1937 
Penygroes Convention. His efforts were fruitful; he constructed a hall for the Bible school 
and stocked its library with essential reference materials, such as Bibles and commentaries 
(TACN, 2003:19). The physical structure of the school was located at Oke-Odo along Omi-
Ayo Street, Oke-Ooye, Ilesa, in Osun State, Nigeria, and it became the foundation of formal 
theological education within TACN. 
In response to Babatope’s appeal, The Apostolic Church in the United Kingdom established 
a Bible college in Ilesa. The foundation stone for the college chapel, later known as the 
Apostolic Teachers’ College, was laid on January 1, 1946. The chapel’s Gothic architectural 
style symbolised the strong foundations of formal theological training that TACN sought to 
build (TACN, 2003:19).

By the 1970s, societal development, technological advancements, and the church's 
exposure to global trends emphasised the need for ongoing training and retraining of 
Christian ministers. Congregations became more sophisticated and demanded more from 
their pastors in terms of biblical knowledge and sermon preparation. Ministers were 
expected to preach with depth, and formal theological training became a necessity. Brock 
highlights this shift in ministry dynamics, asserting that theological education had to evolve 
to meet the increasing expectations of congregants and the complexity of modern ministry 
(Brock, cited in Ogunewu, 2008:60). 
In response, TACN began adopting formal theological training through seminaries and 
religious studies departments in both Nigeria and abroad (Interview with Alakuko, 2013). 
To meet this growing demand for theological education, TACN established various 
institutions, including LAWNA Theological Seminary (LATS) at Ayegbaju in Ilesa, LAWNA 
Bible College at Irojo Quarters in Ilesa, LATS Jos, TAC Bible College in Uyo, TAC Bible 
College in Amumara, and TAC Bible College in Bodo, Rivers State. Additionally, in 
November 2002, TACN expanded its mission to foreign soil by establishing a Bible College 
in Come, Republic of Benin (TACN, 2003:19). 
These institutions played a pivotal role in training the church’s leadership. For instance, 
Pastor Z. A. Alabi, the second national secretary of TACN, was trained both as a theologian 
and as a church leader in the United States. Similarly, Pastor G. O. Olutola, the current 
national president of the church and the chairman of LAWNA Territory, received both 
seminary and secular training (Interview with Ayodele, 2013). Pastor I. G. Fakunle, who once 
led the students at LAWNA Bible College, rose to the position of LAWNA Administrative 
Secretary and became a member of TACN’s national executive council (Adeleye, 2011:5). 
The essence of theological education within TACN is the development of both spiritual and 
intellectual leadership. Theological seminaries focus on providing ministers with the skills 
necessary for proper hermeneutical analysis of the Bible. Hermeneutics – the science of 
interpreting sacred texts – plays a crucial role in ensuring that ministers accurately 
propagate the gospel. Additionally, theological education helps to produce "men of God" 
rather than just skilled individuals, with a focus on enhancing church growth. Key subjects 
taught in seminaries include apologetics, which defends the Christian faith, and 
evangelism, the core of Christian ministry. Furthermore, theological education emphasises 
the importance of maintaining Christ-like relationships and leadership skills, which are vital 
for creating harmony and promoting mutual love within the church (Adeleye, 2011:4). 
The seminaries also foster town-gown relationships, promoting social cohesion through 
education. Theological education helps Christians understand how to maintain harmonious 
relationships, which in turn creates a peaceful environment conducive to evangelism. 
Another essential aspect of TACN’s theological education is the integration of technological 
skills. The use of modern tools, such as mobile phones and social media, has become 
instrumental in evangelising and reaching out to unreached communities (Adeleye, 2011:4). 
Moreover, according to Ayedogbon (2012), theological education in TACN is designed to 
raise the standard of preaching and pulpit ministry. In today’s competitive age, congregants

TACN’s Theological Institutions 
i. 
The Apostolic Church LAWNA Theological Seminary, Ilesa 
The Apostolic Church LAWNA Theological Seminary in Ilesa, Osun State, has a rich history 
that dates back to 1946 when it was first conceived as a Bible Training Centre (BTC) under 
the authority of The Apostolic Church (TAC) in Great Britain. The establishment of this Bible 
School was necessitated by the church’s expanding missionary work, which required the 
services of well-trained evangelists and pastors. The purpose of the training centre was to 
provide basic ministerial education and develop manpower for new entrants into the 
Christian ministry. To support the establishment of this institution, TAC in the United 
Kingdom provided substantial funding, which helped to build the necessary infrastructure 
for the school. The school adopted the motto, For God and Nigeria, symbolising its dual 
commitment to religious service and national development (TACN, 1993:43-44). 
 
Early Developments (1946-1984) 
The first building for the Bible College at Oke-Ooye, Ilesa, was constructed in 1946, serving 
as a space for both classroom instruction and administrative purposes. Before the formal 
establishment of the Bible School, Pastor J. A. Babatope had already initiated a semi-formal 
Bible School where he trained new ministers such as Pastors G. B. Oladele and S. O. 
Akindiya. In his autobiography, Pastor G. B. Oladele mentioned that he attended the Bible 
School in 1943, and was taught by prominent leaders including Pastor Babatope, 
Superintendent Pastor S. G. Elton, and Pastor D. H. McGill (Oladele, n.d.). This earlier 
version of the Bible School laid the groundwork for the formal Bible Training Institute that 
would emerge in 1946 (Ogunleye & Lisoye, 1999:12). 
In 1969, TACN received a prophetic message instructing the church to unify its various 
administrative areas, which were previously linked independently to the International

October 2001. This transformation reflected the church’s commitment to providing a more 
comprehensive theological education for its ministers.

institution’s academic standing and expanded its capacity to train future church leaders. 
The seminary continues to be a key institution within TACN, providing both theological 
education and ministerial training, and contributing to the growth of the church in Nigeria 
and beyond.

The Apostolic Church Theological Seminary, Bodo, Rivers State 
The Apostolic Church Theological Seminary in Bodo, Rivers State, was established as a 
response to the growing need for formal ministerial training in the Maritime Field of The 
Apostolic Church in Nigeria. The seminary was founded in 1978 during the tenure of 
Superintendent Pastor G. Selby. Before the establishment of this institution, TAC in Rivers

On September 23, 1978, The Apostolic Bible College was officially opened in Bodo, which

enrolled in a three-year Diploma in Theology programme. The pioneer principal, Pastor B. 
A. Zorasi, alongside Pastors J. B. Leema and E. G. Kogbara, formed the teaching staff during 
the early years. Between 1982 and 1987, Pastor J. B. Leema served as acting principal while 
Pastor Zorasi pursued further studies. Upon his return in 1987, Pastor Zorasi resumed his 
role and continued to serve the institution until 1992, when Pastor E. G. Kogara became 
acting principal for the following two years (TACN, 2017:7). 
In recent years, The Apostolic Bible College has grown significantly, gaining affiliation with 
Cornerstone University and Seminary of Israel and America, and subsequently being 
upgraded to a full seminary. It now offers degree programmes including Bachelor of Arts 
(B.A.), Bachelor of Theology (B. Th.), and Master of Arts (M.A.) in Theology and Religious 
Education, with courses available at both the main campus in Bodo and its extension 
campus in Yenagoa. 
The seminary expanded its offerings in 1994 by introducing a Bachelor of Theology Degree 
Programme and a Certificate in Theology. This expansion necessitated the creation of new 
administrative positions, such as the Deputy Provost, a role filled by Pastor S. B. Baadom, 
and Registrar, held by Bro. G. A. Obe. The seminary's connection with the Evangelical 
Christian University of America (ECUA), Louisiana, USA, further solidified its academic 
credibility. In 1993, ECUA awarded an honorary Doctorate in Divinity (D.D.) to Pastor B. 
Faara-Vigo, the Rivers Field Superintendent, acknowledging his contributions to

The Apostolic Church Bible College and Seminary, Calabar 
The Apostolic Church Bible College and Seminary, originally known as the Bible Training 
Centre, was established in 1989 with approval from the church council in Calabar (Esuh, 
n.d:259). The creation of the seminary was prompted by the reorganisation following the 
creation of Akwa Ibom State from Cross River State in 1987. Consequently, TAC Akwa Ibom 
Field, which had been part of Cross River Field, became an autonomous entity, and 
properties like the Apostolic Bible College in Nsit Ibom, Uyo, and other TAC assemblies 
located within Akwa Ibom State’s geographical limits were transferred to Akwa Ibom Field. 
The founding principal of the seminary was Pastor I. B. Ita. The Bible Training Centre initially 
operated at 101/31 Marian Road, Calabar, before being moved to The Apostolic Church 
Manse at Queen Duke Street. After some time, it relocated to Ukim Ita for about two and a 
half years, only to later return to its original location at Marian Road, Calabar, where it 
remains today. 
After the death of Pastor I. B. Ita, the church council appointed Pastor Dr B. E. Duke JP as 
the new rector of the institution. Under his leadership, the seminary continued to grow, 
with its admissions policy expanding beyond national boundaries. Pastor Dr D. E. Duke, due 
to administrative and ministerial challenges, eventually handed over leadership to Chaplain 
Dr. Obong E. O. Ita, who was appointed as the acting provost of the seminary on August 1, 
2010.

TAC LAWNA Theological Seminary, Jos, Nigeria 
TAC LAWNA Theological Seminary (LATS) in Jos is one of the key institutions owned by 
TACN's LAWNA Territory, headquartered in Lagos. The seminary was founded in 1994 and 
originally operated from its temporary site at Bida Bidi on Zaria Road. In later years, it 
moved to its permanent site at Gamajigo, off Goodluck Ebele Jonathan Road, in Jos, 
Plateau State. 
The seminary is certified by the Accrediting Council for Theological Education in Africa 
(ACTEA) and affiliated with the University of Jos. It offers regular, summer, and sandwich 
programmes leading to three Diplomas, seven Degrees, a Post-Graduate Diploma in 
Theology (PGDT), and a Master of Arts (M.A.) in Theology and Development Studies. 
TAC LAWNA Theological Seminary provides students with a robust theological and 
practical education, grounded in international development theories and concepts. 
Students receive training in biblical and theological education, ministerial skills, 
evangelism, and peacebuilding, preparing them for diverse roles in ministry. The seminary 
welcomes students from all Christian denominations, without discrimination based on sex, 
nationality, or physical challenges.

Evaluating the Contributions of TACN’s Theological Institutions: Their Role in Shaping 
Doctrinal Teaching and Ministerial Effectiveness 
TACN has established numerous theological institutions over the years, which have played 
a critical role in shaping the church's doctrinal teachings and enhancing ministerial 
effectiveness. These institutions spread across Nigeria and neighbouring countries, 
contribute to the church's mission of equipping ministers with theological knowledge, 
practical skills, and spiritual maturity. Their influence is reflected in the doctrinal 
consistency, growth of the church, and the overall effectiveness of TACN's ministry. 
 
i. 
Shaping Doctrinal Teaching 
TACN's theological institutions have maintained the church's doctrinal purity. As a 
Pentecostal denomination, TACN emphasises the centrality of biblical authority, the work 
of the Holy Spirit, divine healing, and the anticipation of Christ’s imminent return. Through 
structured theological education, these institutions ensure that ministers are well-
grounded in these core beliefs and are capable of transmitting them effectively to their 
congregations.  
For instance, the establishment of The Apostolic Church LAWNA Theological Seminary 
(LATS) in Ilesa and Jos ensured that ministers received formal training in biblical 
interpretation, systematic theology, and Pentecostal distinctives. This doctrinal training 
helps to counter the misinterpretations or dilution of TACN's theological stance. LATS and 
other seminaries have shaped the church’s emphasis on charismatic gifts, aligning ministers 
with the church's teaching on spiritual manifestations and biblical truths (TACN, 1993:44-
46). 
Similarly, The Apostolic Church Bible College and Seminary in Calabar has contributed to 
doctrinal clarity, particularly in southeastern Nigeria. The seminary’s focus on robust 
biblical training and apologetics ensures that ministers are well-equipped to defend the 
faith and articulate TACN's doctrinal positions in a rapidly changing religious landscape. 
This has helped to maintain doctrinal consistency across the diverse regions of Nigeria and 
beyond.

Enhancing Ministerial Effectiveness 
TACN’s theological institutions have significantly improved the ministerial effectiveness of

formal theological education has enabled ministers to handle the increasingly complex 
demands of ministry in contemporary society. Ministers trained at TACN’s institutions are 
now better equipped with practical skills in preaching, pastoral care, leadership, and church 
administration. 
The College Biblique Apostolique Du Benin (COBAB), for example, has trained ministers

The Apostolic Church Bible College in Bodo, Rivers State, also enhances ministerial

programmes in theology, along with leadership training, has enabled ministers to grow 
both spiritually and intellectually. Ministers trained at Bodo are better able to address the

Moreover, TACTSA (The Apostolic Church Theological Seminary, Amumara) provides a 
comprehensive curriculum that covers areas such as homiletics, Christian leadership, and 
moral discipline. These programmes enhance the capacity of ministers to lead their

concepts into its curriculum, equipping ministers with practical skills to address social issues 
such as peacebuilding, interfaith dialogue, and community development. This approach 
enhances the ministers’ ability to engage with broader societal issues, making them more 
effective in their ministerial roles.

Challenges Faced by TACN in Sustaining its Theological Education Programmes 
Despite the significant contributions of The Apostolic Church Nigeria (TACN) in theological 
education, the church faces numerous challenges in sustaining its theological education 
programmes. These challenges impact the effectiveness and reach of its theological 
institutions, limiting their potential to fully serve the church’s mission of producing well-
rounded, doctrinally sound, and spiritually empowered ministers. 
 
i. 
Funding Limitations 
One of the most pressing challenges facing TACN’s theological institutions is insufficient 
funding. The establishment and maintenance of seminaries, Bible colleges, and other 
educational institutions require substantial financial investment. Many of TACN’s 
theological institutions operate on limited budgets, which restricts their ability to attract 
qualified faculty, update infrastructure, and provide necessary resources like libraries, 
research materials, and modern technology. 
The shortage of funds also affects the ability to offer scholarships or financial aid to 
deserving students, reducing access to theological education for individuals from lower-
income backgrounds. For instance, schools like the Apostolic Church Bible College and 
Seminary in Calabar and the LAWNA Territorial Bible College in Ilesa have experienced 
resource constraints that hinder their growth and development. To enhance the 
effectiveness of these theological institutions, TACN must invest in modernising its 
infrastructure, including digital resources and distance learning platforms, to reach more 
students across different regions. 
 
ii. 
Balancing Pentecostal Charisma with Formal Education 
Another significant challenge is the tension between Pentecostal charisma and formal 
theological education. Within TACN, there is a strong emphasis on the direct work of the 
Holy Spirit, spiritual gifts, and charismatic expressions of faith. This has led to scepticism 
among some church members and leaders about the necessity of formal theological 
education.

Some fear that academic training might stifle spiritual spontaneity or foster intellectual 
pride, detracting from the reliance on the Holy Spirit that characterises Pentecostal 
ministry. This aversion to formal education has persisted in parts of the church, making it 
difficult to achieve universal acceptance of theological seminaries, particularly in rural areas 
where charismatic expressions are more prominent. 
 
iii. 
Cultural and Linguistic Barriers 
The diversity of languages and cultures within Nigeria presents another challenge for 
TACN’s theological institutions. While English is the official language of instruction in many 
of these seminaries, a significant number of students come from regions where English is 
not their primary language. This creates a barrier to effective learning, as students may 
struggle to fully grasp the curriculum. 
Additionally, theological education often needs to be contextualised to address specific 
cultural and religious challenges faced in different regions. For example, ministers serving 
in the predominantly Muslim northern regions of Nigeria face different social and religious 
challenges than those serving in the Christian-dominated southern regions. Seminaries 
may not always have the resources or faculty to provide localised or contextual theological 
education that meets the needs of these diverse regions. 
 
iv. 
Resource and Infrastructure Deficiencies 
Many of TACN’s theological institutions lack access to modern resources and infrastructure. 
Digital libraries, online learning platforms, and up-to-date theological texts are often 
unavailable, which limits the ability of students and faculty to engage with contemporary 
theological debates and scholarship. In today’s information-driven world, the absence of 
modern technological infrastructure makes it difficult for these institutions to stay current 
with global developments in theology and ministry. 
The global shift toward digital education, especially during the COVID-19 pandemic, has 
further highlighted the need for technological advancements. For example, TACTSA and 
COBAB have limited access to online learning resources, which could expand the reach of 
theological education to remote areas and non-traditional students. 
 
v. 
Recruitment and Retention of Qualified Faculty 
TACN’s theological institutions also face difficulties in recruiting and retaining qualified 
faculty. Many seminaries rely on part-time or visiting lecturers, which affects the 
consistency and quality of education provided. Limited funding prevents these institutions 
from offering competitive salaries and benefits, which makes it harder to attract and retain 
top-tier theologians and academics. 
The lack of qualified faculty limits the range of subjects that can be offered and affects the 
overall academic rigour of the programmes. Institutions such as TAC LAWNA Theological

Geopolitical and Security Challenges 
Theological education in Nigeria is also impacted by the country’s geopolitical and security 
challenges. In regions affected by religious or political unrest, such as the northern parts of 
Nigeria, students and faculty face disruptions to their studies. Institutions in these areas 
must also contend with the risk of violence, attacks, or instability, which can lead to 
closures, interruptions, and a decline in student enrolment. 
For example, TAC LAWNA Theological Seminary in Jos, located in a region that has 
experienced periodic inter-religious violence, faces significant challenges in maintaining a 
stable learning environment. Students in these areas are often forced to navigate not only 
academic challenges but also safety concerns. 
 
vii. 
Perception of Theological Education 
There is also a broader challenge regarding the perception of theological education within 
the Pentecostal tradition. Many within TACN view theological education primarily as a 
requirement for pastoral ordination, rather than as a lifelong pursuit of spiritual and 
intellectual growth. As a result, some ministers may seek out theological training only as a 
means to advance their careers rather than to deepen their understanding of the faith. This 
utilitarian approach undermines the true purpose of theological education, which is to equip 
ministers for both spiritual and intellectual leadership. 
 
viii. 
Tension between the charismatic, Spirit-led Traditions of the Church and 
the Emphasis on Formal Theological Training 
One challenge is the tension between the charismatic, Spirit-led traditions of the church 
and the emphasis on formal theological training. Some within TACN continue to view 
theological education with suspicion, believing that it can stifle the spontaneous work of 
the Holy Spirit in ministry. Moreover, fostering a balance between Spirit-led ministry and 
formal education will help dispel concerns about the role of theology in Pentecostal 
ministry. A more integrated approach that emphasises the complementary nature of 
spiritual empowerment and theological training will ensure that TACN continues to 
produce ministers who are both doctrinally sound and spiritually dynamic.

education programmes, the following strategies could be adopted: 
 
i. 
Comprehensive Funding Strategies 
TACN should develop comprehensive funding strategies to address the financial 
constraints faced by its theological institutions. This could include establishing endowment

TACN’s seminaries can emphasise the complementary nature of Pentecostal spirituality

Theological education in TACN must be tailored to meet the needs of different cultural and

infrastructure, such as online learning platforms and digital libraries. This would improve

reach of theological education and allow for greater flexibility in course delivery.

Efforts must be made to attract and retain qualified faculty by offering competitive salaries, 
benefits, and opportunities for professional development. TACN should also establish 
faculty exchange programmes with other theological institutions, both in Nigeria and 
internationally, to encourage collaboration and ensure continuous academic improvement. 
 
vi. 
Addressing Security Concerns 
TACN should establish contingency plans to protect students and faculty in regions facing 
geopolitical and security challenges. This could include partnering with local authorities to 
ensure campus security, offering online alternatives for students in affected areas, and 
setting up emergency response systems to minimise disruptions to academic programmes.

Conclusion 
The Apostolic Church Nigeria (TACN) has made notable progress in establishing and 
sustaining theological education, which has been pivotal in shaping the church's doctrinal 
consistency and ministerial effectiveness. From its initial scepticism toward formal 
theological training to the establishment of key institutions like The Apostolic Church 
Theological Seminary in Amumara, The Apostolic Church Bible College and Seminary in 
Calabar, and The Apostolic Church LAWNA Theological Seminary in Jos, TACN has 
demonstrated an increasing commitment to equipping its leaders with the necessary 
intellectual and spiritual tools for ministry. These institutions have played a crucial role in 
maintaining doctrinal fidelity while fostering leadership skills, contributing to the church's 
expansion in Nigeria and neighbouring regions. By offering structured platforms for 
theological and pastoral training, they ensure ministers are well-prepared for the 
contemporary ministry's spiritual and practical challenges. 
However, as this study has shown, TACN faces significant challenges sustaining these 
theological education programmes. Limited financial resources, cultural resistance to 
formal education, and the difficulty of balancing Pentecostal spirituality with academic 
rigour hinder the church’s ability to fully capitalise on its educational infrastructure. To 
address these challenges, TACN must make strategic investments in its institutions, 
focusing on improving infrastructure, integrating technological advancements, and 
enhancing faculty development. Furthermore, there is a need to contextualise theological 
education in a way that reflects the church's unique Pentecostal identity while meeting the 
evolving needs of society. 
Financial constraints of TACN’s theological institutions, as in the analysis, suggest an urgent 
need for diversified funding sources, including partnerships with global theological 
organisations and local church-based funding initiatives. Cultural resistance, particularly 
the perception that academic training undermines spiritual empowerment, can be 
mitigated by integrating spiritual formation into the curriculum and aligning it with the 
charismatic elements that define Pentecostal practice. Additionally, improving faculty 
development by providing opportunities for continuous education and cross-institutional 
collaboration can further strengthen the quality of instruction. 
This study identifies several areas for future research. First, a deeper exploration of the 
specific contributions of individual seminaries, such as College Biblique Apostolique Du 
Benin (COBAB) and The Apostolic Church Bible College in Bodo, could provide insights into 
how these institutions uniquely shape leadership within TACN. Additionally, examining the 
experiences of graduates from these theological institutions would offer a clearer picture of 
how academic training translates into ministerial effectiveness on the ground. Finally, more 
research is needed on the role of women in TACN's theological education programmes, 
which remains an underexplored area despite the growing presence of women in 
Pentecostal leadership roles.
