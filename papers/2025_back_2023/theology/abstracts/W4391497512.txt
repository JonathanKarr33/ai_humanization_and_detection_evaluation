The main focus of interest is the issue of personal responsibility as revealed in the history of Russian culture. The word otvetsvennost (responsibility) is relatively new in Russian vocabulary. Similar, although not exact meanings during the Old Russian period were conveyed by means of the words debt/obligation (dolg), guilt (vina), penance (pokajanie), repayment (vozdajanie), conscience (sovest), virtue (blagodetel). Ten centuries of Russian history have seen variants of the political system promoting the notion of superiority of the collectivist/community orientation over the individualist one. The Orthodox anthropological concept of ‘sobornost’, the Slavophile idea of the peasant commune as the ideal social system for Russia, the principle of krugovaya poruka, the lack of a long-lasting common reference to the Roman legal tradition, the absolute position of sacralised authority, supported by The Russian Church, have been indicated in the text as the main reasons for the dominant feelings of powerlessness experienced by Russian individuals. The experience of total dependence on oppressive authority led to the conviction that they lacked chances for influencing reality. The community itself on the one hand and the authority on the other appeared to be the main sources of moral judgement, leaving very little room for the development of a sense of personal responsibility. Some examples of strong manifestation of feelings of personal responsibility, were results of Western philosophical influences, crucial for Russian sentimentalists. Such conduct frequently revealed its non-typical origin, causing the sense of alienation from the community.
