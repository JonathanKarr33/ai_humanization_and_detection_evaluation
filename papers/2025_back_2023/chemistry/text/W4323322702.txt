Abstract 
25 
Sn-based materials have been demonstrated as promising catalysts for selective electrochemical CO2 
26 
reduction reaction (CO2RR). However, the detailed structures of catalytic intermediates and the key 
27 
surface species remain to be identified. In this work, a series of single-Sn-atom catalysts with well-
28 
defined structures are developed as model systems to explore their electrochemical reactivity towards 
29 
CO2RR . The selectivity and activity of CO2 reduction to formic acid on Sn single-atom sites are shown 
30 
to be correlated with Sn(IV)-N4 moieties axially coordinated with oxygen (O-Sn-N4). Employing a 
31 
combination of operando X-ray absorption spectroscopy, attenuated total reflectance surface enhanced 
32 
infrared absorption spectroscopy, Raman spectroscopy and 119Sn Mössbauer spectroscopy, surface-
33 
bound bidentate tin carbonate species are precisely captured during CO2RR. Moreover, the electronic 
34 
and coordination structure of the single-Sn-atom species under reaction conditions are accurately 
35 
determined. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations further supports the preferred formation of 
36 
Sn-O-CO2 species over the O-Sn-N4 sites, which effectively modulates the adsorption configuration of 
37 
the reactive intermediates and lowers the energy barrier for the hydrogenation of *OCHO species, as 
38 
compared to the preferred formation of *COOH species over the Sn-N4 sites, thereby greatly 
39 
facilitating CO2-to-HCOOH conversion.

Introduction 
43 
The overdependence on fossil fuels leads to an excessive accumulation of greenhouse gas in the 
44 
atmosphere. It is urgent to develop highly efficient and environmentally friendly technologies to 
45 
mitigate the depletion of fossil resources, abate carbon emission and to switch to renewable resources.1-
46 
5 Electrocatalytic carbon dioxide reduction reactions (CO2RRs) offer sustainable opportunities to 
47 
transform CO2 into value-added chemicals and carbon-based fuels.6-8 While various products can be 
48 
produced from CO2RR, some of the C1 products (i.e. formic acid and CO) can be obtained with high 
49 
selectivity and high energy efficiency, and are of great interest to the chemical industry.9-12 Formic 
50 
acid (HCOOH) is widely explored as a hydrogen storage material and chemical fuel for fuel cells.13-15 
51 
Until now, electrocatalysts developed for reducing CO2 to HCOOH have included noble metals (e.g., 
52 
Pd) and transitional-metal-based materials (e.g., Cu, Bi, Pb, In, Sn etc).16-22 
53 
Owing to their unique electronic structure, Sn-based materials have been identified as attractive 
54 
catalysts for electrochemical CO2 reduction to HCOOH and/or CO,23 but suffer from insufficient 
55 
selectivity and activity, especially at large cathodic current densities.24 To this end, past researches 
56 
focused on doping Sn crystalline phases with other metal or nonmetal elements to tune the electron 
57 
density at the Sn active sites and their neighboring dopants to selectively stabilize the reaction 
58 
intermediates and improve the activity and selectivity of CO2RR.19, 25-29 However, the complex 
59 
structure model and the complicated coordination environment of metal sites make it difficult to 
60 
capture the intermediate species and determine the true active sites during the reaction process.  
61 
Single-atom catalysts (SACs) with well-defined and atomically-dispersed metal sites, tunable 
62 
coordination environment and high utilization efficiency, have shown promising CO2RR activity and 
63 
selectivity towards CO, especially Fe-N-C and Ni-N-C materials.30 31 This class of materials provides 
64 
an ideal platform for exploring the structure of active sites and understanding structure-activity 
65 
relationships.32-37 Beyond 3d transition metal SACs, early studies reported that single-Sn-atom sites in 
66 

 
4 
Sn-SACs could selectively reduce CO2 either to HCOOH or CO.2, 10 38 To reveal the intrinsic origin 
67 
for the different CO2RR selectivity of Sn-single-atom sites, it is necessary to pinpoint the exact 
68 
electronic and coordination structure of the catalytic species under CO2RR conditions. This in turn 
69 
requires the rational design of single-Sn-atom materials with well-defined sites and the development 
70 
of advanced operando characterization tools with atomic resolution.  
71 
In this work, a series of Sn-N-C catalysts featuring single-Sn-atoms with tunable and well-defined 
72 
coordination environments were developed. The average Sn(IV)/Sn(II) ratio could be modulated via 
73 
the functionalization of carbon nanotubes, modifying the adsorption energy of Sn phthalocyanine 
74 
(SnPc) molecules. In particular, a model single-Sn-atom catalyst with ~100% axially oxygen 
75 
coordinated Sn(IV) species (O-Sn-N4) was obtained using OH-functionalized carbon nanotubes, 
76 
showing the optimal catalytic activity and selectivity towards HCOOH. Taking advantages of 
77 
operando attenuated total reflectance surface enhanced infrared absorption spectroscopy (ATR-
78 
SEIRAS) and Raman spectroscopy, a surface-bound bidentate tin carbonate species was revealed as a 
79 
key reaction intermediate during CO2RR. Operando 119Sn Mössbauer spectroscopy was applied for 
80 
the first time to follow changes in the electronic and coordination structure of single-Sn-atom species 
81 
under CO2RR. In combination with operando X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), the in situ 
82 
generation of the Sn-O-CO2 catalytic intermediate during CO2RR was clearly identified. Density 
83 
functional theory (DFT) calculations further evidenced the dynamic evolution of the electronic 
84 
structure of O-Sn-N4 site for CO2RR and the catalytic mechanism over single-Sn-atom species was 
85 
unveiled at atomic scale.

Results and discussion 
87 
Characterization of the single–Sn-atom catalysts 
88 
A series of single-Sn-atom catalysts were prepared by immobilizing SnPc molecules on CNTs 
89 
subjected to different functionalization treatments (CNT, CNT-OH and CNT-NH2) via π-π 
90 

 
5 
interaction.39 The respective electrocatalysts are labelled SnPc/CNT, SnPc/CNT-OH and SnPc/CNT-
91 
NH2. The synthesis of SnPc/CNT-OH is schemed in Fig. 1a, and fully detailed in Methods and 
92 
Supplementary Fig. 1. The X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of this series of materials show only one 
93 
diffraction peak assignable to the diffraction line of carbon nanotubes, suggesting that the SnPc 
94 
molecules are well-dispersed and therefore cannot contribute to the diffraction patterns 
95 
(Supplementary Fig. 2). In contrast, Raman spectra display signals from both molecular SnPc and 
96 
CNTs for all materials (Supplementary Fig. 3), indicating that SnPc was successfully loaded onto the 
97 
CNTs. The Sn contents were in the range of 0.37-0.66 wt. % ( Supplementary Table 1). Figure 1b 
98 
and Supplementary Fig. 4 show representative scanning electron microscope (SEM) and transmission 
99 
electron microscope (TEM) images of the catalysts, on which no aggregation of metal species could 
100 
be observed. The surface morphology of SnPc/CNT-OH is similar to that of CNT-OH, indicating 
101 
uniform dispersion of SnPc on CNT-OH, which is also confirmed by energy-dispersive X-ray 
102 
spectroscopy (EDX) mapping analysis (Supplementary Fig. 5). Furthermore, high-angle annular 
103 
dark-field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF-STEM) image of SnPc/CNT-OH in 
104 
Fig. 1c clearly shows Sn atoms dispersed on CNT and the absence of Sn clusters or nanoparticles, 
105 
further suggesting the successful formation of single-Sn-atom catalysts. 
106 
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed to examine the elemental compositions 
107 
and Sn valence states of the single-Sn-atom catalysts. The Sn 3d XPS spectrum of the commercial 
108 
SnPc can be deconvoluted into two peak groups assigned to Sn(II) (486.6 eV, 495 eV) and Sn(IV) 
109 
(488.5 eV, 496.4 eV) (Fig. 1d and Supplementary Fig. 6).40 For the series of catalysts, the 
110 
Sn(IV)/Sn(II) ratio increases strongly in the order SnPc ˂ SnPc/CNT-NH2 ˂ SnPc/CNT ˂ SnPc/CNT-
111 
OH (Supplementary Table 2 and Supplementary Fig. 6), with relative Sn(IV) amounts increasing 
112 
from 18.1 % to 100 %. In particular, only Sn(IV) is observed for SnPc/CNT-OH (Fig. 1e), indicating 
113 
that OH- functional groups on CNT selectively lead to Sn(IV) possibly due to the strength of 
114 
electrostatic interactions, which is supported by DFT calculations. As compared in Supplementary 
115 

 
6 
Fig. 7, CNT-OH exhibits an adsorption energy for Sn(IV) species of -2.379 eV, more negative than 
116 
those of CNT-NH2 and CNT (-1.946 and -2.229 eV, respectively), suggesting that the CNT-OH surface 
117 
more likely adsorbs Sn(IV) species than the other two types of carbon nanotubes. 
118 
To further determine the coordination environment and the electronic structure of the single-Sn-
119 
atom species, we conducted room temperature 119Sn Mössbauer spectroscopy measurements. As 
120 
shown in Fig. 1f, the commercial SnPc shows two quadrupole doublets with distinct values of isomer 
121 
shift (IS) (0.03 and 2.72 mm s-1) and quadrupole splitting (QS) (0.70 and 1.39 mm s-1) (Supplementary 
122 
Table 3). The doublets with low and high IS can unambiguously be assigned to Sn(IV) and Sn(II), 
123 
respectively.41 Moreover, the IS and QS values for the main high-IS doublet of the SnPc commercial 
124 
product match well with those reported for Sn(II)Pc.42 Interestingly, only one doublet with an IS value 
125 
of 0.04 mm s−1 and a QS value of 0.50 mm s−1 was observed for SnPc/CNT-OH (Fig. 1g), which can 
126 
be unambiguously assigned to Sn(IV) species. Based on IS and QS values calculated for a set of 
127 
hypothetical site structures, the fitted parameters of this doublet match well with those calculated by 
128 
DFT for the axially oxygen coordinated Sn(IV) species (O-Sn-N4) structure (Supplementary Fig. 8).  
129 
As displayed in Fig. 1h, the absorption edge of Sn K-edge X-ray absorption near edge structure 
130 
(XANES) spectrum of SnPc/CNT-OH shifts towards higher energies compared to that of SnPc, 
131 
suggesting a higher average Sn valence state in the SnPc/CNT-OH catalyst,26 in line with XPS analysis. 
132 
The Fourier transforms of the extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectra of both SnPc 
133 
and SnPc/CNT-OH exhibit the dominant Sn-N coordination at 1.5-1.7 Å, distance not corrected for 
134 
phase shift (Supplementary Fig. 9). No obvious peaks related to Sn-Sn coordination could be 
135 
observed, confirming the atomic dispersion of all or most of the Sn atoms. In addition, the EXAFS 
136 
spectra for SnPc and SnPc/CNT-OH could be well fitted with the structural models of Sn-N4 and O-
137 
Sn-N4 (as optimized from DFT), respectively (Supplementary Fig. 10). The fitted parameters are 
138 
summarized in Supplementary Table 4. As depicted in Fig. 1i and Supplementary Fig. 10, the Sn 
139 
atom in SnPc is coordinated, on average, with four adjacent N atoms (Sn-N4), whereas the Sn atom in 
140 

 
7 
SnPc/CNT-OH, on average, has four neighbouring N atoms and one axially coordinated O atom (O-
141 
Sn-N4). All these results clearly show the successful development of a model single-Sn-atom catalyst 
142 
with 100% axially oxygen coordinated Sn(IV) species anchored on OH-functionalized carbon 
143 
nanotubes.

Electrochemical CO2RR performance 
153 
The electrochemical CO2RR performance of SnPc/CNT, SnPc/CNT-OH and SnPc/CNT-NH2 were 
154 
examined in an H-type cell filled with CO2-saturated 0.5 M KHCO3 (pH = 7.3) electrolyte, and the 
155 
CO2RR catalyst loading was fixed at 1 mg·cm−2.43 The CO2 reduction activity of the single-Sn-atom 
156 
catalysts was first investigated by linear scan voltammetry (LSV) measurements. As shown in Fig. 2a, 
157 
the current density for CO2 reduction over SnPc/CNT-OH is the highest among this series of catalysts. 
158 
Fig. 2b summarizes the potential-dependent CO2RR Faradaic efficiency for SnPc/CNT, SnPc/CNT-
159 
OH and SnPc/CNT-NH2. The main CO2RR product is HCOOH, along with a small amount of CO, 
160 
while H2 is produced in significant quantities on all three catalysts. The HCOOH Faradaic efficiency 
161 
over SnPc/CNT-OH can reach 75.4% at -1.13 V vs. RHE, which is higher than that over SnPc/CNT 
162 
(48%) and SnPc/CNT-NH2 (18%). The same trend is observed at other potentials, SnPc/CNT-OH 
163 
systematically leading to higher FE towards HCOOH than the other two catalysts. As displayed in 
164 
Supplementary Fig. 11, the SnPc/CNT-OH exhibits an optimal HCOOH partial current density 
165 
(jHCOOH) of -14.3 mA·cm−2 at -1.13 V vs. RHE, higher than that over SnPc/CNT (-5.2 mA·cm−2) and 
166 
SnPc/CNT-NH2 (-2.4 mA·cm−2). Besides excellent activity and selectivity, both the FE towards 
167 
HCOOH and the current density remained nearly constant over SnPc/CNT-OH during 10 h at -1.13 V 
168 
vs. RHE, indicating promising stability (Fig. 2c). The correlation of Faradaic efficiency, partial current 
169 
density, and relative contents of Sn(II), Sn(IV) and nitrogen species are shown in Fig. 2d and 
170 
Supplementary Fig. 12. The data  reveals that the catalytic performance towards formic acid 
171 
formation is positively correlated with the relative content of Sn(IV) (Supplementary Fig. 12d). This 
172 
suggests that the Sn(IV) single atom species is the dominant catalytic site for electrochemical CO2 
173 
reduction to formic acid, in line with recent reports.44

Identification of the catalytic intermediates and the surface reaction species 
183 
XRD, TEM and XPS characterizations of the SnPc/CNT-OH catalyst were performed and 
184 
compared before and after CO2RR, to investigate if an irreversible structural evolution occurred 
185 
(Supplementary Fig. 13-15). Interestingly, no obvious morphology and valence state changes were 
186 

 
10 
observed, further confirming the high stability of the SnPc/CNT-OH catalyst during the CO2RR 
187 
process. 
188 
To gain insights into the surface reaction intermediates over the single-Sn-atom catalyst during 
189 
electrochemical CO2 reduction to HCOOH, operando ATR-SEIRAS was carried out.45 Control 
190 
experiments were firstly conducted in Ar-saturated 0.1 M K2SO4 solution (Supplementary Fig. 16); 
191 
only one peak centered at 1238 cm-1 was observed, which can be assigned to SO42- in the electrolyte. 
192 
During the potential stepping from 0 to -1.5 V vs. RHE and from -1.5 V vs. RHE to 0 in CO2-saturated 
193 
0.1 M K2SO4 solution (pH = 4.5), the intensity of the absorption peak at 1650 cm−1 originating from 
194 
water vibration was observed to slightly increase when stepping the potential from 0 to -1.5 V vs. RHE, 
195 
and to decrease for the same potential step but in the opposite direction (Fig. 3a and b). The weak 
196 
bands  at 1950 and 2000 cm−1 are related to the linearly bonded CO (COL) because of the CO2 to CO 
197 
reaction pathway on CNT-OH (Supplementary Fig. 17). Additional absorption peaks at 1285 and 
198 
1115 cm−1 appeared at -0.8 V vs. RHE and their intensities increased when further decreasing the 
199 
potential from -0.8 V to -1.5 V vs. RHE. These peaks can be ascribed to the C-O bond vibration in the 
200 
bidentate tin carbonate.46 The intensity of the tin carbonate peaks slowly decreased and finally 
201 
disappeared when reverting the potential scan from -1.5 to 0 V vs. RHE. All these results clearly 
202 
evidence the involvement of bidentate tin carbonate intermediates during the CO2RR over SnPc/CNT-
203 
OH.  
204 
Operando Raman spectra recorded at varying potentials in CO2- or Ar-saturated 0.5 M KHCO3 
205 
solution are shown in Fig. 3c-d and Supplementary Fig. 18-19.47 As displayed in Fig. 3c, at open 
206 
circuit potential (OCP), two characteristic Raman peaks at 1342 cm−1 and 1505 cm−1 were observed 
207 
on SnPc/CNT-OH, which can be assigned to C=C and C-N vibrations of the phthalocyanine ring, 
208 
respectively.48 With potential decreasing from -0.7 to -1.2 V vs. RHE, the peak intensity of C=C and 
209 
C-N gradually decreased and eventually vanished, which is most probably due to the generation of 
210 
surface-bound tin-carbonate (bidentate) species with a symmetric structure. Upon switching the 
211 

 
11 
electrode potential back to OCP, the Raman spectrum could almost be restored to that recorded under 
212 
the initial OCP condition, suggesting that the potential-induced changes for the SnPc/CNT-OH catalyst 
213 
are reversible. Moreover, operando Raman spectra measured in Ar-saturated 0.5 M KHCO3 solution 
214 
showed no significant changes in peak intensity, further reflecting that the observed reversible change 
215 
of characteristic Raman peaks probably resulted from the in situ formation of surface-bound bidentate 
216 
tin carbonate species, which was captured by the operando ATR-SEIRAS measurements.

To further probe the structure of the catalytic intermediates, operando XAS measurements were 
224 
performed. The Sn K-edge XANES and EXAFS spectra were recorded in CO2-saturated 0.5 M KHCO3 
225 
solution at various potentials. For the XANES spectra (Fig. 4a), a negligible reduction was observed 
226 
for the Sn K-edge of SnPc/CNT-OH during the CO2RR, revealing that the oxidation state of Sn in 
227 
SnPc/CNT-OH could be maintained at Sn(IV). In addition, in the EXAFS spectra shown in Fig. 4b, 
228 
the slight increase of the Sn-N (or Sn-C or Sn-O) intensity suggests an increase of the coordination 
229 
number (CN) of Sn as the CO2 reduction reaction proceeds. However, negligible changes in the Sn-N 
230 
bond length were observed, indicating that the coordination environment did not change significantly 
231 
during the CO2RR. 
232 
The electronic and coordination structure of the single-Sn-atom species under CO2RR conditions 
233 
47, 49, 50 were further investigated using operando 119Sn Mössbauer spectroscopy in a home-made H-
234 
type operando cell (Supplementary Fig. 20). The CO2-saturated 0.5 M KHCO3 electrolyte was 
235 
replaced every ~1.5 h to maintain the pH of the solution. The current-time response of SnPc/CNT-OH 
236 
during the entire operando Mössbauer measurements was quite stable, supporting that the setup 
237 
performs well and the operando spectroscopic data are reliable  (Supplementary Fig. 21). As shown 
238 
in Fig. 4c, at -1.1 V vs. RHE, the Mössbauer spectrum of SnPc/CNT-OH is different from that of the 
239 
fresh sample, displaying a symmetric signal with an IS value of 0.03 mm s−1 and a QS value of 0.89 
240 
mm s−1, matching well with the theoretical Mössbauer spectrum of Sn-O-CO2 (Fig. 4d). The 
241 
experimental IS and QS values of the catalyst under various operando conditions as well as the 
242 
theoretical IS and QS values of Sn-O-CO2 are shown in Fig. 4e and Supplementary Table 5. The 
243 
results support the in situ formation of Sn-O-CO2 species over O-Sn-N4 sites during CO2RR. After 
244 
removing the applied potential, the Mössbauer spectrum could be fitted by a quadrupole doublet with 
245 
an IS value of 0.07 mm s−1 and a QS value of 0.50 mm s−1, similar to that of the fresh SnPc/CNT-OH, 
246 
further suggesting the reversibility of the single-Sn-atom species during the CO2RR.

The dynamic changes observed in the operando Mössbauer and XAS measurements could be 
248 
further illustrated by the calculated projected density of states (PDOS) of O-Sn-N4 and Sn-O-CO2. As 
249 
shown in Supplementary Fig. 22, similar electron density encloses in the s and p orbital of Sn for O-
250 
Sn-N4 and Sn-O-CO2, which results in the negligible changes in the K-edge of operando XANES 
251 
spectra and IS value of operando Mössbauer spectra. However, compared with Sn(IV) in O-Sn-N4, 
252 
the d orbitals (dxz and dyz) of Sn-O-CO2 change from degeneration to non-degeneration (Fig. 4f), which 
253 
leads to an increase in the asymmetric distribution of the electrons and thus an increase of QS. All 
254 
these results clearly evidenced the in-situ formation of Sn-O-CO2 species over O-Sn-N4 sites during 
255 
the CO2RR.24

Theoretical insights into the catalytic mechanism 
266 
Theoretical calculations were performed to further investigate the mechanism of CO2RR over the 
267 
single-Sn-atom catalysts. The optimized structures of O-Sn-N4 and Sn-N4, and the possible adsorption 
268 
configurations of reaction intermediates are shown in Supplementary Fig. 23. Fig. 5a shows the 
269 
Kohn–Sham MO energy-level correlation diagram between CO2 and the O-Sn-N4 site during the first 
270 
CO2 capture step (* + CO2 → *CO2). There exist covalent overlaps between the fully occupied dz2 
271 
orbital of the O-Sn-N4 site and the non-occupied π* orbital of CO2, generating one fully occupied dz2-
272 
π* bonding orbital (BD) and one empty dz2-π* antibonding (BD*) orbital. Similarly, the fully occupied 
273 
dxz orbital of the O-Sn-N4 site and the 2pz orbital of CO2 also generate two fully occupied dxz-2pz 
274 
bonding orbital (BD) and dxz-2pz antibonding (BD*) orbital. The corresponding MO contour plots of 
275 
these MOs are displayed in Supplementary Fig. 24. 
276 
We further calculated the Gibbs free energy diagram of CO2RR to form HCOOH and CO over O-
277 
Sn-N4 and Sn-N4. As shown in Fig. 5b, for the pathway of generating formic acid, the formation of 
278 
the *HCOOH intermediate via *OCHO/HCOO* hydrogenation is the potential-limiting step; whereas, 
279 
for producing CO (Supplementary Fig. 25), the potential-limiting step is the formation of *COOH. 
280 
The corresponding energy barriers of CO2-to-HCOOH and CO2-to-CO on O-Sn-N4 and Sn-N4 are 
281 
summarized in Fig. 5c. Specifically, the downhill energy barrier of the first CO2-to-*CO2 step of 
282 
CO2RR on O-Sn-N4 is calculated to be -0.58 eV, while Sn-N4 exhibits a downhill energy barrier of -
283 

 
15 
0.13 eV, revealing that the CO2 activation can take place more readily on O-Sn-N4. Furthermore, O-
284 
Sn-N4 exhibits a relatively lower energy barrier at the formation of *HCOOH (ΔG = 0.8 eV) than Sn-
285 
N4 (ΔG = 0.93 eV), which shall lead to a higher electrocatalytic activity for CO2RR to HCOOH over 
286 
O-Sn-N4. Additionally, Sn-N4 displays a much stronger adsorption of *COOH with ΔG = 0.78 eV than 
287 
O-Sn-N4 with ΔG = 1.02 eV, resulting in a better catalytic ability for the generation of CO over Sn-
288 
N4. As illustrated in Supplementary Fig. 26-27, we also assumed that CO2 approached the single-Sn-
289 
atom sites from the back side of O-Sn-N4 (O-Sn-N4-inversion). From the Gibbs free-energy diagram, 
290 
such a convex inversion shows a much higher energy barrier for the formation of both *COOH and 
291 
*OCHO intermediates, thereby prohibiting both the HCOOH and CO production. Therefore, the 
292 
reaction pathways of CO2RR over O-Sn-N4 and Sn-N4 sites are presented in Fig. 5d and 
293 
Supplementary Fig. 28-29. The O-Sn-N4 species can regulate the adsorption configuration of *CO2, 
294 
which effectively reduces the energy barrier for the formation of *OCHO intermediate and facilitates 
295 
the CO2-to-HCOOH conversion.

302 
Conclusions 
303 
In summary, a series of single-Sn-atom catalysts with well-controlled coordination and electronic 
304 
structure were developed to explore the CO2 reduction chemistry. Using a combination of operando 
305 
spectroscopies and DFT calculations on a model single-Sn-atom catalyst in which all Sn atoms are in 
306 
a  positive IV oxidation state, situated in a phthalocyanine ring and additionally coordinated to an axial 
307 

 
17 
oxygen species (O-Sn-N4), the in situ generation of surface-bound bidentate tin carbonate species (Sn-
308 
O-CO2) in CO2RR was captured and the dynamic change of the electronic and coordination structures 
309 
of active sites during CO2RR was identified. Theoretical studies further revealed that the O-Sn-N4 
310 
species could regulate the adsorption configuration of *CO2 with increasing the asymmetric 
311 
distribution of the d orbital electrons of Sn(IV), reducing the energy barrier for the formation and 
312 
hydrogenation of *OCHO species, thus facilitating CO2-to-HCOOH conversion. The results of this 
313 
work provide an in-depth understanding of the detailed structures of catalytic intermediates and the 
314 
key surface species associated with CO2RR and shall pave the way towards the rational design of high-
315 
performance CO2 electroreduction catalysts.
