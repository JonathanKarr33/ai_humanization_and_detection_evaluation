Abstract 
Hydrogen produced with no greenhouse gas emissions is termed “green hydrogen” and will be essential to 
reaching decarbonization targets set forth by nearly every country as per the Paris Agreement.  Proton 
exchange membrane water electrolyzers (PEMWEs) are expected to contribute substantially to the green 
hydrogen market. However, PEMWE market penetration is insignificant, accounting for less than a 
gigawatt of global capacity. Achieving substantive decarbonization via green hydrogen will require 
PEMWEs to reach capacities of hundreds of gigawatts by 2030. This paper serves as an overarching 
roadmap for cell-level improvements necessary for gigawatt-scale PEMWE deployment, with insights from 
three well-established hydrogen technology companies included. Considerations for economies of scale, 
renewable energy prices, governmental policies, durability testing, and component-specific improvements 
will be presented, with focus centralized on necessary scientific research goals.

1. 
Introduction  
Nearly every country has joined the landmark Paris Agreement to achieve global net zero emissions by 
2050. Hydrogen will be essential to reach these targets as it can mitigate emissions for sectors without 
promising decarbonization solutions, such as grid-scale energy storage, long-haul transportation, and 
industrial manufacturing.1,2 It can be used electrochemically to generate electricity or as a chemical 
feedstock to produce commodities such as steel, cement, ammonia, and other valued chemicals.3 Since 
hydrogen usage does not generate greenhouse gases (GHG), as long as the energy used to generate 
hydrogen comes from clean power sources (e.g., solar, wind, geothermal, and nuclear), the hydrogen can 
be deemed “green.” 
A proton exchange membrane water electrolyzer (PEMWE) is a promising green hydrogen 
technology that utilizes electricity to split water into oxygen and hydrogen. When paired with clean power 
sources, PEMWEs can mass-produce green hydrogen and are expected to make up 40% of the green-
hydrogen market, with the remaining 60% composed of alkaline and solid oxide electrolysis.4 However, 
market penetration of green hydrogen is insignificant compared to GHG-intensive hydrogen generation 
methods due to high production costs ($4.0-$9.0/kg vs. $1.0-$2.5/kg).5 As of 2022, global green hydrogen 
capacity was 11 GW (1GW can produce ~500 tons H2 daily6),7 with PEMWEs making up only 0.3 GW. To 
achieve net zero emissions by 2050, it is projected that PEMWE capacity will need to reach 220 GW by 
2030 and 1130 GW by 2050.4 
 
1.1 
PEMWE Overview  
Three types of water electrolyzers are expected to make up the green hydrogen market: alkaline, solid 
oxide, and proton exchange membrane. While alkaline electrolysis—characterized by having two 
electrodes operating in a liquid alkaline electrolyte—has become fully commercialized, its inability to 
operate under highly dynamic loads and at high current densities and pressures restricts its potential for 
large-scale production.8,9 Solid oxide electrolyzers—characterized by high operating temperatures 
(>500°C) and the use of a ceramic electrolyte—are highly efficient and may hold promise. However, its 
developmental stage is still early,9 with only a handful of companies selling these products. We refer the 
reader to the literature for detailed analyses of the different electrolyzer types.8–12 PEMWEs possess several 
characteristics that make them suitable for widespread green hydrogen production: high efficiencies, long 
lifetimes, large range of operational current densities (up to 3 A cm-2)13,14, fast dynamic response times, and 
ability to operate at differential pressure.9 
A generic schematic of a PEMWE cell is shown in Figure 1. Liquid water is fed into the anode side 
where the oxygen evolution reaction (OER) occurs, generating oxygen gas bubbles, protons, and electrons. 
The oxygen exits the cell while the electrons and hydrogen ions (which carry water via electro-osmotic 
drag)15 travel—via current collectors and the membrane, respectively—to combine at the cathode, where 
the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) occurs to generate hydrogen gas.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

 
 
 
 
A PEMWE cell consists of a proton exchange membrane (PEM), two catalyst layers, two transport 
layers, and two separator plates. The PEM (~100 µm)16 is responsible for maximizing protonic 
conductivity while compartmentalizing the oxygen gas to the anode and hydrogen gas to the cathode. Since 
gas permeability is inversely correlated with ionic conductivity,17 achieving low permeability while 
keeping conductivity high is a hallmark challenge. Sandwiching the membrane are two catalyst layers (~1-
10 µm),18 which serve as the electrodes of the system—typically consisting of an iridium-based catalyst for 
the anode and a platinum-based catalyst for the cathode.8 Outside each catalyst layer are diffusion layers: 
the gas diffusion layer (GDL, 100-300 µm)19 on the cathode side and the porous transport layer (PTL, ~250-
500 µm)20 on the anode side. The GDL is primarily responsible for gaseous transport (hydrogen gas out), 
while the PTL is responsible for liquid and gaseous transport (liquid water in; oxygen gas out). Both the 
PTL and GDL require establishing electronic conduction with the adjacent catalyst layer, but the PTL is 
subject to much higher potentials, necessitating material limitations.8 The last component on each side of 
the cell is the separator plate (~1.5 mm),21 which contains flow pathways to distribute reactants (water) 
and remove products (oxygen and hydrogen gas).22 Please note that in a stack, numerous cells are 
combined in series, so separator plates will contain flow fields on both sides (unless they are located at the 
ends). Thus, due to their two-sided nature, separator plates are also called bipolar plates (BPs). Commercial 
stacks contain many cells (~30-200),23,24 each with active areas of ~1000-3000 cm2, with total power 
capacity between ~500 kW-3 MW.  
Although hydrogen is the primary product of PEMWEs, oxygen can also have valuable uses.25 
Interestingly, some PEMWE designs even specialize in oxygen production.26 The oxygen can be used in 
medicine (heart/lung disease patients), defense (fighter aircraft and submarines), spacecraft (life 
support27), and industry (food processing, welding, metallurgy, semiconductors), to name a few. In any 
case, safely handling hydrogen and oxygen is necessary as mixtures present an explosive hazard. Often, 
hydrogen is diluted with air or inert gases (such as nitrogen) and released into the atmosphere to prevent 
ignition. Alternatively, the hydrogen can be circulated to a fuel cell system to feed power back to the 
PEMWE, increasing overall system efficiency.28

1.2 
Configuration Variations 
To demonstrate the versatility of PEMWEs, an overview of their configurations will be presented and the 
advantages to each. To date, four configurations have seen potential in the literature: conventional 
operation, high-pressure operation, high-temperature operation, and cathode water feed operation. 
Conventional operation is characterized by pressures of ~30 bar and temperatures of 50-90°C.9 
Operational pressure is typically defined by hydrogen pressure as the oxygen is usually vented upon 
generation (and thus is at ambient pressure). Pressure is generated directly from hydrogen gas being 
produced, assuming the membrane can withstand any associated mechanical stress.29 This so-called 
“differential pressure” operation provides benefits over isobaric operation because less downstream 
mechanical compression will be required. However, increased pressure comes greater hydrogen crossover 
into the anode, resulting in serious safety concerns. Crossover could result in ignitable hydrogen and 
oxygen gas mixtures on the anode side. Only a 4% volume of hydrogen in oxygen would form an ignitable 
safety hazard (i.e., 4% is the lower explosive limit or LEL).30 Thus, given current safety limitations, state-
of-the-art PEMWEs do not exceed 50 bar. However, as discussed in the next paragraph, there is 
considerable potential to operate at much higher pressures. Regarding temperature, higher operating 
temperatures lead to voltage efficiency improvements.31 In conventional operation, heat is supplied to the 
cell and produced by electrochemical losses to maintain a temperature of 50-90°C. However, increasing 
temperatures above the boiling point of water poses a unique set of challenges as 1) state-of-the-art 
membranes (i.e., PFSA membranes) rely on liquid water-assisted proton conduction, and 2) vapor-fed 
operation is not well studied.32 Potential strategies to enable high-temperature operation will also be 
discussed in this section. 
High-pressure operation is an attractive alternative to conventional operation as substantial 
pressurization or liquefaction of hydrogen is required for many end-use cases: 200 bar for gaseous storage, 
~300 bar for the Haber Bosch process, 350 bar for heavy-duty fuel cell vehicles, and 700 bar for passenger 
fuel cell vehicles.33 Since hydrogen is typically produced at 30 bar, adjusting the electrolyzer to output at 
greater compressions could reduce equipment count, process complexity, capital costs, and maintenance 
costs, while lowering the energy required to perform final compressive steps.33 In the literature, high-
purity hydrogen at differential pressures up to 350 bar has been shown.34 However, gas crossover 
problems that already exist in conventional operation are exacerbated under higher pressures.33 Strategies 
for mitigating crossover will be discussed in Section 4.3.  
Higher operating temperatures result in a lower reversible cell potential (e.g., 1.14 V at 200°C versus 
1.23 V at 20°C35) and lower activation overpotentials.32 However, increased component corrosion (e.g., BPs 
and PTLs) and decreased proton conductivity (due to membrane dehydration) could also result.31 Thus, 
corrosive-resistant materials need to be implemented, and strategies to ensure proton conductivity need 
to be developed—such as incorporating organic fillers into the membrane (to enhance water uptake)36–42 
or increasing the water pressure at the anode.31 
 
Lastly, novel configurations are also possible and can enable performance improvements. One 
design by Hystar43 changes the water inlet from anode to cathode and adds air flow to the anode. With 
these changes, Hystar claims that membrane thickness can be reduced by ~85% (from state-of-the-art 
125µm to 20µm) since any crossover hydrogen will be diluted with the anode air feed, mitigating the risk 
of forming an ignitable gas mixture. Such thin membranes could reduce ohmic losses and increase 
efficiency substantially, as ohmic overpotentials currently comprise a significant portion of voltage losses 
(~40% at a standard operating current density of 2 A cm-2).18 However, several challenges must be 
addressed, including removing N2/CO2 contaminants in the hydrogen outlet.  
 
1.3 
Motivation 
PEMWEs will require cost and performance improvements to compete economically against conventional 
forms of hydrogen production.44 The roadmap for GW-scale deployment is complex, but this paper will 
capture many considerations: deployment targets, government policies, production/capital costs, 
economies of scale, component-specific improvements, durability testing, and safety regulations. Please 
note that this paper will focus on cell-level components instead of stack and balance of plant aspects. Two 

factors distinguish this review from others: 1) broader economic and political considerations will be 
discussed along with scientific research goals, and 2) less focus will be put on the current state of PEMWEs, 
but rather on improvements necessary to reach GW-scale deployment. For reviews on the current state of 
PEMWEs, we refer the reader to the numerous articles in the literature.8,18,45–48  
This paper will be presented as follows: First, global green hydrogen policies will be overviewed to 
orient the reader to the current hydrogen landscape. Then, targets for PEMWE capital costs and hydrogen 
production costs will be outlined. Next, component-specific research will be discussed in the context of 
cost, efficiency, and durability improvements. While research efforts are required for every component, 
emphasis will be put on the anode catalyst layer, membrane, and PTL. Afterwards, durability testing 
protocols (i.e., accelerated stress tests) will be proposed for each component. Penultimately, we will zoom out 
and have three industrial companies—NEL Hydrogen, Plug Power, and an undisclosed company—offer 
their unique perspectives on how global efforts can be directed for PEMWE expansion. To conclude, all the 
insights in this paper will be summarized to present a concise roadmap for GW-scale deployment. 
 
2 
Deployment Targets 
Global hydrogen demand was 94 million tons (Mt) in 2021, accounting for nearly 2.5% of global energy 
consumption. Nearly half of this demand went towards refining hydrocarbons (46%). The rest was 
directed to the production of ammonia (35%), methanol (15%), and steel (4%)—by direct reduction of 
iron.49 Transport is a small but growing source of demand, on the order of 30,000 tons (0.03% of global 
demand), corresponding to a 60% increase over the previous year.50 This demand was primarily for fueling 
trucks, buses, and passenger vehicles, with the potential to expand in the rail, shipping, materials handling, 
and aviation sectors.  
 
The push to meet hydrogen demand has led to the deployment of MW-scale systems in recent years. 
Recent deployments include 1) the world-first floating offshore PEMWE in France, utilizing a 1 MW system 
from Plug Power,51 and 2) a 1.5 MW load-balancing PEMWE in Germany that feeds hydrogen directly into 
a natural gas grid.52 When considering projects under construction and in the final investment decision 
(FID) stages, numerous multi-MW systems can be seen.53

For hydrogen production there are a several distinctions drawn by method of production, and often 
a distinction drawn between “green” and “clean” hydrogen. Clean hydrogen is a more general term to refer 
to processes that result in relatively little or no carbon emissions. Clean hydrogen includes green hydrogen 
but also includes processes such as “blue” hydrogen, where hydrogen is produced via steam methane 
reformation with carbon capture and underground storage (CCUS). Blue hydrogen (and in some cases, grey 
hydrogen—or hydrogen directly produced from SMR without CCUS) is presented as a bridging device112 
to meet short-term demands for hydrogen while green hydrogen technologies gradually become online. 
Short-term demands of clean and green hydrogen include the steel industry of the European Union113 as 
well as transport fuels to support the development of a green hydrogen transport fuel market. In this vein, 
certification schemes for tracking the greenhouse gas emission intensity of hydrogen (e.g., distinguishing 
between clean, green, blue, grey, and other forms of hydrogen) are under development, such as with the 
European Union’s CertifHy114 and Australia’s Guarantee of Origin (GO) scheme.115 
When considering the production of green hydrogen from a life cycle analysis (LCA) standpoint, 
water electrolysis entails the lowest natural resource costs, human health impacts, and ecological impacts, 
when combined with wind or nuclear power (as opposed to solar photovoltaics, due to their relatively 
intense environmental impact from a life cycle standpoint).116–118 When considering external cost factors 
(human health impacts, ecological impacts, resource depletion), electrolysis coupled with wind-power is 
one of the most cost effective methods of hydrogen production, green or otherwise.117 Lastly, in terms of 
global warming potential, water electrolysis is slightly more carbon intensive than biomass gasification 
with carbon capture (which has the potential to reach net negative CO2 equivalent emissions), although the 
latter entails far greater emissions of criteria pollutants and thus has higher externalized cost factors over 
its lifetime.117,119

3 
Cost Targets 

The United States Department of Energy (US DoE) announced cost targets of $2/kg H2 by 2026 and $1/kg 
H2 by 2031 for clean hydrogen. In 2021, hydrogen production costs ranged from $1.0-2.5/kg H2 for steam 
methane reformation (SMR), $1.5-3.0/kg H2 for SMR with carbon capture and $4.0-9.0/kg H2 for 
electrolysis.49 A multitude of factors—including gas supply constraints, energy security concerns ignited 
by the invasion of Ukraine, and the exiting of a global pandemic—led to surging natural gas demand and, 
consequently, prices as high as $85-153/MWh around much of Europe and significant parts of Asia after 
holding steady in a range of ~$10–23/MWh since 2015.128 Thus, for the affected regions, hydrogen 
production costs (via SMR without carbon carbon) skyrocketed to $4.8-7.8/kg H2 in Europe and $5.3-8.6 
$/kg H2 in Asia, equating to $121-198/MWh and $134-218/MWh, respectively.129 While natural gas prices 
may decline in the next few years to 2015 levels, and indeed have already returned to pre-pandemic levels 
across much of the globe, such volatility highlights the potential for cost-competitive electrolytic hydrogen 
powered by diverse and potentially more reliable renewable resources. Furthermore, SMR production of 
hydrogen is highly sensitive to the cost of natural gas. Without carbon capture, SMR consumes 
approximately 0.039–0.055 MWhnat gas/kg H2, while SMR with carbon capture consumes 0.077 MWhnat 
gas/kg H2.119,130 Thus, during volatile natural gas price swings—as recently experienced in Asia and 
Europe—the price of hydrogen from SMR can increase substantially; for example, if natural gas prices rise 
from $20/MWh (2015 levels) to $100/MWh (2022 levels), the cost of SMR-produced hydrogen would 
increase $3.1-6.2/kg H2 (depending on whether carbon capture is used), quickly closing the price gap 
between SMR and electrolysis. 
 
3.1 
Cost Factors 
 
3.1.1 Electricity Costs  
The ability for electrolytic hydrogen to become cost competitive is strongly dependent upon the availability 
of low-cost electricity.131 However, even with relatively cheap electricity prices of $0.05-0.06/kWh, the cost 
of the electricity alone to make electrolytic hydrogen would equate to $3/kg (with no consideration for 
capital or other operating expenditures), making it difficult to compete with SMR (with total costs being 
$1.0-2.5/kg H2).132 Nonetheless, thanks to the increasing availability of lower cost renewable electricity in 
the form of solar and wind power, electricity prices equivalent to $0.02/kWh have been observed, which 
would enable $1/kg H2 in electricity costs.133 To meet a competitive cost point for electrolytic hydrogen, 
capital expenditures (CapEx) of electrolysis—defined as the cost of the electrolyzer and ancillary 
equipment, along with installation and other fixed costs—would also need to meet an equivalent of $1/kg 
H2 whilst utilizing this low-cost electricity. This may be currently feasible with high capacity factors 
(defined as the % of system utilization), but for pairing electrolysis with renewable energy sources that 
provide low-cost electricity, capacity factors on the order of 30-40% are more realistic.134,135  
 
3.1.2 Renewable Energy Coupling 
Increasing capacity factor is the second most important cost factor, behind only electricity costs (Figure 
4).136 However, to couple PEMWEs with renewable energy, periods of low capacity factors will be 
inevitable. Thus, the temptation is to constantly have PEMWEs draw power from the grid, even during such 
periods. However, coordinating with the grid to provide energy during these times is not always possible 
and is generally prohibitively expensive.  Furthermore, even if there is enough power locally, utilities prefer 
PEMWEs only to draw power during periods of excess renewable supply so as not to exasperate grid 
balancing issues. Thus, grid control algorithms and management strategies are required.

Since current PEMWEs are optimized for constant load operation, load fluctuations from renewable 
energy coupling can also accelerate the degradation of critical components, leading to lower system 
lifetimes. For example, anode CLs are especially sensitive to such fluctuations and can be the limiting factor 
in stack lifetimes.137 BoP components (e.g., pumps, driers, and hydrogen compressors) can also suffer from 
intermittent operation. Since system lifetimes directly influence overall H2 costs, innovative strategies to 
provide more consistent power—such as utilizing energy storage, dynamic controls, or multiple renewable 
sources—are necessary. Lastly, degradation can be minimized via intelligent control strategies for larger 
systems.

given the limitations of electricity costs and renewable energy coupling, reductions in CapEx will be 
necessary to meet hydrogen cost targets.

reductions in capital costs than those stated by the DoE are required for both PEMWE and renewable 
electricity sources.139 The OpEx of electrolytic hydrogen can be reduced by R&D towards more efficient or 
durable PEMWEs and by operating in lower current density regimes (although the latter entails a trade-off 
with increasing CapEx due to low capacity factors, leading to variable optimal current density regimes as a 
function of electricity costs).13 
Current CapEx of PEMWEs are observed in a wide range of around $800-1500/kW. 140–144 Future 
capital costs associated with PEMWE have been estimated in the literature by several methods including 
literature surveys,131,145 industry surveys,146,147 learning curve estimates,144,148 and bottom-up cost 
analyses.142,149 Across the breadth of these studies, future capital costs of PEMWE are generally expected 
to hit the range of $200-600/kW, with high variation largely due to differences in assumptions regarding 
1) the rate of production scale-up, and 2) the impacts of economies of scale and learning rates on cost 
reduction.150 Figure 6 shows several recent cost estimate projections using a variety of techno-economic 
(TEA) methods, along with notable cost targets based on decarbonization goals. Cost targets clearly hinge 
on optimistic CapEx reduction scenarios or lie outside of current projections. Considerable research and 
development are required to bridge this gap between CapEx targets and current cost projections.

3.1.4 Carbon Taxes 
Before discussing methods to reduce CapEx, it should be noted that placing a price on carbon emissions for 
hydrogen production—either in the form of carbon credits or carbon taxes—can be a significant economic 
driver for green hydrogen. For PEMWEs to reach cost parity with SMR-based H2 production, a carbon price 
of $230-550/ton CO2e (“e” is global warming potential equivalent) is required. SMR with carbon capture is 
expected to reach cost parity with SMR (no carbon capture) with a more modest $50-60/ton CO2e cost of 
carbon.53 Carbon markets have grown tremendously in recent years, with 24% of global emissions covered 
by some form of a national carbon pricing program. Notably, emissions trading systems (ETS) such as the 
E.U. ETS and California’s Cap and Trade Program experienced prices of $60/ton CO2e and $40/ton CO2e  for 
the year of 2023.  
 
3.2 
CapEx Reduction 
The primary driver to decreasing CapEx for PEMWE over the next decade is increasing manufacturing 
capabilities in both unit size and production volume, with companies aiming for MW-scale unit sizes 
(compared to kW-scale) and GW/yr production capacities. In addition to improving equipment utilization 
and spreading fixed costs (such as labor) over a greater production capacity, these cost reductions also 
entail implementing more effective manufacturing, which include automation and roll-to-roll assembly.155  
PEMWE systems consist of the stack and the balance of plant (BoP), which provides the fluids 
handling, thermal conditioning, power conditioning, and controls needed to facilitate the reactions 
occurring in the cells. According to a detailed TEA of CapEX as a function of stack size (Figure 7), a cost 
split of roughly 40% stack and 60% BoP is observed at lower manufacturing volumes for 1 MW systems,149 
which is generally in-line with other analyses.134,141,142 As production rates scale-up, the BoP expenditures 
becomes increasingly dominant, comprising upwards of 70% of the manufacturing costs. A third of the 
manufacturing costs can be attributed to power supplies alone, which is required to convert high voltage 
AC or DC input sources to the low voltage and high current DC electricity needed for electrolysis. AC/DC 
thyristor-type rectifiers are most commonly utilized due to their reliability, technological maturity, and 
comparatively low cost.156 The effect of economies of scale is presumed to not be as impactful on balance 
of plant components, which are largely outsourced. This was observed to be the case with BoP costs for 

PEM fuel cell (PEMFC) power plants as they scaled up, particularly with the AC/DC power conditioning 
equipment.157

At the stack level, cost can be broken down into CCMs, PTLs, GDLs, BPs, end plates, frames, balance 
of stack, and assembly labor. Balance of stack refers to additional components such as wires, insulation, 
and compression hardware and can make up around 13% of the stack cost.158 In order of contribution, 

CCMs are the most expensive component, followed by PTLs, BPs, and then end plates and framing. At higher 
production volumes where labor costs are lower, material costs begin to dominate and MEAs can comprise 
nearly half the stack cost due to the expensive membrane material and the platinum group metal (PGM) 
catalyst used. PTLs are constructed out of titanium and bipolar plates are most commonly stainless steel, 
both of which require coatings that are typically gold or platinum. 
In order to reach the U.S. DoE CapEx target of $150/kW by 2031, stack production costs need to be 
brought down significantly from current values of $237/kW. Several pathways have been identified to 
reduce stack costs, primarily focused on reducing material costs, increasing  system efficiency, or achieving 
higher current density capabilities.159 Although current densities as high as 15 A·cm-2 have been 
observed,160 obtaining greater current densities alone is not sufficient to reduce costs as such high current 
operation is only beneficial when low cost electricity is available.161,162 Thus, focus needs to also be put on 
reducing material costs and improving stack efficiency, both of which will require the optimization of 
individual components at the cell level. 
 
4 
Component Improvements 
This section will discuss all the primary components in a PEMWE cell and establish goals for rapid 
deployment. Challenges in fundamental research and large-scale considerations—such as cost and 
manufacturability—will be thoroughly reviewed. 
 
4.1 
Anode Catalyst Layer   
A conventional anode catalyst layer (CL) consists of a catalyst dispersed in an ionomer (an ionically 
conductive polymer) network coated on either a membrane—in the case of a catalyst-coated membrane 
(CCM) configuration—or a PTL—in the case of porous transport electrode (PTE) configuration. 
Additionally, membrane electrode assembly (MEA) is a general term that collectively refers to the 
membrane and adjacent CLs, with no distinction between CCM and PTE configurations. Multiple methods 
to coat a CL onto a substrate (e.g., a membrane or PTL) can be employed, such as airbrushing, ultrasonic 
spraying, or doctor blade coating.163 The MEA configuration and CL fabrication method can influence CL 
properties; such impacts have been thoroughly discussed in a review by Wang et al.163 Thus, this section 
will focus on smaller-scale considerations, such as loading reductions, catalyst utilization, catalyst supports, 
and CL microstructure. 
The catalyst must be stable under the highly acidic (pH~0) and oxidizing (>1.3V) conditions of the 
anode, which currently limits the material selection to precious metals.164 While ruthenium is one of the 
most active OER catalysts, its lack of stability has resulted in iridium being the primary choice.8 Iridium 
oxide (IrOx) is the most commonly used form since metallic iridium is less active.165,166 Activity and 
durability are highly dependent on the surface state of the iridium, with amorphous IrOx showing higher 
activity and lower durability than crystalline IrOx.167 Mom et al.167 attributed these properties of 
amorphous IrOx to greater deprotonation depth under high potentials. However, the catalyst can undergo 
chemical changes when experiencing varying cell potentials or being in contact with H2 (from cathode 
crossover). Rheinlander et al.168 reported that crystalline IrO2 can be transformed to amorphous IrOx or 
even metallic Ir (based on operating conditions). Thus, changes in the catalyst state should be meticulously 
considered, especially during long-term testing. 
Minimizing the amount of iridium used in anode CLs is vital for reaching cost targets. Between the 
use of platinum in the cathode and iridium in the anode, iridium is the bottleneck in scale-up due to its 
rarity (7.5 tons of iridium is mined per year, compared to ~200 for platinum169).170 In the literature, 
notable progress has been made to reduce loadings from >1 mgIr·cm-2 to below 0.5 mgIr·cm-2.171–177 
However, further efforts must be made to enable large-scale fabrication of these low-loading CLs, such as 
1) obtaining a fundamental understanding of interfacial transport mechanisms, 2) identifying degradation 
modes, and 3) ensuring quality control at greater production capacities.163 Understanding transport 
mechanisms by unveiling the interplay between electrons, protons, water, and oxygen in low-loading CLs 
is required to achieve comparable performance with high-loading CLs. Moreover, identifying degradation 

modes will enable us to tune CL structure for improved durability. Lastly, translating laboratory-scale 
fabrication methods to large-scale production for low-loading CLs is of utmost importance for scale-up but 
has largely been overlooked in the field.  
 
4.1.1 Catalyst Utilization 
Optimizing transport processes in the CL (illustrated in Figure 8) is a prerequisite for high catalyst 
utilization at low loadings. To sustain the OER, the CL must facilitate the transport of protons, electrons, 
liquid water, and gaseous oxygen. Protons move through the ionomer phase in the CL to the membrane. 
Electrons are transported through iridium particles in the CL to the grains in the PTL. Notably, isolated 
iridium particles and particles that are fully embedded in the ionomer phase will likely not conduct 
electrons as ionomers are electronically insulating. Lastly, the PTL allows liquid water to permeate through 
its pores (in the scale of tens of micrometers)178 to the pores in the CL (in the scale of nanometers)179 while 
simultaneously facilitating oxygen gas removal. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 8. Transport processes occurring at the CL-PTL interface. Reactant liquid water transports from PTL pores to CL 
pores. Product gases flow in the counter direction: from CL reaction sites to PTL pores. Ionic transport occurs through the 
ionomer phase and the membrane, while electrical transport occurs through the iridium in the CL and particles in the PTL. 
 

The interface where PTL, catalyst, and ionomer meet—termed the “triple-phase boundary”—plays 
a vital role in catalyst utilization as the OER can only occur here. Notably, iridium particles that do not 
directly contact the PTL can still participate in the OER if they are electrically connected to the PTL through 
other particles. At low iridium loadings, such a connected catalyst network is less likely to exist. Thus, 
particles not directly contacting the PTL are less likely to be utilized, reducing the triple-phase boundary. 
Consequently, when CLs swell (simply due to water uptake from the ionomer) into the void space of the 
PTL interface—known as delamination—the iridium particles in the CL spread apart and become more 
disconnected, leading to lower electronic conduction and, consequently, low catalyst utilization.180 At low 
loadings, poor catalyst utilization resulting from delamination is particularly severe due to the low content 
of iridium particles.  
While low iridium loading MEAs have shown high performance in some cases,163 strategies to 
alleviate issues of electronically insulated—and thus unutilized—iridium particles will likely be required. 
Due to the dependence of catalyst utilization on both the CL and PTL, strategies to improve utilization 
regarding the PTL will be discussed in Section 4.4.4. However, strategies for the CL also exist. The first is 
proposed by Hegge et al.,181 where isolated iridium particles are bridged together by depositing a layer of 
iridium fibers (fabricated via electrospinning) on top of them. The second is by Chatterjee et al.,182 where 
utilization was enhanced by completely changing the catalyst morphology: instead of iridium nanoparticles, 
the authors synthesized nanoporous iridium nanosheets (via electrochemical dealloying of nickel-iridium 
foils) that provide a continuous, connected structure. While these two methods are more novel, utilizing 
catalyst supports to increase utilization has been widely studied. 
 
4.1.2 Catalyst Supports  
 
In principle, dispersing a catalyst on a support should increase its surface area to mass ratio, 
reducing the catalyst mass needed to achieve a desired current. Thus, supports hold significant potential 
for loading reductions. As detailed below, the critical challenge is that the most commercially available 
support, TiO2, is electrically insulating; thus, the benefit of increased catalyst dispersion is partially 
countered by electrically isolating the catalyst. Many research efforts have long sought to develop 
conductive support materials, either by doping TiO2 or finding new materials entirely, but none have 
achieved long-duration durability testing as TiO2 has. If TiO2 is used, the iridium oxide must act as the 
electron conduit (not the TiO2). Therefore, connected pathways of iridium oxide on the catalyst support 
and within the CL are needed to ensure iridium utilization. 
 
4.1.2.1 Material Selection  
For supports to be viable, the material must be stable in the operating conditions. The highly acidic (due to 
the presence of protons from the OER) and oxidizing conditions of the anode render most support 
materials—conductive carbon black included—thermodynamically unstable.183 Most of the remaining 
(more) stable materials are semiconducting metal oxides, which present inherent conductivity concerns. 
Nonetheless, several novel catalyst supports have demonstrated promising performance in rotating disk 
electrode and/or MEA setups, including TaC,184,185 Sb2O5-SbO2 (ATO),186–188 NbC ,185 TiC,185 WC,185 NbO2,185 
and W2B.189 Their accomplishments notwithstanding, this review will not cover these supports given the 
scarce literature with low loadings and stability testing at industrially relevant current densities (greater 
than 1 A·cm-2). We refer the reader to a review on emerging supports,190 and we look forward to more 
progress in novel designs. 
TiO2 as a support has increasingly been tested at industrially relevant current densities with low 
iridium loadings and thus will be the focus of this section. The interest in TiO2 as a support for the OER can 
be traced back to the commercial implementation of RuO2 on TiO2 via dimensionally stable anodes (DSAs®) 
for the chlorine evolution reaction, which has an even higher potential than OER (1.358 V vs SHE).191 This 
technology established the relevance of metal oxides as anode electrocatalysts and supports. There are 
primarily two commercial IrOx/TiO2 (i.e., IrOx supported on TiO2) catalysts in recent literature: the Umicore 
Elyst Ir75 (IrO2/TiO2, diameter ~28 nm)168,192–197 and the Heraeus P2X (IrOOHx/TiO2, diameter 
~284 nm).194,195,197 

 
4.1.2.2 Resistivity Issues  
Despite its high stability, TiO2 supports come with a caveat: high electronic resistivity. While a common 
catalyst support for PEMFCs (Vulcan-XC72) has a resistivity of ~0.4 Ω·m, values for TiO2 (in nanowire form) 
are upwards of 4000 Ω·m.198 Several studies have attempted to bypass this issue by using more reduced 
forms of titanium. At low loadings (<0.3 mgIr·cm-2), IrO2/Ti has been shown to increase ECSA and overall 
outperform unsupported IrO2.199 Others have studied Magneli phase titanium oxides (i.e., 
substoichiometric oxides of the form TinO2n-1), finding increased electronic conductivity relative to TiO2200 
and enhanced performance (using a PTE configuration) relative to unsupported MEAs.201 However, 
whether these supports remained in their reduced state throughout operation was left unaddressed.  
 
Since IrO2 films and single crystals have resistivity values multiple orders of magnitude lower than 
TiO2 (10-8 Ω·m),202,203 it is believed that the deposited IrOx on the titanium support provides the electronic 
conductivity within the CL. In principle, if a core-shell structure is achieved, there will be complete 
conductivity and IrOx utilization. In practice, however, complete films of IrOx on TiO2 are not simple to 
prepare. Instead, electronically isolated islands of IrOx on TiO2 often result. Thus, to achieve high electronic 
conductivity within the CL, interconnected networks of IrOx long enough to reach catalyst particles not in 
direct PTL contact would be required. This type of network would reduce the probability of electrically 
disconnected catalyst particles and thereby maximize catalyst utilization.  
 
4.1.2.3 Electronically Conductive Networks 
Two strategies have been employed to help enable electronically conductive networks: optimizing CL 
thickness and developing core-shell-supported catalyst structures. 
CL thickness is a metric correlated with catalyst utilization, as a CL that is too thin will have less 
interconnected iridium particles and thus a lower probability of forming a complete in-plane (parallel to 
the membrane) electron conduction network throughout the layer.  Hence, it is necessary to maintain a 
certain CL thickness to avoid such issues. Through cross-sectional SEM, the thickness of the CL is shown to 
be linearly proportional to iridium loading.204 Through experiments using Umicore Elyst Ir75 (IrO2/TiO2) 
with various Ir loadings between 1-2 mgIr·cm-2, Bernt et al.193 proposed an optimal anode CL thickness of 
4–8 µm. The lower bound (4 µm) ensures adequate in-plane electronic conductivity, while the upper bound 
(8 µm) limits mass transport overpotentials, as thicker layers suffer from water transport issues. In a later 
study,194 Bernt et al. calculated that a low packing density Heraeus P2X would require a loading of 0.06 
mgIr·cm-2 to form a 4–8 µm thick CL, as opposed to the 0.56 mgIr·cm-2 required for Umicore TiO2. While the 
logic of using larger support particles to enhance CL thickness (without increasing Ir loading) is sound, 
increased CL thickness does not necessarily equal increased CL conductivity if percolating catalyst 
networks are not present.  
Several studies have sought core-shell structures where the percolation of IrOx coatings would 
provide the needed electronic conductivity at low loadings.197,205,206 De Angelis et al.207 demonstrated 
connected films of IrO2 supported on TiO2 in a CL via X-ray ptychographic tomography (a non-destructive 
3D imaging technique), although the loading was very high—3 mgIr·cm-2. Given the well-connected 
pathways for IrO2, the authors concluded that for “any CL where the percolation of the IrO2 is ensured, any 
high-surface area support material can be employed, even if fully electrically insulating.” The concept has 
been extended to PTE configurations as well, with one study featuring IrO2/TiO2 core-shell catalysts (0.4 
mgIr·cm-2) that performed better than the Umicore IrO2/TiO2 catalyst (0.5 mgIr·cm-2).208 While increased 
electronic conductivity is beneficial, it is possible that CL conductivity is not as critical for PTEs as for CCMs, 
given that the catalyst in PTEs is directly deposited on PTL particles, eliminating concerns of unutilized 
catalyst within PTL pores.  
 
In summary, using TiO2 as a support is not a guarantee of increased catalyst utilization as the 
insulating nature of TiO2 can render iridium oxide particles electrically isolated and therefore unutilized, 
unless there is a robust network of connected iridium oxide domains. That said, there have been 
demonstrations of promising performance at loadings below 0.4 mgIr·cm-2, even after thousands of hours 
of testing.209,210 Further investigations in creating percolating iridium oxide pathways under low loadings 

are warranted. Lastly, alternative materials and doped TiO2 are showing promise and should be examined 
for long-term durability, as finding a conductive support (that is stable) would eliminate this percolation 
challenge. 
 
4.1.3 Durability 
While researchers seek to lower catalyst loadings to meet cost targets, care must be taken to ensure that 
durability is not compromised during the process. However, as reviewed by Wang et al.,163 only six MEA 
studies have examined iridium durability at loadings ≤ 0.4 mgIr cm-2. Hence, mechanisms of degradation 
and parameters that exacerbate degradation still need to be identified for low-loading systems.  
To understand iridium degradation mechanisms, Yu et al.173,176 conducted a microscopic analysis of 
an ultra-low loading CCM (0.08 mgIr·cm-2) under industrial conditions (1.8 A·cm-2 constant load, ~30 bar 
pressure, 86 cm2 active area). They found that 70% of the initial iridium was lost after 4500 h of operation. 
Numerous other studies have also found significant iridium dissolution over time, with the membrane, 
cathode CL, and water streams (anode and cathode) being the primary sinks for subsequent 
redeposition.137,211–214 
Parameters shown to be highly degrading to the anode catalyst are OCV operation, dynamic 
operation, and low iridium loadings. Weiß et al.215 investigated the impact of a 1.3 V hold versus an OCV 
hold—where voltage decreases to 0V—in a cycling profile. Two protocols were used (Figure 9a): An OCV 
test (3 A/cm2, 0.1 A/cm2, and OCV steps) and a reference test (3 A/cm2, 0.1 A/cm2, and 1.3V steps). The 
former resulted in more degradation due to the OCV conditions reducing crystalline IrO2 (the starting 
material) to metallic Ir and then oxidizing it to amorphous IrOx (which is less stable). Overall, they found a 
very high degradation rate of ~300µV/h for this test and, surprisingly, a negative degradation rate 
(performance increased over time) for the reference test (both used a high anode loading of 1.75mgIr/cm2). 
Incorporating the OCV hold instead of the 1.3 V hold led to faster iridium dissolution and increased HFR 
(due to the IrO2 transforming to a less-conductive hydrous iridium oxide).

Regarding dynamic operation and loadings, Alia et al.216 examined durability at low loadings (0.1-
0.4 mgIr/cm2) and reported that potential cycling resulted in greater degradation than potential holds. 
Furthermore, square wave cycling was more damaging than triangle wave cycling, suggesting that sharper 
transitions accelerate degradation. They also found that greater cycling frequencies and higher upper 
potentials (Figure 9b) accelerate degradation. Moreover, the impact of catalyst loading on degradation 
was also investigated. While they observed minimal degradation at 0.4 mgIr·cm-2, meaningful degradation 
was found at 0.1 mgIr·cm-2. The authors hypothesized that degradation is not inherently loading-dependent, 
but rather, the extra thickness of the CL at higher loadings acts as a buffer that delays the onset of 
degradation. A 1000 h low-loading MEA stability test by Rozain et al.209 backs up this hypothesis, as they 
found that two CLs with similar thicknesses but a different catalyst and loading (IrO2 at 0.1 mgIr cm-2 and 
pure IrO2 at 0.32 mgIr cm-2) had comparable durability.  
Standardized protocols to characterize degradation will need to be developed to test durability 
more quickly (i.e., without having to operate cells over thousands of hours). Furthermore, standardization 
will allow the community to objectively compare MEAs with different loadings and morphologies. For 
instance, different fabrication methods (e.g., blade coated vs. spray coated) result in distinct CL 
morphologies and the nature of their degradation under low catalyst loadings can be significantly more 
sensitive.163 Thus, universal protocols are a prerequisite for efficient research in developing next-
generation MEAs. Recommendations for such protocols will be discussed in Section 5.1. 
 
4.1.4 Ionomer Binders 
As mentioned previously, CLs typically consist of catalyst particles dispersed in an ionomer network. The 
whole layer must expose the surface of catalyst particles for reactions, maintain charge conducting 
pathways, enable reactant transport to these catalyst sites, and remove reaction products—all of which 
are impacted by the ionomer. Please note that since ionomers are used for both the membrane and CL, 
“ionomer binders” will be used to refer CL ionomers only. In commercial MEAs, the ionomer binder has an 
identical/similar chemical composition to that of membrane ionomers. Some ionomer properties (such as 
ionic conductivity) are beneficially optimized in the same direction for membrane and binders. Other 
properties that are critical to membrane applications prove detrimental to binder applications, including 
high electrical resistance and low gas permeability. As there does not exist a single material appropriate 
for all properties, a similar (often identical) electrode ionomer is employed to enable ionic conduction 
within the catalyst layer, which has a strong impact on the overall optimization of catalyst layer ionic 
conductivity, electronic conductivity, and mass transport. 
The GW-scale deployment of electrodes in PEMWE can leverage decades of fundamental 
understandings of catalyst/ionomer integration from PEMFC research. For example, Raistrick 
demonstrated that by replacing PTFE with an ionomeric binder, platinum utilization was improved to the 
point where loadings could be reduced by an order of magnitude (leading to their ubiquitous inclusion in 
PEM devices).217 Baker et al.218 and Greszler et al.219 identified an unknown transport resistance in a fuel 
cell cathode associated with the ionomer, which was later demonstrated to arise from a local oxygen 
transport resistance due to ionomer orientation at the platinum/ionomer interface.220,221 The findings from 
these studies suggest the importance of considering a holistic set of properties of binder candidates and 
how they impact transport properties in PEMWE CLs, especially as iridium loadings are driven lower.  
While much can be learned from PEMFC research efforts, novel electrolyzer-specific configurations 
are also being explored that have the potential to influence ionomer selection and where it is 
deployed.  Where most electrolyzers feed water to the anode directly, other systems rely on back-diffusion 
from the liquid-fed cathode222 or water vapor anode feeds,223 which could influence the optimal electrode 
composition at the anode in unexpected ways. For example, Peng et al.224 demonstrated a promising MEA 
strategy whereby a low loading of iridium oxide was deposited directly onto the PTL layer, avoiding the 
need for ionomer in the anode entirely. The role of ionomer in scalable, durable, and high performance 
electrodes in PEMWE systems will require further investigation to enable deployment at scale. 
 

4.1.5 MEA Fabrication 
Finally, large-scale production methods for low loading CLs requires more development. The majority of 
the low loading MEAs tested in the literature uses the ultrasonic spray coating technique, which requires 
a catalyst ink with relatively low solids ratios (< 1 wt%). 175,177,181,225–227 However, this technique is not 
suitable for large-scale production due to its low throughput.226 Blade coating techniques on the other hand 
(e.g., doctor blade coating, rod coating, and slot die coating), are more scalable but require inks with higher 
solids ratios (> 5 wt%),227 which tend to result in inhomogeneous catalyst distribution when lower 
loadings are targeted. Thus, more research is required to enable large-scale fabrication of low loading 
MEAs, likely via blade coating techniques. For example, understanding the key parameters of catalyst inks 
that will allow for uniform CLs, and minimizing catalyst loss from ink intrusion into PTL pores during PTE 
fabrication. More examination in topics such as these will be essential in curtailing PGM usage. 
 
4.2 
Cathode Catalyst Layer   
Platinum black supported on carbon (Pt/C) is the standard cathode catalyst for PEMWEs. Studies on Pt-
free catalysts have been reviewed228,229 but remain an active research area. Given the sparsity of durability 
studies with such catalysts, this section will be limited to Pt-based catalysts.  
Although studies utilizing Pt-based cathode catalysts typically assume negligible cathode 
contributions to cell overpotential, the true contribution may not be obvious, especially when considering 
open circuit conditions (relevant during startup and shutdown conditions). On the one hand, one model 
estimated cathode overpotentials (for a single cell) of less than one mV at 3 A cm–2 230 and one experimental 
study found that performance was insignificantly affected by Pt loading reductions from 0.30 mgPt cm–2 to 
0.025 mgPt cm–2.204 However, a modeling study of a 12-cell, 5.6 kW stack found that cathode overpotentials 
can reach up to 63% of the anode overpotential when the cathode pressure is increased to 70 bar.231 
4.2.1 Overpotentials  
Cathode overpotentials were not experimentally verified in (single-cell) PEMWEs until the introduction of 
in-situ reference electrodes, which was recently reviewed.232 In one study with such an electrode 
(3 mg cm–2 Pt black for the cathode, 3 mg cm–2 Ir0.7Ru0.3O4 for the anode), when the current was switched 
from 1 A cm–2 to open circuit conditions, the cathode potential evolved from –0.06 V to 1.05 V and the 
anode potential from 2.00 V to 1.25 V (vs RHE).233 While these measurements confirm that the anode 
overpotential was indeed many times larger than that of the cathode, it also showed that the cathode 
experienced a more significant change in half-cell potentials during the transition from operation to open 
circuit conditions, which was attributed to Pt oxidation due to crossover oxygen (from the anode). A later 
study tracked the evolution of the cathode overpotential alongside in-situ Pt dissolution measurements via 
a hanging mercury drop electrode.234 The resulting heat map of Pt dissolution—as a function of load 
duration (at 1 A cm–2) and open circuit potential duration—found the most degrading condition to be 
cycling between 0.01 h at 1 A cm–2 and 7.9 h at open circuit. Notwithstanding, a 1000 h study utilizing a 
five-cell stack found that a constant 2 A cm–2 hold resulted in larger cell potential increases than alternating 
between open circuit and 2 A cm–2 (for both 10 minutes and 6 hours at each current).235 These two studies 
are not necessarily in disagreement; even if Pt dissolution—and perhaps cathode degradation—is highest 
when alternating between operation and open circuit, the overall cell degradation was still more 
pronounced during operation, agreeing with the consensus that the anode defines the majority of 
degradation.  
The only known AST study utilizing an in-situ reference electrode236 examined the evolution of half-
cell potentials over 1000 h while employing a dynamic load degradation test (defined by the EU 
Harmonized Protocols for Testing of Low Temperature Water Electrolysers). While the anode experienced a 
substantial increase in half-cell potential during the first week and the cathode did not, the rates of half-
cell potential increases were similar for both in the subsequent weeks. However, the authors noted that 
half-cell potential measurements were sensitive to even slight movements of the reference electrode, with 
many results potentially explained by either a physical change or a disturbance of the reference electrode. 
Unfortunately, these events are especially relevant when working with long-duration tests, where 
unanticipated shutdowns and adjustments are common.  

 
4.2.2 Post-Mortem Analyses  
Given the challenges of long-duration in-situ half-cell monitoring, post-mortem analysis becomes 
especially relevant to understanding cathode degradation, although results are sparse. A previously 
mentioned study (in Section 4.1.3) by Yu et al.173 examined low-loading (0.3 mg cm–2 Pt, 0.08 mg cm–2 Ir) 
CCM degradation (4500 h of operation at 1.8 A·cm-2) with cross-sectional SEM coupled with EDS. Pt/Ir 
bimetallic particles were observed in the membrane (on the side closer to the cathode), and Ir particles 
were observed in and adjacent to the cathode CL. Although Ir degradation was the most pronounced—with 
70% of the original Ir lost—Pt degradation was not negligible, with 10% of the original Pt lost (to the 
membrane), which was understood to be due to the transient reduction of Pt oxides at the beginning of the 
test. A similar study using cross-sectional SEM/EDS has also observed Pt and Ir particles in the membrane 
after 1000 h of dynamic loading testing.237 Lastly, Rakousky et al.238 used post-mortem TEM imaging and 
observed that cathode catalyst particle size increased from 3.5 nm to 7.8 nm after 1000 h of operation (at 
2 A cm–2). However, the authors concluded that it was unlikely that this particle growth significantly 
contributed to decreased cell performance, instead citing PTL corrosion and the anode activity reduction.  
 
4.2.3 Outlook  
In conclusion, the assumption that cathode overpotentials negligibly contribute to beginning-of-life single-
cell performance is supported by experimental results, but it is unclear whether this holds for multi-cell 
stacks (especially at higher pressures). In-situ monitoring of half-cell potentials becomes especially 
challenging for extended studies due to unexpected adjustments/changes to the reference electrode. The 
studies presented suggest that cathode contributions to degradation are strongly masked by those of the 
anode for single-cell durability. Future single-cell durability studies could benefit from in-situ reference 
electrodes, although precautions must be made to ensure a stable reference electrode. Post-mortem 
studies can contribute substantially to understanding degradation processes, but there are still gaps in 
linking degradation to PEMWE performance loss; such a gap could be addressed with modeling studies. 
Additionally, future studies utilizing multi-cell stacks and higher pressures could benefit from more clearly 
elucidating the effects of cathode degradation on overall system durability. Such studies would benefit 
from the collaboration between academic institutions and industrial companies, with the former 
spearheading electrochemical/post-mortem studies while the latter provides the necessary equipment for 
such experiments.   
 
4.3 
Membrane 
The type of membrane used in PEMWEs is a proton exchange membrane (PEM) and is responsible for 
conducting protons and preventing gas crossover. While the membrane neither contributes significantly 
to costs nor is a substantial factor for cost reductions through economies of scale (Figure 7b), membrane 
research is a primary lever for voltage efficiency and lifetime improvements. 
State-of-the-art membranes are based on perfluorosulfonic acid (PFSA) chemistry. PFSA is a 
copolymer of tetrafluoroethylene (used in Teflon™) and a sulfonic acid-terminated perfluorinated 
monomer (Figure 10a). This combination of hydrophobic backbone and hydrophilic sidechain results in a 
phase-segregated structure in which cation-conducting water channels form and percolate through a solid 
matrix. Distinctions between PFSA membranes and hydrocarbon alternatives (Figure 10b) will be 
discussed in Section 4.3.5. For many decades, PFSA membranes have enabled PEMWEs to (1) operate at 
high current densities, (2) operate under dynamic electrical loads, (3) achieve high energy efficiency, (4) 
produce high purity hydrogen (up to 99.999%), and (5) achieve long lifetimes.239

4.3.2 Ohmic Losses 
Improving voltage efficiency is largely incumbent on reducing membrane proton transport resistance. For 
a given operating condition (e.g., temperature), two factors can reduce this resistance: (1) improving 
ionomer conductivity and (2) minimizing membrane thickness.  
The conductivity of a PFSA ionomer is a strong function of equivalent weight (EW), defined as the 
mass of polymer per mole of sulfonic acid (g/mol). In an ionomer review,243 conductivity as a function of 
EW for many ionomers was evaluated, and it was shown that many lower EW ionomers exist below that of 
Nafion™ N115 (1000 EW). However, a strong lower bound for EW exists for practical applications dictated 
by a given ionomer’s water uptake/solubility. As water uptake increases from decreased EW, issues such 

as low mechanical strength and high gas crossover can arise that may counteract the benefit of proton 
conductivity increases. Furthermore, the differences in ionomer conductivity from different EWs are 
minimized in the liquid-saturated PEMWE enrivonment.244  
Apart from a significant ionomeric breakthrough, much of the reductions in resistance will likely 
come from thinner constructions. As early as 2015, Lewinski et al.245 reported a 50 µm membrane 
achieving 1.77 V at 3 A cm-2 (80°C), which is ~150-200 mV lower than for standard N115 membranes. 
Furthermore, Garbe et al.246 observed a ~240 mV decrease at 3.0 A cm-2 when comparing a 183 µm and 50 
µm membrane. However, making membranes thinner makes them more susceptible to mechanical 
deformation and gas crossover. Utilizing reinforcements can potentially alleviate these issues.  
Membrane reinforcements have been used in more mature electrochemical devices (e.g., chlor-
alkali and hydrogen fuel cells) for (1) improved mechanical durability (by giving resistance to crack 
formation and through-plane defect penetration), (2) lower expansion in water and other solvents, and (3) 
processability during manufacturing and cell assembly. For PEMWE, an early example of a reinforced 
membrane is the directionally stable membrane (DSMTM) developed at Giner, where holes were formed in 
a flat reinforcing material. While significant morphology optimization occured, it is unclear whether an 
economical route to manufacturing DSMs at scale was ever realized.247 More recent examples include 
woven fabrics and cloths (based on fluorocarbon or hydrocarbon fibers)248 and non-woven meshes (such 
as expanded polytetrafluoroethylene (ePTFE) or electrospun materials).249,250 The ideal reinforcement is 
one that provides increased durability and reduced crossover while being able to be produced at a low cost 
and high volume. 
 
4.3.3 Gas Crossover 
Hydrogen gas crossover from the cathode to anode can lead to the formation of explosive mixtures as the 
lower explosive limit (LEL) of hydrogen in oxygen is just 4% (by volume). Thus, the greater crossover rate 
for thinner membranes is a major issue,251 especially when hydrogen is compressed in-situ. This issue is 
exacerbated during periods of low electrical load (i.e., low current density), where oxygen content is 
minimal.42 Three main strategies can be employed to mitigate gas crossover: (1) improving membrane gas 
barrier properties, (2) implementing a recombination catalyst (RC) to react crossover hydrogen with 
oxygen (which forms water), and (3) utilizing BoP solutions such as pressurizing the anode stream or 
installing external air circulators to dilute crossover hydrogen. The membrane-based strategies—(1) and 
(2)—will be discussed herein; we refer the reader to a review by Stähler et al.252 for a discussion of (3). 
For gas barrier properties of a membrane, a common metric is the selectivity of protonic (H+) versus 
molecular hydrogen (H2) transport (gas crossover). Not only does a higher selectivity reduce crossover, 
but also increases the Faradaic efficiency of the cell (due to less cathodic hydrogen lost from crossover).253 
Several studies have explored the nature of H+/H2 transport in ionomers.254 Two studies by Stolten et 
al.255,256 demonstrated that hydrogen crossover through the membrane is purely diffusive. Fumagalli et 
al.257 studied PFSA ionomeric d-spacing as a function of time and revealed that the size of water channels 
never reach steady state, suggesting that membrane crossover could increase over its lifetime. Future 
ionomer design could incorporate these learnings to enhance selectivity. One strategy to improve 
selectivity (besides using reinforcements) is to incorporate non-ionomeric fillers, such as graphene or 
graphene oxide, which are proposed to block gas crossover while enhancing proton transport.258,259  
 
In addition to improving gas barrier properties, strategies to mitigate existing crossover hydrogen 
will likely be necessary, as some level of crossover will always occur. Several strategies include a 
downstream hydrogen gas recombiner,260 a third electrode in the membrane,261 and a recombination 
catalyst (RC). All three strategies combine crossover H2 and O2 to form water, but RCs are the most 
commonly studied. They can be implemented in several locations, including as a coating on the PTL42 or 
anode CL,252 or within the anode CL262 or membrane.263 Furthermore, RCs can expand the operating 
current range in which PEMWEs can safely operate.264  
RCs within the membrane have been used for decades (first to increase humidity in fuel cell 
membranes 38,265,266) and have several advantages, including 1) isolating the RC from high anodic potentials 
and 2) mitigating both hydrogen and oxygen crossover (which minimizes peroxide formation at the 

CLs).252,267 RCs are typically PGM-based and can be implemented in membranes via (1) reduction of PGM 
salts to form RC nanoparticles in situ268 or (2) direct incorporation of pre-formed RC particles.27 The 
optimization of the RC location (within the membrane) and loading are active areas of study. For example, 
Gubler et al.269 studied three RC distributions in the membrane and found the most effective crossover 
mitigation occurred when the catalysts were uniformly distributed. While research on RC optimization is 
progressing, insights into the long-term effectiveness of RCs over system lifetimes are lacking. Furthermore, 
the cost and complexity (i.e., the number of unit operations) for manufacturing RC-incorporated 
membranes must be minimized for facile uptake in future PEMWE systems.  
 
4.3.4 Durability 
Membrane durability should be one of the most prioritized research efforts, especially as designs tend 
towards thinner membranes for efficiency improvements. As early as 1988, PFSA membranes have 
enabled PEMWE durability above 10,000 hours.270 In 2006, a paper reported lifetimes of PFSA-based 
devices above 50,000 hours at moderate temperatures (~50-60°C).271 This paper illustrates that PFSA 
membranes have long enabled devices to be incredibly durable; devices that were manufactured almost 
two decades ago have lifetimes that nearly reach 2026 targets. However, the significant re-optimization of 
membrane construction necessary to meet performance targets (i.e., incorporating reinforcements, RCs, 
more conductive ionomers, and thinner membranes) will represent a significant challenge to achieving 
future durability targets.  
Fortunately, there have been several recent reviews covering membrane durability in 
PEMWEs.48,272–275 A high-level summary of their findings and perspectives on the most promising future 
research efforts will be provided here. Membrane failure modes can be mechanical or 
chemical/electrochemical. Membrane creep is a mechanical mode associated with membrane swelling and 
PTL/gasket integration (for a visual of how membrane and PTL interact, refer to Figure 8). Other 
mechanical failure modes—such as pinhole formation and membrane dry-out—may arise from stack-level 
defects (e.g., inconsistent heating or water distribution; poor stack assembly). Chemical/electrochemical 
failure modes can also exist, including cation contamination and radical-induced degradation. Furthermore, 
multiple modes can be present simultaneously and could combine to accelerate degradation.276 Lastly, 
while not a failure mode itself, higher temperatures (which are used to reduce kinetic overpotentials and 
transport resistances) can accelerate the rate of each of these modes.271,276–278 
 
4.3.4.1 Membrane Creep 
Membrane creep can occur in both active (participating in electrochemistry) and inactive (gasketed) areas 
of the cell, along with the interface between the two. Creep failures have been identified as a limiting factor 
in cell lifetime for thin membranes. For example, a study from Nel Hydrogen279 reported ~500 hr lifetime 
for a thin (89 µm) membrane compared to ~4500 hours for a thick (178 µm) membrane (both membranes 
were non-reinforced), where the lifetime gap was ascribed to membrane creep. Reinforcements have been 
identified as a solution for creep, as the same study also reported that a 60 µm reinforced membrane 
exhibited similar durability to the thick non-reinforced membrane.279 However, there is a lack of 
mechanistic understanding of this failure mode as well as methods to evaluate its potential impact. The 
successful deployment of thin membranes will depend on understanding and quantifying membrane creep. 
Work tying fundamental mechanical properties to observed creep failures will be critical toward mitigating 
these issues. Since creep might occur in a tensile mode (i.e., penetration into PTLs) and compression mode 
(as a result of compression under gaskets), multiple testing protocols might be required to evaluate creep 
in different cell areas.178,280 
 
4.3.4.2 Cation Contamination 
Cation contamination leads to reduced protonic conductivity and increased overpotentials. Even with a 
pristine proton-form membrane at the beginning of life, several sources of contamination are conceivable, 
including feed water impurities and dissolution of metal cell components (e.g., PTLs and BPPs).281 In 
principle, a small amount of infrequent cation contamination could be reversible, as the in-situ generation 

of protons and excess circulation of liquid water provides a pathway for metal salts to exit the cell.282 
However, flowing a preponderance of metal ions into the ionomer material could render the stack highly 
inefficient or inoperable (which is possible, for example, through an acute upset of water quality). 
Theoretically, the ionomer’s native protonic form could be refreshed by circulating an external acid 
solution.283 The practicality of this approach at GW scales is unclear, and it could potentially extract 
beneficial additives such as RCs or radical scavengers (discussed in the next paragraph). The most cost-
effective strategy to remediate ion contamination is to prevent it in the first place, as is often employed in 
water recirculation loops in the form of a combination of reverse osmosis and ion exchange resins.275  
 
4.3.4.3 Radical-Induced Degradation  
The crossover of H2 into the anode or O2 into the cathode can lead to the formation of H2O2, which acts as 
the basis for radical species generation.284 Certain metals—such as Fe, Ti, Cr, Ni, Cu, Pt, and Ir—can catalyze 
H2O2 into harmful radicals such as H•, HO•, and HOO•.37, 49 48,284 The reaction of these species have been 
documented as a central degradation mechanism in PEMWEs (and PEMFCs),285 but significant effort is still 
required to understand the fundamental nature of such degradation. Possible reactions of these radicals 
with PFSA ionomers are depicted in Figure 11. It should also be noted that changing the chemistry of the 
PFSA ionomer (such as adding bis-sulfonimide groups in the side chain) can potentially cause low stability 
against radical attacks.286

To quantify radical-induced degradation, measurements of fluoride anions liberated as a 
consequence of these reactions are commonly done, known as fluoride release rate (FRR) or fluoride 
emission rate (FER). FRR has been proposed as an overall metric for quantifying membrane thinning, as 

the two have been reported to be directly correlated.277 Measuring FRR is typically done by ion 
chromatography or ion-selective electrodes. In one example, Fouda-Onana et al.277 measured FRRs at the 
anode and cathode effluent water of a PEMWE cell operating at 60°C and 80°C. Substantially higher FRRs 
were observed at the higher temperature as well as in the cathode effluent water, which was attributed to 
crossover oxygen reacting to form H2O2 in the presence of the platinum catalyst. Simpler ways to measure 
membrane degradation may exist, as Marocco et al.293 found a linear relationship between FRR and water 
conductivity by using a simple conductivity meter. This approach may be a way to monitor stack 
degradation at scale, though it would require removing or quantifying other contributors to water 
conductivity.  
Over time, radical-induced degradation could become significant enough to thin the membrane 
significantly, exacerbating gas crossover and potentially causing electrical shorting. However, a wide range 
of outcomes are reported for the magnitude of radical-induced membrane degradation. For example, 
Grigoriev et al.213 reported that membrane thickness was reduced by 75% over 5500 hr. Another study294 
loosely accredited the observed 35 µV/hr degradation rate to ionomer degradation (attributed to 
increased HFR). These results contrast with other studies where membrane degradation is not observed 
or is minor.270,271 To reduce the possibility of contradictory data, researchers must minimize the impact of 
degradation from other cell components (e.g., the PTL or aCL). It should also be noted that the degradation 
impact of different operation protocols (e.g., low current, high current, load-following) is an area of active 
study and may contribute to discrepancies in rates of radical-induced degradation. 
Mitigating radical-induced degradation in PEMFCs has been achieved through (1) fluorinating the 
end groups of ionomers (currently standard in PEMWE membranes such as Nafion™ N115) and (2) 
including radical scavengers (cerium/manganese-based) in the MEA. Although radical scavengers have 
been reported to have benefits in PEMWEs,295 they have also been attributed to catalyst poisoning and 
increased mass transport overpotentials.296 Recombination catalysts may limit chemical degradation by 
lowering crossover gas concentration at the CLs (reducing potential routes to H2O2 formation), but their 
use has been reported to increase FRR in one study.295 Either way, improving the fundamental 
understanding of chemical degradation and the tradeoff of implementing various mitigation approaches 
will be necessary to ensure successful GW-scale deployment.   
4.3.5 Hydrocarbons 
Hydrocarbon-based membranes have long been sought as an alternative to PFSA ionomers owing to their 
potential for lower gas permeability, fluorine-free chemistry, and lower cost.297 Most hydrocarbon 
ionomers consist of a chain of aromatic units linked by various functional groups (Figure 10b). Although 
hydrocarbon membranes have promising attributes that can improve PEMWE performance, challenges in 
manufacturing and durability must be addressed to meet system targets. This section will outline the main 
obstacles for hydrocarbon membrane deployment and strategies to overcome them. 
 
4.3.5.1 Water Uptake 
Hydrocarbon ionomers tend to possess lower proton conductivity than PFSA ionomers given the same 
water volume fraction.298,299 One reason for this property is a result of their conductive groups (sulfonates) 
being connected to a rigid aromatic backbone, which resists phase segregation into conductive percolating 
pathways. Decreased dielectric screening and a lower degree of dissociation of the sulfonic groups in 
hydrocarbon ionomers also limits the proton mobility. As a result, greater water uptake is needed to 
achieve comparable ionic conductivity (e.g., a prominent hydrocarbon ionomer—sPPS—has a water 
volume fraction of 70%, while NafionTM N115 has 40%300). However, this increased uptake makes them 
more prone to mechanical damage. 
 
Several mechanical issues from high water uptake arise from the membrane’s reduced dimensional 
stability, which increases delamination and embeds stress on the membrane structure.301 Additionally, 
since there are spatial restrictions in the PEMWE cell that dictate the allowable extent of expansion, 
residual stress can build up, particularly along edges and under differential pressure (especially for thin 
membranes).302,303 Furthermore, higher water uptake can decrease mechanical toughness and reduce the 
gas permeation advantage of hydrocarbons, as the gas permeability of water is much higher than that of 

the un-hydrated polymer.304 Lastly, mechanical degradation can feedback into chemical degradation 
through factors such as 1) increased ionomer surface area for reactions, 2) thermal concentration in 
deformed areas, and 3) exacerbation of radical generation through increased gas permeation and 
embedded stress.305–310  
Fortunately, strategies to alleviate water uptake issues have been found. The first strategy is to 
utilize membrane chemistries that require less water uptake to achieve a sufficient level of conductivity. 
For example, block copolymer-based hydrocarbon ionomers can efficiently aggregate conductive sites (at 
some sacrifice of permeability) to lower the required water uptake while maintaining conductivity.263 
Secondly, reinforcements (that are strong and acid-resistant) can be added to reduce swelling as well as 
improve mechanical durability. Analogously to what has been applied for PFSA membranes, porous PTFE-
based reinforcements have enhanced dimensional stability—and thus mechanical durability—for 
hydrocarbon systems.311,312   
 
4.3.5.2 Radical-Induced Degradation 
Though hydrocarbon membranes possess lower gas permeability than PFSA counterparts, the significantly 
higher level of chemical degradation—due to the rapid hydroxyl attack of hydrocarbons—may offset this 
benefit.300,304,313,314 While one can slow this degradation with radical scavengers,315 the attack is excessively 
fast to the extent that chemical degradation is challenging to prevent thoroughly without excessively 
compromising ionic conductivity.316 Thus, to successfully benefit from the advantages of hydrocarbons, 
researchers must also weigh their disadvantages. Given that radical attack might be too rapid to be 
effectively prevented using scavengers, novel strategies to bolster chemical resilience should be explored, 
such as 1) performing polymer repair, 2) incorporating interfacial PFSA layers (e.g., in the membrane-CL 
interface), and 3) localizing radical scavenging at points of generation (i.e., catalyst sites).317  
Overall, when pursuing efforts to mitigate outstanding challenges of hydrocarbon membranes, care 
should be taken to ensure that strategies are mutually compatible (i.e., not causing harm to other system 
components). Additionally, the mitigation methods employed must be durable over the lifetime of the 
PEMWE, which can be challenging to test. 
 
4.3.5.3 Hydrocarbon Binders  
Although binders pertain to the CL and not the membrane, they will be discussed here as this section covers 
hydrocarbon ionomers as a whole. As discussed previously, hydrocarbon ionomers may offer some 
technical upside in membranes, but there is not an obvious benefit in binders. Thus, the development path 
for hydrocarbon binders will likely require significant effort to meet performance targets. 
Hydrocarbon binders suffer from a multitude of issues, including 1) kinetic inhibition, 2) sluggish 
mass transport properties, 3) poor mechanical and chemical stability, and 4) CL manufacturing difficulties. 
For kinetic inhibition, while both PFSA and hydrocarbon ionomers contain sulfonate groups that can 
adsorb—and subsequently block—catalyst sites, the latter has been shown to be more inhibiting.318 This 
difference is attributed to the greater presence of sulfonates and the poisoning action of phenyl groups319 
(since hydrocarbon ionomers require more to achieve comparable PFSA conductivities) and to phenyl 
group adsorption on catalyst sites.320–322 Regarding mass transport, binders must rapidly facilitate gaseous 
transport, as lingering gases could inhibit the HER and OER.323,324 While the lower gas permeability of 
hydrocarbons benefits membranes, it is detrimental for binders and leads to increased gas transport (i.e., 
mass transport) resistances.325–327 Poor mechanical stability is tied to high water uptake, which reduces 
dimensional stability and toughness, while poor chemical stability is due higher levels of radical-induced 
degradation.328 Furthermore, delamination of the CL is more commonly reported for hydrocarbon binders, 
causing increased contact resistance and decreased catalyst utilization.329 
304,330,331 Lastly, CL 
manufacturing is also an issue. While PFSA binders readily form percolating networks, hydrocarbon 
binders have been linked to blocked pores (for mass transport)332,333 and electronically isolated networks, 
leading to higher mass transport losses and lower catalyst utilization.333–336 
Although many issues hinder hydrocarbon-based binders, many research levers exist to improve 
their efficacy: 1) polymer structure/property optimization, 2) solvent system optimization, 3) interfacial 

engineering, and 4) alternative designs. Researchers can consider how structural properties—such as pore 
structure, thickness, and tortuosity—can be tuned to facilitate mass transport. Chemical properties, such 
as conductivity and water uptake, can also be adjusted to enable greater mechanical strength. Furthermore, 
varying structural motifs (e.g., phenyl, ketone, etc.) can lead to less catalyst poisoning, reducing kinetic 
overpotentials. Optimizing solvent system parameters (i.e., solvent type, solid ratio, I/C ratio) to ensure 
high dispersity is critical to fabricating a well-connected microstructure. Tuning molecular weight of the 
binder could also help, as this property has been linked to aggregate formation and connectedness.337 
Penultimately, interfacial engineering can be promising in combatting delamination and increasing 
interfacial contact.314,317,338 Lastly, alternative designs can be utilized, such as the ionomer-free PTE 
proposed by Lee et al.,224 or simply using PFSA binders with hydrocarbon membranes.317  
 
4.4 
Porous Transport Layer 
PTLs in PEMWEs are responsible for 1) facilitating H2O/O2 transport, 2) providing electrical conductivity 
between the CL and BP, 3) removing heat from the CL, and 4) mechanically supporting the membrane 
during differential pressure operation. For visualization, we encourage the reader to refer to Figure 1 and 
Figure 8. PTLs significantly impact voltage efficiency since electrical and reactant/product transport 
through the PTL influences ohmic and mass transport overpotentials, respectively. Additionally, the PTL-
CL interface has been found to influence catalyst utilization and, thus, kinetic overpotentials.339,340 All these 
factors underline the criticality of the PTL for performance. One note is that PTLs primarily facilitate 
through-plane (i.e., perpendicular to the membrane) transport, as opposed to BP flow fields, where in-
plane transport dominates. Based on this terminology, a distinction between a standalone PTL and an 
integrated PTL+BPP design (typically used to reduce part count) can be drawn, as only the area where bulk 
through-plane transport occurs is considered the PTL for the integrated design. 
PTLs are often compared to carbon GDLs in PEMFCs regarding functionality. However, the oxidizing 
conditions (i.e., >1.23 V) of the PEMWE anode do not allow carbon to be used due to it undergoing corrosion 
at potentials greater than 0.2 V.341 State-of-the-art PTLs are made of titanium due to its stability under such 
conditions. Still, challenges of surface passivation (via oxidation) persist, which results in decreased 
electrical conductivity. The porous property of PTLs is due to the titanium being produced as felts, meshes, 
foams, or sintered powders.342 More recently, the addition of a microporous layer (MPL)—a more 
porous/less tortuous layer at the surface of the PTL—is being developed to enable improved CL-PTL 
contact, which can enable lower catalyst loadings and thinner membranes. Although several PTL types 
have been studied, the final choice for stack integration will not only depend on performance but also on 
cost and scalability. 
 
4.4.1 Cost  
PTLs contribute ~20% of stack cost, or $47/kW, when considered in a 1 MW system (Figure 7). However, 
through economies of scale for 1 GW/yr deployment, that cost can be essentially halved to ~$25/kW.343 
Another significant cost lever is mitigating PGM usage for coatings.  For passivation protection, PTLs are 
currently coated with platinum and iridium, which significantly adds to material costs. Given the limited 
understanding of degradation mechanisms, coatings are currently overengineered to ensure 
durability.344,345 Considering even a modest platinum coating of 0.1 mg/cm2, the material alone can exceed 
$1.5/kW (assuming Pt cost is $50/g). 
Aside from initial material costs, there are significant expenses for managing and refining materials. 
Each instance of material handling can incur a substantial cost, which can be particularly daunting given 
the ambitious benchmarks required to meet the US DoE's target of $2/kg H2 by 2026. Fortunately, cheaper 
alternative base materials and non-PGM coatings have shown promise. For example, Stiber et al.346 
demonstrated minimal degradation over 1000 h using a stainless steel PTL with a non-PGM coating 
(Nb/Ti).  
 
4.4.2 Development 

The various stages of PTL development are shown in Figure 12. Step one involves structure development. 
PTLs can be created through 1) thermal sintering, selective laser sintering, or electron beam melting of 
titanium powders, 2) heat treatment-induced diffusion bonding of titanium fibers, 3) weaving or expanding 
titanium meshes, and 4) tape casting or vacuum plasma spraying titanium particles.347–349 The most 
common method is powder or fiber sintering. Morphological properties—such as porosity, particle size, 
and thickness—are controlled by tuning process parameters (e.g., laser power, rastering rate, temperature) 
and starting material properties (e.g., particle size). Lastly, morphology can be quantified using techniques 
such as X-ray tomography350, mercury intrusion porosimetry351, or optical profilometry.225 The influence 
of structure on performance is discussed in next section.

Step two is the development of PTL surface coatings for corrosion resistance. State-of-the-art

during this step.  
Transitioning from lab-scale to industrial-scale, step four is manufacturing optimization for large-
scale deployment. Although impressive morphological control can be achieved in lab scales, scaling up 
manufacturing (e.g., deploying roll-to-roll methods or creating batches >1000 cm2) can present quality 
control difficulties. Two methods are usually employed for large-scale manufacturing: 1) sintering—which 
involves combining, compacting, and sintering titanium powder, binders, and lubricants together, and 2) 
tape casting—which entails casting a titanium slurry into a thin layer, followed by drying and sintering. 
Tape casting provides precise control of sheet thickness and surface quality.348 For effective manufacturing, 
in-line quality control systems are necessary to guarantee morphology specifications and remove defective 
parts. Specifically, surface quality, presence of impurities, undesired edge warps or burrs, and 
inhomogeneous morphology need to be inspected. Failing to address these manufacturing-level defects 

can result in stack-level failures that are much more expensive to address. Creating quality compliance 
standards and methods to identify defects are necessary research areas.  
Lastly, step five is techno-economic evaluation. To maximize PTL market readiness, costs and 
manufacturing lead times should be minimized. Significant cost reductions can come from using non-
titanium base materials and reducing PGM loadings for coatings. However, even if such improvements do 
not come to fruition, cost reductions can be achieved with higher production rates.343 For example, PTL 
cost can be almost halved from ~$45/kW to ~$23/kW by increasing production rates from 10 to 100 
units/yr (assuming 1 MW units; refer to Figure 7). 
 
4.4.3 Structure-Performance Relationships 
Several studies have evaluated PTL structures and their effect on PEMWE performance. Apart from 
sintered powder and titanium fiber PTLs, researchers have studied meshes,357 expanded meshes,358 
foams,359 as well as multi-layered combinations of these.360 Each PTL type can result in different interfacial 
contact behaviors with the CL, depending on the form factor of the surface. For example, the interface 
formed via sintered powder PTLs differs from that formed via sintered fiber PTLs, resulting in different 
performances even if their bulk properties (e.g., porosity and mean pore size) may be the same.  
In a comprehensive review, Yuan et al.342 analyzed the impact of PTL morphology on cell voltage 
and current densities (using sintered titanium powder PTLs). The results reveal a scattered relationship 
between mean pore diameter and voltage (Figure 13a). This scatter could be attributed to variations in 
test parameters among the different studies (e.g., temperature, pressure, and membrane thickness); 
nonetheless, a weak logarithmic correlation was observed, indicating that smaller pore sizes may decrease 
cell voltage. When examining porosity versus cell voltage (Figure 13b), higher porosity was loosely 
correlated with decreased voltage, but only at lower current densities of 0.2 and 1 A/cm2. At 2 A/cm2, no 
correlation was clear.

4.4.4 CL-PTL Interface 

The CL-PTL interface is vital for PEMWE performance, especially under low loadings (as discussed in 
Section 4.1.1). The interface can be analyzed by calculating the interfacial contact resistance (ICR), which 
is influenced by contact pressure and deformation between the CL and PTL. For example, Yuan et al.361 
calculated ICR as a function of contact pressure and density of interfacial contact points. To directly 
estimate electrochemical performance, one can measure the triple phase contact area (TPCA), which 
quantifies the area common to the catalyst, ionomer, and PTL. Higher TPCAs result in higher catalyst 
utilization and lower kinetic overpotentials. Kulkarni et al.339 took this concept further and calculated ECSA 
with TPCA values obtained through X-ray computed tomography (CT). Using a fiber PTL and low iridium 
loadings (0.05-0.1 mg/cm2), Peng et al.177 observed that very low TPCAs resulted in limited electron 
conductivity while very high TPCAs limited water accessibility to the CL (due to the catalyst being buried 
under the PTL fibers). Hence, for fiber PTLs with low iridium loadings, the lowest kinetic overpotential was 
observed at intermediate TPCAs between 35.4% and 29.4%, corresponding to a PTL porosity between 
60%-62%. Higher catalyst utilization was also shown to reduce mass transport overpotentials, as higher 
utilization provides well-connected pathways between the CL and PTL for oxygen transport. Work 
by Schuler et al.178 emphasizes this point by showing that mass transport limitations were more related to 
catalyst utilization than bulk mass transport.  
Many modeling techniques have aimed to optimize the CL-PTL interface, given its importance in 
overall PEMWE cost and performance. The types of models used include one-phase flow, two-phase flow, 
image-based, and stochastic. A single-phase (water) 1D model was developed by Ma et al.362 to simulate 
the effects of PTL pore size (at the interface) on catalyst utilization.  A single-phase model sufficed since 
they focused on kinetically-dominated regions, where two-phase flow effects can be assumed negligible. 
They found that smaller PTL pores at the interface and better in-plane electrical conductivity in the CL 
improve catalyst utilization. A two-phase 3D model developed by Weber et al.363 investigated membrane 
hydration and catalyst utilization underneath PTL land and pores. The model showed 1) low catalyst 
utilization underneath PTL pores due to poor in-plane conductivity and 2) local dry-out of the membrane 
under PTL land with increased current density (Figure 14). Sepe et al.364 recently developed an image-
based multiscale modeling method that uses X-ray CT images to resolve the PTL microstructure for use in 
two-phase flow simulations. Using these simulations, the model generates macroscale parameters—such 
as relative permeability and capillary pressure-saturation curves—to predict PEMWE performance. They 
found that the PTL with small pore sizes, a narrower pore size distribution, and the highest area of water-
oxygen contact performed the best. They explained that smaller pores require less pressure for oxygen to 
enter, leading to lower mass transport losses. Lastly, Lee et al.357 used a stochastic pore network model 
to optimize reactant transport and interfacial contact. By tuning porosity and particle size, their results 
showed an optimized PTL with a porosity of 26.5% and a powder diameter of 25 mm under “normal” 
operation (exact operating conditions were not specified). Overall, much insight can be gained from the 
numerous CL-PTL interface models in the literature. However, future models need to include catalyst 
loading effects, as interfacial contact can suffer dramatically at low loadings.

Significant catalyst layer and membrane delamination can occur at the PTL-CL interface, resulting

Schuler et al.368 examined CL deformation post-cell operation and observed microcracks caused by stress 
imposed by the PTL (Figure 15a). The cracks formed below the fibers (highlighted in green) do not impede 
electronic conductivity since no PTL void space is present. However, the cracks next to the PTL void space 
(highlighted in red and yellow) do interfere with in-plane electronic conductivity, resulting in higher 
resistances. Furthermore, with differential pressure operation, CL/membrane creep into the PTL surface 
pores can become severe and propagate existing cracks in the CL. Over thousands of hours, creep can result 
in pinhole formation, especially when thin membranes are paired with PTLs with highly porous surfaces. 
A recent study by Ferner et al.369 employed nano X-ray CT and pFIB-SEM to investigate how CL structure 
changes over time. The study observed that 1) CL thickness decreased, and 2) CL regions compressed by 
PTL fibers (i.e., areas under and directly between PTL fibers) exhibited significantly different porosity and 
pore sizes compared to CL regions in the PTL pores (Figure 15b and Figure 15c). The compressed areas 
had a 20% decrease in porosity (~41% to ~51%) and a 50% decrease in maximum pore size (~600 to 
~300nm).

Optimizing CL-PTL interfacial properties will be essential for reaching high-performing low-iridium

properties. Novel methods can be explored to improve interfacial properties of the PTL, such as laser 
ablation, pore patterning, and laser-induced structuring.225,371,372 
 
4.4.5 Mass Transport 
Establishing efficient water transport and oxygen removal is important for high performance. Ambiguities 
have arisen in the definition of "mass transport" losses, with the term often used to encompass residual 
losses not associated with kinetic or ohmic overpotentials. However, we will exclusively use the term to 
refer to losses associated water and gas transport. Efforts to characterize mass transport in the PTL include 
operando X-ray imaging,373,374 neutron imaging,375,376 optical imaging,377 and numerical modeling,378–380 all 
of which have provided invaluable insights on maximizing performance.180,381,382  
The formation of oxygen bubbles can critically impede CL function and has been associated with 
mass transport losses.383,384 Such losses are more significant under low loadings and high current 
densities,175,177,225 where the increased amount of evolved oxygen gas could mask the already limited 
number of reaction sites. In their modeling study, Moore et al.383 found that mass transport losses can be 
caused by discrete transient events of catalyst site blockage by oxygen bubbles, even though gas saturation 
does not significantly affect liquid saturation across the CL. Bubble formation comprises three stages: 
nucleation on the CL surface, growth to a critical radius, and detachment. Lee et al.384 identified a “critical 
current density,” where a sudden increase in gas saturation—and subsequently mass transport 
overpotential—occurs. They were able to increase the CCD by increasing the water flow rate, suggesting 
that there is a minimum flow needed for efficient oxygen bubble removal, as seen in Figure 16.385 
Regarding heterogeneity, bubble accumulation can lead to current density and temperature 
maldistributions. Fortunately, optimizing the bubble transport properties of the PTL can mitigate the 
aforementioned detrimental effects.

PTL gas transport can be enhanced by tuning wettability or optimizing PTL porosity and 
tortuosity.177 Increased wettability has been shown to promote oxygen removal. Zhao et al.386 treated a 
PTL to increase its hydrophilicity and found increased activity compared to a control PTL. Furthermore, 
lower gas saturation was found in the treated PTL (through neutron imaging). Developing PTLs with higher 
porosity and lower tortuosity have shown reduced mass transport losses.177 However, the balance of mass 
transport and interfacial contact needs to be carefully tuned, as studies have shown an inverse relation (at 
loadings <0.1 mgIr·cm-2).387 Aside from developing new PTLs, modifying existing PTLs is another approach. 
Bazylak et al.388 demonstrated that creating perforations promotes faster gas removal and water intake. 
While having perforations present all the way through the PTL may negatively impact interfacial contact, 
Lee et al.225 showed that perforations that do not traverse all the way through can also improve mass 
transport without sacrificing interfacial contact. Efforts to reduce mass transport losses without 
compromising interfacial contact are required to satisfy performance targets, and the authors foreshadow 
that MPLs may be a solution. 
 
4.4.6 Microporous Layer  
To improve CL-PTL interfacial contact, using an MPL—a layer with smaller pore sizes than the bulk PTL—
is proposed. Improved interfacial contact can lead to decreased mass transport and ohmic losses as well as 
increased catalyst utilization. 
 
Numerous studies have examined the impact of MPLs on mass transport and ohmic losses. Using X-
ray CT, Kulkarni et al.353 demonstrated that a thin (~20 µm) MPL on a PTL allowed for 20% increased 

interfacial contact compared to a baseline PTL, improving mass transport properties. During the 1000 h 
stability test where the current was held at 1.8 A cm-2, this MPL showed a >100 mV performance 
improvement and a lower degradation rate compared to the baseline PTL (at both catalyst loadings of ~0.1 
mgIr/cm2 and ~2 mgIr/cm2). In a pioneering attempt to develop MPLs, Lettenmeier et al.389 vacuum plasma 
sprayed fine titanium powders (grain size < 45 μm) on sintered titanium powders. The resulting structure 
showed a clear bilayer, with the MPL and bulk PTL possessing pore sizes of 1.3 mm and 17 mm, respectively. 
The MPL resulted in marked ohmic improvement compared to the non-MPL, with a staggering HFR 
reduction of 20 mΩ cm2. Following that, Kang et al.390 developed two MPL types, a microporous MPL using 
∼5 μm particles and a nanoporous MPL using 30–50 nm particles. Interestingly, they found that the 
microporous MPL outperformed the nanoporous MPL (20 mV reduction at 2.0 A/cm2) due to increased 
pore access for oxygen and water.  
 
The role of MPLs in improved catalyst utilization has also been investigated. As stated in Section 
4.1.2, conductive catalyst supports are currently lacking. Thus, providing in-plane electrical conductivity 
between catalyst particles—and hence catalyst utilization—at low loadings will likely depend on the PTL 
surface.  MPLs will likely be critical at low loadings, where poor in-plane electrical conductivity could result 
in fatal losses in catalyst utilization. Schuler et al391 constructed PTLs with MPLs of various pore sizes and 
studied their effect on catalyst utilization, albeit at higher loadings (2 mg cm-2). The PTL structures are 
displayed in Figure 17a, and polarization curves in Figure 17b. The bulk PTL had a mean pore diameter 
of 27 mm (the single layer “SL” sample has no MPL), and MPL 1, 2, and 3 had mean pore diameters of 6, 11, 
and 13 mm, respectively. Overall, catalyst utilization for the MPL samples increased by a factor of 2.2-2.8 
compared to the SL sample, leading to ~60-100 mV reductions in overpotentials at 2 A/cm2. These voltage 
improvements were attributed to kinetic and mass transport improvements.

4.4.7 Coatings  
Coatings can help prevent surface oxidation and maintain CL-PTL conductivity. As such, coated PTLs have 
been shown to have increased performance and lower degradation rates than uncoated PTLs.341,392 
Platinum is the most widely used coating material in commercial and academic research. Bernt et al.393 
examined the impact of PTL coatings on cell performance and found that platinum coatings provide the 
necessary in-plane conductivity that low-loading MEAs severely lack. MPL usage at low loadings (~0.27 
mgIr/cm2) resulted in HFR reductions of over 15 mΩ cm2—resulting in ~50 mV improvement in cell voltage 
at 3 A/cm2. Rakousky et al.394 conducted a steady-state durability test for over 1000 hours and found that 

a platinum-coated PTL had an 89% reduction in degradation rate (108→12 µV/h) when compared to an 
uncoated PTL.  
Coating degradation—caused predominately by metal dissolution and delamination—is heavily 
influenced by the application technique. Two widely used methods are electroplating and sputtering. 
Though electroplating is faster and simpler, it has uniformity and adhesion issues. In contrast, sputtering 
provides superior quality and precision (for thickness and material amount) but requires longer times and 
more costly equipment. As a result, coatings from sputtering exhibit prolonged durability even after 
prolonged usage. Regarding material selection, some studies suggest that iridium is a better coating than 
platinum because of improved stability and higher conductivity of its oxides.395,396 Moreover, since the 
iridium sits at the CL-PTL interface, it can offer OER assistance to the anode CL or even enable ionomer-
free CLs. Thus, some argue that the benefits of iridium coatings outweigh the additional costs imparted to 
the stack.  Alternative techniques for surface modification include using novel mixed metal oxide 
coatings397 and acid etching,398 both of which enhance corrosion resistance. Although these metal oxide 
coatings show promise, their long-term durability is yet to be shown. Acid etching results in titanium 
hydride subsurface layers on the PTL, which does not affect electrical conductivity, but enhances corrosion 
resistance by mitigating titanium oxide formation.399  
 
4.5 Gas Diffusion Layer 
The gas diffusion layer (GDL) is the cathode-side equivalent of a PTL and is responsible for thermal 
conduction, electrical conduction, and hydrogen removal. However, the cathode is a low-potential 
environment, and thus, corrosion is not a concern during operation. Due to similarities in property and 
function, PEMWE GDLs have generally been adopted from PEMFC GDLs with minimal modifications.400–403 
Current industrial GDLs (such as those from AvCarb, Freudenberg, and Toray) are carbon-based and are 
usually made from felts, papers, or woven substrates. This combination of material composition and 
structure makes GDLs compressible, unlike PTLs. Thus far, few works exist that study the impact of the 
GDL on PEMWE performance, since unoptimized GDLs adapted from PEMFCs have proved more or less 
adequate. This ease of incorporation is due to low HER overpotentials (relative to OER) and high H2 gas 
diffusion rates (relative to O2 gas and water). 
 
4.5.1 Compression  
 Since GDLs are compressible, they are responsible for stress absorption from membrane swelling and 
anode-related stress (since the PTL is incompressible). Compared to PEMFCs, the membranes used in 
PEMWEs are thicker, resulting in greater swelling and inducing additional stress on the GDL. Aside from 
stress absorption, GDL compression can also help prevent water flooding and promote BPP-GDL and GDL-
CL electrical contact. Since hydrogen diffusivity is high, reductions in porosity due to compression should 
not significantly impact H2 removal. However, GDLs can experience mechanical damage due to 
overcompression if the cell is not properly designed or gasketed.  
On the other hand, GDL decompression can also be an issue, which can result if hydrogen at the 
cathode is being compressed. Such decompression can lead to increased contact resistances and, thus, 
greater ohmic overpotentials. Generally, the frame that hosts the anode and cathode components in 
PEMWEs is made to a specific thickness, but manufacturing complications sometimes prevent the 
components from taking up the desired thickness. To combat the possibility of decompression, a thick layer 
(up to several millimeters) of stiff carbon is added in the cell. For PEMFCs, optimal GDL compression sits 
at around 10-25%; this range provides effective transport and mechanical stability.403 However, optimal 
GDL compression for PEMWEs cannot be generalized as it will depend on the cell configuration (e.g., 
membrane thickness, flow field design, etc.). 
 
 
4.5.2 Wettability  

Since water diffuses through the membrane to the cathode via electroosmotic drag (Figure 1), a diluted 
hydrogen and water mixture ends up in the cathode outlet stream. Thus, hydrophobic GDLs are desirable 
as they mitigate water transport from the membrane into the outlet stream, resulting in pure hydrogen 
(also called “dry hydrogen”). Hydrophobicity can be attained using a hydrophobic base material or by 
incorporating a hydrophobic MPL into the GDL. PTFE is currently used as a hydrophobic additive, but it is 
not electrically conductive and thus contributes to higher GDL-BP interfacial contact resistances. Therefore, 
finding alternative materials to promote hydrophobicity is a research area.  
 
4.5.3 Outlook 
Although research is still needed for GDL optimization, existing designs derived from PEMFCs will be 
sufficient for GW-scale deployment. However, one note to consider is that PEMWE GDLs are usually 
carbonized at high temperatures in vacuum furnaces to eliminate impurities. as PEMWEs are more 
sensitive to cation contamination than PEMFCs. This additional step can increase manufacturing costs. 
However, this step may not be necessary as its impact on long-term performance has not yet been studied 
in the literature. Lastly, it should be considered that although GDL corrosion is typically not a concern, 
PEMWE shutdown events can lead to rapid carbon corrosion due to the presence of reverse currents. Thus, 
care should be taken to ensure that PEMWE durability is not affected by excessive corrosion. 
 
4.6 Bipolar Plate 
BPs are expensive due to material needs and the manufacturing process, contributing 15% to 50% of the 
total stack cost.404 405,406 BPs are responsible for mass transport (H2O in; H2 and O2 out), electrical 
conduction, heat conduction, and mechanical support.404,407–409 Unlike PTLs, which are responsible for 
through-plane mass transport via diffusion, BPs contain flow fields responsible for in-plane mass transport 
via convection. As shown in Figure 18a, they are called “bipolar” because one side is in contact with the 
anode, and the other side is in contact with the cathode.407 For BPs to be effective, they must possess low 
contact resistance and efficiently transport water and gases. Furthermore, just like the PTL, BPs need to be 
made of a corrosive-resistant material that can withstand the acidic and oxidizing conditions of PEMWE 
cells.  
The most common base materials for BPs are titanium and certain stainless steels (SS; e.g., SS316 
and SS403), as shown in Figure 18b i and ii.404,407 Unlike for PEMFCs, graphite is only suitable for the 
cathode environment.410 With the use of titanium or SS, coatings are needed to maintain low interfacial 
contact resistance with adjacent components. For SS specifically, the coating would also offer corrosion 
protection. Precious metals such as platinum, gold, and silver (Figure 18b iii) are typical coating materials, 
with platinum being the most common.405,407 To apply these coatings, electrical plating is conventionally 
used, but methods such as physical vapor deposition are in development.

Developments for BPs can leverage knowledge acquired from PEMFCs, but material limitations 
provide challenges.405,407 Fortunately, flow field designs and manufacturing techniques can more easily be 
adapted for PEMWE BPs. Overall, the primary limiting factor for scale-up is cost. 
 
4.5.1  Flow Field 
BP flow field design influences heat conduction and mass transport in PEMWEs. Since excess water flows 
throughout the cell for heat removal, flow fields must equally distribute this water to avoid hotspot 
formation. Additionally, the pressure drop across flow fields should be minimized to reduce pumping costs 
(which are associated with balance of stack).  
Many methods can be employed to create a flow field, such as electroetching, stamping, electrical 
discharge machining, electrochemical machining, electroforming, and additive manufacturing.412 Three 
types of flow field structures exist: 1) Multi-layered, where layers of a metal mesh or screen are stacked on 
a flat base material (Figure 19). Pores of adjacent layers are offset to construct a 3-D hollow structure for 
transport. Pore sizes and the number of layers are critical design parameters. 2) Electroetched, where 
serpentine flow field channels are etched on a thick metal plate. This process simplifies the BP 
manufacturing process as only one component is needed, but suffers from high processing costs. 3) 
Stamped, where 3D flow structures are stamped onto a base. Stamping onto thin base materials (~0.5 
mm)—especially with stainless steel—is common for PEMFC BPs.412–415 This stamping strategy has been 
shown to enhance mass transport.416 Like the etched structure, assembly costs are reduced due to only 
needing one component. However, complications arise from difficulty controlling stress distortion. 

Improper stress control during stamping could result in uneven surfaces and, ultimately, complications in 
stack sealing (especially under high pressures).

Flow field performance is a strong candidate to be improved through modeling. Distribution profiles

flow field types, as shown in Figure 20.418–421 These simulations require Multiphysics models that couples

(like the PTL) must be included for comprehensiveness. Olesen et al.422 modeled three circular 
interdigitated flow field variations. Reasonably, the regions with the highest temperature and oxygen 
content had the highest current densities. Interestingly, despite the similar overall performance, they 
found significant in-plane variation in temperature and current density between the three designs. Another 
study423 compared a novel interdigitated-jet hole flow field design with two common designs (standard 
interdigitated and single serpentine). They found the novel design to have more homogeneous water 
distribution and more effective gas removal, leading to more uniform current density and temperature 
profiles. Qing Wei et al.424 used a similar model to compare a novel mesh flow field with commonly used 
parallel flow fields. They found that the mesh structure enabled greater temperature and current density 
uniformity (Figure 21) as well as lower energy consumption.

4.5.2 Coatings 
BP coatings must be stable in the highly corrosive environment of a PEMWE anode: oxygen-saturated, 
potentials ~2V, temperatures ≤80°C, and pH~0 (Figure 22).410,425,426 Furthermore, they must also have 
low contact resistance, high through-plane electrical conductivity, good adherence to the BP, low cost, and 
scalable methods of deposition.425,426 Titanium BPs are usually coated with PGMs, as they meet the 
requirements above and can also prevent surface passivation.410 If SS is used as the base material, the 
coating must also prevent ion leaching, which could lead to increased coating costs.425,426 However, SS is 
cheaper than titanium, and thus coated SS BPs could still be cheaper than coated titanium BPs.404,426 The 
method used to apply coatings could also influence costs; common methods include plasma, powder, and 
electrochemical deposition.427

Journal of Power Sources.  
 
BP designs with novel coatings have also been demonstrated. Using a SS base, Gago et al.426 
developed a two-layer coating consisting of a 60 µm titanium layer and a ~1 µm platinum layer. The 
titanium provided corrosion resistance, while the platinum prevented the titanium from passivating. 
Future work dedicated to decreasing the titanium layer thickness can reduce coating costs.426 A different 
study by Rojas et al. 425 concluded that utilizing a SS BP with a multi-layer Ti/TiN coating can reduce 
manufacturing costs by 90% compared to titanium BPs. However, further work must be completed to make 
this technique more scalable.   
 
4.5.3 Hydrogen Embrittlement 
Hydrogen absorption can occur on the cathode side of BPs due to high hydrogen pressures and radical 
generation from the HER, weakening the mechanical integrity of the BP and leading to a phenomenon 
known as “hydrogen embrittlement.”428 Titanium BPs can suffer mechanical failure with hydrogen 
concentrations over 8,000 ppm,429 and it was reported that such plates could absorb over 1000 ppm in 500 
hours of operation (meaning that stack failure could occur in just 4,000 hours).430 Even using platinum 
coatings, hydrogen embrittlement can still occur since hydrogen can absorb in the platinum and form Pt-
H bonds.431 
Two approaches to prevent hydrogen embrittlement are applying a gold coating or a pinhole-free 
carbon coating to prevent direct contact with hydrogen. Gold can help prevent hydrogen absorption, but 
due to its high diffusion coefficient, a thick coating (>200 nm) is required to meet lifetime requirements. 
Such high precious metal usage presents cost issues. Another approach is to have a pinhole-free carbon 
coating, which has been shown to reduce hydrogen absorption.430  However, challenges lie in costs and 
processing difficulties associated with pinhole-free manufacturing. Lastly, a more roundabout strategy 
would be to allow the titanium surface to form surface oxides, which would prevent hydrogen absorption, 
and then partially cover the surface with gold dots to enable adequate electrical conductivity (Figure 
23).432 It was shown that surface coverage of less than 10% with gold dots would meet contact resistance 
requirements. Overall, hydrogen embrittlement is still a significant issue and will require developing 
effective coating materials and scalable coating methods.433

4.5.4 Outlook 
Current BPs are made of titanium coated with platinum, constituting up to 50% of stack costs. For PEMWE 
scale-up, the costs of base and coating materials must be reduced. For the base, stainless steel is an 
attractive option; however, a coating that is inexpensive and durable (towards corrosion and ion leaching) 
is required. Platinum can still be used as a coating, but loadings—which currently sit at ~1-5 mg/cm2—
should be reduced to decrease costs.434 Of course, platinum-free coatings are ideal for cost reduction, but 
further work into effectiveness and scalability is required. Lastly, hydrogen embrittlement presents a 
major durability issue and needs to be addressed with novel coating strategies.  
 
5 
AST Proposals 
ASTs are durability testing protocols that enhance the degradation of components under industrially 
relevant conditions. These tests are needed to determine system lifetimes without numerous years of 
testing. Furthermore, ASTs can help us understand degradation under different operating regimes, which 
may be useful in future scenarios. For example, although current PEMWEs operate at constant loads to 
minimize degradation, techno-economic analysis indicates that dynamic operation following cheap 
renewable electricity (i.e., “load following”) will be critical to producing low-cost hydrogen.435 While 
numerous papers have examined PEMWE durability,436–439 no standardized AST protocols exist.  
The U.S. DoE has published various standardized ASTs for PEMFCs, including widely used 
component-level tests, as well as rarely used MEA-level tests for overall system lifetime predictions.440 
Their “Research, Development, and Demonstration Plan”440 lists ASTs for electrocatalysts, catalyst 
supports, membrane chemical stability, membrane mechanical stability, membrane chemical/mechanical 
stability, and drive cycle durability. While the first five tests target specific components and have been 
widely used in the literature, the last AST (“drive cycle durability protocol”) targets overall system 
durability under light-duty operating conditions and has rarely been reported.441,442 Since lifetime 
predictions are highly dependent on the exact duty cycle used in any particular application, it is more 
common for this type of AST to be specifically developed by original equipment manufacturers (OEMs).  
Partnerships between OEMs and researchers can facilitate durability improvements. OEMs can 
provide researchers with end-of-life materials and data pointing to specific failure modes, so that efforts 
are not misappropriated to irrelevant durability concerns. At the same time, researchers can use their 
resources to understand the fundamental issues governing degradation. Given the criticality of meeting 
lifetime targets, researchers must prioritize the expansion of testing capacities (both ex-situ and operando) 
to probe durability topics and perform ASTs.   

Although less standardized, PEMWE ASTs have been proposed for the anode, cathode, and overall 
system. The EU published a harmonized report on electrolyzer testing and found the “key stressors” for 
PEMWE durability to be temperature, pressure, water flow rate, and water quality.443 Using these four 
stressors, eight ASTs were developed for anode and cathode degradation. Two overall system ASTs were 
also proposed: a “reactive” test with 1 min step durations and a “flexible” test with 15 min step durations 
(each step was a current hold, ranging from 25-200% of nominal current density). Moreover, Aßmann et 
al.444 proposed overall system ASTs based on different operating modes: low load, high load, dynamic load, 
and startup/shutdown. While a handful of overall system ASTs have been proposed, component-specific 
tests are lacking. Here, we propose ASTs for each cell component, summarized in Table 3. 
Lastly, it should be noted that component-specific ASTs could produce unwanted stress on other 
components, leading to seemingly contradictory results. For example, a study by Alia et al.137 found that 
dynamic operation causes more degradation than constant operation, while another study by Frensch et 
al.294 reported the opposite. However, performance losses were attributed to kinetic overpotentials in the 
first study and ohmic overpotentials in the second. Thus, care should be taken to meticulously probe the 
cause of electrochemical performance loss in an AST (e.g., catalyst dissolution, membrane thinning, PTL/BP 
passivation, etc.), regardless of which component is targeted.

5.1 
Anode Catalyst Layer 
PEMFC catalyst ASTs involve cycling between two potentials where platinum is in different oxidation states 
(metallic platinum and platinum oxide/hydroxide) and thus exhibits vastly different dissolution rates.445 
This cycling results in catalyst agglomeration and loss of electrochemically active surface area.441 In 
addition to potential cycling, temperature and relative humidity (RH) also accelerate degradation.446 Thus, 
the AST (for the cathode catalyst) is 0.6V-0.95V cycling (2.5s holds) at 80°C and 100% RH. Furthermore, 
the test is performed in an H2/N2 environment to prevent ionomer degradation. 
PEMWE anode catalyst ASTs can be designed with a similar potential cycling strategy, as transitions 
to lower voltages and OCV conditions were shown to be highly degrading (Section 4.1.3). To assess 
durability representative of standard operation, 1.45V-2.0V cycling can be used; for durability 
representative of shutdown/startup operation, 0.5V-2.0V cycling can be used. For both tests, an elevated 
temperature of 80-90°C can be used, and the potential hold time can be adjusted between 10-30s. This time 
window allows for rapid cycling but also provides adequate time for iridium to undergo oxidation state 
changes. Electrochemical performance at kinetically-dominated regions (i.e., at low current densities) can 
be used to track catalyst degradation. 

5.2 
Cathode Catalyst Layer 
The durability of the cathode CL has drawn some attention regarding start/stop operation. Grigoriev et 
al.447 reported significant platinum dissolution attributed to the 600+ shutdown events in their 4000h test. 
During shutdown, reverse currents can occur (i.e., the system works as a fuel cell) and trigger mechanisms 
of platinum dissolution until all electrolysis gases are exhausted (the authors suggest purging the anode 
and cathode gases as mitigation strategies).447 Voronova et al.448 also observed this degradation 
mechanism, as they found performance losses when cycling between 1.4-2.2V, but not when cycling 
between 1.5-2.2V.  
 
Since shutdown conditions impart the greatest stress on the cathode catalyst, we propose the same 
test as the startup/shutdown AST for the anode catalyst: 0.5-2.0 V cycling. While OCV-load cycling has been 
seen in the literature for cathode degradation,449 we do not recommend incorporating OCV conditions in 
standardized protocols as reproducibility will likely be unsatisfactory. The nature of OCV conditions (i.e., 
how quickly the potential decays, what value the potential will decay to, etc.) will depend on the rate of 
hydrogen crossover, which can vary significantly with membrane thickness and chemistry.    
 
5.3 
Membrane 
Membrane degradation has been identified as one of the main failure mechanisms for PEMWEs450–452 and 
was discussed extensively in Section 4.3.4. A summary of the two degradation modes—chemical and 
mechanical—will be presented here. Chemical degradation can lead to membrane thinning213 and occurs 
when radical species attack the PFSA ionomer (Figure 11). The rate of degradation can be quantified by 
measuring FRRs. A model by Chandesris et al.453 found that temperature accelerates FRRs and that higher 
rates are seen at the cathode. Moreover, current cycling was reported to cause increased FRRs compared 
to steady-state operation.454 Mechanical degradation as a failure mode has been demonstrated with thin 
N211 membranes (~25 µm).455 Membranes can experience mechanical stress through delamination or the 
formation of hotspots. It has been reported that thicker membranes penetrate 19 to 45 µm into the PTL, 
with factors such as membrane thickness and PTL porosity influencing the extent of penetration.456 
Furthermore, flow field designs that lead to non-uniform H2O/O2 distributions across the cell can result in 
membrane hotspots that accelerate degradation.457  
Three membrane ASTs for PEMFCs currently exist: OCV hold in H2/air (for chemical degradation), 
RH cycling (for mechanical degradation), and RH cycling in H2/air (for combined chemical/mechanical 
degradation).442,458 Since mechanisms of membrane degradation are similar between PEMFCs and 
PEMWEs, these ASTs can be adapted for PEMWEs. RH cycling can be replaced by pressure cycling, which 
is more relevant to electrolyzer operation. For chemical degradation, an OCV hold in H2/air at 90°C and 30% 
RH can be performed. For mechanical degradation, pressure cycling between 1-200 bar (which covers 
relevant pressure ranges) in air/air at 90°C can be done. For a combined chemical and mechanical test, the 
pressure cycling can be performed in H2/air. Lastly, a dual membrane and anode catalyst AST can be 
created by combining pressure cycling with the startup/shutdown anode catalyst AST, resulting in cycling 
between 1 bar/0.5 V and 200 bar/2.0 V. Catalyst degradation can be tracked via polarization curves, while 
membrane degradation can be tracked via HFR changes or H2 crossover rates.  
 
5.4 
Porous Transport Layer 
As catalyst utilization/degradation heavily depends on the CL-PTL interface, developing durable PTLs will 
be critical. Surface passivation is a primary PTL degradation mechanism, leading to increased kinetic and 
ohmic overpotentials. Coatings can help mitigate passivation, but the coating itself is subject to degradation 
in the form of dissolution and delamination. Thus, since PTL durability is tied to surface passivation, a 
constant high voltage load of 2.0V would be an appropriate AST. It should be noted that PTL degradation 
is intimately tied with catalyst degradation, so both should be evaluated in conjunction. 
 
 
 

5.5 
Gas Diffusion Layer 
GDLs have been shown to corrode under the oxidizing conditions of PEMFC anodes,459,460 but their 
corrosion under the reducing conditions of PEMWE cathodes is typically not a concern. However, PEMWE 
shutdown events can lead to reverse currents and trigger carbon oxidation. Thus, if frequent shutdown 
events are implemented in an operating strategy, care should be taken to ensure that GDL corrosion does 
not significantly affect system durability. For an AST, since the GDL environment does not experience 
oxidizing conditions during regular operation, cathode cycling between oxidizing (air) and reducing (H2) 
environments could be used—as proposed by Perry et al.460 
 
5.6 
Bipolar Plates 
Despite the BPP’s importance in durability and cost considerations, BPP ASTs have not been proposed for 
PEMFCs or PEMWEs. However, the U.S. DoE has published targets for PEMFC BPPs, including H2 permeation 
and corrosion resistance.461 To quantify corrosion resistance, ex-situ tests are performed where corrosion currents 
are measured via chronoamperometry at relevant potentials in a simulated PEMFC environment—pH 3 with 
0.1ppm of fluoride anions (to represent membrane degradation products). It may seem logical to perform similar 
measurements for PEMWE BPs, but currents are dominated by H2 and O2 evolution at relevant potentials (i.e., 
above 1.5V or below 0V). Nevertheless, an ex-situ test can still be performed to examine ion leaching, H2 
permeation, and contact resistance. The test can be performed at 90°C, pH 3, 0.1 ppm HF, and +2.0V vs. SHE 
(for anode conditions) or -0.2V vs. SHE (for cathode conditions). 
 
However, it should be stated that stack OEMs prefer to see in-situ durability data for BPPs, which can be 
highly time-consuming to obtain. OEMs typically use their own protocols based on targeted operating conditions, 
but for a general in-situ AST, we propose an elevated operating temperature of 90°C to accelerate corrosion and 
hydrogen embrittlement.

Industrial Perspectives 
 
6.1 
Nel Hydrogen 
Nel Hydrogen is a global leader in deployed water electrolyzer systems, commercially selling liquid alkaline 
and proton exchange membrane products worldwide.  While anticipating the hydrogen economy to take 
shape, Nel Hydrogen has been fortunate to sell electrolyzers for industrial applications, such as generator 
turbines (cooling gas), weather balloons (filling), heat-treatment furnaces (atmosphere control), and other 
applications requiring a reducing environment.   Along with a focus on continuous durability and electrical 
efficiency improvements, Nel has aimed to scale up devices to meet emerging industrial and energy storage 
markets for green hydrogen. As shown in Figure 24, significant opportunities exist for green hydrogen to 
decarbonize existing industrial processes.

Nel has supported the hydrogen economy by developing a broad portfolio of systems, spanning kWs 
to hundreds of MWs. The scale-up of equipment and devices not only enables market penetration into areas 
requiring tons of H2 generation per day, but also serves to reduce CapEx. Such CapEx reductions are critical 
to meet green H2 cost targets—such as $1/kg by 2031 (U.S. DOE)—and compete with H2 produced through 
SMR. Figure 25a depicts the two main pathways for cost reduction: 1) technological development, which 
includes factors such as lowering catalyst loadings and using thinner membranes, and 2) scale-up for large-
scale production.  Technological development offers significant opportunities for cost reductions, but 
economic returns gradually flatten as advancements are implemented. Fortunately, scale-up can provide 
further cost reductions, approximated by the rule of “six-tenths” (Figure 25b). According to this rule, for 
a given cost of a PEMWE system (CA), if manufacturing capacities increase by a factor of X, the final cost (Cb) 
decreases by a factor of X0.6.

To enable scale-up, Nel has continuously worked to increase stack size in active area and cell count.  
Such increases have not always been an easy task due to limitations in the existing supply chain. The 
available form factor of existing components has often been a constraint dictating how large these systems 
can go, with stack configurations frequently forced back into design space. However, with the scale-up of 
green hydrogen, this limitation is beginning to diminish.  Thus, suppliers have recognized this time as an 
opportunity to expand their businesses and are open to developing their own manufacturing methods to 
address larger scales. This increase in supplier engagement has additional benefits related to cost: 1) the 
formation of supply chain agreements, which contribute to large orders and volume discounts, and 2) 

direct engagement in the design phase from suppliers, which leads to more conscious stack design tailored 
for automation. These concepts are illustrated in Figure 25c, with the blue plot showing cost reductions 
via supply chain agreements and the black plot showing cost reductions via automation, which includes 
the most rudimentary steps (such as gasket cutting) all the way to stack assembly.    
As renewable electricity costs continue to decline, the acceptance of PEMWE systems is becoming 
more widespread and will continue to build momentum. The challenge is for us to utilize technological 
advancements, increase production scales, obtain supplier engagement, and implement high-volume 
manufacturing processes to position electrolysis as a cost-effective solution to help global decarbonization. 
 
6.2 
Undisclosed Company 
Understanding the broader context of a hydrogen economy is essential when approaching PEMWE 
development. This includes aspects such as large-scale H2 production, storage, and distribution. A 
comprehensive pioneering initiative should involve green H2 production in an interconnected H2-fueled 
ecosystem; a model of this concept is illustrated in Figure 26. In such a system, green H2 generated by 
PEMWEs is distributed across a connected infrastructure to power buildings, fuel both light and heavy-
duty fuel cell vehicles (at H2 refueling stations), and decarbonize industrial plants. Currently, since there is 
no significant market for green H2, the demonstration and deployment of PEMWEs in an ecosystem would 
help build supply and demand. These demonstrations—such as the one in Fukushima462—can provide 
valuable information for understanding the technical feasibility and economic viability of green H2. 
Additionally, such a demonstration serves as an invaluable testbed for assessing performance limitations 
and degradation over time, providing critical real-world data to refine PEMWE technology. 
 
 
 
Figure 26. Schematic of a green hydrogen ecosystem. Green hydrogen production, storage, transportation, and end use 
depicted. 
 

The primary challenge in green H2 is reducing production costs.463,464 In addition to achieving 
economies of scale, there are multiple pathways to reduce costs: low-cost electricity coupling, material 
innovation, and PEMFC synergy. Firstly, electricity prices are the single largest driver for overall 
production costs.  Therefore, it is imperative to couple PEMWEs with low-cost electricity from renewable 
energy sources like wind and solar. Thus, dynamic operation of PEMWE systems needs to be optimized for 
minimal performance degradation.  
Secondly, material innovation plays a pivotal role in cost reduction. The reliance on iridium, one of 
Earth’s rarest elements, presents a significant challenge. Developing catalyst materials with increased 
activity and stability is essential to meet efficiency, lifetime, and catalyst loading targets. Current 
commercial PEMWEs use a loading of ~3 mgIr cm-2; reducing this to 0.1 mgIr cm-2 or less is critical to make 
large-scale deployment economically feasible.465 Furthermore, manufacturing MEAs with such low 
loadings presents significant challenges with existing coating methods, such as doctor blade coating or slot 
die coating. Thus, innovations in catalyst design and large-scale CL fabrication techniques are imperative.  
Thirdly, synergy with already established fuel cell technologies could also reduce costs. Many 
components in PEMWEs—including the cathode catalyst, membrane, and BPP—are common with those 
used in PEMFCs. A strategic synergy between these two technologies can accelerate the initial development 
phase and capitalize on economies of scale, leading to significant reductions in investment costs for 
PEMWEs. Moreover, the shared components can enable greater cost efficiencies—particularly in mass 
production—due to common supply chains and manufacturing processes.466 
With combined efforts in material innovation, cost reduction, and real-world demonstrations, GW-
scale PEMWE deployment can be achieved. Ultimately, demonstrating the technical feasibility and 
economic viability of a green H2 ecosystem is critical for PEMWE manufacturers to establish a sustainable 
business. 
 
6.3 
Plug Power 
PEMWE devices are rapidly moving from niche applications to projects greater than 100 MW.  The U.S. has 
pledged $9.5 billion to hydrogen production hubs and electrolyzer research, while Europe is looking to add 
40 GW of green hydrogen by 2030.  This scale-up brings with it several challenges.  First, the supply chain 
is not currently set up for these developments, and rapid production scaling could put pressure on precious 
PGM materials used as catalysts.  The maturity of regulations, site permitting, and codes vary wildly by 
location, leading to difficulty in design standardization for global deployment. An influx of interest and 
money has led to thousands of new entrants, few with the experience and history necessary for large-scale 
projects. Lastly, with all this considered, safety must be at the forefront of system deployments. 
Scaling up PEMWE systems requires a consistent supply of critical components, such as catalysts, 
membrane materials, and PTLs. Limited production capacities of these components can lead to bottlenecks, 
delays, and increased costs. Additionally, the demand for rare catalyst materials, especially iridium, can 
result in supply chain vulnerabilities. Fortunately, multiple studies have shown that iridium usage is low-
risk, and PEMWEs can reach the terawatt level with decreased loadings, increased current densities, and 
iridium recycling.467,468  Another concern is the PFSA materials necessary for the membrane, binders, and 
gasketing.  Bans on such materials are under consideration in both the E.U. and the U.S.  Though there is 
active research in non-PFSA materials, nothing to date has shown adequate durability under realistic 
conditions (>50,000h at >60°C). 
Large-scale PEMWE projects must adhere to various codes and standards to ensure operational 
reliability and safety. These codes cover equipment design, installation, operation, maintenance, and 
emergency response. Such regulations are most mature in Europe, where stacks usually need to be ATEX 
certified (Atmosphère Explosible; “Explosive Atmospheres”; ensures safe use in explosive environments) 
and must obtain a CE mark (conformité européenne; “European Conformity”; affirms conformity with 
health, safety, and environmental standards). The interior of a stack is generally, and rightfully, considered 
an explosive environment, requiring ATEX certification. Afterward, to obtain a CE mark, the stack must 
meet the “Pressure Equipment Directive,” demonstrating that it can withstand a multiple of the maximum 
allowed working pressure. This process requires additional design, testing, and manufacturing 

certifications from notified bodies. In the U.S., it is generally left to local regulatory bodies who often rely 
on ASME (American Society of Mechanical Engineers) standards.  Predictably, this flexibility leads to wildly 
varying permitting needs and timeframes.  Only recently was a unified path being created for an 
electrolyzer certification scheme—called “U stamp”—for stack quality and engineering requirements, 
developed through an ASME case code. 
Scaling up PEMWE systems involves compliance with local, regional, and national regulations. 
Obtaining hydrogen production, storage, and transportation permits can be complex and time-consuming.  
A good starting point for understanding the hundreds of codes and requirements from the stack to the 
system level is reviewing the guidelines for the U.K. and the U.S. state of California.469,470 Zoning restrictions, 
environmental impact assessments, and public safety must be considered. Collaborative efforts between 
industry stakeholders and regulatory bodies are necessary to streamline permitting procedures and 
ensure efficient project execution. Commonality is critical to bringing hydrogen generation to multiple 
locations, as regulatory uncertainty can defeat promising projects before they begin. R&D is also vital for 
scale-up: innovations in catalyst loading reduction, non-PFSA ionomers, energy efficiency, system design, 
safety measures, and supply chain optimization can collectively alleviate the difficulties associated with 
large-scale implementation. Public-private partnerships, academic collaborations, and government 
support are actively being pursued in these areas, with targets established for cost and performance to 
bridge efforts together.242 
As the scale of PEMWE systems increases, so do safety concerns. Hydrogen, though a clean fuel, is 
highly flammable and poses explosion risks. Inadequate handling and storage of hydrogen can result in 
catastrophic accidents. Proper personnel training, stringent safety protocols, leak detection systems, and 
robust ventilation infrastructure are imperative, especially in large-scale installations. Although codes and 
standards exist for stacks and systems, they are often overlooked due to their immaturity or inconsistency 
between locations. Safe stack operation requires constant monitoring of voltage, water flow, temperature, 
pressure, and—most importantly— H2 content (on the anode side). However, even with constant system 
monitoring, stack designers must plan for worst-case scenarios. As an example, Plug Power tests all system 
components for catastrophic failure; Figure 27 shows the time-evolved progression of one of these tests, 
where a 1 MW stack and its anode outlet are subjected to worse-case H2/O2 stoichiometries that could 
result during a complete failure (i.e., membrane rupture). The H2/O2 gas mixture is then detonated, and 
the system is observed to ensure that physical explosion risks are contained and that the stack maintains 
its physical integrity. To avoid premature ignition, one must remove the catalysts in the stack before testing. 
Similar tests are done on the H2O/O2 separator, which is generally the system component with the most 
significant potential for explosive damage. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

 
 
 
 
Figure 27. Testing of stack detonation under worst-case H2/O2 stoichiometric conditions. Test ensures that the stack 
contains the explosion and that no shrapnel is generated upon failure. 
 
Scaling up PEMWEs holds the key to unlocking hydrogen's potential as a clean energy carrier. 
However, challenges related to supply chain management, safety considerations, permitting procedures, 
and adherence to codes and standards must be effectively addressed. Collaboration among stakeholders, 
regulatory bodies, researchers, and industrial companies is essential to overcome these challenges and 
pave the way for a sustainable and efficient GW-scale PEMWE industry.

7.1.1 Cost  
System and stack CapEx greatly influence H2 production costs. At the system level (which comprises the 
stack and balance of plant), increasing manufacturability capabilities in production volume and unit size 
(from kW to GW capacities) will be the primary driver for cost reductions. Other drivers include improving 
equipment utilization, utilizing roll-to-roll assembly, and increasing automation. At the stack level, 
component costs dictate, particularly for the MEA, PTL, and BPs. While increasing manufacturing volume 
will reduce costs, material costs will begin to dominate at higher volumes (Figure 7a). As the MEA can 
comprise nearly half of the stack cost at these scales, PGM loadings must be reduced while maintaining 
activity and durability. Novel base materials and coatings for BPs and PTLs, which are currently made of 
titanium and platinum, also need to be developed. Stainless steel is a promising base material for both.  
Lastly, production costs will highly depend on electricity prices and capacity factors (Figure 4). 
Load-following for cheap renewable electricity will be critical. Operating at high capacity factors will 
reduce costs substantially, but periods of low load are inevitable. Thus, grid control algorithms and 
management strategies are required. 
 
7.1.2 Durability 
Commercial systems use high PGM loadings for MEAs, PTLs, and BPs to ensure adequate durability, leading 
to prohibitively high costs for large-scale deployment. Thus, developing cost-effective components that can 
reach lifetime targets will be a primary research focus. Optimizing the CL-PTL interface will be imperative 
to mitigate degradation, especially at low loadings. Incorporating an MPL on the PTL surface is a promising 
strategy. Also, membrane durability will become increasingly relevant as thinner membranes are 
implemented. Reinforcements, recombinant catalysts, and radical scavengers can be incorporated to 
combat any increased susceptibility to mechanical and chemical damage. Lastly, cell components are 
currently optimized for constant load operation, but PEMWEs will likely operate dynamically for load 
following. Thus, degradation under such conditions must be thoroughly considered during durability 
testing.  

 
Standardized accelerated stress tests are required to ensure objective comparison between 
datasets and to assess durability under industrially relevant conditions (i.e., load following). While no such 
tests are currently established, ASTs for each PEMWE cell component have been proposed in  
Table 4.    
 
7.1.3 Efficiency 
Achieving targets for stack efficiency (80% LHV) and nominal operating conditions (5A cm-2 @ 1.7V) will 
rely on improvements in the anode CL, PTL, and membrane. Developing highly active low-loading anode 
CLs will require creating homogenous, electrically connected networks. Utilizing TiO2 supports can help, 
but the challenge of forming percolating iridium oxide networks needs to be overcome. Optimizing PTL 
properties—such as porosity and pore size—can reduce mass transport losses and increase catalyst 
utilization. Incorporating an MPL can also increase utilization by enhancing CL-PTL interfacial contact. 
Lastly, reductions in membrane thickness will be imperative due to decreased ohmic overpotentials, but 
care must be taken to limit degradation and gas crossover. 
 
7.2 
Manufacturability 
Given the current deployment of PEMWEs, considerations for large-scale manufacturing have largely been 
overlooked in the literature. Thus, issues may arise when standard lab-scale fabrication techniques cannot 
be employed at high throughput. For example, most of the high-performing low-loading CLs in the 
literature were created using spray coating,137,175,481 which is less suitable for roll-to-roll manufacturing 
than methods involving sweeping a tool over a substrate (e.g., slot-die coating, gravure coating, and screen 
printing). Furthermore, since optimal ink composition varies between methods, transferring knowledge 
acquired from one method to another can be ineffective. The authors recommend that more focus be put 
on fabrication techniques that are more suitable for large-scale production. In addition, the PTL fabrication 
process must also be improved for reliable high-throughput manufacturing. In-line quality controls will be 
necessary to detect surface defects, impurities, edge warps, and inhomogeneities. Without stringent efforts 
to guarantee CL and PTL quality, stack-level failures can occur and impart significant setbacks to large-
scale operations.  
 
7.3 
Policies 
The number of countries implementing green hydrogen policies is growing rapidly, with ~50 countries to 
date. This trajectory must be continued to meet deployment targets. However, despite the ambitious 
policies presented, a vast discrepancy exists between active projects and projects in the planning stages. 
Projects up to final investment decision (FID) constitute only 7% of total announced projects, accounting 
for a mere 0.7 GW of PEMWE deployment. Given that 220 GW are required by 2030 to reach net zero 
emissions, policymakers must substantially accelerate green hydrogen projects.  
 
8 
Conclusion 
The vast global participation for PEMWEs provides much confidence for the future. With concerted efforts 
in every developmental facet, the path to GW-scale deployment can be realized. Fundamental research for 
improving activity and durability while limiting costs is necessary. Large-scale manufacturability must be 
considered in conjunction with any developments. As technological readiness levels increase, a mature set 
of codes and standards must be established to ensure safe operation. Lastly, global green hydrogen policies 
must be monitored to ensure that set targets are reached. This review has captured the current state of 
PEMWEs and provided an overarching road map for gigawatt-scale deployment.
