ABSTRACT 
The Li-S chemistry is thermodynamically promising for high-density energy storage but kinetically 
challenging. Over the past few years, many catalyst materials have been developed to improve the 
performance of Li-S batteries and their catalytic role has been increasingly accepted. However, the 
classic catalytic behavior, i.e., reduction of reaction barrier, has not been clearly observed. Crucial 
mechanistic questions, including what specific step is limiting the reaction rate, whether/how it can be 
catalyzed, and how the catalysis is sustained after the catalyst surface is covered by solid products, 
remain unanswered. Herein, we report the first identification of the potential-limiting step of Li-S 
batteries operating under lean electrolyte conditions and its catalysis that conforms to classic catalysis 
principles, where the catalyst lowers the kinetic barrier of the potential-limiting step and accelerates the 
reaction without affecting the product composition. After carefully examining the electrochemistry 
under lean electrolyte conditions, we update the pathway of the Li-S battery reaction: S8 solid is first 
reduced to Li2S8 and Li2S4 molecular species sequentially; the following reduction of Li2S4 to a Li2S2-
Li2S solid with an almost constant ratio of 1:4 is the potential-limiting step; the previously believed 
Li2S2-to-Li2S solid-solid conversion does not occur; the recharging reaction is relatively fast. We further 
demonstrate that supported cobalt phthalocyanine molecules can effectively catalyze the potential-
limiting step. After Li2S2/Li2S buries the active sites, it can self-catalyze the reaction and continue 
driving the discharging process.

INTRODUCTION 
The interconversion reaction between S8 and Li2S, involving the transfer of 16 electrons per S8 molecule, 
promises a large capacity for the cathode of lithium-sulfur (Li-S) batteries. In commonly used ether-
based electrolytes, the lithiation (i.e., discharging) of S8 is believed to go through a series of soluble 
lithium polysulfides (LPS) to insoluble Li2S2 and then to Li2S solid.1-3 Undesirable migration of LPS 
causes active material loss and side reactions, limiting the Coulombic efficiency and cycle life of the 
battery.4, 5 One of the earlier strategies to tackle this problem is to employ a sulfur host material that 
immobilizes LPS.6-9 Later, it was realized that a good sulfur host should also be able to promote the 
electrochemical conversion of LPS, which led to the emerging of the electrocatalysis concept for Li-S 
batteries.10-13 Thus far, a considerable number of materials, including doped-carbons,14, 15 nitrides,16, 17 
chalcogenides,18-20 phosphides,12, 21-23 borides,24, 25 and single-atom materials,26-28 have been reported as 
catalysts for Li-S batteries. These materials usually combine LPS affinity with electrical conductivity 
to enable easier electron transfer and sulfur conversion.  
While the catalytic role of these sulfur host materials has been increasingly accepted over the past 
several years, the classic catalytic behavior, i.e., reduction of reaction barrier, has not been observed for 
Li-S batteries. Recently, Huang, Sautet, Duan, and coworkers examined the LPS reduction reactions in 
a rotating disk electrode setting and found the number of transferred electrons can be increased by 
applying an electrocatalyst, an important step toward unveiling the catalysis.15 Yet, the effect on the 
battery remains to be enhancement in capacity, power rate, and cycle life, as in other cases of flooded 
electrolyte (i.e., high electrolyte/active material ratio) systems.10, 11 In our prior work, we demonstrated 
that the kinetic barriers of the Li-S reaction, i.e., the charging and discharging overpotentials of the 
battery, are considerably larger under lean electrolyte conditions (i.e., low electrolyte/active material 
ratio) where the LPS intermediates cannot be sufficiently dissolved.13 However, this observation was 
still based on the overall battery reaction. It remains unknown what specific step is limiting the reaction 
rate, whether/how it can be catalyzed, and how the catalysis is sustained after the catalyst surface is 
covered by solid products. Answering these questions is particularly challenging because the reaction 
of interest here is a complex system that involves multiple steps including possible solid-solution, 
solution-solution, solution-solid, and solid-solid conversions.3, 29 
Herein, we report the first identification of the potential-limiting step, i.e. the step that dictates the 

4 
 
overpotential of an electrochemical reaction, of the Li-S battery reaction and its catalysis under lean 
electrolyte conditions. We find that the reduction of dissolved Li2S4 is the potential-limiting step of the 
entire battery reaction, which forms a Li5S3 solid product (1:4 mixture of Li2S2 and Li2S solids). The 
previously believed Li2S2-to-Li2S solid-solid conversion does not occur in the commonly used voltage 
range. We further show that heterogenized cobalt phthalocyanine (CoPc) molecules can effectively 
catalyze this step. Compared to a graphitic carbon surface, the strong interaction between the cobalt 
center and the sulfur of LPS can effectively reduce the energy barrier of Li2S4 reduction to solid Li5S3. 
After the initial deposition, the CoPc active sites are covered by the sulfide solid which can self-catalyze 
the reduction of Li2S4, continuing to propel the discharging reaction.  
 
RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
We first evaluate the performance of a typical Li-S cell with a carbon nanotube and sulfur composite 
(CNT-S) cathode and with an electrolyte-to-sulfur (E/S, μL/mg) ratio of 6, a representative value for 
lean electrolyte conditions under which the LPS intermediates cannot fully dissolve.30, 31 Tested at a low 
current density of 0.2 mA/cm2, the cell shows a discharging and charging voltage profile characteristic 
of the multiple electrochemical transformation reactions described in the classic picture of Li-S batteries 
(Figure 1a).3, 5, 29, 32 During the first-voltage-plateau discharging (2.1-2.4 V vs Li+/Li), the S8 molecular 
solid undergoes a ring-opening reaction to form long-chain Li2S8 molecules that are soluble in the ether 
electrolyte (2.3-2.4 V), which are subsequently reduced to shorter-chain Li2S6 and Li2S4 molecules 
sequentially (2.1-2.3 V). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurement confirms that the 
discharging product at the end of the first plateau corresponds to Li2S4 (Figure S1). In the second-plateau 
discharging at 2.1 V, Li2S4 is firstly converted to insoluble Li2S2 and then reduced to Li2S solid. The 
charging, which does not display an apparent voltage plateau, is believed to be the reverse process of 
the “solid-solution-solid” discharging conversion. At a higher current of 0.8 mA/cm2, the cell 
completely loses the second discharging plateau (Figure 1b), meaning that the deposition of Li2S2/Li2S 
does not occur. Note that this current density, corresponding to 0.12 C, is far from the mass transport 
limiting region for typical batteries and thus serves well for the purpose of kinetic studies. Control 
experiments suggest that this is caused by a large kinetic barrier associated with the reduction of Li2S4: 
when we lower the discharging cutoff voltage from 1.7 to 0.8 V, the second discharging plateau can be 

5 
 
recovered with a large initial overpotential of ~0.8 V (Figure 1c). We note that this kinetic barrier is 
only observed under lean electrolyte conditions (Figure S2), which corroborates our earlier conclusion 
that catalysis, namely reduction of reaction barrier, is most needed for lean-electrolyte Li-S batteries.13 
The charging process has considerably better kinetics than the discharging, as we find the fully 
discharged cell can be effectively recharged at high rates up to 4 mA/cm2 (Figure 1d and S3). Taken 
together, the potential-limiting step of Li-S conversion is the initial reduction of Li2S4 during the 
discharging.

With observation of the potential-limiting step, we seek to catalyze the Li-S battery reaction under 

6 
 
lean electrolyte conditions. We construct an electrocatalyst by supporting CoPc molecules on CNTs 
(Figure 2a). With molecular-level dispersion of CoPc (Figure S4), this catalyst provides abundant well-
defined catalytic sites without considerable alteration of the morphology of the CNT support or the 
resulting sulfur electrode material (Figure S5). With a low CoPc content of 0.5 wt.% in the CoPc/CNT-
S electrode material, the reaction barrier of the potential-limiting step, i.e. the initial reduction of Li2S4, 
is substantially reduced from 0.80 to 0.02 V at the current density of 0.8 mA/cm2 (Figure 2b, c). As the 
current density is increased stepwise to 1.4 mA/cm2, the overpotential increases slightly and remains 
much lower than that without the CoPc catalyst (Figure 2b).

profiles of CNT-S and CoPc/CNT-S electrodes at varied discharging rates under lean electrolyte 
conditions. (c) Reduction of reaction barrier by CoPc at 0.8 mA/cm2. (d) Co 2p XPS spectrum of CoPc

To characterize the catalytic effect of CoPc molecules, we study the chemical interaction between 
CoPc and LPS with XPS. We use a Li2S4 solution prepared from mixing stoichiometric amounts of S8 
and Li2S to represent the reactant. After interacting CoPc/CNT and Li2S4, the Co 2p peaks shift to lower 
binding energies while the S 2p peaks shift to higher binding energies (Figure 2d and S6a), which 
suggests chemical binding between the Co center of CoPc and the S atoms of LPS. No binding energy 

7 
 
shift is observed in the N 1s spectra (Figure S6b), indicating that the N atoms of the Pc ligand do not 
bind to the Li of LPS, likely because the latter is solvated by dimethoxyethane. The chemical affinity 
with LPS allows CoPc-coated CNTs to easily capture Li2S4 and reduce it to Li2S2/Li2S. We then assess 
the ease of depositing sulfide on CoPc/CNT by holding the electrode potential at 2.11 V vs Li+/Li to 
reduce Li2S4 dissolved in the battery electrolyte.33, 34 Compared to CNTs without CoPc, CoPc/CNT 
shows a considerably higher current response (Figure S7) together with a more uniform and thicker 
Li2S2/Li2S deposition layer (Figure 2e, f, Figure S8), indicating easier nucleation and faster growth of 
solid sulfides enabled by the CoPc catalytic sites. Tracking the solid growth process with scanning 
electron microscopy (SEM) reveals that the nucleation indeed starts from the CoPc/CNT sites on the 
carbon fiber paper electrode (Figure S9). Sulfide deposition on a single CoPc-coated CNT is also 
uniform, as evidenced by subnanometer-resolution mapping of the S element with scanning 
transmission electron microscopy (Figure S10). 
Unlike classic catalysis in the gas or liquid phase where the product leaves the catalyst surface after 
reaction, the catalysis here in the Li-S battery generates a solid product (Li2S2/Li2S) that covers the 
catalyst surface. The question, which has not been answered in previous studies, is how the catalysis or 
discharging continues after all the active sites are buried by Li2S2/Li2S. The neat tubular morphology of 
our CoPc/CNT hybrid material allows us to easily discern structural changes on the material surface. 
We first examine electrodes withdrawn from cells that have been discharged for varied capacity into the 
second voltage plateau (Figure 3a). We observe that the sulfide product initially nucleates on the 
CoPc/CNT surface (Figure S11) and then continues to grow into a film which buries CoPc/CNT (Figure 
3b). At the end of the discharging, the Li2S2/Li2S film is dense and bulky (Figure 3c). These results 
suggest that the solid product covers CoPc/CNT at an early stage of the second discharging plateau and 
the originally active CoPc sites are no longer available for catalyzing the subsequent discharging. We 
therefore hypothesize that Li2S2/Li2S solid can form via a self-catalytic process (Figure 3d), i.e., the 
initially formed sulfide solid can catalyze the subsequent reduction of Li2S4 to Li2S2/Li2S.  
To test this hypothesis, we switch to the CNT-S electrode to exclude the catalytic effect of CoPc. 
Having been discharged at a low current of 0.2 mA/cm2 for a capacity of 200 mAh/g into the second 
voltage plateau (Figure 3e), the electrode surface is almost fully covered by a layer of sulfide solid with 
no CNTs exposed (Figure 3f). Interestingly, this electrode can continue to be discharged at a high current 

8 
 
of 0.8 mA/cm2 (Figure 3e), a current at which CNT-S cannot be discharged without a pre-deposited 
sulfide layer (Figure 1b), which unambiguously confirms the self-catalytic function of Li2S2/Li2S. The 
overall discharging process exhibits negligible overpotential (Figure 3e), and the final discharging 
product displays a similar dense structure as in the CoPc/CNT-S case (Figure 3g). The same pre-
deposition operation can enable the CNT-S electrode to work at an even lower E/S ratio of 4.3 (Figure 
S12a). Control experiments verify that the pre-deposited sulfide layer promotes the reduction of Li2S4 
and facilitates the growth of the Li2S2/Li2S solid product itself (Figure S12b-d). We further notice that 
a partially covered sulfide layer, however, is not sufficient to enable high-current discharging (Figure 
S13). These results depict a picture of the lithiation of CNT-S at the second discharging voltage plateau 
(Figure 3h). At the initial stage, Li2S2/Li2S prefers to deposit onto the CNT surface because of its high 
conductivity. Limited by its high kinetic barrier, this deposition can only proceed at low current density 
(Figure S14). After the conductive surface is fully passivated by the discharging product, further 
deposition goes onto the Li2S2/Li2S surface. Discharging can now take place at high current density 
because the sulfide surface self-catalyzes the reaction, which is likely enabled by the good affinity 
between Li2S4 and Li2S2/Li2S via Li-S interaction. Therefore, CoPc functionalization crucially facilitates 
strong interaction with Li2S4 via Co-S bonding, which matches well with the XPS result (Figure 2d), 
and thus makes the CNT surface catalytically active for the potential-limiting step, namely Li2S4 
reduction to Li2S2/Li2S.

The interesting self-catalysis behavior motivates us to investigate the identity of the solid product 
of the potential-limiting step. The S K-edge X-ray adsorption near edge structure (XANES) spectra of 
CoPc/CNT-S electrodes fully discharged at 0.2 and 0.8 mA/cm2 respectively show that both final 
discharging products are consisted of Li2S and Li2S2 (Figure 4a). CNT-S fully discharged at 0.2 mA/cm2 
also has a similar composition, agreeing with the basic catalysis principle that introducing a catalyst 

10 
 
improves the reaction rate but does not change the reaction product. We note that the co-existence of 
Li2S and Li2S2 in the final discharging product agrees well with recent literature.35 Because of the 
catalytic effect of CoPc, the fast discharged (0.8 mA/cm2) CoPc/CNT-S electrode has as uniform 
coverage of the solid product as the slowly discharged CNT-S electrode (0.2 mA/cm2), as revealed by 
X-ray fluorescence imaging (Figure 4b). 
We further carry out XPS measurements to quantitively study the discharge product composition, 
i.e. the Li2S2/Li2S ratio. After removing the top surface with sputtering (Figure S15), we find that the 
solid product only contains Li2S2 and Li2S, and their ratio remains approximately 1:4 at varied 
discharging depth (Figure 4c). The same Li2S2/Li2S ratio is observed for CNT-S discharged at 0.2 
mA/cm2 and CoPc/CNT-S discharged at 0.2 or 0.8 mA/cm2 (Figure S16), suggesting that the 
electrochemical reaction reaches the same product regardless of the discharging rate and presence of a 
catalyst. Expectedly, CNT-S discharged at high current, during which process the second-plateau 
discharging does not occur, contains neither Li2S2 nor Li2S (Figure S17). Based on the final product of 
1:4 Li2S2:Li2S, the theoretical capacity of the overall discharging reaction starting from S8 is calculated 
to be ~1400 mAh/g. The first discharging plateau, which is S8 reduction to Li2S4, should account for 
~30% of the total capacity. This deduction is in line with the experimental results which show that the 
two discharging plateaus have a capacity ratio of approximately 3:7 regardless of the discharging rate 
and presence of a catalyst (Figure S18).

Our finding that Li2S2 and Li2S are produced in almost a constant ratio across the second 
discharging plateau indicates that the conversion of Li2S2 to Li2S in the solid state does not occur, which 
challenges the prevalent understanding of the Li-S battery chemistry that S8 is reduced to Li2S2 and then 
to Li2S.1-3, 5, 29, 32 As Li2S2 reduction to Li2S is believed to be well above the 1.7 V discharging cutoff 
voltage, the absence of this conversion reaction in our batteries suggests that this solid-to-solid 
transition is likely limited by kinetics. To further validate this analysis, we take a fully discharged CNT-
S electrode (cutoff voltage 1.7 V vs Li+/Li) and continue to discharge it to 0.5 V. We find that the 
Li2S2/Li2S ratio of the discharged material decreases from 1:4.2 to 1:4.8 after this additional discharging 
step (Figure 4d). This verifies that the solid-phase Li2S2-to-Li2S conversion indeed demands a large 

12 
 
overpotential and is difficult to go to completion.

CONCLUSION 
The findings in this work allow us to update the understanding of the Li-S battery chemistry under lean 
electrolyte conditions (Figure 5). In the discharging process, the initial stepwise conversion of S8 to 
soluble Li2S8 and Li2S4 molecular species proceeds with relatively fast kinetics. The following reduction 
of Li2S4 to a solid mixture of Li2S2 and Li2S (1:4) is the potential-limiting step and has a large kinetic 
barrier on graphitic carbon surfaces. CoPc molecules supported on the carbon surface can effectively 
catalyze this step and facilitate the initial deposition of Li2S2/Li2S at high current density. As the 
discharging continues, the CoPc sites are gradually covered with the solid product and lose their 
catalytic function. However, the discharging does not stop at this point. The Li2S2/Li2S product can self-
catalyze this reaction and the product continues to accumulate until the discharging is driven to the end. 
Compared to discharging, the charging process is much faster and thus not a rate-limiting factor in this 
system. This work elucidates the catalysis in Li-S batteries and will guide future materials development 
for accelerating the chemistry under application-relevant conditions.
