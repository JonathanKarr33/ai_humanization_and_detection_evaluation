Abstract – Biocatalysis has revolutionized chemical synthesis, providing sustainable methods for 
10 
preparing various organic molecules. In enzyme-mediated organic synthesis, most reactions 
11 
involve molecules operating from their ground states. Over the past twenty-five years, there has 
12 
been an increased interest in enzymatic processes that utilize electronically excited states 
13 
accessed through photoexcitation. These photobiocatalytic processes involve a diverse array of 
14 
reaction mechanisms that are complementary to one another. This comprehensive review will 
15 
describe the state-of-the-art strategies in photobiocatalysis for organic synthesis until December 
16 
2022. Apart from reviewing the relevant literature, a central goal of this review is to delineate the 
17 
mechanistic differences between the general strategies employed in the field. We will organize 
18 
this review based on the relationship between the photochemical step and the enzymatic 
19 
transformations. The review will include mechanistic studies, substrate scope, and protein 
20 
optimization strategies. By clearly defining mechanistically distinct strategies in photobiocatalytic 
21 
chemistry, we hope to illuminate future synthetic opportunities in the area.

1.0 Introduction 
57 
58 
Enzymes are attractive catalysts for chemical synthesis because they carry out 
59 
transformations with catalyst efficiencies and reaction selectivity that surpass what is possible 
60 
with small molecule catalysts.1 A traditional workflow involves identifying an enzyme that can 
61 
catalyze the desired reaction on your substrate of interest. If the starting enzyme fails to provide 
62 
the desired results (activity, enantioselectivity, solvent tolerance, etc.), directed evolution can 
63 
optimize the protein's function.2,3 The advent of sophisticated techniques for generating mutant 
64 
libraries and advances in analytic chemistry has accelerated protein engineering to the point 
65 
where biocatalysts are now considered competitive alternatives to traditional small molecular 
66 
catalysts for reactions such as asymmetric ketone reduction and reductive amination.4,5 Moreover, 
67 
enzymes are now commonly used in chemical manufacturing to streamline the synthesis of 
68 
pharmaceuticals.6,7,8,9 
 
69 
As biocatalysis develops as a tool for synthetic organic chemists, enzymes need to 
70 
catalyze new types of reactions.10,11 As the in-silico design of enzymes remains challenging,12,13 
71 
new biocatalytic reactions are typically developed by mining natural product pathways to 
72 
discover enzymes that catalyze new types of transformations. However, as nature is conservative 
73 
in its synthetic strategies, enzymes catalyze far fewer reactions than small molecule transition 
74 
metal catalysts or organocatalysts. Consequently, efforts have been focused on discovering 
75 
enzymes that possess unrealized catalytic potential. 
 
76 
Over the past fifteen years, visible light photocatalysis has reemerged as a powerful 
77 
platform in chemical synthesis.14,15 In these reactions, a photocatalyst, typically an organic dye or 
78 
transition metal complex, is photoexcited to access an electronically excited state which can 
79 
engage in electron or energy transfer events to form radical intermediates. This activation mode 
80 
has enabled countless synthetic reactions. Moreover, merging photocatalysis with transition 
81 
metal catalysis or organocatalysis allows for reactions that are inaccessible to either catalyst 
82 
individually.16,17 These reports have spurred interest in using light with enzymes. Nature uses light 
83 
in many different contexts. In photosynthesis, large multiprotein complexes convert photonic 

84 
energy to chemical energy to sustain life. Alternatively, light-oxygen-voltage (LOV) domains use 
85 
light to induce conformation changes to regulate proteins and enzymes. Interestingly, only three 
86 
enzymes are known in nature to absorb sunlight to promote a chemical reaction; DNA 
87 
photolyase,18 fatty acid photodecarboxylase,19 and protochlorophyllide reductase.20 While this 
88 
activation mode is rare, these enzymes showcase how enzymes use light to facilitate biologically 
89 
essential transformations. 
 
90 
The burgeoning area of “photobiocatalysis” is focused on developing new biocatalytic 
91 
reactions that involve light.21,22,23,24 This review will organize reports based on the relationship 
92 
between the photochemical step and enzymatic transformation. We will define key concepts and 
93 
focus on mechanistic nuances that make strategies distinct from one another. By clearly defining 
94 
the mechanistic differences between various approaches, we hope to contribute to the overall 
95 
understanding of how light can be used in biocatalytic synthesis. 
 
96 
The first section will focus on photoenzymatic catalysis. We define photoenzymatic 
97 
catalysis as reactions where a cofactor, located within the protein active site, is photoexcited to 
98 
promote electron or energy transfer required for the conversion of starting material to product 
99 
(Figure 1A). Discussion of this topic will be focused on enzymes of value for synthetic organic 
100 
chemistry. The second section will explore synergistic photoenzymatic catalysis (Figure 1B). 
101 
These reactions involve the excitation of an exogenous cofactor to enable a chemical 
102 
transformation within a protein active site. The third section will focus on photobiocatalytic 
103 
cascades (Figure 1C). 
In these reactions, the photochemical transformation occurs in the 
104 
presence of the enzyme but is not involved in the enzyme's mechanism for converting starting 
105 
material to product. This section will contain four subsections; i) reactions where light is used for 
106 
cofactor regeneration, ii) reactions where photoexcitation is involved in modifying the substrate 
107 
of the enzyme in a one-pot, one-step process, iii) reactions where photoexcitation modifies the 
108 
substrate in a one-pot, two-step process. In the final section, we will discuss whole-cell 
109 
photobiocatalytic reactions that use light with cyanobacteria to produce NADPH that enzymes 
110 
can consume to reduce substrates (Figure 1D). In clearly defining these terms, we hope to 

111 
provide precise language for discussing photobiocatalysis and can help researchers understand 
112 
the current limits of the technology to drive new areas.

2.0 Photoenzymatic Catalysis 
117 
118 
Photoenzymatic processes are reactions where a cofactor or amino acid side chain within 
119 
an enzyme absorbs light and uses the photonic energy to convert starting material to product.25 
120 
While this general mechanism is ubiquitous in small molecule photoredox catalysis,26,27 
121 
photoenzymes are relatively rare, with only three currently known in nature. The oldest 
122 
photoenzyme is DNA photolyase, a flavin-dependent enzyme responsible for repairing 
123 
pyrimidine cyclobutane dimers in DNA.18 These dimeric lesions are formed when DNA is 
124 
irradiated with UV light. In this enzyme, the reduced flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) cofactor 
125 
(FADhq–) serves as the photocatalyst, initially reducing the pyrimidine dimer to initiate a retro 2+2 
126 
reaction followed by oxidation of the resulting radical anion by FAD semiquinone (FADsq).28,29 
127 
Many homologs of this enzyme have additional deazaflavin or pterin cofactors to expand the 
128 
wavelengths of light that can promote this reactivity. Another well-studied natural photoenzyme 
129 
is light-dependent protochlorophyllide reductase (LPOR), a nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide 
130 
phosphate (NADPH) dependent enzyme responsible for reducing protochlorophyllide (PChlide) 
131 
to chlorophyllide.30 Among natural photoenzymes, LPOR remains unique in that the reduction. 
132 
occurs via photoexcitation of the PChlide substrate rather than the NADPH cofactor. This excited 
133 
state can oxidize NADPH via single electron transfer to form NADPH+. and PChlide radical anion.

2.1 Fatty Acid Photodecarboxylase 
 
138 
The third natural photoenzyme is Fatty Acid Photodecarboxylase (FAP). As reported in 
139 
2017  by  Beisson  and  coworkers,  this  is  a  natural  photoenzyme  that  catalyzes  the 
140 
hydrodecarboxylation of fatty acids, a reaction with potential synthetic value (Figure 2Erreur ! 
141 
Source du renvoi introuvable.A).31 In contrast to previously known fatty acid decarboxylases, 
142 
which catalyze oxidative decarboxylation to afford a terminal alkene, FAPs facilitate a redox- 
143 
neutral reaction to form an alkane.32,33 The first member of this family was found in the microalgae 
144 
Chlorella variabilis NC64A (EC 4.1.1.106), although putative FAPs are found in many algal 
145 
species suggesting this enzyme family in ancient.34 This flavoprotein is in the glucose-methanol- 
146 
choline (GMC) family of oxidoreductases. Like other GMC oxidoreductases, FAP is a two-domain 
147 
protein where the first domain contains the N-terminal residues and is responsible for flavin 
148 
binding and stabilization, while the second domain is involved in substrate and product binding 
149 
and trafficking.31 The conserved residues in the FAD binding domain interact with the FAD 
150 
cofactor, causing it to adopt a bent “butterfly” conformation 14º degrees from planarity. This 
151 
structural feature red-shifts the ground state FAD absorption spectrum which could aid in light 
152 
absorption.35

2.1.2 
Reaction Condition Engineering 
 
198 
Recently, two studies reported immobilizing fatty acid photodecarboxylase with the goal of 
199 
increasing its stability and activity. Chanquia et al. tested the activity of CvFAP on EziG® beads 
200 
containing Fe(III) cations for His-tag affinity immobilization.39 They found the Amber carrier to 
201 
give the highest degree of immobilization (84%) and to provide 2.8% yield when decarboxylating 
202 
C17 carboxylic acids. The immobilized enzyme could be reused up to three times in aqueous 
203 
media. In this study, they also explored the impact that deep eutectic solvents have on FAP 
204 
activity. When immobilized, deep eutectic solvents diminish reusability. However, when not 
205 
immobilized, the mixture 30% Choline chloride and glycerol in water provides a nearly 20% 
206 
increase in conversion by comparison to water alone, suggesting the eutectic solvents can 
207 
modulate activity. 
 
208 
In another study, Simić et al. developed a strategy for running CvFAP in continuous flow.40 
209 
The authors carefully studied different parameters such as a) carrier selection for immobilization 
210 
and to limit enzyme leaching during the reaction, b) Light condition: intensity, wavelength and 
211 
light penetration, c) Substrate solubility and d) illumination conditions coupled to residence time 
212 
for maximizing the production. They found that immobilization of CvFAP on EziG® Opal provided 
213 
the highest conversions. However, this strategy was incompatible with the continuous flow setup. 
214 
Consequently, moving to a system with Ammoeng 102 as a surfactant to increase the solubility 
215 
of palmitic acid in Tris buffer and optimizing other reaction parameters, the authors report a 
216 
space time yield of 5.7 g.l-1h-1 for the production of pentadecane, that is the highest productivity 
217 
of CvFAP report to date. 
 
218 
Hollmann and coworkers found FAP decarboxylation activity on short and medium carboxylic 
219 
acids (C1-C6) could be enhanced by adding simple alkanes, ranging in size from nonane to 
220 
pentadecane, as molecular decoys (Figure 4A).41 For instance, nearly six times as much methane 
221 
can be generated from acetic acid using CvFAP with pentadecane as a molecular decoy. The 
222 
general strategy was successful across C1 to C6 substrates. This strategy also broadened the 

223 
scope to include unsaturated fatty acids (e.g., acrylic acid, propiolic acid) and short alkyl chains 
224 
(e.g., propionic acid). Additionally, CvFAP catalyzed the decarboxylation of formic acid, releasing 
225 
H2 (Figure 4A). 
 
226 
The authors hypothesize that adding a decoy molecule induces the same conformational 
227 
changes that occur when longer fatty acids bind to the protein, helping to activate the enzyme 
228 
for photoexcitation. A kinetic study of 13CH3−CO2H decarboxylation supports this hypothesis to 
229 
form 13CH4. In this study, the authors found that the reaction rate was only dependent on acetic 
230 
acid concentration, indicating that the additive does not facilitate substrate binding but instead 
231 
activates the enzyme for new photoinduced electron transfer. While primary radicals are 
232 
terminated using the mechanism outlined above, Hollmann proposes a different means of 
233 
termination for unstable methyl radical. DFT calculations suggest that radical termination occurs 
234 
via alkylation of FAD●– to form a covalent dearomatized methyl quinone flavin adduct, which 
235 
upon protonation, forms methane and oxidized FAD quinone (Figure 4B).

In a related study, Hollmann and Wang found that CvFAP can be used to remove free 
251 
fatty acids from common oils.43 High degrees of deacidification is observed in crude vegetable 
252 
oils (83%–98%), soybean oil, rice bran oils, and palm oils which negatively impact their flavor 
253 
profiles. The authors found that CvFAP in whole cells could effectively remove the free fatty acids 
254 
in good yields. Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations suggest that at high concentrations, free 
255 
fatty acids alter the geometry within the protein's active site and change the activity of the 
256 
protein. These simulations also suggest that mixtures of oil and water, compared to pure water, 
257 
increased the solvent-accessible surface area of the enzyme. These data are in agreement with 
258 
a previous study by Aselmeyer et al, that show that FAP displays its highest activity on ionized 
259 
fatty acids present in organized lipid assemblies like microemulsions or large unilamellar vesicles 
260 
compared to bulk fatty acids. Aselmeyer et al. also proposed an interfacial recognition site on 
261 
FAP, the latter being located at the entrance of the hydrophobic channel of the enzyme.44

Flavin-dependent' ene’-reductases (EREDs) are a class of biocatalysts frequently used in 
503 
organic synthesis. This enzyme family, referred to as old yellow enzymes (OYEs), was initially 
504 
discovered in brewer's bottom yeast (Saccharomyces pastorianus) in 1932 and is thought to be 
505 
expressed in response to oxidative stress.66,67 Subsequent studies revealed OYE homologs in all 
506 
domains of life, with members found in natural product pathways and RNA modification.68,69 
507 
Traditionally, OYEs are used for the stereoselective reduction of activated alkenes and provide 
508 
chiral products with high levels of stereoselectivity. This reduction occurs via a hydride transfer 
509 
from FMN hydroquinone (FMNhq) to the electrophilic 𝛽-position to afford FMN quinone and an 
510 
enzyme-bound enolate, which is protonated by a conserved tyrosine. NAD(P)H can reduce FMN 
511 
back to the FMNhq in a ping-pong mechanism.70,71 These enzymes reduce alkenes with trans 
512 
selectivity, making them complementary to traditional metal-catalyzed hydrogenations (Figure 
513 
13).72 
 
514 
In the initial characterization of OYE1, Massey found that when photochemically reduced, 
515 
OYE1 can kinetically and thermodynamically stabilize the anionic flavin semiquinone (FMNsq).73 
516 
While unrealized at the time, the ability of the protein to stabilize open-shell flavin oxidation 
517 
states opens the door to EREDs catalyzing radical reactions. In one possible scenario, FMNhq 
518 
could serve as a single electron reductant (FMNhq/FMNsq = -230mV vs. SCE) of an organic 
519 
substrate.74 The resulting substrate-centered radicals could be terminated via hydrogen atom 
520 
transfer from the FMNsq (BDEcalc = 59 kcal/mol).75

890

891

892

893

894

895

896

897

899

900

901

902

903

904

905

3.0 Synergistic Photoenzymatic Catalysis 
961 
962 
Synergistic photoenzymatic catalysis describes enzymatic transformations where the 
963 
enzymatic mechanism for converting starting material to product involves electron or energy 
964 
transfer  from  an  exogenous  photocatalyst.  This  family  of  reactivity  is  distinct  from 
965 
photobiocatalytic systems for cofactor turnover because the electron transfer event involving the 
966 
photocatalyst is essential for the biocatalytic mechanism for converting starting material to 
967 
product. 
 
968 
3.1 Synergistic Reactions Involving Native Reactivity 
969 
3.1.1 
Cytochromes P450 
970 
 
971 
Cytochrome P450s are powerful catalysts for the asymmetric and regioselective 
972 
hydroxylation of unactivated C–H bonds using molecular oxygen and a reductant. These proteins 
973 
require a heme domain for oxygen activation and hydroxylation and a reductase domain to 
974 
deliver electrons from NADPH to the heme domain for catalysis (via flavin mediators). The most 
975 
widely used cytochrome P450 for biocatalytic transformations is P450BM3, a catalyst that has the 
976 
reductase domain covalently linked to the heme domain.104 Fundamental studies by Gray and 
977 
others demonstrated how electrons travel through proteins. In these studies, small molecule 
978 
photocatalysts were often used to initiate electron transfer events. 105,106 Building on these 
979 
studies, Cheruzel and coworkers demonstrated the heme domain of P450BM3 bioconjugated with 
980 
polypyridyl ruthenium photocatalysts can facilitate the C–H hydroxylation without requiring the 
981 
traditional NADPH reductant. 107 Ru(bpy)2(phenA)Cl2 was linked to the heme domain via cysteine 
982 
bioconjugation to P450BM3-L407C; a variant containing cysteine that can react with the mutant 
983 
via the iodoacetamide functional handle on the phenIA ligand. Perhaps unsurprisingly, the 
984 
location of photocatalyst bioconjugation plays an important role in the efficiency of the strategy, 
985 
with other positions providing lower yields and total turnover numbers. 108,109 To improve the 
986 
driving force of electron transfer, the 2,2’-bipyridine (bpy) ligands were substituted to 4,4’- 

987 
dimethoxy-2,2’-bipyridine ligand resulting in an 80 mV decrease in reduction potential. The 
988 
authors  proposed  the  following  mechanism  for  this  non-natural  system  (Figure  33). 
989 
Photoexcitation of the Ruthenium photocatalyst provides an excited state that is reductively 
990 
quenched by Sodium diethyldithiocarbamate (DTC) to provide a long-lived Ru(I) state, which 
991 
supplies the electrons required for hydroxylation. 
992 
While total turnover numbers (TTNs) could not be compared to the native holoenzyme, initial 
993 
rates of product formation are roughly an order of magnitude lower. However, when compared 
994 
to other reduction strategies, such as electrochemical reduction or photochemical reactions with 
995 
deazaflavin, this synergistic approach performs favorably (Figure 33A). The P450BM3 enzymes 
996 
were also demonstrated to regioselectively hydroxylate the allylic position of 10-undecenoic acid 
997 
in 40% yield and 92.5:7.5 e.r. (Figure 33b).110 In subsequent studies, Cheruzel applied this system 
998 
to the benzylic hydroxylation of trifluoromethylated substrates.111 In this reaction, the authors 
999 
used a photoredox reaction developed by Stephenson et al. to trifluoromethylate aromatic rings 
1000 
with alkyl substituents to form trifluoromethylated products with various regioselectivities.112 
1001 
Next, they used the P450BM3  photocatalyst hybrid to hydroxylate the benzylic position. 
1002 
Interestingly, in cases where the trifluoromethylation protocol produced a mixture of para, meta, 
1003 
and ortho-isomers, the subsequent hydroxylation would occur preferentially on one product, 
1004 
indicating that steric and electronic properties of the substrate strongly influence its reactivity in 
1005 
the hydroxylation reaction. Moreover, the authors found that mutations within the protein active 
1006 
site could influence the selectivity of the hydroxylation step.

4.0 Photobiocatalytic Cascades 
1172 
Photobiocatalytic  cascades  are  biocatalytic  transformations  where  an  enzyme  and 
1173 
photocatalyst are present in a reaction, but the photocatalyst is not involved in the electron 
1174 
transfer events required to convert the starting material to product. In this scenario, the 
1175 
photocatalyst is operating entirely independently of the enzyme. The most common variant of 
1176 
this cascade strategy is cofactor reduction by the photocatalyst or in situ generation of peroxide. 
1177 
More recently, research has been focused on organic transformations that can be run in the 
1178 
presence of enzymes for dynamic kinetic resolutions. This section is organized based on the role 
1179 
of the photocatalyst in the reaction.

Photoredox catalysts generate radical intermediates under mild conditions and are 
1528

1529

1530

1531

1532

1533

1534

1535

1536

1537

1538

1539

1540

1541

1542

1543

The term “photobiocatalysis” has been recently coined to broadly describe the combination 
of light and enzymes for use in synthetic organic chemistry. The field has blossomed 
exponentially to the point where key mechanistic differences have begun to surface, the most 
significant of which is the role of light in the transformation. The goal of this review, therefore, is 
to characterize and thoroughly describe and delineate the roles of light in this new field. We 
have defined three key terms: photoenzymatic catalysis, synergistic photoenzymatic catalysis,

and photobiocatalysis, that encompass the various strategies that have been developed over 
1872 
recent years. 
1873 
We have shown that photoenzymatic catalysis, the direct excitation of the enzyme cofactor, 
1874 
although initially harnessed by nature in one form as fatty acid photodecarboxylase, which has 
1875 
been successfully exploited for its synthetic capabilities (Section 2.1), has primarily been 
1876 
accessed to facilitate new, non-native enzymatic transformations. The excitation of nicotinamide- 
1877 
(Section 2.2) and flavin-based (Section 2.3) cofactors for the creation and manipulation of open 
1878 
shell reactive intermediates has been found to be a general strategy for C – H and C – C bond 
1879 
formations, as well as radical polymerizations. The extension of photoenzymatic chemistry to 
1880 
include the incorporation and excitation of non-canonical cofactors has also been explored 
1881 
(Section 2.4). 
1882 
We have also described the term synergistic photoenzymatic chemistry as the use of light to 
1883 
excite an exogenous photocatalyst that can then perform energy or electron transfer to the 
1884 
enzyme cofactor to carry out the desired transformation. We have shown that this regime can be 
1885 
used to facilitate both native (Section 3.1) and non-native reactions (Section 3.2). This reactivity 
1886 
is distinct from photobiocatalysis, for which light-mediated chemistry is not directly part of the 
1887 
conversion of starting material to product but rather, is used to generate another essential 
1888 
component of the reaction, independent of the action of the enzyme. In the realm of 
1889 
photobiocatalysis, light is used primarily for the generation of active enzyme cofactors (Section 
1890 
4.1) but has recently become particularly popular for the generation of enzyme-active 
1891 
intermediates in dynamic kinetic resolutions and chemoenzymatic cascades (Section 4.2). Finally, 
1892 
we show the use of whole-cell cyanobacteria to facilitate light-driven cofactor regeneration 
1893 
through the manipulation of the cell’s native redox processes (Section 5). 
1894 
We hope that the review clarifies and distinguishes between the multiple modes of 
1895 
combining light and enzymes for use in organic synthesis. As new examples emerged, the 
1896 
nuances of the described works necessitated that these new terms be developed to fully 
1897 
understand and appreciate the mechanistic details of each transformation. We hope that this
