Abstract 
Designing Pb-free relaxors with both high capacitive energy density (Wrec) and high 
storage efficiency (η) remains a remarkable challenge for cutting-edge pulsed power 
technologies. Local compositional heterogeneity is crucial for achieving complex polar 
structure in solid solution relaxors, but its role in optimizing energy storage properties 
is often overlooked. Here, we report that an exceptionally high Wrec of 15.2 J cm−3 along 
with ultrahigh η of 91% can be achieved through designing local chemical clustering in 
Bi0.5Na0.5TiO3-BaTiO3-based relaxors. Three-dimensional atomistic model derived 
from neutron/X-ray total scattering combined with reverse Monte Carlo method reveals 
the presence of sub-nanometer scale clustering of Bi, Na and Ba, which host 
differentiated polar displacements, and confirming the prediction by density functional 
theory calculations. This leads to a polar state with small polar clusters and strong 
length and direction fluctuations in unit-cell polar vectors, thus manifesting improved 

high-field polarizability, steadily reduced hysteresis, and high breakdown strength 
macroscopically. The favorable polar structure features also result in a unique field-
increased η, excellent stability and superior discharge capacity. Our work demonstrates 
that the hidden local chemical order exerts a significant impact on the polarization 
characteristic of relaxors, and can be exploited for accessing superior energy storage 
performance. 
 
 
 

Introduction 
The pressing demand for sustainable and efficient energy storage systems is propelled 
by their diverse applications, ranging from electric vehicles to microelectronics and 
beyond 1- 6. Dielectric capacitors are optimal energy storage systems characterized by 
their ultrahigh power density, ultrafast charging/discharging rates, high-voltage 
endurance, and exceptional reliability, and in particular, being core components in 
high/pulse power technologies 7 - 10 . However, the usage of dielectric capacitors is 
presently limited by their low energy storage density, especially in situations requiring 
large capacitances and compact packaging sizes. Consequently, there has been a surge 
of attention towards developing dielectric capacitors with high energy density and high 
storage efficiency (“dual high”)11- 18. 
The capacitive energy storage properties of a dielectric is controlled by its electric field-
induced polarization behavior and breakdown strength (EB). Typically, a large and 
delayed saturation polarization (Pm), small hysteresis (H), together with a high EB are 
desired 4-6. Chemical composition design is the most common approach to optimize the 
polarization behavior of dielectrics, and a great deal of Pb-free compounds have been 
explored. The corresponding capacity density of bulk ceramics has been rapidly 
improved to 4~8 J cm-3. Very recently, giant Wrec > 15 J cm-3 has been advanced in a 
few NaNbO3 (NN)-based19,20, and Bi0.5Na0.5TiO3 (BNT)-based ceramics21, while their 
corresponding η is relatively low (~80%), owing to the open hysteresis loops at high 
field. High η (> 90%) can be obtained in some BaTiO3 (BT) -based, SrTiO3 (ST) -based 
ceramics, but their Wrec is typically below 8 J cm-3, limited by the relatively low intrinsic 
polarization 22 - 25 . Furthermore, various mesoscopic-structural modulation strategies 
have been proposed to enhance EB2,3,21,25-28. However, owing to the inherent tradeoff 
between Pm, H, and EB in dielectrics, achieving “dual high” performance, for example, 
going beyond the current boundary of Wrec > 15 J cm-3 and η > 90% in bulk ceramics, 
remains a challenge. 
Benefitting from the nanoscale heterogeneous polar structure, relaxor ferroelectrics 
(RFEs) are considered to be an excellent option for “dual high” dielectric capacitors, as 

exemplified by the achievement of Wrec ~ 10 J cm-3 in bulk RFE ceramics7,12,13,29, and 
Wrec ~150 J cm-3 in RFE thin films under an ultrahigh η (> 90%)1. The presence of polar 
nanoregions (PNRs) is a prominent feature of RFEs. The sizes, shapes, dipole patterns 
(including amplitude and symmetry), and volume fraction of PNRs strongly affect their 
macroscopic properties. For instance, the energy storage properties can be substantially 
optimized by, constructing multiple symmetries PNRs30, reducing the size and volume 
fraction of PNRs as superparaelectric state1,14, and enhancing the amplitude of dipole 
to form strong local polar fluctuations7,12. At the atomic level, compositional 
heterogeneity is a prerequisite for engineering polar state. Controlling local chemical 
heterogeneity, such as increasing local cation disorder8,13, modifying chemical short-
range order31 , or introducing clustering32 of RFEs can yield exceptional properties. 
However, the RFEs designed for capacitive energy storage are frequently simplified as 
disordered solid solutions, where different cations are randomly distributed over the 
lattice sites, the important role of local chemical heterogeneity is overlooked. 
Here, we propose a strategy to access “dual high” energy storage performance by 
designing nanoscale atomic clusters in a RFE solid solution (Figure 1a). It is 
hypothesized that the different types of atomic clusters will host diverse PNRs due to 
the differentiated length and direction of unit-cell polar vectors. Benefitting from the 
atomic clusters-derived strong fluctuations in the polar direction and length of PNRs, 
two regions with different E-induced polarization behavior can be generated. Region I 
indicates the presaturation regime where the PNRs can easily rotate with E, 
corresponding to a large ΔP/ΔE. It results in an early polarization presaturation 
accompanied by a large H, which is the typical behavior in RFEs. Region II indicates 
the postsaturation regime where the oriented PNRs can reversibly extend with 
increasing E, especially these PNRs with small polar length. Owing to the unique polar 
extension behavior, this region corresponds to a delay polarization saturation (relatively 
small ΔP/ΔE, but enhanced polarizability) and low H. Such E-induced polarization 
behavior is similar to that found in ion-bombarded relaxor ferroelectric films with the 
point defects 3. Thus, both the Wrec and η can be enhanced as the applied E approaching 

EB. 
To demonstrate the virtue of local chemical clustering, a ternary solid solution system 
of (1-x)(0.75Bi0.5Na0.5TiO3-0.25BaTiO3)-xNaNbO3 (BNT-25BT-xNN) (Figure 1b) is 
designed. The tetragonal BNT-25BT is selected as matrix due to its large tetragonal (T) 
structural distortion among lead-free compounds. Based on the crystal structure 
refinement of BNT-25BT (Figure S1 and Table S1), the estimated average polar 
displacement (δ) of A-site and B-site is 0.3 Å and 0.14 Å, respectively, providing a 
relatively large average polarization (calculated PS ≈ 30 μC cm-2). The considerable 
difference in ionic radius and theoretical ferroelectric activity of A-site (Bi3+: R = 1.34 
Å, δ = 0.8 Å; Na+: R = 1.39 Å, δ = 0.05 Å; Ba2+: R = 1.61 Å, δ = 0.05 Å)33, is conducive 
to form short range chemical order. Further, the NaNbO3 is incorporated into BNT-
25BT to form a RFE state with nanoscale atomic clusters and enhanced EB. According 
to the density functional theory (DFT) calculations (Figure 1c), the atomic 
configurations with nearest Bi-Bi and Na-Na pairs (Figures 1c1 and c2), present much 
lower energy compared with the cross-distribution (Figure 1c3), indicating the 
likelihood of A-site atomic clusters in BNT-25BT-NN. The corresponding electron 
localization function of the atomic configuration with the lowest energy (Figure 1c1) 
was calculated. Strong hybridization between the Bi 6s2 and the O 2p orbitals appears 
in the Bi clusters, while ionic interaction occurs between Ba/Na and O. The strongly 
covalent interaction between the 3d/4d state of Ti/Nb and O 2p can be also observed. 
However, the directions of hybridization between Bi-O and B-O are different, indicating 
the direction disturbance of Bi and B-site polar displacements. In this case, the 
ferroelectric order would be disrupted. Therefore, a RFE state with nanoscale atomic 
clusters-derived strong local polar fluctuations can be obtained (Figure 1). 
Encouragingly, an ultrahigh Wrec of 15.2 J cm−3 along with ultrahigh η of 91% under a 
high EB of 73 kV cm−1 is achieved in the designed Pb-free relaxors of BNT-25BT-xNN 
(x = 0.12), which exceeds the properties boundary of lead-free bulk ceramics. The 
quantitative analysis results from 3D atomistic model constructed by neutron/X-ray 
total scattering combined with reverse Monte Carlo method, and the atomic 

displacements mapping from high-resolution TEM atomic imaging verify the atomic 
clusters-derived unique polar structure. This work demonstrates that modifying local 
chemical order can be an effective strategy to design “dual high” dielectric capacitors.

Results and Discussion 
The BNT-25NT-xNN (0 ≤ x ≤ 0.15) solid solution ceramics were prepared by the 
conventional solid state method to determine the optimal composition. The studied 
composition region yields a pure perovskite phase, and a transformation from T to 
pseudocubic occurs as increasing the NN content (Figure S2). It suggests a FE state to 
RFE state transition, as verified by the recorded P-E loops (Figure S3) and dielectric 
spectrum (Figure S4). Notably, the incorporation of NN component dramatically lowers 

the H, but reduces the Pm slightly. This phenomenon is significantly different from the 
common construction of RFE states, where there is a simultaneous rapid reduction in 
Pm and H, suggesting the unique local structures in BNT-25NT-xNN. Although both x 
= 0.12 and x = 0.15 present relatively low H (< 8.5%), the former with relatively large 
Pm is selected for subsequent research, considering the design concept that reaching 
presaturation at low E and delaying postsaturation at high E. Optimally, x = 0.12 
exhibits a large Pm = 26 μC cm-2 and a negligible Pr = 1.5 μC cm-2 under 8 kV mm-1, 
showing a strong indicator for good capacitive energy storage. This noteworthy RFE 
state can be also identified through the highly frequency-dependent and broad dielectric 
peak (ΔTm = 72 K, γ = 1.76).

The symmetric single peaks without detectable splitting in high-resolution synchrotron 
XRD pattern indicates the long-range cubic structure in x = 0.12 (Figure 2a), which is 
also confirmed by the SAED patterns (Figures 2e-f). No long-range ferroelectric 
domains can be probed. While the high-resolution bright field TEM image and the 
corresponding inverse Fourier transform patterns from local region shows obvious local 
lattice distortion and the presence of PNRs (Figures 2b-d)34. Lattice fluctuations and 
PNRs occurs at about 1-3 nm scale, which should be derived from the local chemical 
heterogeneity. The atomic displacement is probed by atomic-resolution STEM imaging. 
High-angle annular dark-field (HAADF) images were acquired both along [001]c and 
[110]c. The positions and relative intensity of A/B-site atom can be determined by fitting 
two-dimensional Gaussian function35. There appear to be local chemical composition 
fluctuations with Na-rich and Bi-rich regions distributed at several nanometers, 
demonstrated by the relative intensity mapping of A-site atomic columns (Figure S5). 
The atom displacement vectors were calculated as the off-center shift between the B(A)-
and its surrounding four A(B)-site atoms (Figure S6). Figures 2h-m illustrate the atomic 
displacement vector mapping, where the length of the arrows represents the magnitude 
of displacement vector. Correlated direction of displacement vector is marked by the 
color of arrows. 1-3 nm clusters with various orientations including T, R, and 
transitional directions are found. The clusters are not isolated from each other, but 
interact directly with each other. This observation agrees well with the DFT calculations 
as mentioned earlier (Fig. 1c).

refinement based on neutron and X-ray -based total scattering data of BNT-25BT-xNN 
(x = 0.12). (a-f) Fitting results of neutron/X-ray-based G(r), S(Q), and Bragg data. Black, 
red, and blue line represents the observed, calculated, and difference, respectively. (g) 
Initial 3D atomic configuration. (h) A representative refined 3D atomic configuration. 
Direct-measuring nanoscale atomic clusters in solid solutions remains a challenge. The 
local chemical heterogeneity usually manifests as diffuse background along with Bragg 
pattern. Interestingly, neutron/X-ray total scattering technique capturing both signals 
simultaneously, allowing the probe of subnanometre and nanoscale structures 36 ,37 . 
Therefore, neutron/X-ray total scattering were employed to study the nanoscale 
structure of x = 0.12. 3D atomistic model is reconstructed by Reverse Monte Carlo 

(RMC) algorithms 38 - 40 , which fits three forms of scattering data simultaneously, 
including neutron/X-ray real-space pair distribution function (PDF) data, reciprocal-
space S(Q) data, as well as the Bragg data (Figure 3). These six data can be accurately 
fitted, indicating that the refined 3D atomistic model captures the structural features in 
both reciprocal and real space. In particular, the excellent fitting of the PDF in the low-
r region (< 10 Å) indicates that local structural features have been restored. As a 
comparison, the conventional small-box refinements with randomly distributed A-site 
atoms model were also conducted. However, the short-range neutron PDF patterns 
show poor matching, even though the long-range patterns can be well fitted (Figure S7). 
Importantly, high-contrast neutron scattering lengths of the elements presented in BNT-
25NT-xNN (x = 0.12) (Table S2), guarantees the reliability of the extracted element-
specific structural information (Figures S8 and S9). Additionally, the substantial 
variation in renormalized coefficients of different A-A partials (Table S3), allows for 
the analysis of A-site coordination environments. 
 
Figure 4. Local chemical clustering in BNT-25BT-xNN (x = 0.12). (a) Chemical order 
parameter α of various A-site atomic pairs. (b), and (c) the representative slices showing 
the A-site atoms from the refined 3D atomistic model. 
Based on the refined 3D atomistic model, the local coordination environment of A-site 
atoms is analyzed. To describe and statistically quantify the local coordination 

environment in BNT-25BT-xNN (x = 0.12) solid solution, the chemical order parameter 
α, extended from the commonly used Warren-Cowley parameters was calculated41. The 
chemical order parameter α expresses the degree to which the probability (Pij) of finding 
a type of atom j around a certain atom i deviates from its concentration (cj), and can be 
defined as: αij = Pij/cj-1. Specifically, αii = 0 indicates the random distribution, αii > 0 
indicates clustering of alike atoms, while αii < 0 indicates correlation between different 
types of atoms. As shown in Figure 4a, a large positive chemical order parameter α 
value of Bi-Bi, Na-Na, and Ba-Ba, while a negative value for Bi-Na, Bi-Ba, and Na-Ba 
is observed, indicating the presence of clustering of Bi, Na, and Ba. The value of 
chemical order parameter α approach 0 from the 1st coordination shell to 3rd 
coordination shell, indicating that these A-site atomic clusters are at sub-nanometer 
scale. These A-site atomic clusters can be also obviously seen in the representative 
slices from the refined 3D atomistic model (Figures 4b and c). It is worth noting that 
these findings are in agreement with the results from DFT calculations and the local 
compositional heterogeneity observed through STEM. 

 
Figure 5. Local structure and polar structure of BNT-25BT-xNN (x = 0.12) analyzed 
from the refined 3D atomistic model. (a-c) Stereoscopic projection of polar 
displacement vector long [001]c. (d) Mean magnitude of polar displacements. (e) Mean 
unit-cell polar length with respect to the A-site environment. (f) 3D distribution of A-
site atoms. (g) Selected 2D polar vector distribution. (h) Magnitude and (i) direction 
distribution of 3D unit-cell polar vectors corresponding to (g). The angle between the 
unit-cell polar vectors and [001]c is defined as θ. 
The unique arrangement of atom combined with their differentiated ferroelectric 
activity will lead to complex polar structures. Therefore, the atomic-specific polar 
behavior is studied. The atomic polar displacement vector (𝛿𝛿⃗) is calculated as the shift 
between A/B-site ion and the center of their corresponding oxygen cage. The 
stereoscopic projection of 𝛿𝛿⃗ viewed along [001]c is shown in Figures 5a-c. A strong 

preferred directional distribution along T, accompanied by a weaker preferred 
distribution along R is observed for Bi. Such branching polar displacement has been 
also observed in other Bi-based compounds 42 ,43 . A broader distribution with some 
preferred along T is observed for Na, while the random broader distribution is for B-
site atom. Such differentiated orientations of 𝛿𝛿⃗ break the long-range polar order, and 
will result in significant fluctuations in the direction of polar vectors. The Bi presents 
largest displacement δBi ≈ 0.35 Å compared with others, ascribing to its 
stereochemically active “lone” pair of electrons (Figure 1c). The difference in the 
direction and magnitude of atomic-specific polar displacement vectors assemble into 
unit-cell polar vectors with strong direction and length fluctuations. Therefore, the 
length of unit-cell polar vectors depends strongly on the A-site environment (Figure 5e). 
The mean unit-cell polar length increases with the number of Bi in [A8BO6] clusters, 
while decreasing as increasing the number of Ba. Consequently, the Bi clusters possess 
larger polar length than that of Na and Ba clusters. These phenomena can be intuitively 
seen from the 3D atomistic model and the corresponding distribution of unit-cell polar 
vectors (Figures 5g-i). Note that some Bi-rich regions correspond to a polar length of 
80 μC cm-2, providing a strong supporting for polar extension of these regions with 
small polar length (such as Ba/Na clusters) under high electric field. In agreement with 
the displacement mapping from STEM, approximately 1-3 nm polar clusters occur with 
multiple symmetric orientations. As expected, a relaxor state with nanoscale atomic 
clusters-derived strong local polar fluctuations is achieved in BNT-25BT-xNN (x = 
0.12).

measured at the thickness about 50~70 µm. (a) Electric-field constrained unipolar P-E 
loops. The inset image exhibits the prepared ceramic samples. (b) Energy density and 
efficiency are derived from their P-E loops. (c) Comparison of recoverable energy 
storage density in ultrahigh efficiency dielectric bulk ceramics. (d) Comparison of 
energy storage density and efficiency with state-of-the-art Pb-free dielectric bulk 
ceramics. 
BNT-25BT-xNN (x = 0.12) demonstrates superior energy storage capability (Figure 6). 
The P-E loops recorded at various electric fields present consistent characteristics as 
designed (Figure 6a). The polarization rapidly improves to 40 μC cm-2 at a low E of 20 
kV mm-1 mainly due to the E-induced polarization rotation. While at this presaturation 
regime, the harvested Wrec is considerably low ~ 2.5 J cm-3, and the corresponding η is 
only 85%. Subsequently, the polarization increases from 40 μC cm-2 to 75 μC cm-2 as 
E increasing from 20 kV mm-1 to the EB of 73 kV mm-1. Notably, even the Pm reach to 
a large value of 75 μC cm-2, there is still no sign for polarization saturation, which is 

attributed to the enhanced high-field polarizability. This suggests a significant potential 
for film capacitors, as their much higher electric fields endurance. This postsaturation 
regime dominates the energy storage capability and improves η. This E-increased η 
behavior is completely different with previously common observed deceased one as 
increasing E 2,13,15,19. Notably, the relatively high EB can be credited to the high quality 
and dense ceramics along with a small grain size (Figure S10)44, and homogeneous 
mesoscopic compositional distribution (Figure S11), as well as the high electrical 
resistivity (Figures S12a and b). Besides, the Weibull distributions of the EB provide 
the evidence that this high value is not coincidental but can be reproduced reliably 
(Figure S12c)45. Encouragingly, “dual high” energy storage performance in terms of 
Wrec = 15.2 J cm-3 and η = 91% is achieved under EB = 73 kV mm-1, which well 
outperforms the reported state-of-the-art Pb-free dielectric bulk ceramics (Figures 5c-
d, and S13). Note that it goes far beyond the present performance boundary of high Wrec 
(> 10 J cm-3) at high η (> 90%) in Pb-free bulk ceramics. Accordingly, there is very a 
few systems reported with Wrec > 15 J cm-3, while their corresponding η are only about 
80%19-21. As compared with these high efficient (η > 90%) dielectric bulk ceramics, 
BNT-25BT-xNN (x = 0.12) exhibits much higher Wrec. In addition, this performance is 
superior to the reported multilayer ceramic capacitors, which are typically fabricated 
with a much smaller in thickness (< 20 µm)15,16,27,28. 
Further, x = 0.12 exhibits excellent stability in frequency and temperature, as well as 
remarkable resistance to fatigue (Figure S14). Over a broad frequency range of 10 – 
200 Hz, the ceramic displays a lack of frequency dependence, with a nearly constant 
Wrec value of 7.4 ± 0.1 J cm-3 and a variation in η of less than 1.6%. Similarly, in the 
operating temperature window of 30 – 120 °C, Wrec remains stable at 7.8 ± 0.4 J cm-3, 
and η remains consistent at 90.2 ± 2.6%. In addition, the ceramic samples exhibit an 
impressive ability to withstand fatigue. Even after being subjected to up to 107 cycles, 
there were only minor changes (less than 1%) in their energy storage properties. The 
ceramic samples also performs well in discharge tests (Figure S15). The discharge 
density (WD) increases as the electric field increases under overdamping (R = 180 Ω). 

At E = 35 kV/mm, the sample reaches a WD as high as 4.9 J cm-3 under a rapid time 
response (t0.9 = 38 ns). The BNT-25-NN ceramics present excellent stability, reliability, 
accompanied with remarkable discharge performance, making them a promising 
candidate for use as a capacitive energy storage device. 
Conclusion 
We present a feasible local chemical clustering design strategy that enables the 
engineering of the polar structure of Pb-free RFEs, and realizing “dual high” capacitive 
energy storage. Through DFT calculations, atomic-resolution STEM, and 3D atomistic 
model constructed from neutron/X-ray total scattering, it demonstrates that sub-
nanometer scale clustering of Bi, Na and Ba can be formed in BNT-25BT-xNN solid 
solution. This clustering combined with the differentiated atomic polar displacement 
behavior results in a polar structure with strong fluctuations both in length and direction 
of unit-cell polar vectors. Such atomic clusters-hosted polar structure endows the RFE 
state with unique E-induced polarization behavior macroscopically, showing enhanced 
high-field polarizability, E-reduced hysteresis and delayed high-field polarization 
saturation. The favorable features, concurrent with the high EB of 73 kV mm-1, lead to 
an ultrahigh Wrec of 15.2 J cm−3 and ultrahigh η of 91% in x = 0.12, which well 
outperforms the reported Pb-free dielectric bulk ceramics. It also possesses excellent 
frequency/temperature stability, fatigue resistance, and good discharge capability. The 
BNT-25BT-NN ceramics show high potential as high efficiency energy storage 
dielectrics capacitors. Our work offers a pathway for exploring high performance 
dielectric energy storage materials by controlling previously overlooked local 
compositional heterogeneity.
