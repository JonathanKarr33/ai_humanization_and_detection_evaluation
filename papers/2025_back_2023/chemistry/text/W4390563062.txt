Abstract 
Cu-based catalysts hold promise for electrifying CO2 to produce methane, an extensively used fuel. 
However, the selectivity remains insufficient due to the lack of effective catalyst design strategies in 
governing complex reaction pathways. Herein, we introduce a design principle for carbon-supported 
Cu catalysts that combines engineering both the atomic-scale electronic properties of Cu sites and 
mesoscale local reaction microenvironment of carbon support to steer the selective CO2 reduction 
pathway to methane. Block copolymer-derived carbon fiber support with interconnected mesopores 
is developed to offer H2O-deficient and long-range confined mesochannels with prolonged transport 
path for CO intermediate, thereby suppressing hydrogen evolution and boosting deep CO2 reduction. 
Importantly, in-situ X-ray absorption and Raman spectroscopy reveal the dynamic reconstruction of 
unique N,OH-immobilized Cu3 clusters from atomically dispersed Cu under realistic operating 
conditions. The site possesses moderate *CO adsorption affinity and a low barrier for *CO 
hydrogenation yet a high barrier for C−C coupling, enabling intrinsically selective CO2-to-methane 
reduction. The in-situ formed N,OH-Cu3 clusters rooted in bicontinuous mesochannels achieve 
efficient methane generation with an unprecedented Faradaic efficiency of 74.2% at an industry-level 
current density of 300 mA cm−2. This work sheds light on effective design principles for steering 
reaction pathways in complex interfacial catalytic systems.

Introduction 
Electrifying CO2 transformation using renewable energy offers a carbon-neutral solution to CO2 
recycling and fuel generation.1-2 Among various feedstocks from CO2 reduction reaction (CO2RR), 
two electron-enabled CO and HCOOH have approached the performance thresholds for industry 
implementation.3 Compared to lightly reduced products, deeply reduced products, such as 
hydrocarbons and oxygenates, are economically more attractive and hold significant market sizes.1 
Particularly, methane (CH4), an extensively used fuel, is compatible with the existing storage and 
transportation infrastructure. Nevertheless, the current benchmarking performance of CO2-to-CH4 
remains far below the applicable threshold owing to grand challenges associated with the complicated 
eight proton-coupled electron transfer (PCET) steps, adsorption/transformation of multiple 
carbonaceous intermediates, and competition with other reaction pathways beyond CH4.4-6 The 
success in achieving viable CO2 electromethanation relies on the development of high-performance 
electrocatalysts. 
Earth-abundant copper (Cu) is considered the most promising catalyst for reducing CO2 to 
hydrocarbons; however, the CH4 formation competes with C2 products.7-8 Modifying electronic 
properties of Cu by alloying and interfacial interaction can stabilize *CHO intermediate and 
selectively produce CH4 with suppressed C−C coupling on large-size Cu nanoparticles,9-10 while the 
performance needs further improvement to meet the application need. Notably, single-atom Cu 
catalysts have shown great promises to steer reaction pathways in complex catalytic processes thanks 
to their great designability of electronic properties.5, 11-14 To stabilize atomically dispersed metal sites, 
nitrogen-doped carbons are desirable supports due to their remarkably tunable morphology, pore 
architecture, and electronic structure of active M−Nx moieties.15-22 Several pioneering works have 
suggested that carbon-supported isolated Cu atoms are not electrochemically stable under negative 
potentials of realistic CO2RR conditions and would likely be reduced to metallic Cu species, acting 
as the catalytic site for CO2 reduction to CO and C2H5OH.23-25 However, to the best of our knowledge, 
such dynamic reconstruction has not been demonstrated in the CO2-to-CH4 transformation yet, and 

4 
the atomic-level structure of potential-driven Cu sites and their catalytic mechanisms toward CH4 
formation remain ambiguous. 
Besides the atomic structure of carbon-supported Cu sites, the local reaction microenvironment 
also plays a critical role in CO2 reduction, which is highly associated with the pore architecture of 
carbon supports.17, 26-28 To drive CO2-to-CH4 conversion, optimization of CO2/H2O concentration and 
elongation of the transport path of CO intermediate are vital for suppressing the side hydrogen 
evolution reaction (HER) at large overpotentials where CH4 formation occurs and promoting deep 
CO reduction, respectively.27, 29 Challengingly, such expectations cannot be readily realized on 
conventional carbon supports with deficient and short-range pores due to the significant fraction of 
H2O warping within the catalyst surface and the rapid loss of CO intermediates from the surface. We 
hypothesized that constructing bicontinuous mesoporous carbon to host Cu sites could create H2O-
deficient yet CO2-rich surfaces to restrain the HER, because H2O penetration in the pore region of 
catalysts loaded on the hydrophobic gas diffusion layer (GDL) can be suppressed. Moreover, the 
interconnected mesochannels would offer an enhanced long-range confinement space for increasing 
the residence time of the CO intermediate towards enabling effective interactive contact with Cu sites, 
30-31 thereby creating a deep reduction-favorable local microenvironment. 
Taken together, we here sought to develop bicontinuous mesoporous carbon for supporting 
atomically dispersed Cu, aiming to engineer both active site structure and local reaction 
microenvironment toward efficient CO2-to-CH4 conversion. To this end, we implemented the 
molecule-level microphase separation of a polyacrylonitrile-block-poly(methyl methacrylate) (PAN-
b-PMMA) block copolymer to create interconnected mesoporous carbon fiber (IPCF) supports with 
abundant, uniform, and bicontinuous mesochannels for stabilizing isolated Cu−N3 moieties (Cu-
N/IPCF). More importantly, in-situ X-ray absorption and Raman spectroscopy studies revealed the 
dynamic reconstruction of atomically dispersed Cu to nitrogen and hydroxyl-immobilized Cu3 
(N,OH-Cu3) clusters during the operating CO2 reduction conditions, being the actual active sites to 
promote electrochemical CO2-to-CH4 reduction, as elucidated by extensive experimental analyses 

5 
and theoretical calculations. Accordingly, the synergistic coupling of in-situ formed N,OH-Cu3 sites 
and bicontinuous confined mesoreactors effectively suppresses hydrogen evolution and steers 
selective CO2 electromethanation, endowing the newly designed catalyst with a CH4 Faradaic 
efficiency of 74.2% at a commercially viable current density of 300 mA cm−2. 
 
Results and Discussion 
Design and synthesis of catalysts 
In CO2 electrolysis at the gas-solid-liquid triple phase boundary of the gas diffusion electrode (GDE) 
(Figure 1a), H2O is required as a proton source for hydrocarbons. For carbon-supported Cu catalysts, 
H2O is rich on the catalyst surface in the case of conventional pore-deficient carbon (Figure 1b) and 
short-range macro/mesoporous carbon supports (Figure 1c), while the CO2 concentration is limited 
due to its low solubility and reaction with electrolyte, particularly in alkaline conditions.32-33 Such 
CO2-depleted and H2O-rich interface results in overwhelming HER at large overpotentials that are 
needed to drive deep CO2 reduction to CH4.29 To restrain the HER, we intended to design an IPCF 
support with suppressed H2O penetration in the long-range mesopore region on the hydrophobic GDE, 
thus creating a large portion of H2O-deficient and CO2-rich active interface (Figure 1d). It is noting 
that a small amount of H2O can still enter mesochannels along with CO2 stream, providing proton 
source yet with suppressed HER competition. The long-range mesochannels could also promote CH4 
formation by confining CO intermediates in mesochannel reactors to boost its successive reduction 
over the Cu sites;30-31 such benefits are not inherently owned by conventional carbon supports with 
sparse and short-range macro/mesopores.

To experimentally synthesize the as-proposed Cu-N/IPCF, a block copolymer-assisted approach 
was developed via the electrospinning, oxidation, and pyrolysis of the PAN-b-PMMA polymer 
containing cupric acetate (Figure 1e, Figure S1, Figure S2). Due to the microphase separation of the 
PAN-b-PMMA, this approach allows accurate control over the evolution of continuous PMMA 
domain networks rooted in the PAN matrix at the molecular level,34 thus navigating the generation 
of the IPCF support upon the removal of the PMMA template and the formation of atomically 

7 
dispersed Cu−N3 coordination by high-temperature thermal activation. The high homogeneity of 
single Cu sites enables us to probe their dynamic structure during CO2RR. We have also synthesized 
another two types of catalysts as controls to justify the vital significance of bicontinuous mesopores. 
One is Cu−N3 supported on mesopore-deficient carbon fiber (Cu-N/CF) using PAN alone (Figure 
S3). The other is Cu−N3 supported on conventional porous carbon fiber with discontinuous 
macro/mesopores (Cu-N/PCF) using the physical PAN+PMMA blend. 
 
Morphology and pore structure of catalysts 
Owing to the absence of PMMA as pore templates, oxidizing Cu-PAN fibers and then transforming 
into the Cu-N/CF would not yield mesopores. This can be confirmed by scanning electron microscopy 
(SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and scanning TEM (STEM) images. The Cu-N/CF 
exhibited a fibrous morphology with a relatively smooth surface and no visible pores (Figure 2a-d, 
Figure S4-S7). In the Cu-N/PCF, the macrophase separation of the PAN and PMMA blend results in 
the formation of non-uniform and large PMMA domains over the PAN matrix, which act as pore-
forming reagents and can be easily removed through oxidization at low temperatures in air. As a 
result, the Cu-N/PCF displayed discontinuous and non-uniform macro/mesopores (Figure 2e-h). In 
contrast, the microphase separation of PAN-b-PMMA directs the generation of continuous and 
uniform PMMA domains in the PAN matrix,34 thereby creating the IPCF for supporting isolated Cu 
atoms with dense and interconnected mesopores throughout the surface and cross-section (Figure 2i-
l).

The evolution of different pore structures was further monitored by N2 gas adsorption-desorption 
isotherm measurements. The Cu-N/IPCF exhibited distinguishable features with pronounced type H1 
hysteresis confirming the existence of mesopores (Figure 2m), which was not observed with the Cu-
N/CF and Cu-N/PCF samples. As for the pore size distribution, the Cu-N/IPCF showed a considerable 
number of mesopores with a narrow distribution centered at ~9.3 nm (Figure 2n), whereas there are 
insignificant mesopores with broad distribution in the Cu-N/PCF and fewer mesopores in the Cu-
N/CF. Further, the Cu-N/PCF displayed the largest surface area and CO2 adsorption amount than the 
other two counterparts (Figure 2o, Table S1). These results validate the success in the synthesis of 
Cu-N/IPCF with uniform mesopores via the block copolymer-assisted method, which is prominently 

9 
distinct from porosities of conventional Cu-N/PCP and Cu-N/CF prepared using a PAN+PMMA 
blend and PAN alone, respectively. The long-range mesopores could provide efficient CO2 transport 
and prevent CO intermediates from quickly escaping due to the confined diffusion channels. The 
unique porous microenvironment favors the creation of CO2-rich active surfaces for desirable deep 
reduction to CH4. 
 
Atomic-level structure of single Cu sites in catalysts 
We further elucidated the atomic-level structure of atomically dispersed Cu sites and their interaction 
with the carbon support. All catalysts showed a broad feature at ~25° in X-ray diffraction (XRD) 
patterns (Figure S8), suggesting no Cu-containing crystalline species on the partially graphitized 
carbon support, in agreement with high-resolution TEM images and TEM diffraction patterns (Figure 
S9). Energy-dispersive spectroscopy elemental mapping images confirm the homogeneous 
distribution of Cu species in the N-doped carbon matrix (Figure 3a). The actual loadings of Cu in the 
three catalysts were determined to be around 11 wt% by inductively coupled plasma mass 
spectrometry (ICP-MS) (Table S1). The aberration-corrected high-angle annular dark-field scanning 
transmission electron microscopy (HAADF-STEM) images display dense bright spots with a size of 
around ~0.22 nm corresponding to isolated Cu atoms (Figure 3b, 3c, Figure S10, S11). X-ray 
photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analyses show a high N content of around 14.5 at.% in the surface 
layer of catalysts (Table S1). Deconvolution of the high-resolution N 1s spectra confirms the presence 
of pyridinic N (398.3 eV), pyrrolic N (400.1 eV), graphitic N (401.5 eV), and oxidized N (403.1 eV) 
(Figure 3d). Compared to the Cu-free N-doped IPCF (N-IPCF), the position of pyridinic N shifted to 
higher binding energy with an increased percentage over Cu-containing catalysts (Figure S12), while 
the positions of other N species remained unchanged. This observation suggests that the stabilization 
of single Cu metals involves strong coordination with pyridinic N forming Cu−N bonds due to the 
existence of lone-pair electrons on pyridinic N.35-37

The electronic states and local coordination of atomic Cu were further probed by using Cu K-
edge X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS). In the X-ray absorption near edge spectroscopy 
(XANES), the edge positions of the three catalysts were located between the Cu2O and CuO (Figure 
3e, Figure S13). The Cu oxidization state was estimated to be +1.25 based on the correlation between 
the Cu oxidation state and the absorption edge positions. The Cu 2p and LMM Auger spectra further 

11 
confirm the presence of Cu+ species (Figure S14). The Fourier transformed (FT) extended X-ray 
absorption fine structure (EXAFS) presents a notable peak at ~1.45 Å (Figure 3g), in accordance with 
the first Cu−N shell of the CuPc standard; no observable Cu−Cu bond at ~2.35 Å (Cu foil) was 
detected. The Wavelet transformed (WT) EXAFS analyses show only one maximum intensity at 
approximately 3.0 Å−1 associated with the single-metal Cu−N pair in the absence of crystalline 
species (Figure 3f).38-39 The fitting of EXAFS spectra unveils that the coordination number (CN) of 
Cu−N is around 3.18 ~ 3.31 for these catalysts (Figure 3h, Figure S15, Table S2), indicating that the 
isolated Cu is coordinated with approximately three pyridinic N atoms in the first shell forming 
Cu−N3 moieties. The result is consistent with the XANES  and reported finding that the oxidization 
state of Cu species in Cu−N3 is Cu+.40 The precise atomic-level configuration of Cu−N3 was studied 
by comparing the experimental Cu K-edge XANES spectra with theoretical spectra of models 
simulated based on the FDMNES program, where two commonly proposed models were considered, 
out-plane Cu−N3 and in-plane Cu−N3.20, 40 The simulated XANES result of the out-plane Cu−N3 
could fit the main features of experimental XANES spectrum much better than that of the in-plane 
Cu−N3 (Figure 3i, Figure S16), suggesting that the out-plane Cu−N3 is more likely the dominant 
structure in the as-prepared catalysts. Together, the combination of microscopic HAADF-STEM and 
spectroscopic XAS analyses unambiguously validates the well-defined, atomically dispersed, and N-
coordinated Cu−N3 sites at a high mass loading supported on three different types of carbon supports. 
  
Experimental evaluation of CO2 reduction 
In CO2 electrolysis experiments, catalysts were deposited onto a hydrophobic GDL to increase the 
local concentration of gaseous CO2 for achieving industry-level current density (Figure S17). CO2 
reduction performance was measured in a flow cell with an alkaline electrolyte under constant-current 
testing conditions. We detected four gaseous products (H2, CO, CH4, and C2H4) and four liquid-phase 
products (HCOO−, CH3OH, C2H5OH, and CH3COO−) from the direct CO2 reduction based on the 
13CO2 isotope labeling experiment (Figure S18-20). Among them, HCOO− exhibits significantly 

12 
lower Faradaic efficiency (FE, < 2%) than others. This can be attributed to the enhanced reactivity of 
the Cu site to form C-bonded *COOH rather than O-bonded *OOCH intermediate; the former drives 
CO production and deep CO reduction to CH4 and C2 products through hydrogenation and C−C 
coupling, whereas the later produces HCOO− as the final product.41-43 Figure 4a shows FEs at current 
densities from 50 to 500 mA cm−2. The Cu-N/CF achieved a maximum overall CO2RR FE of 40.6% 
at 100 mA cm−2, which decreases drastically at larger current densities with significantly increased 
FEs for the HER. On the contrary, the HER was successfully suppressed on porous catalysts with 
remarkably increased CO2RR: HER FE ratios (Figure S21), achieving the highest overall CO2RR FE 
of 55.2% at 200 mA cm−2 on the Cu-N/PCF and 87.8% at 300 mA cm−2 on the Cu-N/IPCF.

The inhibition of the HER can be attributed to the highly porous structure. In a previous study, 
Surendranath et al.44 demonstrated the mesostructure-induced transport limitations in the HER 
suppression on porous electrodes, which can be quantified using the roughness factor (RF). We 

13 
calculated the RFs of our catalysts by normalizing the electrochemically active surface area of carbon 
support to the geometric area of the electrode (Figure S22, S23), showing 53.3, 280.5, and 621.2 for 
the Cu-N/CF, Cu-N/PCF, and Cu-N/IPCF, respectively. We noted that the increase in RF leads to an 
enhancement in FE ratios of CO2RR : HER (Figure 4b), along with tendencies of increase in CO2RR 
current density and decrease in HER current density (Figure S24), suggesting a strong dependence 
on CO2 reduction on the porous structure of the electrode. Specifically, compared to the H2O-rich 
surface on the less-porous Cu-N/CF, the porous Cu-N/PCF catalyst provides diffusion channels to 
deliver CO2 to the catalyst surface within pores, while H2O penetration is inhibited due to the 
hydrophobic nature of the GDE (Figure 1c). Such mass transport gradient can be substantially 
enhanced on the Cu-N/IPCF because of its denser long-range mesopores (Figure 1d), thereby building 
a CO2-rich yet H2O-depleted environment and enabling efficient CO2 reduction at large currents. 
We then analyzed the catalytic selectivity and activity of CO2 reduction. CO and CH4 are the 
main products of these Cu-containing catalysts. In contrast, there are only trace amounts of CO2RR-
derived products on Cu-free N-IPCF (Figure S25), verifying the highly active nature of Cu 
components toward CO2 reduction. Calculating the FE ratio of CH4 : CO suggests that block 
copolymer-derived mesopores are essential for promoting deep CO transformation to CH4, with the 
trend following the order of Cu-N/IPCF > Cu-N/PCF > Cu-N/CF, particularly at high current 
densities (Figure 4c). This observation can be ascribed to the steric nanoconfinement effect that enrich 
*CO intermediate within bicontinuous mesopores.30-31, 45 This can be corroborated by in-situ Raman 
spectroscopy (Figure S26), in which the bands at around 286 and 382 cm−1 correspond to the 
frustrated rotation of adsorbed *CO and Cu−*CO stretching, respectively.46-47 The surface-absorbed 
*CO appeared on the Cu−N/IPCF upon applying potentials negative than −1.0 V, which, however, 
was not observed on both Cu−N/PCF and Cu−N/CF. Therefore, compared to the Cu-N/CF and Cu-
N/PCF, the Cu-N/IPCF could afford the most enhanced nanoconfinement contribution to prevent CO 
intermediates from escaping long-range mesochannels, considerably improving its residence time and 
successive reduction to CH4 on the Cu sites before becoming gaseous CO (Figure 1b-d). Note that 

14 
although such local CO-rich characteristics also contribute to the improvement in FE ratios of C2:C1 
on the Cu-N/IPCF (Figure S27), the intrinsically better CO2-to-CH4 reactivity than C−C coupling on 
the in-situ reconstructed Cu site guarantees the enhanced selectivity of CH4 than C2 products, as 
supported by the following in-situ XAS and DFT studies. 
A maximum CH4 FE of 74.2% was achieved on the Cu-N/IPCF at −1.21 V, much higher than 
43.4% on the Cu-N/PCF and 19.5% on the Cu-N/CF at −1.11 V. In addition, the Cu-N/IPCF exhibited 
significantly larger partial current density than the Cu-N/CF and Cu-N/PCF at applied potentials 
negative than −1.11 V (Figure 4d), due to the inhibition in the hydrogen evolution rate. As a result, 
high-efficient CH4 generation yields an industry-level partial current density of 222.6 mA cm−2 at 
−1.21 V. Remarkably, the performance of the Cu-N/IPCF places it at the top level among reported 
Cu-based catalysts (Figure 4e, Table S3), despite there still needs significant performance 
improvement to meet the application threshold for profitable electricity-powered CO2-to-CH4 
conversion.48 The catalyst can maintain durable CO2-to-CH4 electrolysis at 300 mA cm−2 for three 
hours (Figure S28a), during which the slight decrease in CH4 FE after two hours is likely as a 
consequence of the declined surface hydrophobicity of GDE (Figure S28b). 
 
Identification of atomic-level Cu site structure under CO2RR 
To understand the actual catalytically active sites, we performed in-situ XAS to investigate the 
possible structure evolution of single Cu metal sites in the optimal Cu-N/IPCF catalyst under 
operating CO2 reduction conditions. The oxidization state of Cu remained unchanged under open 
circuit voltage (OCV) and dry conditions based on XANES spectra (Figure 5a) and first derivative 
profiles (Figure S29). However, it decreased from +1.25 to +1.06 when a negative potential of −0.8 
V was applied. Further sweeping potential from −1.2 to −1.6 V resulted in the continuous reduction 
to +0.89. These results reveal the unstable characteristics of atomically dispersed Cu sites and the 
occurrence of electrochemical Cuδ+ reduction accompanied by CO2RR.

We continued exploring Cu structures by investigating EXAFS spectra under dynamic 
electrochemical potentials. Similar to the fresh catalyst, no Cu−Cu coordination was observed under 
the OCV (Figure 5b). At −0.8 V, the Cu−Cu bond appeared at 2.28 Å, accompanied by a decrease in 
the intensity of Cu−N relative to that at the OCV. Such transformations were intensified from −1.2 to 
−1.6 V, confirming the collapse of Cu−N3 moieties and their dynamic reconstruction to metallic Cu 
species during the CO2 reduction. Notably, the radial distance of Cu−N coordination under the 
realistic CO2 reduction conditions declined by 0.07 Å compared to that at the OCV. The observation 
suggests the change in the first-shell coordination atoms towards Cu. In general, the first-shell 
coordinated atoms could be anchoring atoms from the carbon support, adsorbed species from the 
KOH electrolyte, and intermediates from CO2RR. In the N-doped carbon, N dopants possessing 

16 
electron-withdrawing properties have stronger interactions with Cu than C atoms.16, 25 Thus, N 
dopants on the IPCF support might be the preferable anchoring sites to immobilize metallic Cu 
species dynamically formed from isolated Cu atoms,25, 49 as manifested by the calculated formation 
energy (Figure S30). The presence of adsorbed *CO intermediate and OHad groups can be confirmed 
by in-situ Raman spectrum (Figure 5c),50-51 in which the *CO is from CO2 reduction and the OHad 
can be ascribed to the specific adsorption of weakly solvated OH− ions from the electrolyte. Thereby, 
the declined average bond length of Cu−N might be a result of the addition of Cu−*CO and Cu−OHad 
bonding, generating Cu−N/C/O coordination over the metallic Cu species. 
EXAFS spectra were further fitted to resolve the CNs. As the first-shell coordinated N/C/O atoms 
in the fitting cannot be distinguished, Cu−N and Cu−Cu paths were included in the EXAFS fitting 
using Cu as the core (Figure 5d, Figure S31, Table S4). The CNs of Cu−N is 3.25 at the OCV (Figure 
5e), implying that Cu−N3 remains stable in the CO2-saturated electrolyte without CO2RR. Notably, 
the CNs changed drastically upon applying CO2 reduction potentials, showing 1.87/1.68, 1.85/1.94, 
and 1.75/2.81 for Cu−N/C/O and Cu−Cu at −0.8, −1.2, and −1.6 V, respectively. The results suggest 
that single-atom Cu metals were transformed into the Cu3 cluster at −0.8 and −1.2 V, which turned 
into the Cu4 cluster with the potential down to −1.6 V. We further performed XANES simulations to 
reveal the atomic-level structure of Cu3 at −1.2 V where the best CO2-to-CH4 performance was 
achieved. Given the configuration of N dopants, fitted CN for Cu−N/C/O, and in-situ Raman results, 
three possible structures were considered, including Cu3-N3-CO1-OH1, Cu3-N3-CO2-OH1, and Cu3-
N3-CO1-OH2 (Figure 5f, Figure S32). Among them, the calculated XANES spectrum of the Cu3-N3-
CO1-OH1 shows the best match with the experimental spectrum at −1.2 V than others, suggesting that 
the Cu3-N3-CO1-OH1 might be the dominant configuration derived from the in-situ reconstruction of 
Cu−N3 under CO2 reduction at −1.2 V. Note that the theoretical CN of Cu−N/C/O over the Cu3-N3-
OH1-CO1 is 1.67, close to the experimental value of 1.85. The structure also coincides with the fact 
that the oxidization state of electrochemically reduced Cu species is not zero due to strong electron 
transfer from the metallic Cu3 cluster to surrounding N-doped carbon, *CO intermediates, and 

17 
adsorbed OHad species, leaving cationic Cu moieties. We have also characterized the spent catalyst 
by ex-situ methods, displaying the single atomic characteristics of Cu species (Figure S33, S34). The 
observation suggests the reversion of in-situ formed Cu3 clusters back to isolated Cu in the absence 
of an electric field and/or being exposed to air, in agreement with previous findings.23-24, 52 In sum, it 
is the N,OH-Cu3 cluster, instead of atomically dispersed Cu−N3, that serve as the catalytic site for 
CO2-to-CH4 reduction at −1.2 V. 
 
Theoretical understanding of catalytic mechanisms 
Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed to provide mechanistic insights. In the 
CO2-to-CH4 transformation, the hydrogenation of *CO to *CHO is a key step,4, 53-55 which requires a 
relatively strong *CO adsorption to inhibit its desorption becoming gaseous CO. We first considered 
CO adsorption affinity on the Cu atom of the N,OH-Cu3 and C/N atoms of the N3-C (Figure 6a, Figure 
S35). The Cu site shows a negative adsorption energy of −0.76 eV, indicative of favorable CO 
adsorption. Yet, positive adsorption energy suggests weak *CO binding on the N3-C. This prediction 
demonstrates that the Cu atom of the N,OH-Cu3 is likely the catalytically active site for reducing CO2 
to CH4 because its preferential *CO adsorption ensures deep *CO transformation. Charge density 
analysis displays strong electronic interaction between the *CO and N,OH-Cu3 with electrons 
transferred from the Cu atom to *CO (insert of Figure 6a), enabling stable *CO binding and 
subsequent PCET-driven reduction to CH4 on the N,OH-Cu3 site. We further carried out density of 
states (DOS) analysis to unveil the bonding interaction upon the adsorption of *CO on the N,OH-Cu3. 
It is the Cu 3dxz/dx2 orbitals that participate in orbital coupling with C 2p of *CO adsorbate based on 
their orbital overlapping (Figure 6b, Figure S36).

We further calculated the free energy diagram, in which the reaction pathways involving *COOH, 
*CO, *CHO, *CHOH, *CH2OH, *CH2, and *CH3 intermediates, were considered.10, 54, 56 The 
adsorption configurations of C-bound intermediates are depicted in Figure 6c. The results show that 
free energy barriers are 0.25, 0.85, 0.61, and 0.58 eV for CO2 activation to *COOH, *CO 
hydrogenation to *CHO, transformation of *CHO to *CHOH, and reduction of *CH2OH to *CH2 on 
the N,OH-Cu3 (Figure 6d), respectively. The other elementary steps are thermodynamically downhill. 
Thus, the hydrogenation of *CO to *CHO is the potential-limiting step in reducing CO2 to CH4. In 
CO2 reduction, forming C2 products via C−C coupling is a grand obstacle challenging selectivity 
toward CH4. Here, the free energy barrier for *CO dimerization, a key step to form C−C bond,57-59 
was calculated to be 2.03 eV, significantly larger than that of the CO2-to-CH4 reduction. The results 
manifest the intrinsically enhanced capability in steering improved CH4 selectivity with the 

19 
suppressed C2 pathway on the N,OH-Cu3 site. The promoting role of surface-bound OHad species in 
CH4 generation was also verified based on the increased free energy barrier for *CO hydrogenation 
on the OHad-free N-Cu3 compared to that on the N,OH-Cu3 (Figure S37). Overall, the theoretical 
predictions support the experimental enhanced CO2-to-CH4 activity of the in-situ formed N,OH-Cu3 
clusters derived from monodispersed Cu−N3 moieties under the realistic CO2RR conditions. 
 
Conclusions 
In this study, we demonstrated a catalyst design principle of integrating atomic-level tuning of Cu 
site’s structure with mesoscale engineering of carbon support’s pore architecture to steer the CO2 
reduction pathway on carbon-supported Cu catalysts. The combined experimental and theoretical 
investigations validated the effectiveness of this strategy toward CO2-to-CH4 transformation. 
Especially utilizing the microphase separation of a PAN-b-PMMA block copolymer, a long-
range interconnected mesoporous N-doped carbon fiber support was developed to host atomically 
dispersed and N-coordinated Cu moieties. The bicontinuous mesochannels of the carbon support 
create a favorable local reaction microenvironment that not only offers H2O-deficient and CO2-rich 
interface in the pore region suppressing hydrogen evolution but also builds an enhanced 
nanoconfinement space with an extended transport path for CO intermediate facilitating deep CO 
reduction. More importantly, in-situ electrochemical XAS and Raman spectroscopy demonstrated a 
unique dynamic reconstruction of atomically dispersed Cu sites to Cu3 clusters immobilized by 
nitrogen of the carbon support and hydroxyl species from the electrolyte under the operating CO2 
reduction conditions, serving as the actual catalytic site for efficient CO2-to-CH4 reduction. DFT 
calculations further predicted that the N,OH-Cu3 clusters selectively drive CH4 formation with the 
suppressed C2 pathway along an energetically favored CO2 → *CO → *CHO → *CH2OH → CH4 
route. Under the synergy of favorable in-situ reconstructed N-Cu3 sites and long-range confined 
mesoreactors, we achieved a CH4 Faradaic efficiency of 74.2% at an industry-level current density 
of 300 mA cm−2, showing great promises for practical CO2-to-fuel electrolysis implementation. The 

20 
rational design concept could pave an effective platform for developing a broad of advanced catalysts 
with optimal structures at multiscale, capable of regulating reaction pathways and selectivity for 
complex catalytic systems that have multiple steps and reaction routes.
