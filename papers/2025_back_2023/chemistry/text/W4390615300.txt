Abstract: Bicycloalkanes, cubanes and their structural analogues have emerged as bioisosteres of (hetero)arenes. To
meet increasing demand, the chemical community has developed a plethora of novel synthetic methods. In this review,
we assess the progress made in the field of light-driven construction and functionalization of such relevant molecules. We
have focused on diverse structural targets, as well as on reaction processes giving access to: (i) [1.1.1]-bicyclopentanes
(BCPs); (ii) [2.2.1]-bicyclohexanes (BCHs); (iii) [3.1.1]-bicycloheptanes (BCHeps); and (iv) cubanes; as well as other
structurally related scaffolds. Finally, future perspectives dealing with the identification of novel reaction manifolds to
access new functionalized bioisosteric units are discussed.
1. Introduction
Arene rings are one of the most prevalent motifs in biologically
active compounds. However, this functionality often contributes
to unfavorable pharmaceutical properties in potential drug-
candidates, thereby hampering the success of these molecules in
advanced clinical trials.[1] The replacement of arene rings with
sp3-hybridized bioisosteres has proven to be a practical solution
to address this issue, since it can lead to new analogues with
improved physicochemical and/or biological properties.[2] In
1964, Wiberg discovered one of the smallest caged hydro-
carbons, namely [1.1.1]-bicyclopentane (BCP).[3] It was not until
1996 that Pelliciari and co-workers revealed the potential of this
functional group in replacing the benzene ring of a selective
antagonist of mGluR1a receptors, thus showing the BCP moiety
to be an effective 3D-bioisostere of planar aryl groups.[4] By
avoiding intermolecular π-π stacking and minimising metabolic
degradation
pathways,
the
2D-to-3D
paradigm
(Figure 1)
generally enhances membrane permeability, aqueous solubility
and the half-lifetime of bioactive molecules.[5] For these reasons,
bicycloalkanes, together with cubanes, are increasingly em-
ployed as bioisosteres of different aromatic and heteroaromatic
rings in agrochemicals and pharmaceuticals.[6] In response to this
growing demand, the synthetic community has recently dedi-
cated significant efforts to providing new methods for the rapid
construction of these complex hydrocarbon scaffolds. Diverse
approaches are now available, involving classical polar methods,
the
use
of
transition
metals,
as
well
as
radical-based
approaches.[7] In the latter, the fields of photochemistry and
photocatalysis[8] have offered practical solutions for assembling
molecular complexity from readily available precursors, thus
paving the way for significant progress in this area of research.[9]
1.1. About this review
In this review, we will detail recent reports (from 2019 to
September 2023) on the light-driven construction and function-
alisation of 3D-scaffolds, specifically those validated as effective
bioisosteres of planar aromatic systems. We have organised our
work with particular emphasis on the reaction mechanisms
involved as well as on the type of molecular structures (scaffolds
1–8 in Figure 1). Other synthetic methods involving polar
reactions, thermally-initiated radical processes or electrochem-
ical methods are out of the scope of this review.[7a,10]
Bicycloalkanes – In the last decade, BCPs 1 have been
widely adopted as effective sp3-rich surrogates for para-
substituted arene rings. A considerable amount of work has
been focused on the synthesis of 1,3-disubstituted BCPs 1 via
strain-release radical processes with [1.1.1]-propellane 9 (Fig-
ure 1). The current available photochemical strategies have
been classified as (i) photocatalytic methods (section 2.1), and
(ii) direct excitation methods (section 2.2). Other strategies for
the construction of complex BCPs 1 or 1,2-substituted BCPs 7
have mainly relied on the use of 1,3- or 1-substituted BCPs
containing a functional handle that can participate in photo-
induced single electron transfer (SET) or atom transfer (AT)
processes. These methods are discussed in section 3.
Other bicyclic structures such as BCHs (3 and 6) and
BCHeps 4 have recently emerged as other classes of promising
bioisosteres for non-linear (hetero)arene rings. Specifically, 3
and 4 have been validated as bioisosteres for meta-substituted
arene rings, whereas the structure of 6 has been demonstrated
to be appropriate for mimicking the exit vectors of ortho-
substituted ones. Light-promoted cycloaddition processes have
allowed access to these molecules in a simple manner. In
particular, [2+2]-cycloadditions promoted by energy transfer
(EnT)[11] photocatalysis have been the main approach to
construct [2.1.1]-BCHs 3 and 6 with different substitution
patterns (section 4.1). On the other hand, highly functionalized
[3.1.1]-BCHeps 4 have been accessed from [3+2]-photocycload-
dition manifolds with alkenes or bicyclobutanes 12; or alter-
natively, by a radical ring opening of the [3.1.1]-propellane 13
(section 4.2).
Cubanes – These caged hydrocarbons offer eight attachment
positions that can be exploited to closely reproduce the exit
vectors of para-, meta- and ortho- substituted arene rings
(structures 2, 5 and 8, respectively). Nevertheless, cubanes
remain underutilized in drug design due to the difficulties in
accessing these scaffolds. Section 4.3 discusses the few photo-
chemical approaches that have been reported so far for the
synthesis and post-functionalization of substituted cubanes 2, 5
and 8.

2. Photochemical Strategies for the Synthesis of
Functionalized BCPs
2.1. Photocatalytic Methods
Photocatalytic methods rely on the use of a visible light-
absorbing metal complex, organic molecule or material (i.e.
the photocatalyst), which is capable of using photonic energy
to then activate non-absorbing molecules through SET, EnT
or AT.[8b–f] This mode of catalytic activation allows the
generation of radicals from simple precursors and under
mild reaction conditions, thus being a very convenient
approach for the straightforward synthesis of functionalized
BCPs, via radical opening of [1.1.1]propellane 9.
2.1.1. Use of carbon-centered radicals
In 2019, Anderson and co-workers were the first to provide
a general route to iodo-BCPs 20 under Ir-photocatalyzed
conditions (Scheme 1).[12] An impressive variety of alkyl 18
and (hetero)aryl iodides 19, including complex biorelevant
molecules, were efficiently engaged in atom transfer radical
addition (ATRA) reactions with the propellane 9, leading to
the products 20 in moderate to excellent yields (40–99%
yield). This photochemical radical initiation strategy notably
enabled the use of iodides 18 that were not amenable to
thermal triethylborane initiation.[7n]
The optimized conditions required the use of fac-
Ir(ppy)3 under blue light irradiation to reduce the iodide 18,
while triggering the formation of the key radical I. A strain-
release radical addition process of I with 9 generates
intermediate II, which undergoes iodine abstraction from
another molecule of 18, yielding the product 20 and
propagating the chain. The quantum yield measured with
several iodides 18 supported the viability of an ATRA chain
mechanism (Φ=1.4–682).
In 2020, the group of MacMillan published a light-
promoted three-component reaction (3CR) that allowed
rapid access to a large array of complex 1,3-disubstituted
BCPs 23 (Scheme 2).[13] In particular, a dual photoredox/
copper catalytic platform enabled the efficient coupling of
alkyl radicals generated from hypervalent λ3-iodines 22,
propellane 9 and diverse nucleophiles 22. Cu(acac)2 was
identified as the optimal copper source to favor the
formation of the desired three-component product, while
minimizing the amount of two-component decarboxylative
C  N coupled products (formed between 21 and 22), or
propellane oligomerization pathways.

The Molander group has also harnessed the potential of
EnT photocatalysis[11] to give access to acyl-substituted BCP
boronates 50 in a straightforward manner (Scheme 11).[25]
This transformation capitalizes upon the ability of oxime
esters 49 to engage in a triplet-triplet EnT process with the
photoexcited thioxanthone catalysts (PC*), while triggering
the generation of acyl radicals XL upon decarboxylation.
Rapid addition of XL to [1.1.1]propellane 9 affords the BCP
radical XLI, which undergoes a borylation process with the
oxime-coordinated boryl intermediate XLII (observed by
11B NMR ) to deliver the desired product 50, together with
the stabilized radical intermediate XLIII. The latter can
undergo a decarboxylation process to generate another
reactive acyl radical XL, while sustaining a chain propaga-
tion mechanism. The quantum yield was determined to be
Φ=1.2, thus supporting the proposed propagation pathway.

2.2. Direct Excitation Methods
Electron donor-acceptor (EDA) complex photochemistry
allows the generation of radical species avoiding the use of
external photocatalysts.[34] This approach relies on the
formation of a ground-state association complex between an
electron-rich molecule (the donor) and an electron-poor one
(the acceptor). It may occur that the independent compo-
nents do not absorb visible light themselves, but the
resulting molecular aggregate does. Light-excitation of the
EDA complex promotes a SET event between the compo-
nents that generally leads to the productive generation of
radical intermediates. This strategy, which has recently
gained recognition in the field of modern synthetic photo-
chemistry, has been successfully applied to the functionaliza-
tion of [1.1.1]propellanes 9.

3. Photochemical Post-Functionalization of BCPs
Various photochemical methods have also been successfully
applied for the installation of other fragments of interest at
the bridgehead (1,3) positions of the BCP core. These
strategies mainly rely on the use of a 1,3- or 1-substituted
BCP precursor containing a functional handle that can be
engaged in a light-promoted SET or atom abstraction
process.
One of the first examples of this approach was reported
in 2020 by Yin and co-workers (Scheme 24).[45] The authors
first synthesized diverse BCP-BPin salts 86 through a photo-
induced decarboxylative borylation of N-hydroxyphthali-
mide esters 85.[46] This photochemical process was optimized
in continuous-flow, thus providing a robust and scalable
route to afford the boronic pinacol products 86 in a rapid
manner (up to 200 gr scale). These precursors (86) were
then transformed in the corresponding BCP-BF3K salts 87 in
one additional step.
The BCP-BF3K salts (87) were then engaged in dual Ni/
photoredox cross-coupling with (hetero)aryl bromides or
chlorides 88, affording the corresponding products 89 in
modest to good yields (Scheme 24b). Noteworthy, a set of
complex drug-like aryl halides 88 proved to effectively react
under these conditions (89a and 89b).

4. Photochemical Synthesis and/or

In recent years, there has been a growing interest in the

5. Conclusions and Future Directions
We have discussed the recent reports dealing with photo-
chemical strategies to access functionalized BCPs, [2.2.1]-
BCHs, [3.1.1]- BCHeps and cubanes. These strained ring
systems, validated as effective bioisosteres of (hetero)arenes,
align with the primary research focus of numerous pharma-
ceutical companies and research groups. A significant
emphasis has been placed on synthesizing BCPs via strain-
release radical processes with [1.1.1]-propellane 9. Various
substrates, including alkyl halides, iodonium dicarboxylate
salts, redox-active esters, trifluoroborate- and sulfinate-
salts, have been employed under photoredox conditions to
generate radicals that readily react with the central strained
bond of 9. Notably, the BCP radicals generated after the
initial addition event have been effectively utilized in diverse
processes, such as HAT, atom or group transfer, as well as
Cu or Ni catalytic cycles, enabling the incorporation of
different functionalities in a single synthetic operation.
Furthermore, alternative strategies involving EDA complex
formation or direct excitation of substrates have enabled
functionalization processes without the reliance on external
photocatalysts.
Other strategies for constructing densely functionalized
BCPs have been based on prior synthesis of 1,3- or 1-
substituted BCP, bearing functionalities amenable to SET or
AT mechanisms. This approach allows rapid derivatization
of BCPs from a common synthetic precursor. In particular,
BCP-BF3K
salts,
BCP-N-(acyloxy)phthalimides,
BCP-io-
dides o BCP-thianthrenium salts have served to generate
BCP radicals that can then participate in Cu or Ni catalytic
cycles
and
radical
addition
events.
For
instance,
the
versatility of this strategy has been showcased with the
challenging incorporation of BCP scaffolds on DNA-con-
jugated olefins.[51]
In the context of [2.1.1]-BCHs and [3.1.1]-BCHeps
synthesis, photocycloaddition processes have been instru-
mental. In particular, EnT photocatalysis has a been a
convenient strategy to access the triplet state of a variety of
substrates, including alkenes or bicyclobutanes, while trig-
gering radical cyclization reactions that lead to the target
bicyclic cores. Alternatively, Anderson has recently intro-
duced
a
more
modular
method
for
the
synthesis
of
functionalized [3.1.1]-BCHeps, which capitalizes on the
strain-release radical reactivity inherent in [3.1.1]propellane
13.[71]
Finally, a major breakthrough in the area of synthesis
and functionalization of cubanes has been offered by the
group of MacMillan. Specifically, they have developed
scalable
synthetic
routes
to
1,2
and
1,3-dicarboxylate
cubanes, which can be further functionalized under metal-
laphotoredox conditions to yield a wide array of functional-
ized cubanes.[78] We believe that more is to come in this
research area, since the synthesis and functionalization of
cubanes is still very limited and it involves many synthetic
steps.
Current synthetic challenges in this field involve the
development of more scalable routes to access these F(sp3)-
rich
structures,
thus
meeting
the
increasing
needs
of
pharmaceutical research.[80] For example, the implementa-
tion of more photochemical continuous-flow methods (as
shown in Schemes 24 and 44) will facilitate the production
of the target compounds on a large scale. In addition, the
development of methods enabling direct C  H functionaliza-
tion of bicycloalkanes and cubanes represents a significant
challenge
yet
promises
to
diversify
complex
strained
molecules.[81] Such methodologies would have broad applica-
tions in generating new libraries of functionalized 3D-
bioisosters, thus providing new opportunities to explore
unprecedented chemical space. Methodology for the incor-
poration of fluorine atoms in BCPs, [2.1.1]-BCHs, [3.1.1]-
BCHeps and cubanes is also highly awaited. This pursuit
holds the potential to yield novel bioisosteres exhibiting
enhanced physicochemical properties, such as pKa, LogP or
aqueous solubility. Recently the community has also inves-
tigated the possibility of merging diverse bioisosteric units in
a single molecule (as shown in Schemes 13 and 22). This is
also a promising approach that can lead to the development
of a number of novel bioactive ingredients potentially
having a synergistic benefit on the pharmacokinetic proper-
ties of the parent molecule. We also expect the development
of more photochemical enantioselective strategies for the
construction of chiral bicycloalkane scaffolds.[82]
The properties of BCAs may also have a great impact in
other fields of research, such as materials science or polymer
chemistry.[10b] For example, the bioisosteric replacement
concept can be utilized to create metal–organic frameworks
(MOFs) with improved chemical properties and stability,[83]
or to regulate the backbone structure and properties of
polymers.[84]
We believe that photochemical approaches will increas-
ingly play a key role in solving such complex synthetic
challenges. The accurate mechanistic analysis together with
the design and fine tuning of the photocatalytic systems[85]
will lead to progress in this exciting research field.
