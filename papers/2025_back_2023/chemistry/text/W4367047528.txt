Abstract 
10 
A many times heard mantra of solvent degradation management in amine-based post combustion capture is “keep the solvent 
11 
clean” to minimize solvent consumption. It is assumed that the amine losses would decrease by the removal of metals, 
12 
degradation products, and reactive trace components which are captured from the flue gas, like NO2 (as potentially driving 
13 
components of the amine degradation besides dissolved O2). However, this theoretical hypothesis – based on results from 
14 
laboratory experiments typically generated with fresh amines – disregards the complexity of the solvent matrix, interaction of 
15 
potential metal catalysts with degradation products and oxidizing agents, and specific chemical requirements which must be 
16 
fulfilled before a degradation mechanism can proceed. Degradation of the solvent CESAR1 (aqueous solution of 3.0 molar 2-

26

27

1. Introduction 
32 
Recently, a guidance was published that proposes available techniques which should be the best for preventing or 
33 
minimizing emissions and impacts on the environment from post-combustion carbon dioxide capture [1]. According 
34 
to this guidance, solvent management is a very important factor for the environmental footprint of amine-based CO2 
35 
capture. It is suggested that: 
36 
• amine-based solvents should generally be clear and transparent, 
37 
• “significant” changes of the solvent color should automatically require the application of reclaiming, 
38 
• all non-solvent constituents should be removed from the solvent inventory as fast as they are added during 
39 
operation to avoid their accumulation. 
40 
 
41 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  3 
The underlying idea tries to connect increasing corrosivity of the solvent with increasing concentration of organic 
42 
acid residues formed by amine oxidation that results in increased release of metal ions from the capture plant 
43 
components into the solvent which act together with degradation products as catalysts for the amine degradation. 
44 
Similar guidelines have also been previously proposed in literature. Derived from experience with the 
45 
monoethanolamine (MEA) operation at the Technology Center Mongstad, the authors propose color as a degradation 
46 
indication, suggest “transparent” as a threshold value, and recommend reclaiming in case of any significant color 
47 
change. Additional degradation indicators and threshold values include: ammonia emission (5 ppmv), heat stable 
48 
salts (1.5 wt.% of the amine as HSS), metals (5 ppmv), and the sum of degradation products concentration (3 wt.%). 
49 
Reclaiming is suggested in case any of these variables achieve the threshold limit [2]. 
50 
The published guidelines also link the formation of nitrosamines in the solvent with solvent management and the 
51 
concentration of NOx in the flue gas. It is assumed that solvents using secondary amines would readily form stable 
52 
nitrosamines in the capture plant in contradiction to primary amines, which would form no such products especially 
53 
if a high level of reclaiming is used to remove degradation products [1]. Simple approaches for the reduction of the 
54 
nitrosamine concentration in solvents based on secondary amines, like the thermal decomposition of nitrosamines, 
55 
are unfortunately not mentioned [3] 
56 
 
57 
However, to a large extent the experimental basis for the underlying assumptions and recommendations is limited 
58 
to simplified laboratory experiments or testing campaigns at pilot plants which are short compared to the time-scale 
59 
of solvent degradation processes, and are related to the solvent 30 wt.% aqueous monoethanolamine (MEA) [1,2]. 
60 
Quite often drastic artificial parameters, regarding partial pressure of gaseous components, temperatures, and 
61 
concentrations of components in the solvent, are applied for laboratory tests to reduce the testing time and in the 
62 
hope to achieve strong and conclusive effects (accelerated degradation tests), but the individual effects of the 
63 
specific parameters must be carefully evaluated for each solvent system [4-6]. Test campaigns at capture plants are 
64 
often interrupted by exchanges of the solvent inventory caused by operational issues, like corrosion of components 
65 
or problems regarding the flue gas quality and interfering tasks in testing programs so that the effective testing time 
66 
on degradation is limited  [2]. Buvik et al. have recently assessed 29 individual campaigns on solvent degradation in 
67 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  4 
18 different pilot plants which are generally not longer than 4,000 h [39]. Also for the slowly degrading CESAR1 
68 
solvent the reported testing campaigns have durations of only 1,000 and 1,500 operating hours [40,41]. 
69 
 
70 
It is important to validate the proposed solvent management approaches and the hypotheses about solvent 
71 
degradation against results from real long-time pilot plant or full-scale tests using fully representative, industrial flue 
72 
gases, over representative periods. This will help to avoid waste streams and efficiency losses by reclaiming that 
73 
may result from contra-productive overshooting efforts for solvent management. The consequences of the proposed 
74 
guiding principles are directly connected with the economics of amine-based CO2 capture, conflicting environmental 
75 
goals due to cross-media effects (management of solvent consumption, decreased energetic efficiency, generation of 
76 
waste-streams) and consequences for the permitting, operation and implementation of CO2 capture plants.  
77 
As part of the transatlantic project LAUNCH (Project No. 299662; co-funded by the ERA-NET Accelerating 
78 
CCS Technologies initiative), a long-time test campaign (testing time up to now 40 months; 24/7 operation) at the 
79 
CO2 capture pilot plant at the lignite-fired power plant in Niederaussem with the CESAR1 solvent (aqueous solution 
80 
of 3.0 molar (26.7 wt.%) 2-amino-2-methylpropan-1-ol (AMP) and 1.5 molar (12.9 wt.%) piperazine (PZ)) is carried 
81 
out. Three solvent management strategies with different effect mechanisms are investigated and evaluated: a) 
82 
removal of ionic compounds and elements from the solvent by ion exchange using cationic and anionic exchange 
83 
resins, b) adsorptive removal of trace elements from the solvent by active carbon, and c) removal of NO2 from the 
84 
flue gas by pretreatment with an aqueous thiosulfate/sulfite solution. Also the effect of solvent refills and plant 
85 
downtime on the degradation rate is evaluated [7, 8]. 
86 
The results of this unique test campaign of solvent management technologies with CESAR1 are contrary to the 
87 
above mentioned general recommendations on best available technologies. In this publication it is shown that: 
88 
• solvent treatment by active carbon reduced the visible absorbance of the solvent, as well as the iron, nickel, and 
89 
chloride concentration, but this has no clear effect on the degradation rate and performance of CESAR1, 
90 
• anionic compounds, trace components and metal complexes can be effectively removed from the solvent by ion 
91 
exchange using an anion exchange resin and cations by a cation exchange resin, but removal of anions alone 
92 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  5 
which explicitly comprises also negatively charged metal complexes of iron, nickel and zinc causes a significant 
93 
increase of the degradation of CESAR1, 
94 
• NO2 removal from the flue gas upstream the CO2 absorber by pre-treatment with a thiosulfate solution did not 
95 
show a reducing effect on the amine degradation, and no significant reduction of the concentration of 
96 
nitrosamines in CESAR1 could be observed (being a secondary amine, PZ is expected to readily form 
97 
nitrosamines according to [1]), 
98 
• Removal of all contaminants from CESAR1 as fast as they are formed is contra-productive and would cause 
99 
unnecessary loss of solvent and capture plant efficiency as well as increased volume of waste streams without 
100 
performance benefits.

2.2. Testing campaign with solvent CESAR1 
112 
The CESAR1 solvent was prepared from the individual amine components in aqueous solution. AMP has been 
113 
provided by BCH Brühl - Chemikalien Handel GmbH as 90% solution. PZ was provided as anhydrous chips by 
114 
Julius Hoesch GmbH. The piperazine chips were dissolved in pre-heated water and a solution with 30% PZ was 
115 
prepared. The final composition of CESAR1 according to the target concentrations of AMP (26.7 wt.%, or 3 M) and 
116 
PZ (12.9 wt.%, or 1.5 M) was produced by mixing the two solutions in the solvent tank of the capture plant. No 
117 
additives or corrosion inhibitors have been used. Figure 1 shows an overview about the overall testing campaign. 
118 
The testing campaign with CESAR1 started on 08.04.2019. The capture plant has been operated almost 
119 
continuously. During the usually short times when no CO2 was available from the power plant unit, the capture plant 
120 
was operated in “hot mode”. During the “hot mode” the solvent recirculation is maintained and the solvent 
121 
temperature in the desorber is kept at approx. 85°-100°C, but the flue gas feed is stopped, hence no CO2 is captured. 
122 
This mode of operation was chosen to further thermally stress the solvent allowing continued solvent degradation 
123 
and release of metal ions into the solvent by corrosion. During the phase of a major overhaul of the power plant from 
124 
the end of July to the beginning of October 2020, during a short stop in December 2020, and an overhaul in May 
125 
2022, the solvent was drained into the solvent tank, which was emptied from fresh solvent before to avoid dilution of 
126 
degradation products in the solvent.  
127 
In the first 1,078 days of operation with CESAR1, the concentration of AMP and PZ and the concentration ratio 
128 
AMP/PZ were not kept constant in the solvent, but actions were taken to maintain the water content. For this, the 
129 
addition of make-up water to the solvent loop of the capture plant has been adapted according to the results of daily 
130 
analyses of the water content. The make-up water is added to the inventory of the water wash section at the top of 
131 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  7 
the absorber which is connected with the solvent loop. With increasing content of degradation products and trace 
132 
components in the solvent the amine concentrations decreased, and the solvent circulation was increased (from 
133 
approx. 2,600 kg/h to 3,100 kg/h within 21,600 h and 3,500 kg/h after 24,500 h) in order to keep the CO2 capture 
134 
rate of 90%. After two years of operation and an increase of the solvent circulation of 8% no change of the specific 
135 
reboiler duty was observed. All in all the sensitivity of the specific reboiler duty regarding degradation and L/G ratio 
136 
is relatively low for the CESAR1 solvent  (specific reboiler duty/solvent flow: day 92: 3,11 GJ/t CO2/2,741 kg/h; 
137 
day 171: 3,10 GJ/t CO2/2,641 kg/h; day 367: 3,10 GJ/t CO2/2,592 kg/h; day 751: 3,07 GJ/t CO2/2,842 kg/h). By 
138 
removal of degradation products in the campaigns on ion exchange from the solvent and the recovery of the initial 
139 
amine ratio, the solvent circulation rate achieved the initial value. To compensate the solvent losses by sampling, 
140 
degradation and emissions and to guarantee the operability of the capture plant regarding the necessary solvent 
141 
inventory, fresh solvent was refilled eight times (between 10 and 25% of the solvent inventory of the capture plant 
142 
per refill): refills after 79 days (1,896 h), 280 days (6,720 h), 315 days (7,560 h), 458 days (10,992 h), 637 days 
143 
(15,288 h), 784 days (18,816 h), 970 days (23,280 h) and 1,078 days (25,872 h) from the solvent tank (3 molar 
144 
AMP, 1,5 molar PZ, aqueous solution; in the last refill the initial amine ratio was adjusted). This means the volume 
145 
equivalent to one solvent inventory was added to the plant in a period between 600 and 800 days of operation. For 
146 
comparison, as a result of the ALIGN-CCUS long-term campaign with MEA, the solvent inventory change rate was 
147 
estimated between 200 and 270 days [11]. 
148 
A commercially available silicon-based anti-foaming agent (SAG 7133, BASF; injected amount about 50 ml) was 
149 
added occasionally (one time per month) to the solvent, when fluctuations of the differential pressure in the desorber 
150 
column indicated incipient foaming. Despite the accumulation of trace components and degradation products, no 
151 
operational problems by corrosion, precipitation or increasing solvent viscosity have occurred. 
152 
Four tests have been carried out to investigate the degradation rate of CESAR1 and the destruction of 
153 
nitrosamines in the solvent at elevated desorber pressure of 2.4 bar(a) and corresponding reboiler temperature of 
154 
130°C (for 7 days after 337 day testing time, for further 5 days after 354 days, 7 days after 822 days, and for 12 days 
155 
after 912 days during the campaign with NO2 removal). 
156 
After parametric studies using CESAR1 at the beginning of the overall campaign, the proprietary dry bed 
157 
configuration was almost continuously used from day 400 (9,600 h) in addition to the normal water wash at the top 
158 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  8 
of the CO2 absorber column to effectively minimize the AMP and PZ emissions. This significantly reduces the 
159 
emissions without generating waste streams [8, 12] (reduction of AMP from 35 mg/m³ to < 5 mg/m³ and PZ from 
160 
10 mg/m³ to <3 mg/m³). The AMP, PZ and NH3 concentrations in the CO2-lean flue gas downstream the water wash 
161 
section are measured by a GasMET CX/DX 4000 FTIR analyser. Heated lines are used to connect the sampling 
162 
point and the analyser. The detection limits for AMP and PZ are approx. 1 mg/m³ (STP). The analyser is zeroed with 
163 
nitrogen once a week. An online THC measurement system (Total Hydrocarbons, Fidamat 6, Siemens) is used to 
164 
investigate the emissions of organic compounds semi-quantitatively. 
165 
The acid wash is a separate process component and no liquids from the acid wash have contact with the water 
166 
wash or the solvent loop. Therefore, an effect of its operation on the solvent degradation is not possible. During the 
167 
considered period with tests of solvent management technologies the acid wash was not active.

2.3.2. NO2 removal by thiosulfate/sulfite dosing 
204 
The NOx concentration in the feed gas is mainly formed from nitrogen in the lignite and not by thermal formation 
205 
from the nitrogen content of the combustion air. At lignite fired power plants, the NOx concentration is controlled by 
206 
primary measures (air staged combustion) and not by SCR or SNCR as for hard coal-fired power plants. This 
207 
explains the stronger dependence of the NO2 formation on the nitrogen content of the lignite and the fact that there is 
208 
no NH3 in the flue gas (which would have otherwise resulted from a slip of the DeNOX system). During the NO2 
209 
removal campaign by pre-scrubbing with an aqueous thiosulfate/sulfite solution, the NO2 concentration in the flue 
210 
gas was in the range between 1-4 mg/m³, dry (STP) up-stream the pre-scrubber. The NO concentration in the feed 
211 
gas is fluctuating between 100 and 160 mg/m³ and under full load conditions of the power plant no steplike changes 
212 
of the NO concentration or extended phases with different concentration levels occur. Only at part load operation 
213 
(these phases normally last less than 1 day) the oxygen concentration in the flue gas increases from 5.5 to 7.5 vol.%, 
214 
dry (STP), NO decreases from 140 to 110 mg/m³ dry (STP) and NO2 increases from 2.5 to 5 mg/m³ dry (STP).  
215 
In the NO2 removal campaign at Niederaussem the flue gas pre-treatment comprised the dosing of separate 
216 
solutions of sodium thiosulfate, NaOH and sodium sulfite in the scrubbing liquid loop of a pre-scrubber (normally 
217 
used as proprietary aerosol removal module) which was equipped with a high-performance packing (2.24 m high 
218 
structured packing; specific surface approx. 600 m²/m³) to achieve a sufficient high mass transfer of NO2 from the 
219 
gas phase into the liquid. This set-up was used instead of dosing into the DCC to avoid the loss of the agents by 
220 
drained condensate from the DCC. The thiosulfate and NaOH dosing system was integrated into the control system 
221 
of the capture plant. Alternatively, condensate from the DCC or demineralized water can be used to fill the 
222 
scrubbing system.  
223 
For measuring the NO2 concentration downstream the DCC at the inlet of the CO2 absorber, an UV spectrometer 
224 
was used (SICK GMS 810-DEFOR, measuring range 0.0-50 mg/m³, accuracy approx. 0.5 mg/m³). During the test, 
225 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  11 
one time per day for a period of 15 to 30 minutes, the pre-scrubber was bypassed to measure the NO2 concentration 
226 
without the countermeasure to allow for the evaluation of the effect of the countermeasure. The target concentration 
227 
of thiosulfate in the scrubbing liquid was 120 mmol/kg at a pH value between 7 – 8 in the scrubbing liquid to 
228 
achieve an expected NO2 removal rate of >80%. The thiosulfate concentration was monitored off-line by an analyzer 
229 
based on Raman spectroscopy and iodometric titration. The results of both methods coincided well. Also, by 
230 
iodometric titration, the sum of thiosulfate and sulfite concentration in the scrubbing solution was analyzed. 
231 
The long-time test of the NO2 removal by dosing of thiosulfate started at day 854. The analysis of the scrubbing 
232 
solution resulted in concentrations of thiosulfate up to 15,000 mg/l (134 mmol/l) and sulfite up to 16,500 mg/l 
233 
(21 mmol/l). By reducing the thiosulfate dosing rate, a minimal sum concentration of thiosulfate and sulfite of 
234 
70 mmol/l was identified which is necessary to remove >80% of the NO2. At lower sum concentrations an increase 
235 
of the NO2 concentration in the flue gas at the inlet of the absorber occurred. A decrease of the sulfite concentration 
236 
in the scrubbing fluid is caused by low SO2 freights in the flue gas in phases of part load operation of the power 
237 
plant. A consequence of these phases is a slight increase (typically 0.7 to 1.0 mg/m³) of the NO2 concentration in the 
238 
flue gas entering the CO2 absorber.

2.3.3. Ion exchange with anion exchange resin and serial configuration of cation and anion exchange resins 
240 
To evaluate the effects of solvent treatment by ion exchange a mobile system (MIONTEC) was used. Proprietary 
241 
anion and cation exchange resins provided by the supplier of the ion exchange system were used to remove ionic 
242 
trace elements from the solvent. In the first campaign the solvent treatment system was first activated after 646 days 
243 
(15,504 h) of operation. During each ion exchange cycle, approx. 130 l of solvent were treated, corresponding to ca. 
244 
4% of the inventory. The lean solvent was taken out of the solvent storage tank which was filled on demand with the 
245 
needed amount of aged solvent from the solvent loop. After each cycle, the resin was regenerated with 4 wt.% 
246 
NaOH solution. The cleaned solvent was fed back to the absorber sump. After 17 ion exchange cycles (approx. 2.2 
247 
m³ of solvent was treated, or approx. 75% of the total solvent inventory), the solvent treatment was stopped for 18 
248 
days to monitor the behavior of the solvent degradation and the concentrations of the trace components in the 
249 
solvent. After 687 days (16,488 h), the ion exchange module was operated again until in total 48 ion exchange cycles 
250 
have been carried out on day 721 (17,304 h). Approx. 6.2 m³ of solvent were treated in total or approx. 2 times the 
251

  12 
total inventory. It is noted that, after 690 days (16,560 h), the particle and active carbon filter line was closed and 
252 
emptied into the solvent loop of the capture plant to refill the plant without adding fresh solvent to the solvent hold

up. The particle filter was immediately afterwards activated again, but the active carbon filter not until day 1213

3.2. Effect of active carbon 
378 
Solvent treatment by active carbon is usually applied in capture plants to avoid operational issues - like foaming 
379

380

381

382

383

384

385 
solvent [1]. However, for the hypothesis that coloring contaminants are accelerating the degradation and lowering 
386 
the performance of the solvent, no evidence is resulting from the long-time test of CESAR1 at all. When the active 
387 
carbon filter is operated, it removes coloring contaminants from the solvent, see Figure 6. Additionally, the 
388 
concentrations of iron and nickel are reduced (iron from 8 to 4 mg/kg and below the detection limit after two refills; 
389

390 
stopped which is exactly what is proposed for these three specific contaminants named in [1] to ensure satisfactory 
391 
capture plant operation, see Figure 7.

4. Discussion   
576 
4.1. Role of metal ions for amine oxidation 
577 
At first glance, the process of the oxidation of amines, but also of their degradation products which are 
578 
accumulating in the CO2 capture solvent, seems to be a simple reaction between the residual oxygen in the flue gas 
579 
and the organic compounds. After reaching the thermodynamic equilibrium, it can be expected that in an alkaline 
580 
solution dissolved HCO3-, NO3- and H2O are received as oxidation products, because of their strong negative Gibbs 
581 
functions for formation of the compounds (ΔfG°molar at 298.15K: H2O(liquid) -237.1kJ mol-1; HCO3-(aq) -586.8kJ mol-1; 
582 
NO3-(aq) -108.7kJ mol-1[17]). However, these oxidation reactions do not proceed spontaneously and it is a matter of 
583 
fact that the oxidation mechanism is highly complicated and comprises several elemental steps [18]:  
584 
• 
dissolution of the gaseous oxidizing species in the flue gas (e.g. O2, NO, NO2/N2O4, SO3) into the solvent in 
585 
the CO2 absorber comprises diffusion of the oxidant from the gas phase to the flue gas-solvent interface, 
586 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  26 
• 
transfer of the species across the interface hydrolysis/ionization of the reactant in the solvent phase if possible, 
587 
• 
diffusion of the ionic and non-ionic species inside the solvent, and a wide range of possible chemical reactions 
588 
inside the solvent.  
589 
An explanation why organic compounds in the solvent are not instantaneously oxidized by O2 is the fact that 
590 
organic substrates (amines, alcohols, ketones and acids) in the ground state all have closed shell electronic structures, 
591 
and therefore are in a singlet state, while O2 in the flue gas is a diradical (triplet O2, ground state with the two 
592 
unpaired electrons is the triplet spin state). Chemical interactions are governed by various selection rules, among 
593 
which the ones due to Wigner formulate the constraint of overall spin-conservation [19]. For the reaction to occur, 
594 
i.e. to preserve the spin, the transition state has to be a combination of the spins of the reactants and the products. 
595 
That is the algebraic sum of the spins S of the reactants has to have at least one term in common with the algebraic 
596 
sum of the spins of the products (|SReactant±SOxygen|=|SProduct1±SProduct2-Oxygen|) [20, 21]. An implication of these spin-
597 
selection rules is the inertness of 3O2, if the other reactant and the product are spin singlets, as this reaction is spin-
598 
forbidden.  
599 
Reactions between organic substrates in singlet state with oxygen would be allowed by spin selection rules when 
600 
the oxygen molecule is in an excited singlet state with paired electrons in different π* orbitals. The necessary 
601 
excitation energies are 156.9 kJ mol-1 for O2(1Σg+) and 94.14 kJ mol-1 for O2(1∆g) related to the ground state O2(3Σg
602 
ˉ). Singlet O2 can be photochemically produced by electronic energy transfer from photo-excited sensitizers or by 
603 
thermolysis of peroxides. However, due to the lack of radiation with appropriate wavelength and dye-sensitizers, 
604 
singlet oxygen seems to be irrelevant for the solvent degradation.  
605 
There are different options to circumvent the reaction barrier for triplet oxygen [22]. Metal complexes can 
606 
provide spin-allowed pathways for reaction, due to their abilities to act as radicals with varied spin multiplicities if 
607 
the number of electrons on a ternary complex (metal ion + amine + O2) remains constant throughout the reaction 
608 
[23]. Here, the reaction of 3O2 with a paramagnetic metal LM(n)+ ion (L represents complexation of metals like 
609 
Fe2+,3+, Cu2+,  Co2+,3+, Ni1+,2+,3+ by one or several ligands) can follow an activation route by sequential electron 
610 
transfer summarized as: triplet oxygen 3O2, superoxide O2-, peroxide O22-and oxide O2-. These reduction 
611 
intermediates are weak acids and may exist protonated in the rich amine solution. Alternatively, free-radical 
612 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  27 
formation or autooxidation can be catalyzed by metal ions. Here, a metal ion catalyzes the formation of free radicals, 
613 
and thus the initiation of chain reactions that are responsible for the oxidative amine degradation. Moreover, the 
614 
formation of hydroxyl radicals which react with the amine by hydrogen abstraction and formed by protonation of 
615 
peroxides is catalyzed. Finally, the formation of organoperoxy radicals due to the direct one-electron oxidation by a 
616 
metal ion can also be catalyzed [24].  
617 
After 25,000 testing hours at the capture pilot plant at Niederaussem, the concentration of iron in the CESAR1 
618 
solvent reached concentrations around 10 mg/l (0.19 mmol/l). Assuming that all iron atoms are present as dissolved 
619 
Fe3+ ions which oxidatively attack AMP and PZ according to their molar ratio, the calculation of the specific amine 
620 
loss results in only 0.016 g/l (0.011 g/l AMP and 0.005 g/l PZ). As the amine concentration is 400 g/l, it becomes 
621 
immediately clear that an oxidation mechanism based on iron is negligible in the case that Fe(III) is only consumed 
622 
(and transformed to Fe(II)) and not somehow recovered. It is very important to note that the reduced metal must be 
623 
regenerated by the oxidation agent to act as a catalyst. The regeneration can be performed by oxygen from the flue 
624 
gas which is dissolved in the CESAR1 solvent under formation of Fe(III). Therefore, the iron cation and other metal 
625 
ions could act as a catalyst in the oxidation processes in a spin-allowed reaction. However, metal cations do not exist 
626 
as naked ions in the solvent, but are complexed with water, amines and degradation products. If this would not be 
627 
the case, precipitation of ferric hydroxide Fe(OH)3 would occur at the high pH value of the amine solvent [10]. The 
628 
donation of electron density from the ligand to the metal centre is an important factor for the properties, stability, 
629 
and reactivity of the complex as well as for the lability of the remaining ligands in a mixed ligand complex. In 
630 
chelate complexes polydentate ligands, like polyamines (ethylendiamine), aminocarboxylates (e.g. amino acids, like 
631 
glycine), and polycarboxylates (e.g. oxalate), additionally show the effect of ligand steric hindrance on the 
632 
mechanism or the kinetics as the coordination cage surrounding the metal cation causes steric constraints around the 
633 
metal centre.  
634 
It is therefore postulated that different amines – and degradation products – may deactivate metals which could 
635 
otherwise act as catalysts for amine degradation. Hence, and based on the evidence from this work, it seems 
636 
premature to prescribe a generalized threshold for metal concentration in amine solvents. 
637 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  28 
4.2. The role of ligands on catalytic effect of metal ions 
638 
The reactivity of metal ions in chemical and biological systems comes from the fact that the displacement of 
639 
ligands is an imported step in complex-formation reactions and in many redox processes. Intensive investigations of 
640 
the reactivity of Fe(III)/Fe(II) complexes in amine solutions have been carried out in connection with treatment of 
641 
sour gas streams to remove H2S by oxidation with O2 or to reversibly bind NO in gas cleaning applications. Here, 
642 
especially iron chelate complexes with ligands from the group of aminocarboxylates, like the prominent hexadent 
643 
ethylenediaminetetraacetate (edta), but also small amino acids have been studied as absorbent [23, 25-27]. 
644 
Alkanolamines are not used in these industrial processes because of their lower oxidation stability, but this does not 
645 
mean that they could not form chelate complexes. The chelated iron is the oxidation catalyst in aqueous solution and 
646 
the ligands determine the rate and overall equilibrium constants, as well as the nature of the substitution mechanism 
647 
by which O2 or NO coordinate to the selected complexes. The binding in the NO complexes is similar to the 
648 
coordination of dioxygen to the metal centers, although the nitrosyl products are far more stable. There is, however, 
649 
no simple correlation between the rate and activation parameters and the selected donor groups or overall charge on 
650 
the Fe(II) complexes. It was found that high-stability of Fe(III) chelates do not form satisfactory redox catalysts and 
651 
that ligands that form suitable iron complexes as catalysts have both oxygen and nitrogen donors [25]. However, not 
652 
only the metal is oxidized, but also the ligands can be attacked [24]. To act as a catalyst in amine degradation, the 
653 
difference in stabilities of the Fe(III) and Fe(II) chelates must be low enough so that the Fe(III) form can be reduced 
654 
and that the Fe(III) chelate can be regenerated by the dissolved oxygen in the solvent at alkaline pH. The impact of 
655 
the ligands becomes clear when exemplarily the redox potential of [Fe(III)(edta)]-/[Fe(II)(edta)]2- of 0.129 V is 
656 
compared with that of [Fe(III)(H2O)6]3+/[Fe(II)(H2O)6]2+ of 0.771 V. For the reduction of [Fe(III)(edta)]-, quite 
657 
strong reductants are needed. Chelating ligands are existing in aged CESAR1, like ethylendiamine (EDA) and oxalic 
658 
acid in sufficient amounts for the complexation of iron. Moreover, also complexes with positive charges are known 
659 
with less voluminous ligands, like the basic iron acetate [Fe(III)3O(acetate)6(H2O)3]+. It is unknown if the chelate 
660 
complexes given above or similar compounds are existing in the solvent. However, there is no doubt that the 
661 
potentially as catalyst acting metals in the solvent are complexed and that their accessibility for reaction partners and 
662 
their redox behavior are tuned by the ligands of the solvent matrix. A lower concentration limit for catalytically 
663 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  29 
active metal ions from which the catalytic effect can be expected to be negligible is not known. The solvent and the 
664 
specific matrix of contaminants are more decisive for the degradation than the metal ion concentrations. 
665 
Additionally, it is extremely unlikely that industrial-scale application of amine-based CO2 capture could be realized 
666 
with metal-free solvents. Even if capture plants could be completely built from non-metallic materials, as 
667 
investigated in the project LAUNCH [7], already the pure technical amines will contain metal traces from their 
668 
production processes and small metal or rust particles will enter the capture plant via the feed gas, so that metal ions 
669 
will always be part of the solvent inventory. When cleaning of the solvent removes contaminants that hinder the 
670 
effectivity of the catalysis, more damage than good is done. Excessive cleaning of the solvent would mainly waste 
671 
valuable solvent.

4.3. Role of NO2 for amine oxidation 
673 
If the removal of NO2 from flue gas by pre-treatment based on scrubbing with a thiosulfate/sulfite solution can 
674 
be realized in industrial scale and if this approach is able to significantly reduce the degradation of the solvent, 
675 
depends on its physical and chemical properties. A very first indication for the ranking of the importance of NO2 for 
676 
solvent degradation compared to other oxidizing species in the gas phase can be derived from their molar 
677 
concentrations in the flue gas. Values of Henry’s law constants for aqueous solutions depend on the composition of 
678 
the solution, i.e., on its ionic strength and on dissolved organics. In general, the solubility of a gas decreases with 
679 
increasing salinity (“salting out”). An increase in ionic strength, mainly achieved by loading of the amines with CO2 
680 
accompanied by protonation of the amines and to a lower degree by accumulation of ionic degradation products 
681 
(e.g. organic acids R-COO- residues and ammonium NH4+) and trace compounds from the flue gas (e.g. dissolved 
682 
Cl-, SO22-) leads to decreasing solubility of the gaseous species [28]. The O2 solubility in amine-based solvents is 
683 
typically in the range between 0.1 and 0.4 mmol/l for CO2-unloaded solutions. At plant conditions with CO2-loaded 
684 
solutions at 40ºC and above, the oxygen content is below 0.1 mmol/l and cannot be properly quantified up to now 
685 
[29]. The low partial pressure of the nitrogen oxides and sulfur trioxide compared with molecular oxygen would 
686 
suggest a special importance of oxygen for solvent degradation. Table 5 shows the typical molar concentrations of 
687 
oxidizing species in the flue gas and some important properties for the mass transfer into the solvent and the 
688

5. Conclusion 
745 
The results from the 40 months campaign with CESAR1 at the capture plant in Niederaussem show that some 
746 
suggested measures for solvent management need to be revised and cannot be generalized. It was demonstrated that 
747 
the solvent matrix of the specific solvent and the accumulated contaminants which depend on the specific site 
748 
conditions (flue gas composition, capture plant process configuration, plant materials, and operating characteristic) 
749 
must be taken into consideration to better predict the degradation rate. Neither the solvent color nor the metal 
750 
concentration in the solvent are appropriate measures to control solvent management technologies or to describe the 
751 
performance of the solvent. Ion exchange can effectively remove anions and cations from the solvent, but the 
752 
consequential effects on the solvent degradation rate must be carefully considered. Based on the results of the 
753 
campaign with CESAR1 the removal of ligands from the solvent which form complexes with metal cations can 
754 
increase the degradation rate. More investigation is needed regarding the interaction of metal cations, which have 
755 
the potential to act as a catalyst for oxygen activation and solvent degradation, and degradation products with 
756 
capability to form complexes to avoid the opposite of the expected effect when activating solvent management 
757 
technologies. Also NO2 removal from the flue gas upstream the CO2 absorber by flue gas pretreatment with 
758 
thiosulfate solution did not show a reducing effect on amine degradation in CESAR1. 
759 
The trade-off between a simplified laboratory experiment, to focus on postulated core processes of solvent 
760 
degradation and the necessity to cover all relevant aspects of the real process configuration, comprises on one hand 
761 

 
GHGT-16 Moser et al. 
  33 
the risk of oversimplification and on the other hand the challenge to understand and correctly connect all necessary 
762 
details. For that reason, it is essential to evaluate results from laboratory tests and artificial short-time test campaigns 
763 
on solvent degradation based on the facts received from long-time testing with real flue gases. Without proof of the 
764 
efficacy of specific solvent management technologies for individual solvents in the real application case, theories on 
765 
effect mechanisms and derived guiding principles for solvent management in capture plant operation can be 
766 
misdirected. The proposed guidelines should be reviewed. This can contribute to avoid waste of valuable solvent 
767 
and resources and to optimize the environmental benefit from amine-based carbon capture. An intelligent 
768 
combination of complementary laboratory experiments - making also use of solvent from real operation - with 
769 
meaningful pilot plant tests of sufficient duration and innovative approaches, like the head-to-head operation of CO2 
770 
capture plants of different size realized in the project LAUNCH, offer the opportunity to clarify the effects caused 
771 
by the interventions of solvent management and effective key factors for solvent degradation in industrial CO2 
772 
capture.
