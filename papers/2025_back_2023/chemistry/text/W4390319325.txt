been intensively researched as an alternative to traditional Haber-Bosch process. However, 
industrial viability is challenged by low concentration NO3RR owing to the mismatch between 
hydrogenation and nitrate reduction in a nitrate-rich condition. Herein, we demonstrate that by

Introduction 
Electrolysis in a concentrated electrolyte (≥ 2 M) is required for economically viable industrial 
production in view of unusual mass transfer and reaction kinetics, wider electrochemical window, 
and less generation of waste solutions1-5. Successful deployment of concentrated electrolytes 
(e.g., 1 to 6 M KOH aqueous solution) have been demonstrated in alkaline fuel cells, seawater 
electrolyzers and zinc-air batteries6-8. However, the switching of dilute to concentrated 
electrolyte does not follow simple linear correlation between performance and electrolyte 
concentration. Unlike the presence of solvent-separated ion pairs and free solvent molecules in 
dilute electrolyte, contact ion pairs and cation-anion aggregates are usually involved in extremely 
concentrated electrolyte due to the scarcity of solvent molecules9,10. Such unique solvation 
chemistry leads to significantly different electrochemical profiles and reaction kinetics. 
Particularly, the availability and mobility of proton in aqueous electrolysis are dependent on salt-
induced perturbance of hydrogen bonding network and formation of strong water-ion pairs, 
influencing the hydrogen acquisition and transfer kinetics11,12. 
Industrial adoption of electrochemical nitrate reduction reaction (NO3RR) as an alternative to 
the Haber-Bosch process requires quantitative conversion of concentrated nitrate (up to 2 M in 
industrial and agricultural wastewater) for ammonia electrosynthesis13-16. The difficulty in 
operating with high concentration of nitrates is a bottleneck preventing the process to be 
adopted17-21.  The conundrum is that good NO3RR catalysts (such as Cu2O and other Cu-based 

catalysts) typically have strong binding interactions with nitrates19,22,23, which inevitably results 
in nitrate poisoning of the adsorption site for atomic hydrogen generated from hydrogen 
evolution reaction (HER), preventing subsequent hydrogenation steps in NO3RR. The 
mismatched reaction kinetics in HER and NO3RR has hampered the use of concentrated 
electrolyte in NO3RR, urging for the design of co-catalytic platform to optimize the reaction 
kinetics24,25. For instance, the introduction of secondary metals (such as Ni, Pd, Rh and Ru)18,26-29 
into copper-based catalyst provides extra hydrogen binding sites and reduces the reaction 
barriers in hydrogenation steps. The catalytic mechanism could be sensitive to ion concentration 
and local electric field, for example in dilute electrolyte, it has been reported that Ru single atoms, 
Ni or Ru dopants serve as the catalytic active sites for nitrate.18,26,27 The underlying mechanism 
with respect to the concentration dependence remains unclear. 
Herein, we have chosen a benchmark NO3RR catalyst (ruthenium modified cuprous oxide, Ru-
Cu2O) to illustrate the working principle of reaction kinetics regulation by secondary metals. The 
correlation between electrolyte concentration and hydrogen transfer kinetics is carefully studied 
in the H-cell setup from very diluted (0.01 M) to nearly saturated electrolytes (3 M). Ruthenium 
clusters served as hot spots in hydrogen adsorption from bulk electrolyte and promotes 
subsequent hydrogen transfer to the Cu2O matrix for the hydrogenation of nitrate adsorbents, 
thus enabling much enhanced activities at high concentrations by pairing reaction kinetics. When 
assembled in a 16 cm2 flow electrolyzer, our Ru-Cu2O catalyst allows an ammonia production cost 
of 6.87 USD per kilogram of ammonia, yielding a remarkable ~100% Faradaic efficiency toward 
ammonia in 1 M nitrate at 10.0 A full cell current. 
Results and discussion 
Geometry and electronic configuration of benchmark Ru-Cu2O catalyst. As depicted in Figure 1, 
we have synthesized ruthenium cluster-decorated Cu2O nanowires (Ru-Cu2O) catalyst for NO3RR 
at high nitrate concentrations, where ruthenium domains serve as reaction hot-spots to promote 
hydrogen acquisition and transfer kinetics. Typical preparation of Ru-Cu2O involves anodic 
electrochemical deposition of Cu(OH)2 nanowires on Cu foam, impregnation with RuCl3 precursor 
and subsequent annealing in a hydrogen/argon mixture to furnish the Ru-Cu2O30. The metal 

loading of Ru on Cu2O was determined as 0.91 wt% from inductively coupled plasma optical 
emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES). Uniform distribution of Cu2O nanowires on Cu foam is observed 
in the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images in Figure S1, showing a (111) facet in high 
resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM), and the predominance of (111) facets is 
also reflected in X-ray diffraction (XRD) in Figure S2 and S3. EDS mapping in Figure S6 confirms 
the existence of Ru throughout the catalyst matrix. The surface coverage of Ru on the Cu2O is 
calculated as ~ 5% by comparing the electrochemical active surface area (ECSA) from hydrogen 
underpotential deposition and capacity testing in Figure S7 and S8. For comparative study of the 
role of Ru and Cu2O in NO3RR, we also prepared a reverse structure where Cu2O adopts the form 
of clusters on a Ru substrate, where the exposed coverage of Ru is higher than that of Cu2O with 
a thick, fully-covered Ru layer by overpotential electrodeposition in the RuCl3 solution (Figure S1). 
The valence state of Ru-Cu2O was examined by X-ray absorption near-edge structure (XANES) 
and X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The Ru K-edge XANES spectrum of Ru-Cu2O reveals 
a slightly positive charge (higher white line intensity than Ru foil) in Figure 2c owing to charge 
transfer between Ru and Cu2O. Likewise, spin-orbit doublets attributed to positively charged Ru 
species (Ruσ+) could be observed at 462.08 and 484.86 eV in the XPS Ru3p spectrum in Figure 2e31. 
This is reflected in the peak shifting of 0.3 eV in the XPS Cu2p spectra after Ru decoration (Figure 
2f). Further analysis on the coordination environment of Ru-Cu2O by extended X-ray absorption 
fine structure (EXAFS) spectroscopy confirms the clustering of Ru in our samples. A prominent 
Ru-Ru peak at 2.4 Å was observed in the Fourier transform-EXAFS spectra in Figure 2d.  
Co-catalytic role of ruthenium clusters. It is well-known that apparent activity of catalyst usually 
does not scale linearly with the concentration of electrolyte, particularly for those reactions 
involving multiple proton-coupled electron transfers32. The kinetic limitation arises owing to 
mismatched capability in the activation of two or more reactants (such as hydrogen and nitrate 
in NO3RR). 
The importance in regulating the reaction kinetics is reflected in the yield of ammonia using 
various catalysts at nitrate concentration ranging from 0.01 M to 3 M in Figure 3a. The production 
rate of ammonia using Cu2O catalyst increases with concentration in the dilute regime (0.01 to 

0.1 M) due to promoted mass transfer (Fick’s law) and reaction kinetics. However, the apparent 
activity of Cu2O experiences a decline of 32% from 0.1 to 3 M owing to poisoning of surface sites 
by nitrates. Similar phenomenon has been observed in the Pt-catalyzed NO3RR33, where a 
negative reaction order for the rate-determining step (RDS) in the conversion of nitrate to nitrite 
was found at high concentrations (> 0.1 M). This indicates the quasi-equilibrium of nitrate 
adsorption and conversion on the catalyst surface. Reactive species such as atomic hydrogen (*H) 
must compete for surface sites under nitrate-rich condition. Therefore, our hypothesis is whether 
by introducing ruthenium hot-spots on Cu2O surface, we can provide a sink for atomic hydrogen34. 
As shown in Figure 3a, our Ru-Cu2O enables a remarkable 13-fold increase in ammonia 
production rate when increasing the nitrate concentration from 0.01 to 1 M. No significant 
performance decay is observed until a very high concentration of 3 M. It is worth mentioning that 
3 M is close to the saturated solubility of KNO3 at 298.15 K (3.76 M). Control samples using fully 
covered Ru CL-Cu2O (Figure S16) and reversed Cu2O-Ru CL-Cu2O (Figure S17) exhibit no major 
difference with pure Cu2O catalyst, suggesting that a particular spatial segregation profile of Ru 
and Cu2O is necessary to optimize the rate of nitrate and hydrogen activation. 
Detailed investigation on the NO3RR performance of Ru-Cu2O was conducted in a H-cell setup at 
various concentrations. The production rate of ammonia was quantified by colorimetric and NMR 
methods in Figure S10. As shown in Figure 3b, Ru-Cu2O achieves a nearly 100% Faradaic efficiency 
toward ammonium at - 0.1 V and -0.2 V in 0.1 M nitrate, and maintains a > 90% Faradaic efficiency 
at - 0.4 V at a current density of 1.26 A cm-2. At higher concentration (1 M), Ru-Cu2O exhibits an 
almost 1.5 times higher current density (2.01 vs. 1.39 A cm-2) and Faradaic efficiency (75% vs. 
49%) than pure Cu2O between 0 V and -0.4 V. This corresponds to a remarkable ammonium yield 
rate of 7.02 mmol h-1 cm-2 in Figure 3c. 1H NMR spectra were also collected for Ru-Cu2O catalyst 
at - 0.2 V to confirm the origin of ammonia using by 15N isotope labeling. As shown in Figure S11, 
the electrolyte after K15NO3 reduction reveals a typical doublet of 15NH4+ at 6.86 and 7.00 ppm, 
without the presence of the triplet of 14NH4+ in the 1H NMR spectra. This confirms the formation 
of ammonium originated from nitrate rather than nitrogen in air35. The concept of regulating the 
hydrogen and nitrate activation can be further extended to other secondary metals (such as Pd). 
The Pd-Cu2O catalyst exhibits a similar enhancement to Ru-Cu2O owing to strong hydrogen 

adsorption properties, giving a maximum ammonium yield rate of 4.71 mmol h-1 cm-2 in 1 M 
nitrate (Figure S18). However, further increasing the metal loading of Ru to 3.6 wt% (Figure S19) 
will lead to over-competition of HER against NO3RR, resulting in a 40% decline in the current 
density in 0.1 M nitrate (Figure S20). 
To validate the influence of hydrogen adsorption and transfer kinetics on nitrate reduction, we 
have performed HER and NO3RR measurements in water and deuterium oxide using linear sweep 
voltammetry (LSV). Kinetic isotope effect (KIE) values are expressed by the ratio of current 
density measured in H2O and D2O. Typically, a KIE value greater than 1 implies the proton-coupled 
transfer step (primary KIE), rather than electron transfer step, is rate limiting during the 
electrochemical reactions (secondary KIE). As shown in Figure S12, the incorporation of Ru leads 
to a higher energy barrier (higher KIEs) for hydrogen evolution compared to pure Cu2O.31 In the 
presence of nitrate (Figure 3d), pure Cu2O exhibits a much higher KIE value of 3.08 at - 0.4 V (vs. 
~ 2 in HER in the absence of nitrate). This implies the difficulty in the acquisition of protons or 
atomic hydrogen from water splitting. In contrast, Ru-Cu2O retains a considerably low KIE value 
of 1.7 under the same condition. We speculate that this may be due to the hydrogen trapping 
effect of Ru hotspots. The lower KIE value in NO3RR compared to HER for Ru-Cu2O also suggests 
favorable hydrogen transfer to nitrate rather than the recombination in hydrogen evolution. The 
KIE values of Ru-Cu2O in NO3RR maintain a steady value throughout the measurement range, 
whose preference on NO3RR explains the excellent Faradaic efficiency to ammonia compared to 
control catalysts. Further details on the electrochemical profile of various catalysts are provided 
in Figure S13 to S21. 
We also employed operando Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) to monitor the 
adsorption configuration of reactants and intermediates at different concentrations of nitrate. 
The setup for operando FTIR is shown in Figure S22. When operating in 0.1 M nitrate, only one 
peak assignable to the *NO species could be observed at ~1612 cm-1 for pure Cu2O (Figure S23a), 
whereas an additional signal from the *NH2OH species is seen at 1184 cm-1 for Ru-Cu2O in Figure 
23b33,36,37. The appearance of hydrogenated intermediates on Ru-Cu2O validates the promotion 
effect of Ru on the hydrogenation kinetics. At a lower concentration of 0.01 M, signals related to 
the *NO2, *NHO and *NH3 are detected in the FTIR spectra of Ru-Cu2O owing to the abundance 

of surface hydrogen (Figure 3f)38. On the other hand, control sample using Cu2O decorated Ru 
reveals a significantly decreased intensity for *NO and *H2O (Figure S24). This implies that HER 
dominates over NO3RR on the Ru-rich surface, which also reduces the faradaic efficiency for 
ammonia production. In conclusion, Ru clusters are highly effective in splitting water and 
capturing hydrogen while a delicate balance is necessary for pairing the HER and NO3RR kinetics 
(Figure 1). 
Mechanistic insights on the hydrogen acquisition and transfer. Ab-initio molecular dynamics 
(AIMD) simulation was performed at room temperature (300 K) to study how hydrogen is 
transferred to the *NO3 intermediates. The major argument lies in the function of Ru in reaction 
pathway: a true active site for nitrate as claimed in previous works18, or a promoter on hydrogen 
transfer kinetics. As the calculation results in Figure XX ~ XX show, Cu2O is a more favorable 
binding site for nitrate against Ru. This is supported by the poisoning effect at high nitrate 
concentrations and the negative influence in NO3RR on a Ru-rich surface. The visualized MD 
pathway in Figure 4a suggests that water or hydroxide molecules play an important role in H 
transfer. Proton couples strongly with nearby H2O and hydroxide molecules. Such effect increases 
the effective interacting area between hydrogen atoms and *NO3 intermediates in Helmholtz 
layer. When the *NO3 intermediate on Cu2O captures two H from H-bonding networks (i to iii), it 
will generate H2O and the *NO2 intermediate (iii to iv). The hydrogenation of *NO2 to *HNO2 is 
believed as the rate determining step, which is difficult to inspect in the following MD process. 
Meanwhile, the formed hydroxide due to nitrate reduction to nitrite captures another H from Ru 
clusters to regenerate the H2O for further hydrogen transfer (v to vi). Considering the abundance 
of water and hydroxide molecules in the electrolyte, the above processes occur extensively on 
the catalyst surface. Thus, hydrogen atoms can be readily transferred from Ru to *NO3 in 
Helmholtz layer, which is responsible for the promoted reaction rates.  
Balancing the kinetics of HER and NO3RR is important because excessive HER decreases the 
faradaic efficiency for ammonia production, yet if HER is suppressed too much, the rate of 
hydrogenation of nitrates will be negatively affected. At high concentration of nitrate, the surface 
sites for hydrogen adsorption become blocked, resulting in the suppression of surface-mediated 
hydrogenation process. To gain insight into how a co-catalytic system can address this problem, 

we calculate the adsorption energies of hydrogen on the Cu2O and Ru-Cu2O in the presence of 
nitrates. We calculated the adsorption energy barrier of an additional atomic hydrogen on Cu2O 
and Ru-Cu2O with various nitrate coverages ranging from 1/12 to 1/4 monolayer (Figure 4b). For 
pure Cu2O, there is no obvious variation in energy to adsorb another atomic hydrogen at a nitrate 
coverage of 1/12 or 1/6. However, a much higher adsorption energy (0.93 eV) appears when the 
nitrate coverage increases to 1/4 monolayer owing to their competitive adsorption. In contrast, 
the introduction of ruthenium hot-spots will reverse the trend in the adsorption energy. The H 
adsorption energy is -0.37 eV at 1/12 coverage, which decreases slightly to  -0.25 eV at 1/4 
coverage. This phenomenon further proves that ruthenium serves as reservoirs of surface 
hydrogen and promotes hydrogen transfer kinetics on the Cu2O surface, which facilitates high 
concentration nitrate reduction. 
Finally, we have conducted DFT calculations to identify the reaction pathway of NO3RR on the 
Cu2O and Ru-Cu2O catalysts in Figure 4c. Both catalysts share the same reaction pathway from 
*NO3 to *NO2, *NHO, *NH2OH and finally *NH3 as demonstrated by operando FTIR analysis. The 
formation of *HNO3 and*HNO2 intermediates are found to be rate-determining steps in NO3RR. 
Owing to the electron transfer from Ru, the free energy barrier is reduced by 0.07 and 0.14 eV in 
both rate-determining steps.  
Industrial-level nitrate reduction in a 16 cm2 flow electrolyzer. By optimizing the spatial 
distribution of Ru clusters on Cu2O to achieve the best performance, we demonstrated the 
operation of flow electrolyzer at an industrial current level of 10.0 A with an active electrode area 
of 16 cm2 39. The flow rate of electrolyte was set to 3 mL min-1. Monolithic Ru-Cu2O catalyst (16 
cm2) and dimensionally stable anode (DSA) were used as cathode and anode. As shown in Figure 
5a, full cell current gradually increases with increasing cell voltages and nitrate concentrations, 
affording a maximum current of 10.0 A at 4.2 V in 1 M KNO3. Further analysis on the Faradaic 
efficiency in Figure 5b reveals a nearly 100% Faradaic efficiency toward ammonia in such 
condition, corresponding to an ammonia yield rate of 46.7 mmol h-1 and a single pass conversion 
of 26% from nitrate into ammonia. At lower concentrations, the flow electrolyzer retains 
excellent Faradaic efficiency (73% at 0.32 A and 99% at 3.10 A for 0.01 and 0.1 M nitrate). The 
single pass conversion of flow electrolyzer greatly improves to 74% and 94% for the operation in 

0.01 M and 0.1 M nitrate (Figure 5e). This outperforms the existing flow electrolyzer performance 
for NO3RR in literature reports (Table S2).  
Process durability is another key criterion for practical ammonia electrosynthesis. The stability of 
Ru-Cu2O catalyst was examined in 1 M KNO3 at a full cell voltage of 3.8 V in Figure 5f. Dynamic 
flow rate testing from 1 to 5 mL min-1 was performed to exclude the influence of mass transfer 
limitation in our flow electrolyzer. After initial stabilization, a steady full cell current of ~7.50 A 
was obtained throughout the whole measurement (800 mins).  
Finally, we have also estimated the electricity consumption and production cost of our ammonia 
production based on the market price of electricity in Singapore (0.12 USD per kWh). As shown 
in Figure 5c, an electricity consumption of 164.5 kWh or 21.4 USD in equivalent is needed to 
produce 1 kg of ammonia using 0.01 M nitrate. The operation at higher concentration (1 M) 
significantly reduces the production cost to 6.87 USD (50 kWh) per kilogram of NH3 and affords 
a much wider operational window from 2.8 to 4.2 V. This is beneficial for actual operation in high-
concentration nitrate wastewater.  
Conclusion 
In summary, we have investigated NO3RR at high molar concentration of nitrate. By introducing 
ruthenium hot-spots on Cu2O catalyst to form a co-catalytic system, the reaction kinetics of 
NO3RR and HER can be paired well, which results in a substantially improved electrocatalytic 
performance. We demonstrated a NO3RR flow electrolyzer that operates at 10.0 A with a nearly 
100% Faradaic efficiency toward ammonia and a single pass conversion of 26% when using 1 M 
nitrate as electrolyte. Our study highlights the importance of pairing the reaction kinetics of 
NO3RR and HER in a co-catalytic system for productive electrosynthesis. 
Experimental Section 
Electrochemical deposition of Cu(OH)2 nanowires on Cu foam: Cu foam was rinsed by 1 M HCl 
solution and DI water. A two-electrode system was used, where the copper foam was used as 
the anode and platinum wire electrode was used as the cathode, and a current of 10 mA cm-2 
was applied for 20 mins without stirring in an electrolyte of 100 mL of 3 M NaOH solution. After 

deposition, the entire copper foam changed into blue color. The Cu(OH)2 nanowires deposited 
Cu foam was washed by DI water and dried at room temperature. 
Ru decorated Cu2O catalyst on Cu foam (Ru-Cu2O): The Cu(OH)2-grown Cu foam was immersed 
in 100 mL of 0.25 mM RuCl3 for 2 hours at 500 rpm stirring, and washed by DI water for three 
times before drying at room temperature. The sample was annealed in a gas mixture of hydrogen 
and argon (50:50 sccm) at 200 ℃ for 2 hours at a heating rate of 10 ℃ min-1 to obtain the Ru-
Cu2O catalyst. Unmodified Cu2O and other metal-modified (such as Pd) Cu2O catalysts were 
prepared by a similar method. 
Electrochemical nitrate reduction in a flow-cell setup: Measurements were conducted in a 16 
cm2 flow electrolyser separated by a anion exchange membrane (SELEMION AMVN) at room 
temperature using an Arbin BT-G workstation, which has a maximum support current of 10 A 
[Caution: This exceeds the safe current limit for human and may cause fatality if not handling 
properly]. The flow cell was assembled with two stainless-steel cover plates, two gold-coated 
copper plates as current collector, two monopolar graphite plates with parallel paths for 
electrolyte distribution. Flow rate was controlled by two Watson Marlow 120S peristaltic pumps, 
and the actual flow rate was determined by measuring cylinder. A dimensionally stable anode 
(DSA, RuIr/TiO2 on Ti-mesh, 4 × 4 cm2) and a catalyst modified Cu foam electrode (4 × 4 cm2) were 
used for a two-electrode system in an aqueous electrolyte containing 1 M KOH and 0.01/0.1/1 M 
KNO3. Chronoamperometry (CA) was performed at constant potential. The flow-out solution 
from the catholyte chamber was immersed into a glass vial containing 6 mL of 1 M HCl to acidify 
the ammonia solution. Stability test was conducted at various flow rates for a continuous 
operation of 800 minutes at a full cell voltage of 3.8 V. The amount of ammonia from catholyte 
were also determined by the indophenol blue and NMR method. 
Material characterization. The following equipment were used: STEM-HAADF (200 kV by FEI 
Titan Themis 60-300), EDS (200 kV by Super-X EDS system), TEM (200 kV by FEI Titan), SEM (JEOL 
JSM-6701F), XPS (Thermo Fisher Scientific K-Alpha+, monochromatic Al Ka), XRD (Bruker D8), NMR 
(Bruker Advanced 500 MHz), UV-Vis (Shimadzu UV-3600), ICP-OES (Perkin Elmer Avio 500, ppm 
level accuracy), Electrochemistry (Autolab PGSTAT30 & Arbin BT-G), in-situ FTIR (Bruker Vertex 

80). Ru K-edge XAS: 250 mg of 0.91 wt% Ru-Cu2O powder (without Cu foam) was finely ground 
using a mortar and pestle before being pressed into a 10 mm pellet. Measurements were carried 
out in the 7-BM beamline at the National Synchrotron Light Source II at Brookhaven National 
Laboratory in the fluorescence mode (Ru K-edge). Data analysis and simulation were carried out 
on Athena, Artemis, and Hephaestus (Version 0.9.23).
