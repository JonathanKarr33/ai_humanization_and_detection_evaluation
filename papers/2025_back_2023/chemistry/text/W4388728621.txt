Strategies for achieving asymmetric catalysis with azaarenes have traditionally fallen short of 
accomplishing remote stereocontrol, which would significantly enhance accessibility to distinct 
azaarenes with remote chiral centres. The primary obstacle to achieving superior 
enantioselectivity for remote stereocontrol has been the inherent rigidity of the azaarene ring 
structure. Here, we introduce an ene-reductase system capable of modulating the 
enantioselectivity of remote carbon-centred radicals on azaarenes through a mechanism of chiral 
hydrogen atom transfer. This photoenzymatic process effectively directs prochiral radical centres 
located more than six chemical bonds, or over 6Å, from the nitrogen atom in azaarenes, thereby 
enabling the production of a broad array of azaarenes possessing a remote γ-stereocenter. Results 
from our integrated computational and experimental investigations underscore that the hydrogen 
bonding and steric effects of key amino acid residues are important for achieving such high 
stereoselectivities. 
Introduction  
Remote stereocontrol, where a single functional group influences the stereochemical outcome of 
a reaction at a distant location, represents a formidable challenge in synthetic chemistry due to the 
decreasing control exerted by the catalyst's chiral environment with increasing distance to the reaction 
site1. Yet, the complex chiral structures of enzymes endow them with a natural ability to exercise 
remarkable control over stereochemical outcomes when forming remote chiral centers2-4. Azaarenes are 
nitrogen-containing aromatic heterocycles which are commonly found as functional groups in chiral 
bioactive molecules such as pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals, and natural products (Fig. 1a)5,6. Despite 
their utility, the planar rigidity of azaarenes prevents the nitrogen atom from acting as a flexible guide 
for remote stereocontrol7, unlike the more adaptable carbonyl group8,9. As a result, existing 
methodologies are unable to differentiate the prochiral face distant to a single azaarene group (Fig. 1b) 
7,10,11. 
The advent of photoenzymes has provided an exciting avenue to expand enzymatic catalysis to 
unexplored reaction domains12-20. These transformations, especially those involving ketoreductases and 
ene-reductases, have shown promise in controlling the enantioselectivity of radical prochiral centers in 
carbonyl group-containing substrates via enantioselective hydrogen atom transfer (HAT).21-33. However, 
as these reductases' native function involves catalyzing the hydrogenation of ketones or activated 
alkenes containing carbonyl, nitrile, and nitro groups, most new-to-nature reactions rely on the carbonyl 
group, thus constraining their applicability in asymmetric catalysis34. In this report, we demonstrate the 
successful utilization of a flavin mononucleotide (FMN)-dependent ene-reductase for enantioselective 
radical hydroalkylation reactions. The reactions feature α-methyl substituted vinyl arenes and 

2 
 
bromomethyl azaarenes, allowing us to achieve remote stereocontrol with azaarenes. Unlike 
conventional carbonyl-containing halogenated substrates, bromomethyl azaarenes establish similar 
hydrogen bond interactions with the ene-reductase. This facilitates effective substrate recognition and 
activation. Furthermore, the mutual π-π interactions favor charge-transfer (CT) excitation, making the 
azaarene structure amenable to electron reception from the enzyme, which then generates more stable 
benzyl radicals. These radicals can be more effectively seized by alkenes, thus aiding the formation of 
chiral products through an enzyme-catalyzed hydrogen atom transfer mechanism (Fig. 1c). Such 
chemical processes expand the application range of photoenzymes for remote chiral center synthesis by 
manipulating the long-range stereochemistry of azaarenes. This approach also enhances the versatility 
of asymmetric photoenzymatic catalysis concerning substrate functional groups, moving beyond the 
conventional reliance on carbonyl groups.  
Results and discussion 
Mechanistically, we propose that the FMN in ene-reductases is reduced by the glucose 
dehydrogenase (GDH)/nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADP+)/glucose (Glu) system, 
subsequently forming a CT complex with bromomethyl pyridine. Upon excitation by visible light, the 
aza-benzylic radical produced via dehalogenation is captured by α-methyl styrene, yielding a prochiral 
carbon-centered radical. This radical then undergoes enzyme-mediated enantioselective HAT to 
generate azaarenes bearing a γ-stereocenter (Fig. 1d). After examining various enzymes, visible light 
wavelengths, additives, and solvents in the reaction (Supplementary Methods and Supplementary 
Tables 1–6), the old yellow enzyme (OYE1) from baker’s yeast emerged as the optimal catalyst, 
selectively producing the desired product in high yield (80%) and excellent enantioselectivity (97%) 
(Fig. 1d). This approach is not only applicable to 2-bromomethyl pyridine but also to 4-bromomethyl 
pyridine (4-BMP) [up to 400 total turnover number (TTN), 95% yield, and 97% enantiomeric excess 
(ee)] (Extended Data Fig. 1), suggesting that the radical prochiral center, located further from the 
pyridine's nitrogen atom (> 6Å), can still be directed for enantioselective hydrogen atom transfer.  
Substrate scope for enantioselective hydrogen atom transfer 
To demonstrate the synthetic utility of the azaarene-directed remote stereocontrol, we investigated 
a broad range of azaarenes for hydroalkylation with α-methyl styrene under the optimal reaction 
conditions (Fig. 2a). The different substituted 2-bromomethyl pyridines underwent the hydroalkylation 
smoothly to afford the corresponding products (1 to 14) with high enantioselectivities (84 to 99% ee), 
though electron withdrawing substituted 2-bromomethyl pyridines needed more catalyst loading (2 
mol%) to give moderate yield (45 to 61%). 4-bromomethyl pyridines were also suitable substrates for 
the reaction and could give satisfactory outcomes with less catalyst loading (15, 0.5 mol%). 4-
Bromomethyl pyridines with different substituents generally exhibited high enantioselectivities, and the 
groups (-Me and -Cl) at the 2-position led to good and moderate yields (77 and 53%, respectively) and 
excellent enantioselectivities (97 and 93% ee, respectively) (16 and 17). The 3-substituted and the 
disubstituted 4-bromomethyl pyridines gave the desired products (18 to 20) with 91 to 99% ee. Other 
azaarenes including quinoline and pyrimidine also afforded the products (21 to 23) with satisfactory 
enantioselectivities (87 to 90% ee), though the 4-(bromomethyl) quinoline gave a lower yield (22, 33%).  
Next, we investigated the scope with respect to α-methyl vinyl arenes in the hydroalkylation of 4-
bromomethyl pyridines (Fig. 2b). α-Methyl styrene with different substituents afforded the desired 
products in good to high yields (60 to 96%) with excellent enantioselectivities (90 to 99% ee) (24 to 
32). Two vinyl arenes (thiophene and benzodioxol) were tolerated, giving high yields (72 and 92%, 

3 
 
respectively) and excellent enantioselectivities (98 and 99% ee, respectively) (33 and 34). Furthermore, 
2-chloro or 4-chloromethyl pyridines were also suitable substrates for the reaction and afforded the 
corresponding products (1 and 15) in high yields with excellent enantioselectivities (97% ee). It is 
important to emphasize that substrates containing substituents in the ortho-position of the bromomethyl 
group may experience steric hindrance, resulting in the generation of methyl azaarene side products due 
to hydrogen atom transfer to the intermediate aza-benylic radicals. This can significantly reduce the 
yield of the desired product.  
Mechanistic characterization through mutagenesis 
In our quest to shed light on the catalytic mechanism and the structures of the reaction 
intermediates, we utilized a harmonious blend of experimental and computational methodologies. It is 
intrinsic to the ene-reductase OYE1 to offer two hydrogen bonding sites for carbonyl-type substrates, 
namely H191 and N194 (Fig. 3a). Compared to the two lone pairs of electrons on the carbonyl oxygen, 
the pyridine nitrogen—with its single lone pair—may manifest distinct patterns of hydrogen bonding 
interactions with residues. To gain deeper insights into the pivotal roles played by residues H191 and 
N194, we engineered OYE1 H191X and N194X mutants via site-directed mutagenesis and evaluated 
their catalytic activity and enantioselectivity in hydroalkylation under standardized reaction conditions 
(Fig. 3b). While mutations at H191 show modest influence on reactivities—with H191A and H191G 
mutants demonstrating enhanced product yield and high enantioselectivities (99% ee)—mutations at 
N194 also have a noticeable effect on reactivity. Most N194 mutations lead to a slight, yet noticeable, 
reduction in both yield and enantioselectivity. These experimental observations suggest that N194 plays 
a discernible role in substrate interaction and influences reaction activity and selectivity to a certain 
extent, while the H191 residue appears less involved in forming a hydrogen bond with 4-BMP during 
the reaction. Further, the lowest energy conformation, ascertained through molecular docking, indicates 
that 4-BMP is predisposed to interact more with N194, forming an essential hydrogen bond that paves 
the way for substrate ingress into the reaction site for subsequent transformation (Fig. 3c). 
The genesis of chiral HAT was subsequently probed through deuterium labelling experiments. 
Within the Glu/GDH/NADP+ framework, glucose serves as a substrate for GDH, enabling the reduction 
of NADP+ to NADPH. This, in turn, reduces FMN to FMN hydroquinone (FMNhq) in the OYE1 
cofactor recycling system (Fig. 3d). The reaction was executed using in situ deuterated FMND, derived 
from D-glucose-1,2,3,4,5,6,6-d7, resulting in a product yield of 39% and a 89% deuterium labelling at 
the novel stereocenter. When deuterated glucose was amalgamated with an acetate buffer in H2O, a 
deuterium labelling ratio of 53% was retained. Conversely, the use of non-deuterated D-glucose in a 
deuterated buffer led to a deuteration rate (9% D) at the chiral center (see Supplementary Fig. 1 for 
details). This evidence points towards hydrogen atoms originating primarily from FMNhq, and the in 
situ generated FMNH(D) potentially exchanging hydrogen with the solution, thereby influencing the 
deuteration rate. We also took note of the proximal tyrosine residues, namely Y82, Y196, and Y375, 
due to their close proximity to the reaction site, raising the possibility of their involvement in 
transferring hydroxyl hydrogen atoms to the substrate and acting as a hydrogen atom shuttle. To clarify 
their roles, we mutated these residues to phenylalanine, eliminating their hydrogen-donating capabilities, 
and assessed the resultant mutants under optimal conditions (Fig. 3e). The findings from these 
experiments, including those from multi-site mutants, revealed that despite modifications, the mutants 
produced products with comparable enantioselectivities to the wild-type OYE1. Even the 
Y375F_Y196F_Y82F mutant, while yielding less product (56%), maintained a high enantiomeric 

4 
 
excess (92%). These results led us to conclude that these tyrosine residues do not primarily dictate the 
chiral HAT process. This supports our hypothesis that the FMN semiquinone35 (FMNsq) is likely 
responsible for the direct transfer of hydrogen atoms to the substrate, thereby controlling the 
enantioselectivity of the reaction. 
Quantum cluster model computation for mechanistic understanding 
To delve into the intricate nuances of the reaction mechanism, we performed a comprehensive 
computational exploration by a synergistic approach of molecular docking and cluster modeling36-38. 
Initially, potential conformations of 4-BMP in conjunction with the enzyme were scrutinized via 
AutoDock39, employing the X-ray crystallographic structure of OYE1 as a benchmark. Subsequently, 
amino acid residues in close proximity to the substrate within the primary shell were isolated for each 
conformation, and their terminal carbon atoms were fixated (for an exhaustive explanation, see 
Supplementary Methods). Utilizing the quantum chemical cluster model, the complex with the most 
favourable energy disposition was ascertained, whereby the extracted 13 amino acid residues played a 
pivotal role in the construction of an open pocket, functioning in tandem with FMNhq (Extended Data 
Fig. 2). This structural configuration paved the way for substrate accessibility to the reaction site 
through hydrogen-bond interactions involving residue N194 (Structures of alternative higher-energy 
complexes are provided in Supplementary Fig. 3). Following this, studies elucidating the mechanics of 
radical initiation were initiated. Time-dependent density-functional theory (TDDFT) calculations were 
conducted to shed light on the fundamental nature of the CT complex between FMNhq and 4-BMP. The 
calculations underscore that the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the CT complex 
predominantly resides in the π orbital of FMNhq (πFMNhq
 ), while the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital 
(LUMO) is nearly situated in the π* orbital of the substrate (π4-BMP,*) (Fig. 4a)40,41. The combined 
influence of HOMO and LUMO surpasses 90% in charge-transfer excitation, while the interfragment 
charge transfer (IFCT) method based on the Multiwfn program41 revealed that 0.94 electrons are 
transferred from FMNhq to 4-BMP during absorption. This CT excitation would generate FMNsq directly 
and cause the substrate’s antibonding orbital (π4-BMP,*) to accumulate electrons, thereby facilitating 
substrates to undergo debromination to produce aza-benzylic radicals following inter-system crossing 
(Fig. 4b). The spin density analysis on the IntA(triplet) illustrates that the diradical's two electrons are 
respectively delocalized on the azaaryl ring and the FMNsq plane, which bolsters the ensuing 
nucleophilic attack and hydrogen atom transfer process (Structures of alternative higher-energy IntA 
are provided in Supplementary Fig. 4). 
The genesis of enantioselectivity was subsequently probed computationally with diradical IntA 
serving as the initiating intermediate. Computational insights suggest that styrene in its unbound state 
tends to interact with the aza-benzylic radical, culminating in IntB, which then participates in radical 
nucleophilic attack via transition states TSNA, yielding intermediates IntC-triplet (Fig. 4c). As 
indicated by the deuterium substitution experiment and tyrosine mutation experiment displayed in Fig. 
3, the intermediate is poised to receive a hydrogen atom directly from FMNsq, culminating in the 
generation of the product. This step requires a transition in electron spin state from triplet to singlet. 
Accordingly, to pinpoint the most stable transition state structures, we executed computations on chiral 
HAT with singlets, spin-singlets, and triplets, resulting in 29 unique HAT transition state conformations 
(Supplementary Fig. 6 and Table 7). It was conclusively established that the system exhibits the lowest 
energy for HAT in the spin-singlet state, while the highest energy for HAT was observed in the triplet 
state. The spin state transition calculation illustrates the propensity of IntC-triplet to give rise to IntDS-

5 
 
triplet and IntDR-triplet via swift conformational tautomerism, which subsequently undergoes inter-
system crossing to afford precursors IntDS-spin and IntDR-spin for HAT, respectively. Electron spin 
density analysis elucidates the specifics of such a spin transition of the intermediate and transition states 
from triplet to spin singlet (Extended Data Figure 3). The most favourable two spin-singlet transition 
states, TSHATS-spin-I and TSHATR-spin-I, for HAT possess larger free energy barriers (ΔΔG = 21.2 
kcal/mol) relative to other steps, indicating that the HAT process governs both the rate-determining and 
enantioselectivity-determining steps. Aligning with experimental observations, the computed energy of 
transition state TSHATS-spin-I was 2.7 kcal/mol lower than that of TSHATR-spin-I (Fig. 4c), 
facilitating the favoured (S)-product of hydroalkylation (for the determination of absolute configuration, 
refer to Supplementary Fig. 7). 
The computational structures corresponding to the lowest-energy transition states for the primary 
and secondary enantiomers of the resultant product are detailed in Fig. 4d. These states exhibit 
substrates that are secured through a hydrogen bond between N194 and azaarene nitrogen, facilitating 
a swift hydrogen atom transfer from the N-H of FMNsq to the radical prochiral center, which results in 
product formation. To investigate the cause of significant enantioselectivity, we used the SambVca42 
online tool to create topographic steric maps, centered on the nitrogen atom of FMN. This approach 
yields a three-dimensional portrayal of the catalytic pocket, wherein zones accentuated in red depict 
areas of marked steric hindrance. Our findings revealed that quadrant III, influenced by Y375 and T37, 
exhibits the greatest steric hindrance, impacting how the substrate approaches the FMN plane during 
hydrogen bond formation with N194. This interaction guides the phenyl group towards the least 
hindered quadrant IV, while the methyl group is steered towards the more hindered quadrant III, 
producing the S-configuration product. Further exploration, involving mutagenesis of key residues in 
the catalytic pocket, suggested a correlation between decreasing steric hindrance at the Y375 site and a 
decrease in the reaction's enantioselectivity (Extended Data Figure 4, entries 1-6). Interestingly, the 
T37A mutant showed only a modest decrease in enantioselectivity, unlike other key residues substituted 
with alanine, which had no noticeable effect (Extended Data Figure 4, entries 7-14). The steric 
hindrance maps for Y375A and T37A mutants suggest that the mitigation of steric hindrance, 
particularly at Y375, results in a lesser disparity between quadrants III and IV's steric hindrances. This 
shift impacts the enzyme's discrimination capability between methyl and phenyl groups, thus driving a 
reduction in enantioselectivities.  
Conclusions  
In this study, we have demonstrated a novel approach to achieving remote stereocontrol with a 
single azaarene group, by utilizing the photoinduced promiscuity of flavin-dependent ene-reductases. 
Our results highlight the key roles of specific amino acid residues in determining enantioselectivity, 
emphasizing the unique capabilities of biocatalysts in controlling stereoselectivity at remote sites. We 
also present a viable strategy for incorporating an azaarene pharmacophore into 'ene'-containing 
compounds, opening up potential avenues for creating value-added products from biomass-derived 
alcohols. Importantly, our work addresses a long-standing challenge in asymmetric catalysis by offering 
a viable strategy for remote stereocontrol with a single functional group. By demonstrating this novel 
chemistry, we hope to expand the current boundaries of asymmetric catalysis and contribute to the 
development of more efficient and sustainable synthetic methodologies.

Typical procedure for enantioselective radical hydroalkylation reaction (large scale). The acetate 
buffer (120 mM, 13 mL), GDH-105 (2.0 mg), NADP+ solution (2.2 mg, 0.003 mmol), glucose solution 
(54 mg, 0.3 mmol), 2-bromomethyl pyridine solution (15.2 mg, 0.06 mmol), and α-methyl styrene (14.2 
mg, 0.12 mmol) were introduced into a 40 mL glass vial in an N2-filled glovebox. After OYE1 (1 mol%) 
was added into the vial, the reaction mixture was subsequently loaded on the following photocatalytic 
setup and illuminated with 40 W 445 nm blue LED at 25 ℃ for 12 h. Afterwards, 5 mL saturated 
Na2CO3 were added to the reaction mixture, which was extracted with 2 x 10 mL EtOAc. The organic 
layer was dried over MgSO4 and filtered to remove the drying agent. The residue was then purified by 
preparative TLC (ethyl acetate/hexane) to give the pure product with isolated yield.  
Typical procedure for enantioselective radical hydroalkylation reaction (small scale). The acetate 
buffer (120 mM, 800 µL), GDH-105 (~5U in 20 µL), NADP+ solution (10 mM, 20 µL), glucose solution 
(500 mM, 40 µL), 2-bromomethyl pyridine solution (200 mM, 40 µL), and α-methyl styrene DMSO 
solution (100 mM, 40 µL) were introduced into a 4 mL glass vial in an N2-filled glovebox. After OYE1 
(1 mol%) was added into the vial, the mixture was stirred at 25 °C. The reaction mixture was 
subsequently loaded on the following photoreactor and illuminated with 540 mW 445 nm blue LED at

25 °C for 3 h. Afterwards, dodecane and 1 mL saturated Na2CO3 were added to the reaction mixture, 
which was extracted with 2 x 1 mL EtOAc and loaded on GC-MS for characterization. The GC yield 
was calculated according to the calibration curve constructed with pure racemates of the products. 
Cloning, expression, purification and concentration determination of enzymes 
Cloning and expression. The OYE1 ene-reductases employed in this research were originally isolated 
from baker’s yeast. The plasmids pET30a-OYE1 with His-tag were gifts from Dr. Francesco G. Gatti’s 
lab. The plasmids were transformed into Escherichia coli BL21. For expression of OYE1, E. coli BL21 
colony harboring pET30a-OYE1 was inoculated in 5 mL LB containing 50 µg/mL kanamycin and 
grown overnight at 37 ºC. These overnight cultures were used to inoculate 600 mL of TB medium 
containing 50 µg/mL kanamycin, which were grown at 37 ºC until ODs of ~0.6 were reached. Protein 
expression was subsequently induced with the addition of 0.1 mM IPTG. The induced cultures were 
placed at 25 ºC, 250 rpm, for 16 h for protein production. 
Purification. Following expression, E. coli BL21 cells were collected by centrifugation (10,876 × g for 
15 min) and resuspended with NaPi buffer, pH = 7.4. For the purifications of protein, FMN-Na (1 
mg/mL) was added to the cell suspension. The mixture was then lysed by sonication and clarified by 
centrifugation (4 ºC, 24,470 × g for 60 min). The protein was subsequently purified by affinity 
chromatography using two 5 mL HisTrap HP columns fitted to an ÄKTAxpress FPLC system (GE 
Health Life Sciences, Pittsburgh, PA). The purified proteins were buffer-exchanged against 100 mM 
NaPi buffer, pH 7.4 using an Amicon Ultra concentration tube with 30 kDa cut-off. The proteins were 
stored in 20% glycerol at −80 ºC.

Cluster approach Setup. The initial structure was derived from the crystal structure (PDB code: 3tx9), 
which includes 2-(Hydroxymethyl)-cyclopent-2-enone. A cluster model was established by extracting 
residues Thr37, Met39, Tyr82, Gln114, Trp116, His191, Asn194, Tyr196, Arg243, Phe250, Pro295, 
Phe296, Tyr375, and the flavin mononucleotide (FMN) from the crystal structure. The phosphate 
portion of FMN was excluded and terminal atoms were fixed. In terms of amino acid residues, terminal 
carbons were fixed during geometry optimizations to restrict unrealistic residue movements. 
Subsequently, 2-(Hydroxymethyl)-cyclopent-2-enone was eliminated from the system and its binding 

14 
 
position was substituted with 4-bromomethyl pyridine. AutoDock Vina was employed to dock 4-
bromomethyl pyridine into the active site post ligand removal. We considered three possible bindings 
of 4-bromomethyl pyridine (to H191, N194, or both H191 and N194), with a total of six conformations 
(Supplementary Fig. 3). The deprotonation states of H191 were assigned based on pKa values obtained 
from the PROPKA44, complemented with meticulous visual inspection of local hydrogen-bonded 
networks. When α-methyl styrene was involved in subsequent reactions, the dissociated bromide was 
removed from the system to generate carbon radical intermediates (IntA) with six total conformations 
(Supplementary Fig. 4). For detailed calculation methods, please refer to Supplementary Methods.  
DFT method. DFT calculations were carried out using the Gaussian 16 program package, Revision 
C.02 software45. The B3LYP method46,47 and the dispersion energy corrections by D3 version of 
Grimme's dispersion with the original D3BJ damping function48 were used. The def2svp basis set49,50 
of Ahlrichs and coworkers for all the atoms has been selected for geometry optimizations and 
calculation of Gibbs energy corrections at 298 K temperature in the gas phase. Single-point energies 
were calculated at the M062x-D3 level51 of theory with def2tzvpp for all atoms with the value of the 
dielectric constant ε = 4. The SMD solvation model52 was used to model the effects of the rest of the 
enzyme that was not included in the model. Energies reported herein are thus those for the large basis 
set (which include dispersion) corrected for zero-point energies (ZPE) and solvation effects. Reaction 
paths were traced by the intrinsic reaction coordinate method for all transition states. 
TDDFT method. The initial structures of CT complex were first optimized at B3LYP-D3BJ/def2svp 
level. Then, the single-point TDDFT calculations at wB97XD/def2-TZVP level53 were performed to 
compute the five low-lying singlet excited states. All TDDFT calculations were performed with 
Gaussian 16, Revision C.02. The software Multiwfn 3.8 was used for wave function analysis. 
Topographic steric maps generation. Topographic steric maps were generated using the SambVca 
online tool (https://www.molnac.unisa.it/OMtools/sambvca2.1/index.html). The process began with the 
single crystal structure of the protein (PDB ID: 3tx9). After removing the ligands, amino acid residues 
within a 7 angstrom radius of the FMN were selected for input into the SambVca tool for steric 
topographic mapping. To set up the map, hydrogen atom was added to the nitrogen atom of the FMN. 
The N-N-H angle of the FMN was adjusted to 127 degrees to align with the angle of chiral hydrogen 
atom transfer as determined from DFT calculations. This setup established the N-H direction as the Z-
axis and the FMN plane as the XZ plane for the steric map. To accurately represent the steric effect 
within the active site, residues W116 and Y82 were removed from the model. Although these residues 
are in proximity to FMN, they are positioned above it and do not contribute to steric hindrance when 
the substrate is in close contact with FMN to acquire a hydrogen atom. The exclusion of these residues 
ensures that the map more accurately reflects the steric environment encountered by the substrate.
