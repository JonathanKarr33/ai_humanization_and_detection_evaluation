KEYWORDS: Zinc ion battery; Sacrificial Solvation Shell; Fluoride-Rich Interphase; Gradient Interphase; High Areal Ca-

ABSTRACT: Zinc metal batteries are strongly hindered by water corrosion since solvated zinc ions would bring the active water 
molecules to the electrode/electrolyte interface constantly. Herein, we report a sacrificial solvation shell to repel active water mole-
cules from the electrode/electrolyte interface and assist in forming a fluoride-rich, organic-inorganic gradient solid electrolyte inter-
face (SEI) layer. The simultaneously sacrificial of methanol and Zn(CF3SO3)2 would result in the gradient SEI layer with organic-
rich surface (CH2OC- and C5 product) and inorganic-rich (ZnF2) bottom, which can combine the merits of fast ion diffusion and high 
flexibility. As a result, the methanol additive enabled corrosion-free zinc stripping/plating in copper foils for 1000 cycles with an 
average Coulombic efficiency of 99.95%; a record high cumulative plating capacity of 10 Ah/g at 40 mA/cm2 in Zn/Zn symmetrical 
batteries and, more importantly, at an ultra-low N/P ratio of 2, the practical VO2//20-μm-thick-Zn plate full batteries with a high areal 
capacity of 4.7 mAh/cm2 stably operate for over 250 cycles, establishing their promising application for grid-scale energy storage 
devices. Furthermore, directly utilizing the 20-μm-thick-Zn for the commercial level areal capacity (4.7 mAh/cm2) full zinc battery 
in our work would simplify the manufacturing process and boost the development of the commercial zinc battery for stationary 
storage. 
 
 

 
INTRODUCTION 
Due to their low cost, high ionic conductivity, high safety, 
and environmental friendliness, aqueous zinc ion batteries 
(ZIBs) have been extensively studied in recent years, showing 
great potential to be applied as large-scale energy storage de-
vices.1 As anode electrode in ZIBs, zinc metal offers a high vol-
umetric capacity of 5855 mAh/cm3, more than 2.5 times the vol-
umetric capacity of lithium metal (2061 mAh/cm3). Coupling 
non-flammable aqueous electrolytes with zinc metal anode, 
ZIBs present fast kinetics and high safety.2, 3 However, ZIBs do 
not satisfy the market demands since zinc metal anode suffers 
from moderate irreversibility during repeated zinc stripping and 
plating process.4, 5 Corrosion of zinc at the interface, mainly due 
to the active water corrosion6 and its related low Coulombic ef-
ficiency (CE), are the main drawback to develop zinc anode, 
especially at high utilization. Moreover, the current research on 
zinc batteries is usually tested in an ideal state, which cannot 
represent the actual working conditions for stationary storage.7 
The balance of cathode and anode in terms of charge and high 
areal capacity of over 2 mAh/cm2 should be taken into account 
but largely ignored.  
Significant research efforts have been dedicated to enhanc-
ing zinc electrode reversibility. Numerous approaches have 
been reported, such as: introducing an electrolyte additive, de-
veloping zinc-based alloy, building solid-electrolyte interphase 
(SEI) film, and using water-in-salt electrolytes. Wang et al. de-
veloped highly concentrated aqueous electrolytes based on Zn 
and lithium salts.8 By decreasing the number of water molecules 
in electrolyte, the water-in-salt electrolyte can modulate the 
Zn2+-solvation-sheath structure, leading to significantly im-
proved Zn anode reversibility.8, 9 Therefore, tuning the zinc 
solvation structure would play an important role in adjusting the 
zinc plating behavior. To tune the zinc solvation structure, elec-
trolyte additive is a promising strategy for practical application 
due to its low cost and facile preparation process. A tremendous 
research effort has been dedicated to exploring effective addi-
tives. Various electrolyte additives like dimethyl sulfoxide10, 
ethylene glycol11, lignin12, diethyl ether13, and so on have also 
been studied to tune the zinc plating/stripping behavior. Re-
cently, in terms of broadening the working temperature, metha-
nol as an antisolvent additive in aqueous ZnSO4 electrolyte to 
realize wide working temperature ZIBs, and they believe that 
methanol would minimize water activity and weaken Zn2+ solv-
ation.14 They further took a step forward to utilize the triple-
function C3H7Na2O6P to form a self-healing SEI layer to realize 
the stable cycling of zinc battery15. Saccharin is introduced to 
regulate H2O-poor electrical double layer to create a corrosion-
resistant water layer for the zinc anode.16 More recently, a self-
adaptive electric double layer is created to enable high areal ca-
pacity (3.38 mA h cm−2) cycling of zinc metal battery coupled 
with a 40-μm-Zn plate 17. Moreover, an alkylammonium salt is 
doped as an additive in an aqueous electrolyte enabling the in-
situ formed solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) film, which has 
been demonstrated to increase the electrochemical stability and 
suppress the H2 evolution.18 More recently, methanol has been 
directly utilized as solvent for electrolyte with excellent zinc 
plating/stripping efficiency especially at low temperature of -40 
oC in the sacrifice of the high ionic conductivity compared to 
aqueous electrolyte19.  However, the zinc solvation in an aque-
ous-based electrolyte will inevitably lead to a direct Zn-H2O 
solvation structure and this will bring active water to the elec-
trode/electrolyte interface and in this way, the water corrosion 
in the zinc plate is regarded as unavoidable.20-21 Moreover, the 
design of the SEI layer remains to be unexplored. 
Herein, we demonstrate the first report on a sacrificial solv-
ation shell by using methanol as electrolyte additive to enable 
the formation of a fluoride-rich, organic-inorganic gradient 
solid electrolyte interface layer and repel the water molecules 
from the electrode/electrolyte interface for stable zinc plat-
ing/stripping. It is found that with the addition of methanol in 
the aqueous electrolyte, the solvation structure of zinc ions 
would become Zn(H2O)2(CH3OH)22+ to bring the methanol 
molecules rather than water to the electrode/electrolyte inter-
face. The unique sacrificial solvation shell accompanied with 
the decomposition of Zn(CF3SO3)2 promotes the formation of 
fluorinate-rich interphase as evidenced by depth profile X-ray 
photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and secondary ion mass 
spectrometry. In this way, the water corrosion onto the zinc an-
ode is mitigated and the hydrogen evolution rate is suppressed, 
and a stable interface is formed. As a result, the Zn//Cu asym-
metric batteries using Me56 electrolyte deliver an ultra-long cy-
cle life of 1000 cycles (2000 hours) with 99.95% coulombic ef-
ficiency, showing excellent reversibility of the Zn plating/strip-
ping process. Even at a high current density of 40 mA/cm2 with 
a capacity of 10 mAh/cm2, the Zn//Zn symmetric batteries can 
deliver a long cycle life of 1000 hours with steady and dendrite-
free zinc stripping/plating, corresponding to the ultrahigh cu-
mulative plated capacity (CPC) of 10 Ah/cm2. The positive role 
of methanol additive is further demonstrated in VO2//Zn full 
batteries, which deliver a high energy density of 210 Wh/kg at 
10 A/g for 3000 cycles and can be operated at various tempera-
tures from -30 to 25 oC. More importantly, at an N/P ratio of 2, 
the high-areal-capacity VO2//Zn full batteries (4.7 mAh/cm2) 
stably operate for over 100 cycles, demonstrating its potential 
application for grid scale energy storage devices.

Compared

Zn(CF3SO3)2 aqueous electrolyte, M56 electrolyte exhibits a 
similar X-ray absorption near-edge structure (XANES) spec-
trum, proving that Zn2+ ion shows similar valence state of zinc 
in the electrolyte (Figure 1e). However, the Fourier transfor-
mation of the extended X-ray absorption fine structure 
(EXAFS) spectrum of M56 electrolyte shows a broad peak at 
1.60 Å (Figure 1f), while aqueous electrolyte at 1.72 Å 
in R space, suggesting the obviously different Zn2+ coordina-
tion structure (Figure 1h). Additionally, the Zn-CF3SO3 coordi-
nation is very weak, suggesting that CF3SO3- doesn’t participate 
in the Zn2+ solvation. We further calculate the sequential bind-
ing energies of Zn-H2O in Zn(H2O)2(CH3OH)22+ and Zn(OH)62+ 
through DFT to elucidate the desolvation process. By analyzing 
the binding energy, it can be concluded that the 
Zn(H2O)2(CH3OH)22+ would initially lose the two water mole-
cules, while the CH3OH molecules bind strongly with Zn2+. 
Such a desolvation process is able to get rid of bringing the wa-
ter molecules to the electrolyte/electrode interface and in this 
way, active water-related decomposition in the electrolyte/elec-
trode interface is avoided. On contrast, the desolvation process 
of Zn(H2O)62+ would bring the active water molecule to the 
electrolyte/electrode interface, which would boost the active 
water-related decomposition in the electrolyte/electrode inter-
face (Figure 1g). Additionally, the desolvation energy of 
Zn(H2O)2(CH3OH)22+ is much lower than that of Zn(OH)62+, 
which is promising for removing the water shell from the solv-
ation shell and repelling water from the electrode/electrolyte in-
terface (Figure 1h).  
Wettability on Zn foil directly affects the energy barrier for 
Zn nucleation behaviors. Therefore, a sessile drop contact angle 
technique investigated the wettability of different electrolytes. 
In Figure 2a, with an aqueous electrolyte, Zn metal presents a 
high contact angle of 73.5 °. With the addition of 36 mol % 
methanol, the contact angle is decreased to 51.2 °. With the 
methanol fraction increased to 56 mol % and 100%, the contact 
angle is significantly decreased to 29.2° and 19.6 °, respec-
tively. These results indicate that methanol has high wettability 
on zinc metal surface. Methanol is believed to have a preference 
to absorb on the surface of zinc metal due to the higher absorp-
tion energy, which may significantly impact the Zn nucleation 
process and the water-induced H2 evolution.

Temperature coefficients (TC) were tested under isothermal 
conditions for the VO2/Zn coin cells with different electrolytes. 
Specifically, after cycling at 30 mA/g for 20 cycles, the batteries 
were charged to 50% state of the total capacity and then placed 
in an oven. Open circuit voltage (OCV) was tested and recorded 
for the batteries at different temperatures. As shown in Figure 
2b, all the batteries with different electrolytes present a de-
creased OCV with increased temperature, suggesting negative 
TCs. TCs of VO2/Zn full batteries are only related to the entropy 
change of electrolytes, which present different Zn-ion solvation 
and desolvation processes. TC results were obtained by fitting 
the OCV against temperature. As shown in Figure 2c, compared 
with the TCs value of the batteries with pure aqueous electrolyte 
(0.363 mV/K), the batteries with Me56 electrolyte and pure 
methanol electrolyte show the lower TC value of 0.237 mV/K 
and 0.242 mV/K. This is owing to the smaller entropy change 
correlated with the addition of methanol into the electrolyte, 
which is another supportive for the facile desolvation process 
of Zn(H2O)2(CH3OH)22+.22 Furthermore, linear sweep voltam-
metry (LSV) curves are further applied to investigate the corro-
sion potential of different electrolytes. As shown in Figure 2d, 
with Me56 electrolyte, Zn plate shows an increased corrosion 
potential of -0.88 V, which is much higher than that of pure 
aqueous electrolyte (-0.93 V), confirming the positive role of 
the methanol additive. Moreover, the oxygen evolution reac-
tions of water significantly confine the upper potential limits of 
the aqueous electrolyte. 
As the LSV curve of Zn/Ti half batteries shown in Figure 2e, 
the oxygen evolution is suppressed, and the electrochemical 
window is significantly expanded in comparison with that in 
aqueous electrolyte. The aqueous electrolyte exhibits an onset 
potential of 2.21 V for oxygen evolution. With the addition of 
methanol, the onset potential increases to 2.65 V in M56 elec-
trolyte. In addition, at the current density of 0.025 mA/cm2, the 
cathodic potential was further increased from 2.57 to 2.96 V, 
showing high stability at high potential. In addition, the poten-
tiostatic current-time transient curve is recorded to study the ef-
fects of methanol additive on the nucleation and growth of zinc 
dendrite. The hydrogen evolution rate is further quantified by 
in-situ gas chromatography (Figure 2f), and a constant current 
of 10 mA/cm2 is applied. The gas evolution in the aqueous elec-
trolyte shows an average hydrogen evolution rate of ~8 mmol 
cm-2 h-1, while in the Me56 electrolyte, the hydrogen evolution 
rate is greatly suppressed to ~0.2 mmol cm-2 h-1. The results ver-
ify the results from LSV curves, confirming that the corrosion 
of zinc anode is significantly suppressed. As shown in Figure 
S1, with a constant overpotential of 150 mV applied, rapidly 
increased current response can be observed in 20 seconds with 
a pure aqueous electrolyte.

To characterize the deposition behavior, the in situ optical 
microscopic is carried out to in-situ observe the zinc plating be-
havior at a constant discharge current of 1 mA cm−2 in Figure 
3a-3h. The zinc plate in the aqueous solution showed observa-
ble dendrite formation after 5-min deposition and large den-
drites after 15- and 20-min deposition. Additionally, a large 
bubble is observed, and it suggests severe gas evolution accom-
panied, indicating the corrosion of zinc plate, which is consen-
sus with the LSV curves in Figure 2d. On the contrast, the zinc 
plate in Me56 electrolyte shows minimum dendrite formation, 
and no bubbles are observed, suggesting uniform zinc deposi-
tion and prevention of active water corrosion of zinc plate. In 
order to better illustrate the uniform deposition of zinc plate, the 
SEM image of zinc plate after deposition at 1 mAh cm-2 in dif-
ferent electrolytes (aqueous electrolyte, Me36, Me56, and 
Me100 electrolyte) is shown in Figure 3i-3l. With the increasing 
amount of methanol, the surface of zinc plate becomes more 
and more smooth. In the aqueous electrolyte (Figure 3i), the 
surface is covered with plates which is vertical to the zinc plate. 
In contrast, in Me56 electrolyte, the surface is pretty smooth. 
Additionally, we notice that in Me56 electrolyte, the zinc plate 
becomes black, while no color change is observed in aqueous 
electrolyte, suggesting the possible solid electrolyte interface 
(SEI) formation in methanol-containing electrolyte. To probe 
the possible formation of SEI layer, X-ray photoelectron spec-
troscopy (XPS) is carried out to analyze the composition. In the 
C1s spectrum (Figure 4a), the surface is consisted of -CF3 
(288.7 eV), which originated from electrolyte residentials of an-
ion CH3SO3-, while with the increased time of Ar+ etching, the 
-CF3 and C=C peaks disappeared and the -CO3 peaks at 290.3 
eV increases, suggesting the formation of ZnCO3, which is be-
lieved to be beneficial for reversible zinc deposition.18, 23-24 This 
is also confirmed by the O 1s spectrum (Figure 4b), where car-
bonate and hydroxide peak become dominate. Additionally, the 
reduced peaks for both C-C and C- O suggest methanol decom-
position.

ZnF2-rich SEI layer, which is reported to be able to suppress 
dendrite formation from growing in the vertical direction due to 
the high interface energy and prevent the surface from active 
water corrosion and its related parasitic reaction. We proposed 
the ZnF2-rich SEI layer reaction route based on the pathway 
from dimethyl ether in the weak acid electrolyte.25 The triflate 
would be reduced through the breaking results above. 
Initially, methanol will undergo the dissociative of C–F 
bonds and generate -(SO3)F2C species, which reacts with dime-
thyl ether through highly exergonic polymerization reaction by 
forming large polyanion and ZnF2.25 The formation of ZnCO3 
is believed to be related to the dissolved CO2 in the electrolyte. 
This is also confirmed by the EDX mapping of the SEI layer, 
which contains F, S, C, and O in the surface (Figure 4e). The 
cross-sectional SEM image reveals the uniform SEI film with a 
thickness of 1.8 μm (Figure 4f). In summary, the SEI layer com-
prises polyanion species along with ZnCO3 and ZnF2, precipi-
tating on zinc electrode/electrolyte interface in situ, which is be-
lieved to provide physical protection against active water cor-
rosion26 (Figure 4g).

 The reversibility of zinc metal anode in the different elec-
trolytes was investigated in Zn/Cu half cells. As shown in Fig-
ures 5a and 5b, the addition of methanol slightly increases the 
overpotential of Zn stripping and plating on the copper foil sur-
face. This is attributed to the improved interfacial resistance and 
reduced ionic conductivity at room temperature. However, with 
Me56 electrolyte, the Zn//Cu batteries demonstrate a high aver-
age Coulombic Efficiency   (CE) up to 98% for 300 cycles at 
the current of 1 mA/cm2 in Figure 5c. By contrast, Zn plat-
ing/stripping in 1 M aqueous electrolyte presents a low CE of 
80%, while the battery fails at 57th cycle.

To further investigate the efficiency of the methanol addi-
tive, VO2/Zn full batteries were assembled with Me56 and 
aqueous electrolytes, respectively. As shown in Figure S10a, all 
the CV curves at different scan rates show two pairs of redox 
peaks, which are attributed to the insertion/extraction of zinc 

 
ions into/out of VO2 lattice. For both Me56 and aqueous elec-
trolytes, the peak position and the shape of CV curves have few 
differences, revealing the same electrochemical reactions with 
different electrolytes. Figure 6a shows the cycling performance 
at the current density of 1 A/g. After 100 cycles, the batteries 
with Me56 electrolyte deliver the specific capacity of 283.4 
mAh/g, maintaining 98% of the initial specific capacity (288.8 
mAh/g), exhibiting high capacity and favorable cycling stabil-
ity. The batteries with pure aqueous electrolyte provide a capac-
ity of 183.1 mAh/g after 100 cycles, retaining only 57.7% of the 
initial capacity (317 mAh/g). Figure 6b shows the rate perfor-
mance of batteries, and Figure 6c illustrates the corresponding 
galvanostatic charge-discharge curves. At the current densities 
of 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 A/g, batteries with Me56 electrolyte de-
liver the specific capacity of 311, 290, 256, 180, and 100 
mAh/g, respectively, showing much higher specific capacity 
than those batteries with aqueous electrolyte at each current 
density. Thus, to further quantify the resistance of SEI and 
charge transfer, the EIS spectra after 5 cycles in different elec-
trolytes are presented in Figure 6d and S9. To share in-depth 
insight in the kinetics of the SEI and charge transfer, tempera-
ture dependent experiment is carried out from 30 to 55 ℃. All 
the EIS spectra show two semicircles and one inclined curve. 
The first semicircle at high frequency represents the SEI related 
resistance while the second at rather lower frequency represents 
the charge transfer resistance (e. g. desolvation process, zinc ion 
transport in the electrolyte). All the semicircles in the M56 elec-
trolyte are smaller than those in the aqueous electrolyte. This 
suggests that despite the lower zinc ion conductivity in the M56 
electrolyte, the charge transfer resistance is still lower, which 
indicate that the desolvation process is the rating limiting step 
for charge transfer resistance rather than zinc ion conductivity. 
The reduced resistance is attributed to the promoted interfacial 
charge transfer kinetics with reorganized Zn2+ solvation sheath, 
enabling stable and reversible cycling and fast (de)intercalation 
of Zn2+ into cathodes. Furthermore, the temperature dependent 
EIS suggested that the SEI and charge transfer resistances fol-
lows temperature activated process. Thus, we are able to apply 
Arrhenius equation to calculate the activation energy of the SEI 
and charge transfer resistances. Both activation energy (7.2 
kJ/mol, and 11.2 kJ/mol) in M56 are substantially lower than 
their counterparts in aqueous electrolyte, further confirming the 
advantage of the methanol assisted fluoride-rich, organic-inor-
ganic gradient solid electrolyte interface layer as well as facile 
methanol incorporated desolvation process.  
Therefore, the ultralong cycling stability is achieved at the 
high current density of 10 A/g. As displayed in Figure 6e, with 
Me56 hybrid electrolyte, the VO2/Zn batteries deliver the high 
specific capacity of 152 mAh/g after 1500 cycles with almost 
100% Coulombic efficiency (CE), retaining about 90% of the 
highest capacity (180th cycle). However, with aqueous electro-
lyte, the capacity decays a lot in the first 100 cycles, and finally, 
the cell fails at about 200th cycle. The intercalation behavior of 
zinc ions in the cathode electrode is further studied. As illus-
trated in Figure S10, with the scan rate increased from 0.1 to 2.0 
mV/s, the anodic peaks move to a higher voltage and the ca-
thodic peaks shift to a lower voltage. This is owing to the in-
creased diffusion resistance at a higher scan rate. The electro-
chemical kinetics were analyzed via the power law formula in 
equation S1. As observed in Figure S10b, the b value from the 
slope of peak 1 and 2 are 0.899 and 0.785, implying that both 
diffusion and capacitive processes contribute to the capacity. 
The contribution ratios at different scan rates are further calcu-
lated based on equations S2 and S3. As displayed Figure S10b, 
at the scan rate of 0.1 mV/s, ~51% of the capacity is contributed 
by the capacitive process, suggesting that the electrochemical 
reactions are main affected by the capacitive process. In order 
to probe the high utilization of zinc anode, cycling performance 
with low N/P ratio of 2 were tested at a high areal capacity of 
4.7 mAh/cm2. The assembled cell with Me56 electrolyte shows 
excellent cycling stability for 250 cycles at 10 mA/cm2, main-
taining a Coulombic Efficiency of 100% (Figure 6g). The initial 
capacity increase is originated from the thick electrode and high 
current density.  In contrast, the capacity of the cell in aqueous 
electrolyte decays quickly in the initial 20 cycles, and the ca-
pacity becomes minimum afterward. The VO2/10-μm-thick Zn 
cell with an ultralow N/P ratio of 1 is also tested as a proof-of-
concept, and the cell is able to operate for 100 cycles with 1.1 
mAh/cm-2 retained (Figure S11). To investigate the practicabil-
ity of electrolyte additive, pouch cells were assembled with dif-
ferent electrolytes and tested their cycling performance with 
low N/P ratio of 2.5 at a high areal capacity of 2.2 mAh/cm2. 
The assembled pouch cells with Me56 electrolyte show excel-
lent cycling stability for over 100 cycles at 1 A/g, retaining 
about 82% of the initial capacity, and maintaining a Coulombic 
Efficiency of approaching 100% (Figure S13). In contrast, the 
capacity of pouch cells with aqueous electrolyte plummeted to 
a mere 155 mAh/g after 100 cycles. Notably, a rapid decline in 
capacity was observed as early as the 53rd cycle, indicating the 
depletion of the zinc anode. This finding further solidifies the 
exceptional reversibility of zinc in the presence of Me56 elec-
trolyte. 
CONCLUSION 
In summary, we have successfully demonstrated the concept 
of a sacrificial solvation shell to retard the active water 
corrosion and facilicate the fluoride-rich, organic-inorganic 
gradient SEI layer to enable highly reversible zinc metal 
battery. The methanol would promote the formation of 
Zn(H2O)2(CH3OH)22+ solvation structure. During the sacrificial 
desolvation process, two methanol molecules would bind 
strongly with zinc ions, leading to the Zn(CH3OH)22+ structure, 
preventing the solvated water molecules from the interface. The 
simultaneously sacrificial of methanol and Zn(CF3SO3)2 would 
result in the gradient SEI layer with organic-rich surface 
(CH2OC-,) and inorganic-rich bottom, which can combine the 
merits of strong fast diffusion and high flexibility. As a result, 
the sacrificial solvation shell enabled dendrite-free zinc 
stripping/plating in copper foil for 1000 cycles with coulombic 
efficiency of 99.95%; ultrahigh cumulative plating capacity of 
10 Ah/g at 40 mA/cm2 in Zn/Zn symmetrical battery. More 
importantly, at the low NP ratio of 2, the high-areal-capacity 
VO2//20-μm-Zn plate full cell with ultra-high areal capacity of 
4.7 mAh/cm2 shows stable cyclability for over a hundred cycles, 
establishing a viable option for practical application. Our work 
paves a significant step towards commercialized zinc metal 
batteries by direct utilizing a 20-μm-Zn plate to achieve 
commercial-level areal capacity, which would simplify the 
manufacturing process and boost the development of 
commercialized zinc batteries.
