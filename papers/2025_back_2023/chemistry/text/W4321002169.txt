ABSTRACT 
23 
The dual roles of minerals in inhibiting and prolonging extracellular enzyme activity 
24 
in soils and sediments are governed by enzyme adsorption to mineral surface. 
25 
Oxigenation of mineral-bound Fe(II) generates reactive oxygen species (ROS), yet it is 
26 
unknown whether and how this process alters the activity and functional lifespan of 
27 
extracellular enzymes. Here, the effect of mineral-bound Fe(II) oxidation on the 
28 
hydrolytic activity of a cellulose-degrading enzyme β-glucosidase (BG) was studied 
29 
using two pre-reduced Fe-bearing clay minerals (nontronite and montmorillonite) and 
30 
one pre-reduced iron oxide (magnetite) at pH 5 and 7. Under anoxic condition, BG 
31 
adsorption to mineral surface decreased its activity but prolonged its lifespan. Under 
32 
oxic condition, ROS was produced, with the amount of •OH, the most abundant ROS, 
33 
being positively correlated with the extent of structural Fe(II) oxidation in reduced 
34 
minerals. •OH decreased BG activity and shortened its lifespan via conformational 
35 
change and structural decomposition of BG. These results suggest that under oxic 
36 
condition the ROS-induced inhibitory role of Fe(II)-bearing minerals outweighed their 
37 
adsorption-induced protective role in controlling enzyme activity. These results 
38 
disclose a previously unknown mechanism of extracellular enzyme inactivation, which 
39 
have pivotal implications for predicting the active enzyme pool in redox-oscillating 
40 
environments. 
41 
Keywords: β-glucosidase activity; clay mineral; Fe(II) oxidation; magnetite; reactive 
42 
oxygen species;  
43

decreased β-glucosidase activity and shortened its lifespan.

INTRODUCTION 
47 
Extracellular enzymes, which catalyze the rate-limiting steps of carbon and 
48 
nutrient cycling, exhibit variable activity and stability across soil and sedimentary 
49 
ecosystems 1-5. Free enzymes are commonly short-lived and sensitive to environmental 
50 
perturbations 6, 7, including temperature 8, pH 9, soil organic matter (SOM) content 10, 
51 
moisture 11, substrate availability 12, and other stresses 13, 14, whereas enzymes adsorbed 
52 
onto mineral surface display repressed activity but longer functional lifespan. Mineral 
53 
surface was historically considered as unreactive sorbent of enzymes 15, 16, such that the 
54 
dual roles of minerals in inhibiting and prolonging enzyme activity were thought to 
55 
depend on the binding capacity of enzymes to mineral surface 17. Recent studies, 
56 
however, have shown that mineral surface with catalytic properties (e.g., manganese 
57 
oxides) can be effective agent of proteolysis 18, 19, and thus decrease the functional 
58 
lifespan of enzymes 17. Mineral surface can also catalyze chemical reactions, and thus 
59 
plays an important role in transformation of SOM 20, 21. However, the role of redox-
60 
active properties of minerals in regulating enzyme activity is poorly understood, thus 
61 
hindering efforts of incorporating extracellular enzymes into biogeochemical models. 
62 
Fe oxides and Fe-bearing phyllosilicates (interchangeably called clay minerals) 
63 
are common hosts of Fe in soil and sedimentary environments  22, 23, and can undergo 
64 
redox cycles under O2-fluctuating condition 24-26. Under such conditions, the Fe and C 
65 
redox cycles are often coupled across diverse environments including wetlands 27, rice 
66 
paddy soils 28, and groundwater table fluctuating zones 29. Upon oxygenation, structural 
67 
Fe(II) in minerals is an efficient generator of reactive oxygen species (ROS) (such 
68 
as ·OH, ·O2-, and H2O2) and/or high valent Fe [i.e., Fe(IV)], which can decompose SOM 
69 

5 
 
 
and affect nutrient cycling 21, 30-33. The generated reactive oxidants are considered 
70 
harmful to organisms and thus inhibit cell growth 34-36. As a defense mechanism, 
71 
microorganisms can eliminate these oxidants via intracellular metalloenzymes 37.  
72 
There are both positive and negative effects of ROS on enzyme activity. ROS 
73 
production from oxidation of dissolved Fe(II) has been shown to promote the activity 
74 
of phenol oxidase, an oxidative enzyme 27, 38, 39, via stimulating its substrate (i.e., 
75 
phenolics) decomposition or co-precipitation (with Fe oxides) 40. In contrast, 
76 
photochemically produced ROS from chromophoric dissolved organic matter can 
77 
oxidize amino acids within enzyme structure 41, thus decreasing the activity of 
78 
hydrolytic enzymes such as leucine aminopeptidase 42. However, it is currently 
79 
unknown whether reactive oxidants generated from mineral surface affect the activity 
80 
of extracellular enzymes. Generation of oxidants from mineral-bound Fe(II) is more 
81 
environmentally-relevant since this process is active over a large pH range across 
82 
diverse soil and sedimentary ecosystems (pH 3-9) 30, 31. 
83 
For this reason, a mechanistic understanding of enzyme activity and functional 
84 
lifespan upon oxidation of mineral-bound Fe(II) is highly desirable. We hypothesize 
85 
that reactive oxidants decrease the activity and shorten the functional lifespan of 
86 
extracellular enzymes; and the extent of these impacts depends on the amount of 
87 
oxidants generated from mineral surface. To test this hypothesis, we chose β-
88 
glucosidase (BG) as a model enzyme, a well-known soil enzyme that catalyzes the final 
89 
step in the breakdown of cellulose to glucose 9, 43-46. It is soluble, exhibits β-strand and 
90 
α-helix secondary structures, and unfolds upon adsorption to minerals. Fe-bearing 
91 
minerals (clay minerals and iron-oxide) were pre-reduced in preparation for subsequent 
92 
oxidation. The kinetics of Fe(II) oxidation and ROS generation, enzyme activity, and 
93 
potential conformational changes of BG were measured. The results are profound in 
94 

6 
 
 
understanding enzyme-mineral interactions, with important implications for 
95 
biogeochemical cycles of carbon and other elements in soil and sedimentary 
96 
environments.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
98 
Preparations of Enzyme, Chemicals, and Minerals 
99 
BG was used without any pretreatment (lyophilized powder, ≥2 units/mg, molecular 
100 
weight=135 kDa, Sigma-Aldrich). 4-Methylumbelliferyl β-D-glucopyranoside (MUB-
101 
linked substrate) and  4-Methylumbelliferon (MUB) (standard for the catalytic product 
102 
of MUB-linked substrate) were purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, USA. Clay minerals 
103 
and Fe oxides are ubiquitous in soils and sediments 18. Nontronite and montmorillonite 
104 
are smectites with different Fe content and are capable of generating different amounts 
105 
of oxidants upon oxygenation  31, 35. Furthermore, these minerals do not adsorb sodium 
106 
benzoate (SB), a ·OH quencher, which make it possible to quantify the role of ·OH in 
107 
enzyme activity 21. Fe-rich nontronite NAu-2 [21.2% total Fe, 98% of which is Fe(III)] 
108 
and Fe-poor montmorillonite SWy-2 [2.3% total Fe, 97% of which is Fe(III)] were 
109 
selected as model clay minerals. Magnetite (particle size >1 µm), which has >90% BG 
110 
adsorption capacity 17, was chosen as a model iron oxide. The details for sample 
111 
preparation and characterization were described in Supporting Information (SI) Section 
112 
S1 and Table S1. 
113 
To prepare reduced minerals, structural Fe(III) was reduced to Fe(II) by the citrate-
114 
bicarbonate-dithionite (CBD) method (SI Section 1). After chemical reduction, mineral 
115 
suspensions were centrifuged (13,000 g for 15 mins) and filtered through 0.22 μm nylon 
116 

7 
 
 
filters. Aqueous Fe(II) in the supernatant was negligible in reduced NAu-2 (hereafter 
117 
as rNAu-2) and SWy-2 (rSWy-2), and <5% in reduced magnetite (rMagnetite) (SI 
118 
Section S3). Aqueous Fe(II) was removed by multiple washes. X-ray powder diffraction 
119 
(XRD) results (SI Section S2) showed that chemical reduction did not change the 
120 
mineralogy (Figure S1). 
121 
After removal of aqueous Fe(II), the remaining solids were used for quantification 
122 
of structural and adsorbed Fe(II) by sequential extraction (SI Section S3). The 
123 
extraction results showed that 48.4% of Fe in rNAu-2 was Fe(II) [45.9% structural  + 
124 
2.5% adsorbed], and 65.1% of Fe in rSWy-2 was Fe(II) [63.0% structural + 2.1% 
125 
adsorbed]. While unreduced magnetite initially contained 26.8% Fe(II) (partially 
126 
oxidized 47), rMagnetite contained 31.1% structural + 0.8% adsorbed Fe(II). The 
127 
reduced mineral solids were resuspended in acetate buffer inside an anaerobic chamber 
128 
(98% N2 and 2% H2) to achieve pH 5 and 7 (Section S1). pH 5 was chosen because BG 
129 
is most active at this pH 48, while pH 7 was used for a comparison but is also typical in 
130 
many soils. Furthermore, Fe(II) oxidation and oxidant generation from reduced 
131 
minerals are pH-dependent 31, 35, which is useful to exam the effect of oxidants on 
132 
enzyme activity. In all subsequent experiments, pH was monitored and variation was 
133 
within ± 0.15. All prepared mineral suspensions were stored in serum bottles under N2 
134 
atmosphere.

Detection and Quantification of Oxidants from Fe(II) Oxidation 
136 
To initiate  Fe(II) oxidation experiments, an aliquot of stock mineral suspensions was 
137 

8 
 
 
transferred to a fresh serum bottle in acetate buffer to yield 1 g/L suspension with air as 
138 
the headspace gas. Throughout the Fe(II) oxidation period (24 h, the time when ROS 
139 
generation mostly stops 34, 35), change of Fe(II) speciation (including structural, 
140 
adsorbed and soluble forms, SI Section 3) was monitored.  
141 
To quantify •OH production, aliquots of the rNAu-2, rSWy-2, and rMagnetite stocks 
142 
were mixed with excess sodium benzoate (SB, 30 mM) and exposed to air for 24 hours 
143 
(SI Section S4). •OH generation by total Fe(II) and structural Fe(II) was independently 
144 
measured (Section SI S4). The oxidation products of SB, including 4-hydroxybenzoic 
145 
acid (4-HBA), 3-hydroxybenzoic acid (3-HBA), salicylic acid (2-HBA), and 
146 
dihydroxybenzoic acid (2,5-DHBA) were quantified with high-performance liquid 
147 
chromatography (HPLC) to calculate •OH generation (SI Section S4). 
148 
H2O2 concentration was measured using the N,N-diethylphenylenediamine (DPD) 
149 
method (SI Section S5). The generations of •OH and •O2− during oxygenation of 
150 
structural Fe(II) in rNAu-2 and rSWy-2 have been previously confirmed via electron 
151 
paramagnetic resonance (EPR) in our previous studies 34, 35.

Mössbauer Spectroscopic Analysis 
153 
To further study Fe speciation during the Fe redox cycle of the minerals, Mössbauer 
154

155 
promising tool for determination of Fe(IV) species 49, 50, another oxidant that may affect 
156 
enzyme activity as well. Three forms of NAu-2 and magnetite, including unreduced, 
157 
chemically (CBD)-reduced, and air re-oxidized samples, were analyzed at 30 K (for

9 
 
 
nontronite) and 125 K (for magnetite, slightly above Verwey transition temperature 51)

(SI Section S6).

Enzyme Adsorption and Activity Measurement 
161 
BG adsorption to mineral surface is the first step in its interaction with oxidants 
162 
generated from oxygenation of mineral-associated Fe(II). Thus, the amount of BG 
163 
adsorption onto both unreduced and reduced minerals was determined by mixing BG 
164 
(ranging from 0.02 to 2 mg/L) and mineral suspension (1 g/L) under separate oxic and 
165 
anoxic conditions. By using a constant mineral concentration but a range of BG 
166 
concentration, the range of the BG/mineral weight ratio (0.02 to 2×10-3) bracketed that 
167 
of extractable protein concentration in natural soils (1 to 4×10-3 g per g soil) 52. As a 
168 
control, free BG activity (without minerals) was also measured. One hour was sufficient 
169 
for BG adsorption to reach equilibrium 17, 53. The amount of BG adsorption (mg) was 
170 
quantified by subtracting the residual BG concentration in solution from the initial BG 
171 
concentration (SI Section S7). To study the desorption of previously adsorbed BG, the 
172 
supernatant in the equilibrated mineral-BG suspension was replaced with fresh acetate 
173 
buffer 54, 55, and any desorbed BG concentration was measured.

Impact of Fe(II) Oxidation on BG Activity 
193 
To initiate the Fe(II) oxidation experiment, BG was added to the pre-reduced 
194 
mineral bottles, and the headspace of N2 gas was replaced with air. Because the highest 
195 
Fe(II) oxidation rate is typically observed in the first 2 to 4 h 21, BG was pre-exposed 
196 
to air for 2 h, and BG activity was measured afterwards. Because Fe(II) oxidation and 
197 
oxidant generation should have continued from 2 to 4 hours, the time period needed for 
198 
enzyme activity measurement, both BG and substrate should have been exposed to the 
199 
oxidants. In this case, altered BG activity may be due to the impacts of oxidants on both 
200 
BG and the substrate. To eliminate this effect, BG activity was also measured after 24 
201 

11 
 
 
h pre-exposure to oxidants, because at this point oxidant production should have 
202 
stopped 21, 30, 31 and the substrate was no longer exposed to oxidants during BG activity 
203 
measurement. Thus, 24-h measurement would have reflected the impact of oxidants on 
204 
BG only, not on the substrate. Anaerobic experiments (i.e., N2 as the headspace gas; 
205 
degassed buffer as the working solution) were performed as controls. XRD was used to 
206 
study whether there was any intercalation of BG or any decomposed products into the 
207 
clay interlayer as a result of Fe(II) oxidation (SI Section S2). To separately assess if the 
208 
substrate and standard were decomposed by ROS, the substrate and standard 
209 
decomposition experiments were conducted (SI Section S9).  
210 
To further investigate the roles of different types of ROS in affecting enzyme 
211 
activity, BG activity was measured after SB (30 mM), TEMPO (2 mM), and catalase 
212 
(450 U/mL) were added to scavenge •OH, •O2-, and H2O2, respectively 35. Adsorption 
213 
experiments confirmed that SB and TEMPO did not adsorb to the minerals under the 
214 
experimental conditions (SI section S10). Furthermore, because of their excess amounts 
215 
and interaction probability between mineral-adsorbed scavengers and mineral-
216 
produced ROS, adsorption of scavengers to mineral surface was not expected to 
217 
significantly decrease their effectiveness. To eliminate any potential effects of 
218 
scavengers on enzyme activity, control experiments were conducted in the presence of 
219 
scavengers but without minerals.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
229 
Adsorption of β-glucosidase to Reduced Fe-bearing Minerals 
230 
The BG adsorption isotherms were dependent upon mineral types (Figure S3).  BG 
231 
adsorption to the two clays (>99% at both pH values) was higher than to the magnetite 
232 
(>90% and >95% at pH 5 and 7, respectively), likely due to the much higher SSA of 
233 
the clays (Table S1) 17, 59. There was no intercalation of BG into the interlayer space of 
234 
the clay minerals, as evidenced by lack of increase of d(001) spacing in XRD patterns 
235 
(Figure S4), consistent with previous observations that proteins prefer to adhere to clay 
236 
external surfaces when protein versus clay ratios are lower than 1: 4 54, 60, 61. 
237 
Furthermore, the zeta-potentials of clay minerals were more negative than that of 
238 
magnetite (Table S2). As a result, the calculated extent of opposite charge overlap 
239 
between BG (positively charged due to the isoelectric point of 7.3 44, 62) and clay 
240 
minerals (>90%) was higher than that between BG and magnetite (<76%) (Table S2). 
241 
Nonetheless, surface-induced structural changes of BG may result in conformational 
242 
entropy gains large enough to allow its adsorption to even “electrostatically hostile” 
243 
surfaces 15, 16, resulting in >90% adsorption of BG to magnetite. Other non-electrostatic 
244 
forces, such as ligand-metal binding between the carboxyl group of BG and hydroxyl 
245 

13 
 
 
group of Fe minerals may be important as well 18. All these strong binding forces may 
246 
have accounted for low levels of desorption in acetate buffer (<5%, Figure S5), in 
247 
concert with other studies 54, 63, 64. 
248 
The extent of substrate/standard adsorption was negligible under the experimental 
249 
condition (<4% in all cases, Figure S6), suggesting that the mobility of the substrate 
250 
was not limited. Therefore, any change in BG activity should be due to the interaction 
251 
between BG and mineral surface during adsorption and/or Fe(II) oxidation process, not 
252 
due to any decreased probability of encounter between adsorbed BG and the substrate. 
253 
Generation of ROS upon Fe(II) Oxidation in Reduced Minerals 
254 
Under anoxic condition, there was no oxidation of structural Fe(II) in all reduced 
255 
minerals at both pH values (Figure 1a, b). Under oxic condition, structural Fe(II) was 
256 
oxidized, i.e., Fe(II) concentration significantly decreased under oxic condition than 
257 
under anoxic condition (t-test, p<0.001, Table S3). The Fe(II)-rich rNAu-2 showed the 
258 
highest extent of total Fe(II) oxidation, followed by the Fe(II)-poor rSWy-2 and 
259 
rMagnetite (Figure 1a-b). Total and structural Fe(II) exhibited nearly identical oxidation 
260 
patterns (Figures 1a-b & S7a-b), because adsorbed and soluble Fe(II) concentrations 
261 
were negligible throughout the entire oxidation period (Figure S7c-f). Fe(II) oxidation 
262 
was confirmed by Mössbauer spectroscopic analysis (Figure S8), showing an increased 
263 
Fe(II)/total Fe ratios upon oxygenation of pre-reduced minerals.   
264 
The accumulative production pattern of •OH was similar to those of Fe(II) oxidation, 
265 
exhibiting the order: rNAu-2 > rSWy-2 > rMagnetite (Figure 1 c, d). Similar production 
266 

14 
 
 
patterns were observed for H2O2 but with fluctuations (Figure S9). Significant positive 
267 
correlations between the accumulative production of •OH and the extent of Fe(II) 
268 
oxidation illustrated that Fe(II) oxidation was responsible for the production of •OH 
269 
(Figure 1e, f). Although the extent of Fe(II) oxidation was higher at pH 7 than at pH 5 
270 
(Figure 1a, b), the •OH and H2O2 yields were actually higher at pH 5 (Figure 1c, d), 
271 
because ROS generation was favored under acidic condition 31, 34. 
272 
Owing to the low levels of mineral-adsorbed and soluble Fe(II) (Figure S7c-f), ROS 
273 
should be predominantly generated from oxygenation of structural Fe(II), as evidenced 
274 
by the same yield of •OH from structural Fe(II) and total Fe(II) (t-test, p>0.05) (Figure 
275 
S10). In addition to ROS, oxidation of Fe(II) may form Fe(IV), especially from soluble 
276 
Fe(II) 35. However, Mössbauer spectra of rNAu-2 and rMagnetite (Figure S8) did not 
277 
detect any characteristic Fe(IV) signals 49, 50, consistent with the low level of soluble 
278 
Fe(II) in all treatments (Figure S7e-f). 
279 
In terms of production efficiency of •OH [defined as •OH yield (μM) per mM Fe(II) 
280 
oxidized], rSWy-2 was the highest (the highest slope in the correlation results in Figure 
281 
1e-f). At a given mineral concentration, structural Fe(II) in Fe(II)-poor rSWy-2 should 
282 
be distributed more distantly from one another than that in Fe(II)-rich rNAu-2, hence 
283 
resulting in more scattered ROS production sites and a less self-quenching effect 31. 
284 
Furthermore, the generation of •OH depends on the mineral crystal chemistry and 
285 
density of •OH production sites such as the metal-oxygen bonds 31, 65, which may be 
286 
different between rSWy-2 and the other two minerals. 
287 

15 
 
 
Effect of Fe(II) Oxidation on β-glucosidase Activity 
288 
 In the absence of minerals, the activity of free BG decreased by 50% from pH 5 to 
289 
7 (optimal pH for BG is 4-6 48) and by 30% from 2 to 24 hours (t-test, p<0.05, Table 
290 
S4), but did not show difference under oxic and anoxic conditions (Figures 2).  
291 
In the presence of minerals, the enzyme activity has two dimensions of interest: a) 
292 
absolute decrease in enzyme activity (hereafter simply termed activity for brevity), and 
293 
b) time it takes for enzyme activity to equilibrate at a reduced level (functional lifespan) 
294 
17, 66.  Both dimensions were sensitive to redox condition and pH (Figures 2, S11 and 
295 
S12 for rNAu-2, rSWy-2, and rMagnetite, respectively). Under anoxic condition, the  
296 
decreased activity due to the presence of minerals (Figure 2) was ascribed to BG 
297 
adsorption onto mineral surface 17.  However, the mineral-adsorbed BG activity did not 
298 
decrease from 2 h to 24 h (p>0.05, Table S4), in contrast to free BG. This result 
299 
suggested that BG adsorption to mineral surface under anoxic condition decreased its 
300 
activity, but increased its functional lifespan. This protective role of reduced mineral 
301 
surface under anoxic condition was similar to the role of unreduced and redox-inert 
302 
minerals under oxic condition 17, 66.  
303 
Under oxic condition, ROS were produced from oxygenation of structural Fe(II) in 
304 
the reduced minerals. Due to their non-selective nature, these surface ROS would attack  
305 
the materials (e.g., enzyme and substrate) in the solid-liquid boundary layer and 
306 
adsorbed on the mineral surface 67, thus affecting enzyme activity 21, 34, 35. However, 
307 
ROS did not decompose the substrate (Figure S13). Therefore, under oxic condition the 
308 
substrate remained intact and BG was available to interact with it (i.e., BG adsorption 
309 

16 
 
 
on external surface, not intercalated into clay interlayers, Figure S4), any change in BG 
310 
activity should be attributed to alteration of the BG structure, as described below. 
311 
The oxidation of Fe(II) in the reduced minerals decreased the activity of mineral-
312 
adsorbed BG (Figures 2, S11 & S12). This effect was evidenced by the significant 
313 
difference in BG activity between anoxic and oxic conditions (solid vs. hollow symbols) 
314 
for all three minerals (p<0.05, Table S4), while especially true for rNAu-2 (Figure 2). 
315 
A longer exposure time to ROS decreased the BG activity to a greater extent, i.e., the 
316 
activity was significantly lower in 24-hour relative to that in 2-hour (Table S4), 
317 
suggesting that the lifespan of BG was shortened by ROS. These results challenge 
318 
previous understandings that minerals normally prolong enzyme lifespan 5, 17, 66. 
319 
The extent of BG activity inhibition by ROS was mineral-dependent. rNAu-2 
320 
exhibited the highest level of inhibition (up to 5-fold from comparing the activity under 
321 
anoxic and oxic conditions) (Figure 2b, d), in concert with its highest ROS generation 
322

did not inhibit enzyme activity (Figure S15). Consistent patterns were observed at both

343

344

345

346

347

348 
of rSWy-2 (Figure S16a), indicating the possible effect of •O2− on BG activity.  
349 
The effects of Fe(II)-induced ROS on enzyme activity have been documented in 
350 
natural environments but only indirect pathways are described. For instance, the activity 
351 
of BG is positively correlated with soluble or HCl-extractable Fe(II) concentration in 
352 
some soils and sediments 27, 69. This correlation is likely caused by a combined effect 
353 

18 
 
 
of ROS-induced degradation of phenolics 40 and its co-precipitation with short-range-
354 
ordered Fe oxides 70, thereby reducing the toxicity of phenolics to BG and increasing 
355 
its activity 71. Additionally, phenolics that have been regarded as toxic materials to BG 
356 
do not always accumulate in soils 72, thus the factors manipulating BG activity may not 
357 
be limited to this indirect pathway. In the current study, the predominant mechanism of 
358 
BG inactivation was via attack by ROS produced from oxidation of structural Fe(II) in 
359 
minerals, uncovering a direct pathway for enzyme inactivation. These Fe-bearing 
360 
minerals, particularly clays, can be cycled multiple times without losing their capacity 
361 
for ROS generation 30. Thus, redox-active Fe-bearing minerals likely play a significant 
362 
role in regulating enzyme activity and functional lifespan. 
363 
Conformational and Structural Changes of BG upon Fe(II) Oxidation 
364 
Unlike small and rigid molecules or ions, enzymes undergo irreversible 
365 
conformational change upon adsorption onto mineral surface 17, which affects enzyme 
366 
activity. ATR-FTIR spectra of BG adsorbed on minerals comprised the characteristic 
367 
bands of Amide I, Amide II, Amide III, and side-chain δ CH2 and υs COO- 73 (Figure 
368 
4a). Amongst these characteristic peaks, Amide I band (1700-1600 cm−1), mainly the 
369 
result of carbonyl stretching vibrations of the C=O bond of amide, is commonly 
370 
considered the optimal predictor for quantitating the secondary structures of proteins 9, 
371 
55, 64, 73. Hence, it was deconvoluted into α-helices, β-sheets, β-turns, and random coils 
372 
73 (Figure 4b). In comparison to free BG (“Control” in Figure 4c), the relative 
373 
proportions of these four secondary structures of Amide I varied after BG adsorption to 
374 

19 
 
 
the unreduced minerals (Figure 4c). Such conformational change was previously 
375 
documented to be a function of the surface area-normalized amount of adsorption 17. In 
376 
comparison to the free BG and unreduced minerals, the significantly decreased α-helix 
377 
proportion for adsorbed BG to rNAu-2 and rMagnetite upon Fe(II) oxidation suggested 
378 
BG unfolding, which may be a cause for declined activity 15, 17. Considering that 
379 
multiple mineral surface properties, including electric properties (Table S2) and surface 
380 
area 35 all changed after reduction, this type of BG unfolding upon Fe(II) oxidation 
381 
could be attributed to a combined effect of altered surface property during chemical 
382 
reduction and ROS attack.  
383 
Relative to the reduced minerals without scavenger, addition of SB significantly 
384 
increased the proportion of α-helix for all three minerals (p<0.05), hinting at a refolding 
385 
of BG due to quenching of •OH by SB 55, 64. The proportion of α-helix increased more 
386 
after scavenging of •OH than after scavenging of H2O2 and O2-, suggesting that •OH 
387 
was likely the main ROS in causing unfolding of the BG molecule. The protein-mineral 
388 
interaction would result in a loss in the helical structure (α-helix) and increase in 
389 
random coils and β-sheets, while the intensity of such interaction force was tightly 
390 
related to particle size, surface area, hydrophobicity, and polarity of a mineral surface 
391 
15, 17, 64. Our study suggests that the redox activity of mineral surface can also induce 
392 
potential enzyme conformational changes. 
393 
XPS may be able to probe the variations of aliphatic and carbonyl carbons within the 
394 
mineral-adsorbed BG molecule 9. Relative to BG adsorbed to the unreduced mineral 
395 
surfaces, BG molecule adsorbed to rNAu-2 and rSWy-2 surfaces upon Fe(II) oxidation 
396 

20 
 
 
displayed lower proportions of C-N/C-O and C=O (Figure S17), and rMagnetite 
397 
displayed decreased C-C/C-H and C=O proportions. These functional groups were 
398 
likely derived from the characteristic amide groups of the BG molecule 9. Relative to 
399 
the reduced minerals, addition of SB (•OH scavenger) enhanced the C-N/C-O fraction 
400 
for all three reduced minerals and C=O fraction for rMagnetite, again underpinning the 
401 
role of •OH in the BG molecular structure. These results evinced the selective attack of 
402 
ROS on the BG molecular structure, yet this attack might build upon the initial 
403 
conformational change of BG adsorbed to mineral surfaces (involves a whole cascade 
404 
of angular rotation or structural reorganization 16). The effect of ROS generated via 
405 
mineral-bound Fe(II) oxidation on BG decomposition is analogous to its role in 
406 
promoting SOM decomposition 21, 28, 39, 74, ultimately resulting in enzyme inactivation.

ENVIRONMENTAL IMPLICATIONS 
408 
Minerals as adsorbents have been demonstrated to play dual roles in inhibiting and 
409 
protecting extracellular enzyme activity: (a) decrease in enzyme activity and (b) 
410 
prolonged functional lifespan 17, 66.  Because most Fe(II) in soils and sediments occurs 
411 
as mineral-bound form 75, 76 and Fe-bearing minerals can undergo multiple Fe redox 
412 
cycles in soil and sedimentary environments 22, 25, 77, our results disclose a previously 
413 
unknown mechanism of enzyme inactivation. Extracellular enzymes first bind to 
414 
mineral surface, initially resulting in a decreased activity. Subsequently, the roles of 
415 
minerals in altering the enzyme functional lifespan depend on three circumstances: (1) 
416 
If the mineral surface is redox inert, relative to free enzyme, the functional lifespan of 
417 

21 
 
 
mineral-adsorbed enzyme is prolonged; (2) If the mineral surface is redox-active, the 
418 
enzyme functional lifespan is still prolonged under anoxic condition; (3) If Fe(II)-rich 
419 
minerals are exposed to air under oscillating redox condition, ROS-induced BG 
420 
decomposition further inactivates enzyme activity and shortens enzyme lifespan. Under 
421 
the latter two circumstances, the interactions between Fe minerals and enzymes may be 
422 
long-lasting  over multiple Fe redox cycles.  
423 
Mineral-enzyme interactions typically occur at a microscopic scale but the effect is 
424 
often profound at the global scale. The association of SOM with minerals has been 
425 
proposed or proven to protect SOM from degradation via physical-chemical protection 
426 
mechanisms 18, 78, however, there is the other side of the coin, i.e., a significant amount 
427 
of SOM decomposition may occur in redox-active environments due to ROS attack 
428 
upon oxygenation of structural Fe(II) in minerals 21, 33. Given the relevance of 
429 
extracellular enzymes to SOM decomposition and biogeochemical cycles of other 
430 
elements, our results demonstrate that Fe-bearing minerals can significantly decrease 
431 
enzyme activity, and thus offer additional protection to SOM. In this light, our study 
432 
suggests that it is pivotal to consider the inhibitory effect of the redox-active minerals 
433 
on SOM-degrading extracellular enzyme activities, which would, in turn, affect SOM 
434 
turnover at a global scale. Therefore, the ultimate fate of SOM depends on a fine 
435 
balance between protection (through physical/chemical protection of SOM and 
436 
inhibition of extracellular enzyme activities) and destruction by ROS 21, 39.
