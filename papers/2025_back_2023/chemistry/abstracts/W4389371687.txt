Rational design and regulation of atomically precise photocatalysts are essential for constructing efficient photocatalytic systems tunable at both the atomic and molecular levels. Herein, we propose a platform-based strategy capable of integrating both pore space partition (PSP) and open-metal sites (OMSs) as foundational features for constructing high-performance photocatalysts. We demonstrate the first structural prototype obtained from this strategy: pore-partitioned NiTCPE-<i>pstp</i> (TCPE = 1,1,2,2-tetra(4-carboxylphenyl)ethylene, <i>pstp</i> = partitioned <i>stp</i> topology). Nonpartitioned NiTCPE-<i>stp</i> is constructed from six-connected [Ni<sub>3</sub>(μ<sub>3</sub>-OH)(COO)<sub>6</sub>] trimer and TCPE linker to form 1D hexagonal channels with six coplanar OMSs directed at channel centers. After introducing triangular pore-partitioning ligands, half of the OMSs were retained, while the other half were used for PSP, leading to unprecedented microenvironment regulation of the pore structure. The resulting material integrates multiple advanced properties, including robustness, wider absorption range, enhanced electronic conductivity, and high CO<sub>2</sub> adsorption, all of which are highly desirable for photocatalytic applications. Remarkably, NiTCPE-<i>pstp</i> exhibits excellent CO<sub>2</sub> photoreduction activity with a high CO generation rate of 3353.6 μmol g<sup>-1</sup> h<sup>-1</sup> and nearly 100% selectivity. Theoretical and experimental studies show that the introduction of partitioning ligands not only optimizes the electronic structure to promote the separation and transfer of photogenerated carriers but also reduces the energy barrier for the formation of *COOH intermediates while promoting CO<sub>2</sub> activation and CO desorption. This work is believed to be the first example to integrate PSP strategies and OMSs within metal-organic framework (MOF) photocatalysts, which provides new insight as well as new structural prototype for the design and performance optimization of MOF-based photocatalysts.
