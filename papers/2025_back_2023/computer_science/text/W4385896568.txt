Analog in-memory computing (AIMC) with resistive memory devices promises to reduce the latency and energy con-
sumption of deep neural network inference tasks by performing computations directly within memory. However, to
achieve end-to-end improvements, AIMC must be combined with on-chip digital operations and communication to
realize a full inference workload entirely on-chip. Here, we present a multi-core AIMC chip designed and fabricated in
14-nm complementary metal–oxide–semiconductor (CMOS) technology with backend-integrated phase-change mem-
ory (PCM). The fully-integrated chip features 64 256x256 AIMC cores interconnected via an on-chip communication
network. It also implements the digital activation functions and processing involved in ResNet convolutional neural
networks and long short-term memory (LSTM) networks. We demonstrate near software-equivalent inference accu-
racy with ResNet and LSTM networks while implementing all the computations associated with the weight layers and
the activation functions on-chip. The chip can achieve a maximal throughput of 63.1 TOPS at an energy efﬁciency of
9.76 TOPS W−1 for 8-bit input/output matrix-vector multiplications.
The need to repeatedly shuttle around synaptic weight values from memory to processing units has been a key source of energy
15
inefﬁciency associated with hardware implementation of artiﬁcial neural networks. Analog in-memory computing (AIMC) with
16
spatially instantiated synaptic weights is arguably the most promising approach to overcome this challenge. Early works on per-
17
forming neural network inference with AIMC showed promising accuracy results in mixed hardware/software implementations,
18
where functionalities such as digital-to-analog and analog-to-digital conversions, activation functions, and other necessary digital
19
operations were implemented with off-chip software or hardware1,2. Since then, various single- or few-core chips using SRAM3,
20
Flash4, RRAM5,6, PCM7, and MRAM8 memory technologies have been fabricated with fully-integrated data conversions and
21
sometimes activation functions4,5,7, demonstrating a competitive energy efﬁciency of ≥10 TOPS W−1 for reduced-precision
22
matrix-vector multiplication (MVM). In such works, only small networks (≲100k weights) on simple benchmark tasks such
23
as MNIST digit recognition have been implemented to ﬁt entirely on the core4–7, whereas larger networks have been emulated
24
by programming each layer one at a time onto the same core3,8. Recently, multi-core chips supporting larger networks (> 1M
25
weights) have demonstrated promising inference accuracy results on more difﬁcult benchmarks, such as image recognition on
26
the CIFAR dataset, and high MVM energy efﬁciency9–14.
27
Despite those advances, signiﬁcant challenges remain to build a complete AIMC accelerator to demonstrate end-to-end in-
28
ference tasks with accuracy and efﬁciency that outperform the already existing digital accelerators. Because of the rather large
29
size of the multi-bit SRAM plus switched-capacitor unit-cells, the existing SRAM-based implementations need off-chip weight
30
buffers to hold network weights, which are then partially transferred to the AIMC cores because an entire network cannot ﬁt
31
fully on-chip13. The current implementations are not dense enough to hold > 10MB of weight data at reasonable chip area,
32
limiting their efﬁciency gains over digital approaches to rather small networks that ﬁt entirely on-chip15. Another limitation is
33
the volatility of the SRAM cells, which prevent the on-chip weights to be retained throughout power cycling of the accelerator.
34
The potential to achieve smaller unit-cell sizes with resistive memory technologies could make it possible to hold such large
35
networks entirely on-chip in a non-volatile way and eliminate the overhead of off-chip weight data access. In this context, an
36
AIMC accelerator based on such devices becomes a natural choice. Both digital9,11 (1 device corresponds to 1 weight bit) and
37
analog (1 device encodes an entire weight)10 storage of weights in those devices have been explored in the recent AIMC chips.
38
Digital storage offers high (almost digital-like) accuracy, but the throughput is limited due to small number of rows that can
39
be operated at a time (sometimes as low as 4)11 without degrading the signal quality. This approach also limits the achievable
40

weight density because several devices are needed to encode a weight. Analog weight storage offers high weight density and
41
a larger number of rows (up to 256)10 have been possible to operate simultaneously with this approach. However, it suffers
42
from accuracy degradation because of the noisy analog weights and higher latency due to the need of slow high resolution
43
analog-to-digital converters (ADCs) that often have to be multiplexed across columns. Moreover, extensive network-speciﬁc
44
chip tuning, such as per-network recalibration of the chip to mitigate non-idealities of the devices and peripheral circuitry,
45
and chip-in-the-loop ﬁnetuning of weights through backpropagation, has so far been necessary to achieve acceptable inference
46
accuracy with this approach1,10. Finally, at the system level, none of the current AIMC chips based on resistive memory support
47
all the computations involved in processing convolutional and LSTM layers.
48
To address those challenges, we present Hermes: a 64-core AIMC chip based on back-end integrated PCM in a 14-nm CMOS
49
process. The chip delivers simultaneously four key advances over the previous implementations discussed above. (i) High weight
50
capacity is enabled through the analog storage capability of PCM, using only 4 PCM devices to encode a weight. (ii) Low MVM
51
latency, high parallelism, and high throughput are achieved by using compact current-controlled oscillator ADCs placed on each
52
unit-cell row to perform fully-parallel MVMs. (iii) High MVM and inference accuracy are achieved without application-wise
53
chip re-tuning, thanks to proper on-chip ADC calibration circuits that remove unwanted nonlinear response. (iv) Finally, all
54
non-MVM computations involved in processing convolutional and LSTM layers are supported using dedicated high-precision
55
digital units. We report measured inference results at near software-equivalent accuracy on CIFAR-10 image recognition, Penn
56
Treebank (PTB) character prediction, and image caption generation tasks with all convolutional and LSTM layer computations
57
performed on-chip. The measured MVM throughput per area of the chip is > 15× higher than comparable multi-core resistive
58
memory AIMC chips, while achieving the highest accuracy on the CIFAR-10 benchmark.

I.
CHIP ARCHITECTURE
60
Fig. 1a shows a snapshot of the chip inside the electronic design automation (EDA) tool. The chip has a square dimension
61
of 12mm × 12mm. Its 64 cores, each of size 1.2mm × 1.16mm, are placed in eight rows and eight columns. This subdivided
62
structure is detailed in the schematic illustration in Fig. 1b. Each core contains a PCM crossbar array capable of storing a
63
256×256 weight matrix and performing an analog MVM using the input activations provided from outside of the core. Hence,
64
up to 4,194,304 weights can be stored on-chip for performing in-memory MVMs.
65
At the center of the core grid, between rows four and ﬁve, a row of eight global digital processing units (GDPUs) is placed.
66
They provide the additional digital post-processing capabilities needed when running LSTM networks. The high-frequency
67
clock signals for the digital circuitry in the cores and GDPUs are received at the center of the chip and are distributed in a
68
balanced tree across the chip.
69
Finally, the core outputs and GDPU inputs are interconnected by a grid of horizontal and vertical digital communication links
70
(see Fig. 1b), yielding a DNN acceleration architecture where weight elements are stationary, and only the activation vectors are
71
transmitted. There are 418 physical communication links in total, which together implement a 5 Parallel Prism communication
72
fabric topology16 (see Extended Data Fig. 1 for a map of all possible link connections). A summary of the chip speciﬁcations
73
can be found in Extended Table I.

II.
THE COMPUTATIONAL MEMORY CORE
75
Fig. 1c depicts the architecture of a single Hermes core and its various components. The design is based on the fully-integrated
76
standalone PCM-based single-core chip presented in Ref. 7, with the addition of the link controller for sending and transmitting
77
of input activations between different cores. At the center of the core, there is a 256 × 256-sized crossbar array of PCM-based
78
unit-cells. As shown in Ext. Data Fig. 2a, four PCM devices are used per unit-cell: two each for each polarity, such that positive
79
and negative weights can be represented17,18. The devices in a conductance pair are weighted with equal signiﬁcance.
80
To program the individual conductances, a diagonal decoding scheme18 is employed to select 256 different devices, one per
81
row and column. The whole procedure starts with the dedicated per-core programming ﬁnite-state machine (FSM) instructing
82
the diagonal selection decoder to enable one diagonal of cells that contains the devices that are to be programmed (see Ext. Data
83
Fig. 2b). Subsequently, these selected devices can be programmed by the current-steering digital-to-analog converter (DAC)
84
based programming units located on top of the PCM array. The DAC units are capable of producing all required programming
85
pulse shapes for the PCM devices based on the instruction provided by the FSM. This includes high current RESET pulses,
86
triangular-shaped SET pulses, and programming pulses with variable current amplitude and time duration.
87
Once all programming operations are concluded, the core can be used to perform analog MVMs. To this end, the input
88
modulator applies pulse-width modulated (PWM) read voltage pulses to the PCM array, which are digitized by an array of 256
89
time-based current-ADCs. These ADCs ﬂank the PCM-array from the left and right side, such that all unit-cell rows are covered
90
by a dedicated ADC. This is achieved by matching the ADC height to that of two unit-cells, which results in a skewed aspect
91
ratio, as is shown in the layout in Ext. Data Fig. 2c. By choosing a current-based ADC topology, area-intensive and noise-prone
92

3
current-to-voltage conversion steps can be avoided. Furthermore, by opting for a time-based architecture, a highly digital design
93
is obtained in which the output precision can be adjusted by changing the conversion time.
94
To counteract manufacturing mismatch and other non-idealities, the ADC includes an internal resistor ladder-based voltage
95
DAC that allows correcting the read-voltage offset. Furthermore, the internal current mirror, which drives the attached current-
96
controlled oscillator (CCO) unit, contains trimming registers that allow matching the gain between the different ADCs per core.
97
Thus, after performing an initial calibration procedure, the conﬁguration parameters are stored on-chip such that the ADCs
98
exhibit tightly distributed transfer curves. Note that this calibration is independent of the deployed application and does not have
99
to be redone for different weight sets or networks.
100
Ext. Data Fig. 3a illustrates one row of the computational memory during an analog MVM. The per-row ADC regulates the
101
BL to a ﬁxed common-mode voltage, such that the top electrodes of all PCM devices in the 256 unit-cells are on the same
102
potential. Their respective SL potentials, however, are connected to one of three potentials based on the respective input value,
103
i.e., the positive read voltage V + to subtract current, the negative read voltage V −when adding current, or high impedance when
104
no current from the cell is to be added. Thus, all combinations of products between a positive or negative weight and a positive
105
or negative input can be performed in the analog domain in a single modulation, as shown in Ext. Data Fig. 3b. In addition,
106
a 4-phase mode is supported that is shown in Ext. Data Fig. 3c. Therein, the four combination of ±weights and ±inputs are
107
performed in four separate operations while only using the negative read voltage V −. As a result, a higher accuracy of the
108
measured current is achieved, as all PCM devices are read in the same polarity by the current-based ADC.
109
The ADC delivers the ﬁnal results as two 12-bit unsigned integers, one for the positive and one for the negative current. By
110
using tri-state buffers on a 24-bit wide bus, the results are transmitted from each ADC to the local digital processing unit (LDPU)
111
for post-processing. In the ﬁrst step, the results are transferred to a register array, such that the operation becomes fully pipelined.
112
Consequently, the initial latency of the LDPU does not affect its throughput (one data point per clock cycle). Next, the results are
113
transferred one by one to the ADC-convert-and-scale block, which ﬁrst converts the 12-bit integers to single-precision ﬂoating-
114
point format (FP16) and then employs two fused multiply-add (FMA) units to remove gain and offset errors.
115
Two ADC-convert-and-scale blocks produce outputs every second clock cycle and feed them one after the other to the acti-
116
vation function block through a multiplexer unit. There, afﬁne scaling per channel, as well as an optional ReLU operation, are
117
applied. The intermediate value is then combined with an 8-bit signed integer (INT8) input coming from the neighboring cores,
118
or from off-chip, through the link controller receiver (RX). This value is ﬁrst converted into FP16 format and then scaled to
119
the optimal range. Finally, the output undergoes another optional ReLU operation before conversion into INT8, ready to be
120
shipped by the transmitter (TX) of the link controller. The existence of both ReLU operations allows the possibility to apply
121
the activation both before and after adding the RX link input. The activation block is fully pipelined so that its latency does not
122
affect the LDPU throughput.
123
The link controller implements a state machine that enables transmission and reception of data within at most six neighboring
124
cores (receiving and transmitting cores are not necessarily the same, see Ext. Data Fig. 1). The core-to-core input-output links
125
are implemented as eight parallel channels, each transferring one bit every clock cycle. The selection of the data source for the
126
TX and of the data destination for the RX is implemented through a routing table, conﬁgured before runtime. Speciﬁcally, the
127
transmitter-side link controller prepends a preamble to the payload being sent on the links. The preamble contains information
128
to uniquely identify a routing path for a particular type of payload. If the receiving core is enabled to receive data from the
129
transmitting core in the routing table, the link controller samples the incoming data. Furthermore, the link controller in the
130
transmitting and receiving cores can select a portion of the payload according to another set of routing registers.

III.
THE GLOBAL DIGITAL PROCESSING UNIT
132
The GPDU is responsible for the digital processing required in LSTM networks. The Hermes chip contains eight GDPU slices
133
in total, each connected to the fourth row core above a GDPU slice. The cores in a column above a GDPU slice can aggregate
134
their outputs in a cascaded fashion in the LDPUs of those cores. The ﬁnal aggregated output produced by the LDPU of the
135
fourth row core is then sent to its corresponding GDPU slice. Each slice takes up to 256 inputs serially (one in each clock cycle),
136
out of which 64 inputs of input gate’s (I), cell input (A), forget gate’s (F), and output gate’s (O) pre-activation vector. Those
137
pre-activation vector inputs arrive in an alternating fashion from four neighbouring BLs. By mapping columns of the I, A, F, and
138
O weight matrices in an interleaved fashion, a single element processor per GDPU slice (instead of 64) can be used to process
139
the 256 inputs serially, which signiﬁcantly reduces the number of resources needed in the GDPU. Each GDPU slice produces
140
one output every four clock cycles for up to 64 outputs. It also contains cell state memory registers for up to 64 elements.
141
Fig. 1d depicts one GDPU slice architecture in detail. All computations inside the GDPU slice are performed in the FP16
142
format with inputs and outputs in INT8 format. Hyperbolic tangent activation functions are realized using a lookup table (LUT)
143
method. First, a bank of 17 comparators is used to ﬁnd the corresponding bin (out of 18) in which an input is contained. Then, the
144
output value is interpolated in a fused-multiply-add (FMA) unit using the slope and offset read from the LUT for the determined
145
bin. The output activations of the four gates are further processed by two multiply blocks, three FMA blocks, and one more
146
hyperbolic tangent LUT block to compute the cell state and the hidden state output of the LSTM unit.

IV.
MVM ACCURACY
148
We characterized all 64 cores of Hermes to assess the PCM yield and how accurately MVMs can be performed with them.
149
Fig. 2a shows the SET and RESET distributions of the unit-cells of all 64 Hermes cores. The Hermes unit-cell comprises
150
four PCM devices (two per polarity). Therefore, we deﬁne a unit-cell in the RESET state (GRESET) when all PCM devices are
151
programmed to RESET, and a unit-cell in the SET state (GSET) when one PCM device is programmed to SET and the others to
152
RESET. On 63 out of the 64 cores, more than 99% of the unit cells can be programmed to |GRESET| smaller than 5 ADC counts
153
and GSET larger than 50 ADC counts. The yield of the remaining outlier core is 98.4%. The high yield achieved on all cores
154
implies that it is possible to program most of the unit-cells to a given conductance value via iterative programming, as long as it
155
is within the range of their GRESET and GSET.
156
We studied two programming algorithms using either strictly one device (ODP) or up to two devices (TDP) to map a weight
157
to a unit-cell conductance. A weight is written by programming the unit-cell devices that correspond to the weight polarity.
158
Devices of the opposite polarity are RESET to near zero conductance. For a given unit-cell at row m and column n in a Hermes
159
core, the corresponding weight Wmn is mapped to a target conductance value Gmn according to
160
Gmn = Wmn · Gmax

(1)
where W max is the maximum absolute weight value to be programmed in the Hermes core, and Gmax is the maximum reliably
161
programmable unit-cell conductance. For ODP, Gmax should ideally not be larger than the least conductive SET device of the
162
core. We deﬁne Gmax = 80 ADC counts based on SET distributions of Fig. 2a, as the tenth percentile of the core with the least
163
conductive SET states. For TDP, Gmax should be set according to the least conductive unit-cell when both devices of positive or
164
negative polarity are in SET state. Here, we choose Gmax = 160 ADC counts.
165
We use a closed-loop iterative programming scheme in which the programming current is updated proportional to the dif-
166
ference between the read conductance of unit-cell (m,n) and Gmn19. In each iteration, the devices are read individually, and a
167
programming pulse is applied to the unit-cells that did not yet converge within a pre-deﬁned margin. For ODP, one out of the two
168
unit-cell devices corresponding to the weight polarity receives the iterative programming pulses, and the other is RESET. For
169
TDP, if Gmn exceeds GSET of both devices, one is programmed to the SET state and the other receives the iterative programming
170
pulses. Otherwise, the device with the highest GSET receives the iterative programming pulses and the other is RESET (see
171
Methods and Extended Data Fig. 4). Performing the device selection this way ensures that a maximal amount of devices are at
172
RESET or SET, which are the least noisy states. Because of this, reduced programming errors are achieved compared with prior
173
multi-device programming approaches that attempt to program each unit-cell device via iterative programming up to the ODP
174
Gmax20 (see Extended Data Fig. 4c).
175

V.
DEEP NEURAL NETWORK INFERENCE DEMONSTRATIONS
200
Having established the MVM accuracy of Hermes, we now assess the inference accuracy when running different neural
201
networks on the chip. We implemented three networks that cover all usable features of the chip: a ResNet-9 convolutional
202
neural network (CNN) for CIFAR-1022 image classiﬁcation, an LSTM network for predicting characters of the PTB dataset23,
203
and an LSTM network for generating captions of images in the Flickr8k dataset24 (see Methods). Prior to being deployed
204
on the chip, the networks are trained in a hardware-aware manner by injecting noise on the synaptic weights to improve their
205
resilience to hardware nonidealities2, using the publicly available IBM Analog Hardware Acceleration Kit25 (see Methods for
206
details about the training procedure). The trained weights are then mapped to conductance values to be programmed on the
207
PCM unit-cells using Eqn. (1). Gmax is determined on a per-core basis while constraining the BL current to not exceed the
208
maximum supported by the ADC (see Methods). For the three networks, all operations involved in the processing of weight
209
layers, including the MVMs, activation functions, batch normalization, LSTM cell-state computation, data aggregation when
210
a layer is split onto multiple cores or for residual connections, bias addition, and all the necessary intra- and inter-layer afﬁne
211
scaling, are implemented on-chip. Apart from the max-pooling in ResNet-9 and the character/image/word embedding in the
212
LSTM networks, which are not supported by the chip, no other computation is performed in software in the network executions.
213
The custom ResNet-9 network (1,866,536 synaptic weights and 10 biases) and its corresponding implementation on Hermes
214
are shown in Fig. 3a and Fig. 3b, respectively. On-chip links are conﬁgured to aggregate MVM results in the LDPUs of the
215
cores implementing layers that cannot ﬁt onto a single one (all layers except the ﬁrst and the last). The batch normalization and
216
ReLU activation for all convolution layers are performed in the LDPU of the core that receives the aggregated data. The residual
217
connections are implemented by sending the residual data to the LDPU of the receiving core, and performing the addition inside
218
the LDPU. For the last dense layer, the bias is programmed into the afﬁne scale registers of the LDPU so that it gets added to the
219
MVM result. The CIFAR-10 test accuracy results for ODP and TDP weight programming are shown in Fig. 3c. Hermes with
220
TDP achieves an accuracy of 92.81%, which is less than 1% below the software baseline of 93.67%, whereas ODP achieves
221
92.23%. We also compare the experimental results with simulations that include the per-core weight error for this network
222
extracted as in Fig. 2c, and the 8-bit quantization from the PWM and LDPUs (see Methods for details on the simulation model).
223
The biggest accuracy drop from the software baseline comes from the weight error, and the digital quantization adds another
224
0.2 −0.3% drop on top of it (see Fig. 3c). The accuracy obtained by simulating only those two effects nearly matches the
225
experimental accuracy, which indicates that any additional nonlinearity from the peripheral circuitry of the chip does not have a
226
signiﬁcant impact compared with the weight and quantization errors.
227
The LSTM network for PTB character prediction (1,299,312 weights and 2,066 biases) is shown in Fig. 4a, and the corre-
228
sponding Hermes implementation in Fig. 4b. Each character is ﬁrst embedded into a 128-long random orthogonalized vector
229
which is then input to the on-chip input gate. Then, MVMs from the input and hidden gates are summed vertically on-chip by
230
the LDPUs of the 8 cores in domains 3 and 4, using vertical on-chip links to transmit the data. The data produced by the LDPUs
231
of the 8 cores of domain 4 are then sent to the 8 corresponding GDPU slices using on-chip links. The GDPU then performs the
232
sigmoid and tanh activations, the cell-state computation, as well as the necessary input/output data scaling. Finally, the output
233
of the GDPU is sent to the dense layer which outputs the next predicted character in the sequence, as well as to the hidden gate
234
as input for the character of the next timestep. The performance of the character prediction task is evaluated in bits per character
235
(BPC), which is a measure of how well the model is able to predict samples from the true underlying probability distribution of
236
the dataset (a lower BPC corresponds to a better model). The BPC results for ODP and TDP weight programming are shown in
237
Fig. 4c as well as the corresponding simulation results. Hermes with TDP achieves a BPC less than 0.1 higher than the software
238
baseline of 1.336 (adequate baseline on this benchmark for a network of this size26), whereas the ODP BPC is only ∼0.02
239
higher than TDP. Unlike for ResNet-9, the high per-core utilization of the LSTM network leads to a high enough current at the
240
ADC input with ODP to achieve a good signal-to-noise ratio, therefore using TDP only marginally improves the BPC. Here
241
again, the simulated accuracy with weight noise and quantization nearly matches the experimental accuracy.
242
The third network that we implemented is meant to assess the inference accuracy on a workload that uses all 64 cores of
243
Hermes. We chose an image caption generation task implemented with a single LSTM unit and a dense layer (4,080,384 weights
244
and 6,080 biases), similar to the PTB LSTM network described above (Fig. 5a). Here, at the initial timestep, the features of an
245
input image extracted by a CNN (implemented in software) are fed to the on-chip input gate. As above, the MVMs from the
246
input and hidden gates are summed on-chip and sent to the GDPU via on-chip links. The GDPU output is sent to the dense layer
247
that produces the ﬁrst word of the caption, which is stored in an off-chip caption buffer. Then, this word is embedded, sent to the
248
input gate, and the GDPU output is sent to the hidden gate, in order to generate the next word of the caption (Fig. 5b). The latter
249
process is run until the entire caption is generated, for at most 24 timesteps per image. The performance of the image caption
250
generation task is evaluated using the BLEU-n score27, which measures the match of word n-grams between a generated caption
251

6
and multiple reference captions. The BLEU scores measured over the full test dataset on Hermes are shown in Fig. 5c, along
252
with three representative generated captions in Fig. 5d. The BLEU scores achieved by Hermes are essentially the same, within
253
variation, as the software ones. It is observed that captions of some images differ between software and Hermes, sometimes
254
one being better or worse than the other, as shown in the examples of Fig. 5d. However, when averaged over all images of the
255
dataset, the Hermes BLEU scores are very similar to the software ones.

VI.
PERFORMANCE
257
Table I summarizes the measured performance achieved by the Hermes chip for three use-cases: (i) the chip performing only
258
MVMs at full utilization, (ii) the processing of one input to a deep layer of ResNet-9, and (iii) the processing of one timestep of
259
the LSTM unit of the image captioning network (see Methods). Hermes achieves a peak MVM throughput of 63.1 TOPS at an
260
energy efﬁciency of 9.76 TOPS/W and MVM area efﬁciency of 1.55 TOPS/mm2 for the 1-phase read mode, when all 64 cores
261
are fully utilized to perform MVMs in parallel. For the 4-phase read mode, the former metrics are approximately 4 times lower
262
due to the ∼4x higher latency. Even then, the raw MVM throughput of Hermes in 4-phase read mode is higher than the previous
263
state-of-the-art AIMC chips based on RRAM, PCM and SRAM, and is comparable to the nor-Flash chip presented in Ref. 12,
264
while achieving the highest accuracy on the CIFAR-10 benchmark (see Extended Data Table II). Although the SRAM-based
265
accelerator presented in Ref. 13 shows a higher energy efﬁciency and throughput density, it requires the use of an off-chip weight
266
buffer to store the total weight data of a network, leading to additional overhead during inference execution. In contrast, our
267
implementation enables to store a model entirely on-chip, therefore reducing external memory accesses during execution as well
268
as being able to retain the weight data throughout power cycling.
269
Finally, regarding the efﬁciency of the neural network layers including the digital operations, a single input to the ResNet-9
270
layer is processed in 1.52 µs and consumes 1.51 µJ, whereas a single timestep of the LSTM unit is executed in 1.43 µs while
271
consuming 5.24 µJ. Reducing the precision of the digital computing from FP16 to lower integer precision, such as INT8, will
272
allow in the future to parallelize the digital computations across ADC outputs to reduce the latency gap with the MVM-only
273
latency.
274
VII.
CONCLUSIONS
275
We have presented the Hermes chip, a 64-core AIMC chip based on 14-nm CMOS with backend-integrated PCM that can be
276
used for deep neural network inference tasks. The chip can implement > 4M weights split across 256x256 cores, perform MVMs
277
through AIMC, and support all non-MVM computations involved in convolutional and LSTM layers via dedicated digital units.
278
The chip achieves a peak MVM throughput of 16.1 −63.1 TOPS at an energy efﬁciency of 2.48 −9.76 TOPS/W in 4-phase
279
(high-precision) −1-phase (low-precision) read mode for 8-bit input/output MVMs. The measured MVM throughput per area
280
of 400 GOPS/mm2 in 4-phase read mode is > 15× higher than previous multi-core AIMC chips based on resistive memory,
281
while achieving the highest CIFAR-10 accuracy of 92.81% among them. The next challenge for multi-core AIMC chips will be
282
to enable the layer-to-layer activation transfers through the communication links, supporting the intermediate activation storage
283
in local SRAM with appropriate appending and reorganization, in order to run a fully pipelined end-to-end inference workload
284
on-chip. Further improvements in weight density will also be highly desirable for AIMC accelerators to be a strong competitor
285
to existing digital solutions, which could be foreseen by integrating the PCM devices closer to the transistor-level at a denser
286
pitch or via 3D stacking of memory layers.

METHODS
288
Chip and PCM device fabrication.
289
Our experimental results are demonstrated on chips from 300 mm wafers fabricated with PCM inserted in a 14 nm back-end-
290
of-line (BEOL) at the IBM AI Hardware research center in Albany, NY. The BEOL fabrication including PCM mushroom cell
291
formation is done on top of the foundry-sourced front-end wafers. The PCM mushroom cell is comprised of a ring heater for
292
the bottom electrode and a Ge2Sb2Te5 and top electrode ﬁlm stack which is subtractively patterned to form the mushroom top.
293
Wafers are characterized through several different in-line electrical tests to assess their quality before identifying wafers and die
294
to send for dicing and packaging. Testing is done on discrete single structures embedded in the BEOL and also done on array
295
diagnostic monitors (ADM) with functional peripheral circuitry.
296
Experimental platform.
297
The experimental platform is built around a single Hermes chip packaged in a 1,525 pin ball grid array (BGA). Through
298
controlled collapse chip connection (C4), the chip can be mounted onto an interposer printed circuit board, which is placed on a
299
socket for testing on a dedicated test board. For every core, 72 dedicated C4 pads provide analog and digital supply voltages in
300
addition to a small number of pads used to implement a serial communication interface to control the core. The supply voltages
301
of the cores in each row are grouped on the interposer. The resulting partition into eight power domains reduces the required
302
number of components on the test board while maintaining a high degree of redundancy and resilience against faulty cores, for
303
example due to manufacturing failures.
304
The Hermes chip is interfaced to a hardware platform comprising one FPGA module and a base board. The base board
305
provides the chip socket, chip cooling, multiple power supplies per power domain as well as the voltage and current reference
306
sources for the chip. A Trenz TE0808-04 module with Xilinx Zynq UltraScale+ ﬁeld-programmable gate array (FPGA) is used
307
to implement overall system control and data management as well as the interface with the Hermes chip. The experimental
308
platform is operated through Ethernet from a host computer, and a Python environment is used to coordinate the experiments.
309
The base board provides current measurement for all the Hermes chip supply rails by using high-side current sensing before the
310
voltage regulators, allowing averaged current measurements with voltmeters.
311
Weight programming.
312
The gate signals of the unit-cells are connected in a diagonal fashion. The goal of this is to allow as many devices to be
313
programmed in parallel as possible without causing an excessive current ﬂow on the bitlines (BLs) and source lines (SLs).
314
During programming, only one gate signal is enabled, as depicted in Extended Data Fig. 4a. These gate selection signals are
315
controlled by the diagonal selection module.
316
The programming circuit controls the application of programming current proﬁles to the SLs of the crossbar array. There are
317
a total of 32 parallel digital-to-analog converters (IDACs) that can apply digitally-controlled current values to the SLs. Each
318
IDAC is assigned to 8 consecutive SLs of either negative or positive polarity (16 in total). A programming ﬁnite-state-machine
319
is in charge of controlling these IDACs from the conﬁguration values written to its registers, as well as selecting the active SLs
320
of the PCM devices to program.
321
To program a set of devices, a single diagonal must be selected before the IDACs are activated. This way, current can be
322
applied to all SLs in parallel without causing any current congestion in the chip. In practice only 32 SLs are programmed in
323
parallel due to area and power constraints. Therefore, it takes 8 programming cycles to completely program one diagonal of
324
devices for a particular polarity and device ID (A or B). Both square current pulses (RESET) and current pulses with a trailing
325
edge (SET) are supported with conﬁgurable pulse widths and amplitudes. In the experiments presented here, we use a RESET
326
pulse width of 125 ns with amplitude of 700 µA, and a SET pulse width of 250 ns with trailing edge of 50 ns and amplitude of
327
125 µA.
328
When programming a weight onto a unit-cell, all 4 PCM devices are ﬁrst RESET, then the devices corresponding to the weight
329
polarity are SET. For ODP, the SET pulses are sent only to the A device and for TDP to both A and B devices. Thereafter, the
330
device which will receive the subsequent iterative programming pulses is selected. For ODP, device A is always selected. For
331
TDP, if the target conductance value |Gmn| exceeds GSET of both A and B devices, the device with the lowest GSET is selected
332
for programming and the other is left as-is. Otherwise, the device with the highest GSET is selected for programming and the
333
other is RESET. Extended Data Fig. 4b shows a diagram of the TDP algorithm.
334
Iterative programming involving a sequence of program-and-verify steps is then used to program the selected devices to
335
the desired conductance values.19 After each programming pulse, a verify step is performed, and the value of the unit-cell
336
conductance programmed in the preceding iteration is read. The read is performed by applying long PWM pulses of 0.2 V
337

8
amplitude with 512 ns width and 256 ns precharge time to the selected diagonal, to ensure reliable read of the small individual
338
conductance values by the ADCs. The programming current applied to the PCM device in the subsequent iteration is adapted
339
according to the value of the error between the target level and the read value of the unit-cell conductance. The programming
340
pulses are square pulses of 125 ns width and amplitude varying between 125 µA and 700 µA. The programming sequence ends
341
when the error between the target conductance and the programmed conductance of the unit-cell is smaller than a margin of 5
342
ADC counts or when the maximum number of iterations (30) has been reached.
343
ResNet-9 on CIFAR-10 training.
344
The CIFAR-10 dataset22 is a well-known image classiﬁcation benchmark that comprises a total of 60,000 RGB images be-
345
longing to one of 10 classes. The dataset is split into a training (50,000 images) and test (10,000) set. Each image has a size of
346
32×32.
347
We use ResNet-928 since it is one of the most commonly used architectures for image classiﬁcation. The architecture com-
348
prises 8 convolutional layers (all followed by a batch norm and ReLU activation layer) which can be attributed to two main
349
blocks. Each block has two convolutional layers and a residual layer that contains another two convolutional layers. The number
350
of ﬁlters for each convolutional layer in order of appearance is [56,112,112,112,224,224,224,224]. This network yields a total
351
of 1,869,122 trainable parameters.
352
We ﬁrst train a model without any hardware-aware training for N = 300 epochs and later, as part of the hardware-aware
353
training, ﬁnetune the pretrained model for additional N = 200 epochs. Note that, both the training phases are fully executed
354
off-chip in software. The batch size is set to B = 128. In both phases of the training, we use SGD where the initial learning
355
rate η = 0.05 is gradually reduced using a cosine annealing scheduler with Tmax = 200. In order to improve performance, we
356
randomly crop the training images to a shape of 32 ×32 (padding=4) and apply Cutout29 with a patch size of 4 ×4. We apply
357
the following methods during hardware-aware ﬁnetuning of the model. After every update, we clip the weights of each layer to
358
a constant value (α = 1) around zero. The tiling of layers is simulated during training with a tile size of 256 × 256. In order
359
to make the model more robust to noise, we perturb the weights of each tile according to a model simulating two times the
360
PCM programming noise derived experimentally in Ref. 30. Additionally, we add additive Gaussian noise on the output of each
361
per-tile MVM with standard deviation of 10% of the maximum output range. Finally, DACs and ADCs at the output of each tile
362
are simulated during training with a resolution of 8 bits.
363
PTB-char LSTM network training.
364
The Penn Treebank (PTB)23 corpus is a collection of articles that is commonly used for evaluating sequence labelling ap-
365
proaches, as well as the language modelling capabilities of models on a per-word or per-character (PTB-char) level. We use
366
PTB-char which has a vocabulary size of 50, i.e. there are 50 different characters in the dataset. During preprocessing, every
367
character is embedded to a random orthogonal vector (dimension E = 128) drawn from a standard Gaussian distribution. The
368
training sequence of length 5,017,482 is split up into smaller sequences of length 150, which are shufﬂed after each epoch. The
369
validation sequence is split up into smaller sequences of size 128, which we then use to determine the best model during training.
370
The best model is ﬁnally evaluated on the whole test sequence of length 442,423. Since we are considering a language modelling
371
task, the target sequence corresponds to the input sequence shifted to the left by one character.
372
We use a one-layer LSTM31, where the input gate projects the embedded inputs for each time-step to the LSTM cell (dimen-
373
sion G = 2016). The projected input is then added to the equally shaped projection of the hidden state (H = 504). The LSTM
374
cell then processes the combined input and produces the next cell- and hidden state. The latter is used as input to the output layer,
375
which applies dropout32 (p = 0.25) and maps the input to the output dimension, which is equal to the number of characters in the
376
vocabulary. For the embedding, we use vectors sampled from a standard Gaussian that are orthogonalized using Gram-Schmidt
377
orthogonalization.
378
The training procedure is split into the standard training phase, followed by the hardware-aware ﬁnetuning. In the standard
379
training phase, we train the model for N = 200 epochs using the Adam33 optimizer with an initial learning rate of η = 0.01,
380
which is reduced by a multiplicative factor of γ = 0.1 every 50 epochs. We use a batch size of B = 128 and clip the gradients
381
to have a maximum l2-norm of 5.0 in order to avoid training instabilities. After the standard training phase, we set the dropout
382
probability to p = 0.0 and inject Gaussian noise into the weights during each forward pass2. Given the weight matrix W, each
383
weight Wi,j follows the distribution N (Wi,j,ζ · |Wmax −Wmin|), where ζ = 0.075 controls the amount of noise relative to the
384
dynamic range of the weights. In order to avoid weight outliers, we clip the weights of each layer to α = 2.0 times the layers’
385
standard deviation. We ﬁnd that for randomly distributed time-steps, the inputs to the hidden gate contain performance-critical
386
outliers that can not be clipped post-training. We therefore also clip the inputs to the hidden gate (which are also the outputs of
387
the LSTM cell) to a constant value of β = 0.06.
388

9
LSTM network for image caption generation training.
389
We use Flickr8k24, which is a collection of images from Flickr with 5 human-generated captions per image. The dataset
390
is split into a training, validation, and test set with 6k, 1k, and 1k samples, respectively. Due to size limitations incurred by
391
the hardware, we reduce the vocabulary size from the original 8,918 to 4,064 (16 cores with output size 254) by removing the
392
least frequently used words. As a consequence, 12% of the available captions are eliminated. Nine images are removed from the
393
dataset altogether because every caption contains an eliminated word. Using BLEU-4, which is the most common BLEU variant,
394
we veriﬁed that removing 12% of the captions did not signiﬁcantly alter the performance of the network (0.1609 →0.1557 for
395
the whole and reduced dataset, respectively). As a ﬁnal preprocessing step, we apply basic tokenization on the sequences.
396
Similar to34, we extract 2,048 features from each image using an InceptionV335 model that was pretrained on ImageNet36.
397
The extracted features are then projected to a 504-dimensional subspace, followed by batch normalization37. As a generative
398
model, we use a one-layer LSTM31 architecture (dimension G = 2,016). At time t = 0, the LSTM receives as input the embedded
399
image features and an all-zero hidden state vector. At time t > 0, the LSTM receives as input the embedded word (learnable
400
embedding vectors initialized with uniform distribution) and hidden state that was generated in the previous timestep. At each
401
timestep, the output is generated by passing the 504-dimensional hidden state of the LSTM through a dense layer, producing an
402
output vector of shape 4,064, over which a softmax is computed. At each timestep, the word with the highest softmax probability
403
is chosen. The LSTM runs for at most 24 timesteps or until an end-of-sequence token is generated.
404
We ﬁrst train a network without any hardware-aware training for N = 70 epochs using a batch size of B = 120. We use the
405
Adam33 optimizer with an initial learning rate of η = 0.001. To avoid overﬁtting, we apply dropout to the activations of the input
406
layer with a dropout probability of p = 0.5. We then ﬁnetune the pretrained model for another 50 epochs using hardware-aware
407
training. During this phase, we inject Gaussian noise (the same type as for PTB-Char, ζ = 0.08) and evaluate with slightly
408
less noise (ζ = 0.035). After every update, we clip the weights to α = 2.0 standard deviations and apply l2-regularization
409
with λ = 1e−4. To avoid exploding gradients, we clip the gradient l2-norm to 0.5. During training, we maximize the sum of
410
the negative log likelihood of the correct word at each step. As evaluation metric, we use the BLEU27 score, which measures
411
the precision of word n-grams between a generated and multiple reference captions. We report the n-gram BLEU score for
412
n ∈{1,2,3,4}.
413
Weight mapping onto Hermes cores and functional modeling.
414
The mapping of weights onto Hermes is done by converting a weight matrix for each layer into 256x256 conductance sub-
415
matrices, one for each core the layer is mapped onto. The weight matrix is split into the smallest possible amount of sub-matrices
416
which have the same number of rows and columns, both less than 256. For example, the 2016x224 weight matrix of a deep layer
417
of ResNet-9 is converted into 8 252x224 sub-matrices. Each sub-matrix is then reshaped into a 256x256 matrix by zero-ﬁlling.
418
Finally, the weight values of each sub-matrix are converted to conductance values using Eq. (1), with Gmax being determined on
419
a per-core basis. The Gmax value of a core is set to the minimum value between the maximum conductance supported by the
420
unit-cell (80 ADC counts for ODP and 160 ADC counts for TDP, as determined in Fig. 2a) and the maximum conductance that
421
leads to currents of all BLs to be less than the maximum current supported by the ADC (approximately 100 µA). For higher
422
BL currents, the ADC response starts to saturate, which introduces nonlinearity in the resulting MVMs and therefore must be
423
avoided. This additional constraint means that for some layers the full unit-cell conductance range cannot be utilized. For ODP,
424
the Gmax value used for all networks is 80 except for the image captioning network, where the hidden gate layer uses 70 and
425
the output layer values between 40 and 80 depending on the core. For ResNet-9 with TDP, we use Gmax of 160 for conv0 and
426
dense layers, values between 80 and 120 for conv1-4 and values between 120 and 140 for conv5-7. For PTB-char with TDP, we
427
use Gmax of 100 for the LSTM unit and 120 for the output layer. Therefore, only ResNet-9 takes signiﬁcant advantage of TDP,
428
because the lower utilization of the cores (especially for conv0 and dense layers) allows to use larger Gmax values that improve
429
the MVM accuracy.
430
Given the conductance mapping provided by the above procedure, we built a functional model of Hermes that includes the
431
quantization from the digital peripheral circuits and the hardware-measured weight noise. The Hermes quantization model
432
includes the quantization resulting from all data conversions performed by the Hermes chip during inference execution. It
433
includes the 8-bit input quantization from PWM, 12-bit quantization from ADC, 8-bit data conversion from LDPU and a GDPU
434
functional model (including the discrete tanh LUTs and 8-bit output data conversion). The weight noise model includes the per-
435
core weight error measured when performing MVMs with the network weights being programmed. We perform multiple MVMs
436
on each core and compute the resulting weight error std(W −ˆW)/W max, where std(W) is the standard deviation computed over
437
all elements of W, as shown in Fig. 2c. We then ﬁt the experimental weight error data points as a function of target weight with
438
a polynomial function for each core. Weight noise during inference is simulated by injecting Gaussian noise to the synaptic
439
weights with a weight-dependent standard deviation given by this polynomial function.

Power measurements.
441
We measured the Hermes chip energy efﬁciency and latency for three different use-cases. The ﬁrst one is the chip performing
442
only MVMs at full utilization (all 64 cores active in parallel), which provides the maximal MVM efﬁciency of the chip that can
443
be compared with other works. The reported MVM energy includes the PCM crossbar array, ADCs, and PWMs (operations
444
performed by the LDPU and GDPU used in neural network executions, such as activation functions, are not included in the
445
MVM energy). The second use-case is the processing of one input to a deep layer of ResNet-9 that spans 8 cores, including
446
the MVMs, data aggregation with on-chip links and LDPU operations. The third use-case is the processing of one timestep of
447
the LSTM unit of the image caption generation network, including the MVMs, data aggregation with on-chip links, LDPU and
448
GDPU operations.
449
For each use-case, the power consumed by the Hermes chip is measured both in standby mode (static) and while performing
450
the computations (dynamic) to calculate the energy. The average current drawn by the ADC and digital supplies operating at
451
0.85 V is measured using voltmeters (Fluke models 87V and 185). The dynamic power consumption is obtained for all the
452
monitored supplies by sending a stream of MVM commands with a period of 5 µs, and measuring the averaged power during
453
continuous operation of a few seconds. The dynamic energy is computed by multiplying the dynamic power by the MVM
454
command period. The total energy is calculated by adding the static and dynamic contributions for all monitored supplies. The
455
latency is computed by running the register transfer level (RTL) code for each use-case.

a, Crossbar array during programming. b, Proposed TDP algorithm to
program a target conductance value G on a unit-cell. c, Weight error comparison between TDP of this work and previous approaches. TDP
- Max-ﬁll refers to programming the two devices with iterative programming up to the ODP Gmax, as proposed in Ref. 20. Due to the wide
SET distribution shown in Fig. 2a, some devices in the core either cannot achieve Gmax, or conversely could be programmed to much higher
