Rethinking Responses to the World’s Water Crises 
1 
Abstract 
2 
The world faces multiple water challenges; pollution, water-related ecosystems degradation, 
3 
overextraction, ﬂooding and inequitable access. Insuﬃcient funding, ineﬀective implementation and, 
4 
in part, a misdiagnosis of the causes have contributed to inadequate progress and prioritized some 
5 
responses (e.g. water infrastructure) over others (e.g. water reallocation). We rethink the possible 
6 
responses to the world’s water crises with a Beyond Growth framing and compare it to mainstream 
7 
thinking. Beyond Growth, systems thinking informed by justice principles and relationality, prioritizes 
8 
the most disadvantaged and seeks to decouple economic growth from environmental degradation by 
9 
overcoming policy capture and inertia and fostering justice-principled institutional changes.

Humanity is not delivering improvements in basic water services fast enough while failing 
49 
to halt further increases in global water withdrawals and water-ecosystems degradation 
50 
This is despite increasing water infrastructure investments, rising levels of water-use 
51 
eﬃciency, and multiple policies to mitigate market failures. What is needed is a rethinking 
52 
of the responses to the water crises of the 21st Century. We argue for a Beyond Growth 
53 
framing, systems thinking informed by justice principles and relationality, to complement 
54 
current eﬀective responses, and to overcome policy capture and inertia and foster justice-
55 
principled institutional innovations to deliver a sustainable water future for all.

Water: Too Much, Little, Dirty and Stressed 
57 
The proportion of people with access to safe drinking water globally has approximately doubled over 
58 
the past 50 years, but the rate of progress is insuﬃcient to deliver Sustainable Development Goal 
59 
(SDG) 6 by 2030: ‘Ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all’. 
60 
Over 50% or more of people in Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, South-eastern Asia and Latin America 
61 
and the Caribbean are predicted to not have access to safely managed drinking water services 1.  
62 
SDG 6 is unattainable unless deﬁciencies in water governance, including policy capture and inertia 
63 
and a lack of long-term planning 2, are corrected to decouple economic growth from water 
64 
withdrawals 3. This requires: i) governments and donors to invest more in monitoring and regulating 
65 
all water supply systems, especially in rural and remote areas, that are ﬁt for purpose, people, and 
66 
place 4; ii) urgent action to prevent the degradation of rivers, aquifers and other water sources from 
67 
unsustainable withdrawals and pollution; iii) water is allocated toward just and sustainable water 
68 
access for the disadvantaged; iv) integrated bottom-up and top-down local and place-based 
69 
management; v) water planning that accounts for the adverse impacts on supply and quality from 
70 
climate change including preparing for increased frequency and intensity of ﬂoods and more 
71 
frequent and longer duration droughts; and vi) policies that prioritize delivery of the targets and 
72 
indicators of SDG 6.   
73 
Due to population and economic growth, global water withdrawals have increased about ﬁve-fold 
74 
over the past 50 years (see Fig. 1). Water stress, deﬁned as the ratio of total water withdrawals to 
75 
available renewable freshwater supplies, is magniﬁed at a catchment scale by poor water quality 5. 
76 
Water stress currently exceeds 80% in large parts of India, China, Middle East and North Africa, 
77 
Southern Europe, Western United States, South-east Australia, and South Africa 6. One outcome of 
78 
water stress, and dams, weirs and reservoirs that disconnect river systems with associated water 
79 
withdrawals, is that since 1970 the global area of wetlands has declined by about a third, with some 
80 
regions retaining less than 10% of their original area 7.  
81 
[FIG 1 HERE] 
82 
With agriculture using around 70% of the world’s freshwater withdrawals, demands for food, energy 
83 
and water security must be carefully balanced. This is because, in key water-stressed regions, 
84 
extractions for irrigation are unsustainable 8 and some business-as-usual practices are increasing 
85 
both the frequency of low stream ﬂows and ground water depletion 9. Compounding these 
86 
challenges is climate change that will exacerbate regional patterns of over-extraction 10  and increase 
87 
ﬂooding risks due to more frequent and higher intensity storms. Under moderate Greenhouse Gas 
88 
(GHG) emissions, about one third of the world’s population is projected to experience either wetter 
89 
or drier conditions by 2100 11.

Three Framings of the World’s Water Crises 
91 

3 
 
Responses to the world’s water crises are inﬂuenced by worldviews or framings – a set of beliefs and 
92 
values about the world that inﬂuence thoughts and actions. We consider three growth framings 
93 
(Economic Growth, Green Growth and Beyond Growth) and their implied responses to four key 
94 
water crises and their associated SDG 6 Targets. These crises are: i) unsafe and/or unaﬀordable 
95 
access to safe drinking water (SDG Target 6.1) and sanitation (SDG Target 6.2); ii) water pollution 
96 
(SDG Target 6.3) and environmental degradation, including of water-related ecosystems (SDG Target 
97 
6.6); iii) water overextraction from both surface and groundwater (SDG Target 6.4), and for which 
98 
improvements in water use eﬃciency is claimed to be  a key response (SDG Indicator 6.4.1); and iv) 
99 
inadequate protection for water-related ecosystems (SDG 6.6) from unmitigated social-economic-
100 
ecological system vulnerabilities, including ﬂoods and droughts (SDG Target 6.6 and SDG Indicator 
101 
6.6.1).  
102 
We review these water crises based on three growth framings: i) Economic Growth that considers 
103 
growth in the economy as central to economic development; ii) Green Growth that includes both 
104 
economic growth and conservation of key environmental assets as necessary for economic 
105 
development 12; and iii) Beyond Growth, not yet mainstreamed, that responds to increasing global 
106 
environmental degradation 13 by prioritizing sustainability and more equitable outcomes achieved 
107 
through institutional and policy reform. 
108 
The three growth framings are visualized in Fig. 2 (as ‘keywords’ and ‘key publications’) through 
109 
textual analyses of United Nations publications and conference proceedings, informed by a historical 
110 
perspective. Fig. 2 is illustrative only of the relative nature of the three growth framings. This is 
111 
because there are policy lags between the principles espoused in UN publications and agreements 
112 
and their implementation at national and sub-national levels.

Economic Growth Framing 
115 
Economic growth is a measure of the change in the total market value-add of goods and services, 
116 
expressed as the Gross Domestic Product (GDP), produced in an economy over time. Economic 
117 
growth has been the predominant prescription for socio-economic development and, until at least 
118 
the 1990s, was widely held to be the only pathway towards progress and prosperity. This is despite 
119 
observations by John Kenneth Galbraith in the 1950s that ‘aﬄuent societies’ have unresolved social-
120 
economic-environmental problems, including wealth inequality, inadequate public services, and 
121 
excessive environmental costs 14. In the 21st century, a growing number of economists accept that 
122 
the level (and growth) of GDP is a ﬂawed measure of welfare and progress 15. 
123 
Economic Growth framing is about increasing prosperity by raising GDP per capita. It is underpinned 
124 
by ‘growth theory’ 16 that includes three stylized facts: i) low-income economies can converge to the 
125 
levels of per capita income in high-income countries but only by increasing their produced capital per 
126 
worker through private and public investments; ii) sustained economic growth, in the long-run, 
127 
requires technological progress which is increased by investments in research and development; and 
128 
iii) high-income countries have accumulated higher per capita levels of produced and human capital 
129 
than low-income countries. While higher levels of GDP since the 1950s have been accompanied by 
130 
very large improvements in global health (e.g. reduced child mortality and morbidity), they have not 
131 
yet adequately met the needs for improved water supply and sanitation, especially in rural areas. 
132 
Further, the growth dilemma is that historical patterns of economic growth have contributed to large 
133 
negative environmental impacts 17, including increased carbon dioxide emissions globally and the 
134 
degradation of water-related ecosystems at local and regional scales.  
135 

4 
 
Green Growth Framing  
136 
Green Growth is challenging Economic Growth as the dominant policy framing in international fora, 
137 
especially in high-income countries. It seeks sustainable development, meeting the needs of the 
138 
present without compromising the needs of the future. The Organization for Economic Cooperation 
139 
and Development (OECD) deﬁnes it as “…fostering economic growth and development while 
140 
ensuring that natural assets continue to provide the resources and environmental services on which 
141 
well-being relies” 12. Actions in support of Green Growth include: pricing environmental costs and 
142 
beneﬁts not ‘internalized’ in market transactions (e.g. water pollution discharges); repurposing 
143 
and/or eliminating subsidies (e.g. fertilizer, energy, infrastructure) that increase water withdrawals, 
144 
especially in agriculture; removing barriers to sustainability-enhancing investments (e.g. water 
145 
pricing that fails to cover costs of supply)3 18; and promoting innovation and ‘green’ technologies (e.g. 
146 
improved water-use eﬃciency) to increase economic productivity and reduce environmental 
147 
degradation.  
148 
Green Growth retains per capita GDP growth as a key indicator of policy success and seeks to 
149 
stimulate green technologies to reduce the intensity of natural resource use per dollar of GDP over 
150 
time. In several middle- to high-income countries, some of the resources consumed for every dollar 
151 
of GDP have decreased, and in a select few countries, outright reductions in the use of certain 
152 
materials have been observed. Despite these improvements, there has not been an overall global 
153 
decrease in the overall consumption of materials 19, global greenhouse gas emissions, or the total 
154 
amount of water withdrawals.  
155 
Countries that have reduced their environmental footprint have, in part, achieved this by imports 
156 
such as through ‘virtual water’20 in the form of food and clothing. Given the critical need to rapidly 
157 
reduce key environmental impacts (e.g. global GHG emissions) to avoid potentially catastrophic 
158 
climate risks 21, there is skepticism that Green Growth, as currently practiced and in the timeframe 
159 
available, can deliver increased human well-being 22. Nevertheless, a ‘Green New Deal’ that 
160 
incorporates much of the thinking around Green Growth has become an important policy platform in 
161 
some countries (e.g. United States). The key focus of Green New Deal is on public sector spending 
162 
and policy reform to increase green employment, stimulate green technologies and promote low-
163 
carbon manufacturing 23.  
164 
Beyond Growth Framing 
165 
Beyond Growth framing was ﬁrst presented by Daly 24 but has antecedents in earlier work, such as 
166 
the primacy of ‘society’ over the economy, and global projections of the limits and consequences of 
167 
unrestrained economic growth. Here, Beyond Growth emphasizes: i) the inter-connections between 
168 
the biosphere and anthroposphere, biophysical limits, and that economies exist within complex 
169 
social-ecological systems and ii) actions that are inclusive in terms of who gets listened to, what 
170 
knowledge gets recognized, what challenges get considered and what solutions get selected (see Fig. 
171 
3). That is, Beyond Growth takes a ‘systems thinking’ view of the causes and consequences of the 
172 
world water crises and is inclusive and justice-principled in its responses. 
173 
[FIG. 3 HERE] 
174 
Key policy goals of Beyond Growth include: rapid decoupling of economic growth from critical 
175 
environmental impacts (e.g. climate change, biodiversity loss, groundwater depletion), including the 
176 
global material footprint 19 and reduced inter-generational inequalities associated with 
177 
environmental degradation and economic growth 25. Key outcomes in support of these goals, 
178 
proposed at multiple scales (local, national, regional, and global), include: i) reducing environmental 
179 

5 
 
degradation (e.g. improving water quality, reduced depletion of aquifers); ii) rising human well-being 
180 
(e.g. higher quality and longevity of life); iii) declining poverty rates (e.g. increasing access to safe 
181 
drinking water and sanitation); iv) increasing system resilience (e.g. recovery that supports a more 
182 
sustainable state of the world following negative shocks from droughts and ﬂoods) 26; and v) growing 
183 
participatory and deliberative democracy 27 (e.g. compulsory voting, proportional representation 
184 
voting, nested neighborhood-to-regional-to-national citizen assembles, and tools for canvassing 
185 
opinions or locating consensus) 28.

Responses to the World’s Water Crises 
193 
Responses to the world’s water crises by decision-makers depend, in part, on their understanding of 
194 
causes and consequences as well as their goals. While there are common goals between all three 
195 
framings (e.g. reduced intra-generational inequality) the core goals are diﬀerent and, thus, so are the 
196 
associated responses (see Fig. 5). All three framings acknowledge that policies, institutions, and 
197 
investments should respond to market and government failures but diﬀer over what is prioritized 
198 
among market and government failures. 
199 
In the water sector, the two principal market failures include: i) externalities or side-eﬀects in both 
200 
the production and consumption of water resources 29 such that the costs imposed on others, for 
201 
example, from groundwater depletion or water pollution, are not fully accounted for by those 
202 
causing the harm; and ii) collective action failures that result in inadequate provision of public goods 
203 
(e.g. ﬂood protection) because the beneﬁts are non-exclusive such that while everyone beneﬁts from 
204 
its provision, some choose to ‘free ride’ and not pay.  
205 
[FIG. 5 HERE] 
206 
Government failures in the water sector include: i) inadequate institutional, ﬁnancial or technical 
207 
government capacities to support eﬀective water planning and collective action (e.g. the provision 
208 
and monitoring of drinking water services); ii) regulatory capture whereby private actors redirect 
209 
government expenditures  (e.g. size and location of water infrastructure) for their personal gain 30; iii) 
210 
misaligned regulations and incentives 31 that cause unintended consequences (e.g. energy subsidies 
211 
to farmers that incentivize additional groundwater pumping); iv) ‘Crowding Out’ of self-organized 
212 
collective actions (e.g. sustainable, locally managed aquifers) by externally-imposed rules 32; and v) 
213 
failures of omission, including monitoring and enforcing of government regulations and standards in 
214 
relation to water, as well as a lack of accessible information and data (e.g. ﬂood and drought risks, 
215 
sea-level rise, drinking water quality). 
216 
Economic Growth has prioritized the eﬃcient allocation of goods and services through markets and 
217 
capital accumulation with infrastructure-led development as pathways to prosperity. In this framing, 
218 
the water crises are primarily overcome by targeted public and private investments in water 
219 
infrastructure and other incentives that support the provision of water-related goods and services.  
220 

6 
 
Green Growth prioritizes the internalization of market failures that create environmental costs. 
221 
Nevertheless, it recognizes the need to respond to government failures with organizational reforms 
222 
(e.g. operational independence of regulatory agencies; stable funding to environmental agencies; 
223 
intersectoral coordination and multilevel governance; repurposing and redesigning subsidies to 
224 
reduce water pollution and water withdrawals). It shares with Economic Growth the goal of 
225 
overcoming infrastructure gaps and ‘derisking’ water-related investments. Green Growth also seeks 
226 
policies, institutions, and investments that provide economic incentives for ‘green’ technologies and 
227 
so-called ‘nature-based-solutions’. The related policy agenda of the Green New Deal highlights public 
228 
investment and incentives for private investment to create green jobs and investments. 
229 
Beyond Growth responds to both market and government failures by prioritizing mitigation of water-
230 
related risks 33 and eﬀective partnerships to achieve good quality water access for the poorest 34. Like 
231 
Green Growth, it recognizes the importance of economic incentives and regulations to (re)allocate 
232 
water and the need for appropriate water pricing and a redesign and/or elimination of subsidies to 
233 
avoid incentivizing greater water withdrawals. Such subsidies could beneﬁt existing rural 
234 
beneﬁciaries by repurposing funding towards research and development and extension services for 
235 
farmers, such as promoting climate-smart agriculture (e.g. better production, better nutrition, better 
236 
environment and a better life for all). Repurposed subsidies can also help overcome the much higher 
237 
rural supply costs per person 35, relative to urban areas, to improve rural water service delivery to the 
238 
poor, especially those not yet connected to safe water supply systems 3.  
239 
Beyond Growth diﬀers from Economic Growth and Green Growth in that it is explicit about who has 
240 
power and inﬂuence over water, who does not get listened to by key decision-makers, and what 
241 
knowledge and data has been excluded in decision-making. That is, Beyond Growth highlights the 
242 
need for innovations in policy interventions and institutional arrangements 36 that ‘opens up’ the 
243 
process of ‘who gets what water and when’ in terms of who participates, what problems and 
244 
solutions are considered, and how do actions gets implemented justly (see Fig. 3).  
245 
Table 1 summarizes key responses across the three growth framings in relation to the world’s water 
246 
crises. The responses are ‘nested’ in that those listed for Economic and Green Growth are also 
247 
possible options for Beyond Growth. That is, Beyond Growth options include all responses, but how 
248 
they would be implemented may be radically diﬀerent from Economic Growth and Green Growth. 
249 
For example, inter-catchment water transfers to increase water availability elsewhere could only be 
250 
considered a desirable response for Beyond Growth after full and proper engagement with all 
251 
stakeholders, and full consideration of: i) system risks; ii) all economic costs and beneﬁts (e.g. social-
252 
economic-ecological); iii) compensation to mitigate distributional and inter-temporal impacts; and iv) 
253 
implementation of regulations and standards. Nine responses are exclusive to Beyond Growth and 
254 
three responses are common to Beyond Growth and Green Growth: i) transparent and real-time 
255 
water audits; ii) Nature-based storages; and iii) legal protections for households and ecosystems.  
256 
Economic Growth Responses 
257 
Increasing the stock of built or gray infrastructure (e.g. dams, piped infrastructure, water treatment 
258 
and desalination plants) has been the primary method of Economic Growth to respond to 
259 
inadequate access to safe water and sanitation and to mitigate water risks (e.g. ﬂooding). A ‘build 
260 
and grow’ approach (see Table 1) has been highly successful in providing improved water quality in 
261 
urban areas in most regions of the world and has supported water growth opportunities (e.g. 
262 
irrigation, energy-intensive outputs). Investments in water storage infrastructure have also provided 
263 
multiple beneﬁts, including improved water availability and public goods (e.g. ﬂood control). 
264 
Nevertheless, due to poor planning and execution, large-scale built water storages have created 
265 

7 
 
unintended consequences (e.g. reduced stream ﬂows, displacement of people, interruptions of ﬁsh 
266 
migrations).  
267 
To achieve SDG Targets 6.1 and 6.2, built infrastructure must be complemented by other 
268 
interventions, especially in rural areas where the number of households without access to safe water 
269 
and sanitation is ﬁve times greater than for the urban poor 37. In rural and remote locations, safer 
270 
water access is facilitated by: i) oﬀ-grid investments that are ﬁt for purpose, people and place 4; ii) 
271 
water users who pay for operations and maintenance complemented by full transparency about 
272 
costs with full accountability of water suppliers and service providers (e.g. results-based contracts) 38; 
273 
and iii) investments in green infrastructure and nature-based solutions (e.g. watershed forest 
274 
conservation, riverbank repairs) 39.  
275 
With an Economic Growth framing, the delivery of SDG Targets 6.3 and 6.6 related to water pollution 
276 
and water-related ecosystems is promoted by investments and regulations that complement public 
277 
demands for a better-quality environment. This prioritization is reinforced by the so-called 
278 
Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) that posits that increases in per capita GDP eventually result in a 
279 
peak in environmental degradation with rising incomes, followed by a decline. That is, for some 
280 
decision-makers a possible remedy to increasing environmental degradation is more economic 
281 
growth until degradation peaks, rather than the prioritization of remedial actions. Empirical tests of 
282 
EKC relationships, however, suﬀer from misspeciﬁcation and misinterpretation and have not yet been 
283 
observed at a global scale for water withdrawals or for key pollutants (e.g. world GHG emissions). 
284 
Where rising GDP per capita is associated with reduced environmental degradation (e.g. water 
285 
pollution) at a local level, this has occurred because of eﬀective water planning and active 
286 
government interventions, not by economic growth alone.

Green Growth Responses 
289 
Under Green Growth, the need to respond actively to market failures, especially in relation to 
290 
environmental degradation, has encouraged a series of market-based approaches to achieve SDG 6’s 
291 
water pollution and ecosystem health targets (see Table 1). These include: i) charges imposed on 
292 
point sources of water pollution intended to reduce or eliminate discharges; ii) a small number of 
293 
‘cap-and-trade’ systems that constrain overall level of water discharges but allow point sources, and 
294 
in some cases nonpoint sources, to trade their permitted allowances40; and iii) the use of payments 
295 
for watershed services that have, in some cases, proved eﬀective at improving watershed 
296 
cooperation, watershed integrity 41 and water quality 42. 
297 
Market-based approaches require eﬀective monitoring, compliance, and enforcement, and are not 
298 
‘set-and-forget’ responses to polluted waterways. Such monitoring is facilitated by real-time 
299 
measurements of water use, consumption, and water quality via the ‘Internet of Things’ and remote 
300 
sensing, along with open-access data. As with ‘command-and-control’ approaches that directly 
301 
regulate source point discharges, practices and technologies, there is a critical need to assess the 
302 
beneﬁts and costs of improved water quality. This can be facilitated by non-market valuation of the 
303 
beneﬁts of better drinking water supply and wastewater treatment 35 and environmental 
304 
improvements 43.  
305 
Volumetric water pricing, when complemented by eﬀectively communicated water prices to 
306 
households along with water metering, monitoring, and compliance, assist incentivizing water 
307 
conservation for domestic water use. Full economic water cost recovery provides incentives for water 
308 
utilities to maintain and/or improve water services and supply (e.g. reduce non-revenue water). 
309 

8 
 
Water pricing, however, must be ﬁt-for-purpose and consider water aﬀordability, especially in 
310 
locations where the costs of treatment and distribution are high but the ability to pay is low (e.g. 
311 
informal urban settlements, remote areas).  
312 
Policy mechanisms to assist disadvantaged households to access water include: i) a free basic water 
313 
volumetric allowance; ii) ‘social’ or reduced volumetric water prices for low-income households; and 
314 
iii) a water bill rebate. Whatever the selected approach, cost-free or subsidized water, including for 
315 
other basic needs (e.g. energy, and housing), should target those most in need 44. This involves either 
316 
higher-volume water users cross-subsidizing essential water use by the poor, and/or transfers from 
317 
general revenues, along with full transparency and accountability of the economic costs of water 
318 
supply.  
319 
Concerning SDG 6.4, formal and informal water markets provide another way to allocate and 
320 
reallocate water across users and uses. For example, formal water markets in the Murray-Darling 
321 
Basin (MDB), Australia, have placed a ‘cap’ on ground and surface water use. Formal water markets 
322 
have proven to be valuable for irrigators when responding to droughts in Australia, and elsewhere, 
323 
but are not suitable where appropriate institutional arrangements are absent 45 or if there is 
324 
inadequate built infrastructure (e.g. water storage). In the MDB, formal water markets have 
325 
facilitated the purchases of water rights by governments from irrigators. These government 
326 
purchases have increased stream ﬂows but have been insuﬃcient and, to date, have failed to resolve 
327 
past injustices whereby Indigenous Australians have been dispossessed of their land and water by 
328 
European colonisation 46.  
329 
A key goal of Green Growth is to facilitate eﬃcient technologies, which links to SDG Target 6.4: 
330 
“…substantially increase water-use eﬃciency across all sectors and ensure sustainable withdrawals 
331 
and supply of freshwater to address water scarcity”. The challenge is that increased water-use 
332 
eﬃciency, measured by the United Nations as USD per cubic meter of water, only accounts for the 
333 
market value-added of water. Consequently, because of how ‘water-use eﬃciency’ is measured, 
334 
increases in eﬃciency may be concomitant with reduced water quality and increased inequality of 
335 
access. Policies to promote the adoption of higher technical eﬃciency (ratio of the volume of water 
336 
withdrawn or applied to the volume of water beneﬁcially consumed) may also penalize downstream 
337 
water users because of reduced return ﬂows to streams and rivers. Thus, policy-induced increases in 
338 
water-use eﬃciency must account for: i) the overall water consumed via evaporation and 
339 
transpiration and changes in return ﬂows from eﬃciency improvements and ii) ‘rebound eﬀects’ 
340 
whereby increases in irrigation eﬃciency incentivize further water use 47.  
341 
Beyond Growth Responses 
342 
Beyond Growth highlights the importance of responding to both market and government failures 29, 
343 
especially in terms of procedural justice (e.g. who gets listened to, and how?) and epistemic justice 
344 
(e.g. whose and what knowledge gets accepted and acted on?) while highlighting the need for intra-
345 
generational, inter-generational and inter-species justice 48. Justice is critically important when 
346 
responding to the world’s water crises because if decision-makers ignore, or do not prioritize the 
347 
most disadvantaged in relation to safe water access and/or those without legal rights to water, then 
348 
SDG 6 cannot be achieved, including in the wealthiest countries 49. 
349 
We highlight two important actions for a ‘Water Beyond Growth Agenda’ in support of the 
350 
decoupling of economic growth from environmental degradation and prioritization of the most 
351 
disadvantaged: i) overcoming policy capture and inertia and ii) fostering justice-principled 
352 
institutional innovations at diﬀerent spatial scales geographical (e.g. catchments) (see Table 1).  
353 

9 
 
Overcoming policy capture and inertia is a Beyond Growth priority because of: “…unwieldy 
354 
bureaucracies…Corruption in high places frequently produces governments more oriented toward 
355 
promoting the ends of a few powerful individuals than toward addressing needs for governance.” 29. 
356 
This challenge is applicable for all countries, low, middle and high-income, especially those with 
357 
ﬂawed or failing democracies 50 or authoritarian regimes.  
358 
Capture of public policy by narrow interests has two important implications: i) “…there is no basis for 
359 
assuming that actual governments will play eﬀective roles in addressing needs for governance.” 29; 
360 
and ii) government decision-making may result in “… important biases in policy responses [that 
361 
hamper] eﬀective public policy making.” 51. In its most extreme form, policy capture includes both 
362 
petty and grand corruption that is illegal, and legal corruption that is mediated through donations 
363 
and ‘sliding doors’ of decision-makers between the public and private sectors.  
364 
Policy capture results impedes achievement of SDG 6 Targets, and other goals, and the delivery of 
365 
improved water services and public interest outcomes. Large-scale water projects, because of the 
366 
size of the investments, are at particular risk of grand corruption that beneﬁts few at the cost of the 
367 
public interest, including when there is theft of state water resources 52. Policy capture can also 
368 
manifest itself through institutional assumptions and approaches that limit policy actions. Such 
369 
constraints on ‘thinking’ limit the methods that could otherwise be used but which can be revealed 
370 
by bottom-up systems analysis and other approaches 53. In turn, this restrains the set of policy 
371 
actions at diﬀerent institutional and geographical scales while narrowing the communities of 
372 
knowledge and practices that are acceptable to decision-makers.  
373 
Policy inertia and capture can both be mitigated through civil participation, competent public 
374 
administration (e.g. accountability and transparency), and a robust legal system 54 that operates in 
375 
the public interest. Transparency about corruption and/or implementation failures are especially 
376 
important if they increase the accountability and policy responsiveness of decision-makers. For 
377 
example, in 2017, an Australian media outlet exposed alleged water theft by a few irrigators in the 
378 
state of New South Wales, and a ‘blind eye’ to these infractions by the state regulator 55. The political 
379 
response - an independent inquiry - established a new and independent regulator, a much greater 
380 
use of water audits for monitoring and compliance, and enforcement actions. 
381 
Justice-principled reforms in institutional and policy arrangements 56 are essential and, wherever 
382 
possible, should build on past learnings in water governance. As responses to the world’s water 
383 
crises, Beyond Growth innovations in institutional arrangements seek: i) justice principles, that is, a 
384 
‘fair equality of opportunity’ (e.g. intra-generational justice) and a ‘just savings principle’ (e.g. inter-
385 
generational and interspecies justice) 57; and ii) relationality 58, that is, ethical behaviors, respect, and 
386 
reciprocity to others 46 and place-based decision-making that fully considers the consequences to all 
387 
(e.g. people and non-humans). Relationality in Indigenous conceptualizations of water include ‘living 
388 
waters’; water is a Living Relation such that rivers have a life force for which people are custodians 
389 
with well-deﬁned obligations and a duty of care 59. Justice principles and relationality are not 
390 
exclusively anthropocentric and give an alternative framing about how to respond to the world’s 
391 
water crises. Nevertheless, all responses are constrained by history, past policy priorities and 
392 
investments (e.g. dams constrain steam ﬂows), and existing laws 60. 
393 
Innovations in institutional arrangements 56 consistent with Beyond Growth need: i) the inclusion of 
394 
all relevant stakeholders, ii) be focussed on local (e.g. catchment) and regional (e.g. basin) 
395 
governance, planning and operations, iii) consider ‘who gets what water and when’, iv) recognize 
396 
current and historical property rights arrangements (e.g. land and water), and v) be aware of the 
397 
barriers to water allocation and reallocation. Innovations in institutional arrangements can be both 
398 

10 
 
‘bottom-up’ and ‘top-down’, but must be connected, accountable to communities of interest, and 
399 
have a degree of ﬂexibility to implement integrated programmes based on proper planning 
400 
processes. These governance bodies may be nested spatially and/or institutionally, and beneﬁt from 
401 
horizontal coordination given the close connections between social, economic, and environmental 
402 
injustices 61.

404

406 
the concerns of the aﬀected and marginalized and is facilitated by representative membership in 
407 
citizen deliberation on key water decisions 62 and deliberative polling, among other approaches.  
408 
Examples of innovations in institutional arrangements and practices consistent with Beyond Growth 
409 
occur at multiple institutional and geographical scales and include: i) some elements of the 
410 
‘Singapore water story’ that have delivered safe drinking water and sanitation to its residents and 
411 
reduced water pollution63; ii) participatory processes in smallholder irrigation schemes in Tanzania, 
412 
Mozambique, and Zimbabwe that have empowered communities to better manage their local water 
413 
resources 64; iii) results-based contracts that support safe drinking water services in 17 countries 38; 
414 
iv) improved information through regular monitoring of water quality in India through the Safe Water 
415 
Network 65; v)  the Jal Jeevan (Water for Life) Mission goal (not yet delivered) to serve every Indian 
416 
rural household with piped water while supporting decentralized and community-managed water 
417 
services; vi) Indigenous and non-Indigenous partnerships in the Ambato River Basin, Ecuador, that 
418 
have reduced downstream water user costs 66; vii) smartphone applications that regularly report on 
419 
water services (e.g. disruptions, water quality) to Costa Rican community water management 
420 
committees and households 67; and viii) multiple case-studies of eﬀective nature-based practices for 
421 
ﬂood control 68.  
422 
Fostering complementarities between top-down and bottom-up innovations in institutional 
423 
arrangements can overcome information asymmetries between water users and gaps in funding and 
424 
capacity - weaknesses that can be more pronounced at local and informal levels of water 
425 
management. One eﬀective institutional innovation is regional integrated catchment management 
426 
where planning and operations are eﬀectively embedded within a top-down and bottom-up set of 
427 
institutions. Catchment management has been eﬀective where it has been underpinned with 
428 
statutory powers, an independent overview commission to set standards, and review processes to 
429 
report on outcomes 69. Another institutional innovation is to apply the public trust doctrine whereby 
430 
natural resources are held in trust and are protected for common use. Speciﬁc examples of Public 
431 
Trust include Common Asset Trusts, whereby trustees are appointed with a ﬁduciary duty to ensure 
432 
inter-generational sustainability of common-pool resources, such as wetlands 70.  
433 
A valuable initiative in institutional arrangements is the centralized provision of water-related 
434 
information complemented by aﬀordable data access. Digitized information (e.g. maps of ﬂood plain 
435 
risks and projected sea-level rise) is non-rival and can support locally informed water management 
436 
practices 71. For example, relevant and timely analyzed data can assist water managers, individuals, 
437 
and communities to prioritize the size, location, and timing of their actions (e.g. planting of crops) 
438 
and investments (e.g. construction of levee banks). Frequently missing but valuable data at multiple 
439 
institutional scales include: i) measures of the diverse values of nature 72; ii) water-quality measures 
440 
and experiential scales, such as the Household Water Insecurity (HWISE) and the individual Water 
441 
Insecurity Experiences (IWISE) 73; and iii) real-time and spatial (e.g. remote sensing) estimates of 
442 
water use and consumption that support adaptive water governance 74.

Timely information, at multiple institutional and spatial scales, is needed for: i) climate adaptation of 
444 
gray and green water infrastructure;  ii) risk assessments, in collaboration with those exposed to risks 
445 
33; iii) resilience-management actions about the ‘what’ (e.g. catchment scale), ‘for whom’ (e.g. 
446 
riparian communities), and ‘to what’ (e.g. ﬂoods) responses to shocks75; and iv) eﬀective policy, 
447 
regulations and planning that, in turn, can be facilitated, for example, by water accounting and 
448 
auditing, complemented by non-market valuation. 
449 
Conclusions 
450 
The Economic Growth framing that dominated thinking in the 20th century recognizes the 
451 
importance of private and public investments to improve prosperity. Its focus on built (gray) water 
452 
infrastructure has provided multiple beneﬁts but has also resulted in unintended environmental 
453 
consequences. Green Growth, the increasingly dominant policy paradigm, highlights the need to 
454 
internalize the external costs that water polluters and users impose on others, and the importance of 
455 
technical innovation to reduce environmental ‘bads’ (e.g. water pollution).  
456 
Despite Economic Growth and Green Growth framings dominating the responses to the world’s 
457 
water crises since the 1950s, and steady progress in improving access to safe water and sanitation, 
458 
the Sustainable Development Goal of ‘Water for All’ will not be delivered by 2030. Many people in 
459 
rural areas still lack safe access to drinking water and sanitation and, in most countries, there is 
460 
widespread water pollution, depletion of groundwater, and ongoing degradation of water-based 
461 
ecosystems. Rather than continue with mainstream thinking, we argue for a rethinking about the 
462 
responses, across multiple scales of governance and geographies, to the world’s water crises to 
463 
accelerate delivery of the SDGs.  
464 
We contend that a Beyond Growth framing, systems thinking informed by justice principles and 
465 
relationality, provides the foundation for necessary eﬀective, eﬃcient, and sustainable responses 
466 
(see Table 1) to the water crises of the 21st century. Its goal is to decouple both water investments 
467 
and economic growth from further environmental degradation and to achieve a sustainable water 
468 
future for all. Beyond Growth, not yet mainstreamed, builds on the successes of Economic Growth 
469 
(e.g. delivery of large-scale and ‘public good’ water infrastructure) and Green Growth (e.g. ﬁt-for-
470 
purpose water pricing, repurposing of subsidies, implementing ‘polluter pays’ charges). But it goes 
471 
well beyond these two mainstream framings to prioritize two key actions; overcome policy capture 
472 
and inertia and foster justice-principled innovations in policy interventions and institutional 
473 
governance.
