1
International Investment Law
History, Rules and Procedures
The legal foundation of international investment regulation (IIR) is vast,
complex and varied, across numerous substantive and procedural rules. It is
laid down in close to 3,000 different international investment agreements
(IIAs).1 This network of investment treaties is complemented by two key
arbitration treaties: the 1958 New York Convention for the enforcement of
arbitral awards,2 and the 1965 International Centre for the Settlement of
Investment Disputes (ICSID) Convention,3 which provides a forum and
procedural rules for investment treaty arbitration (ITA). This complex
landscape thus legitimately begs the question: how have we come to this
network of legal instruments, and what do they entail?
1.1
Historical Development of International Investment Law
International investment law (IIL) is a rather new subﬁeld of inter-
national law; however, the need for foreign investment protection is
not a modern phenomenon. The historical development of IIL can be
followed through several stages, spanning the colonial period to modern-
day globalisation.

Investors

agreements and a feature of customary international law (CIL), while the

treatment (MST), but the content of this standard was vague and

treatment according to the Calvo doctrine. The compromise between the
two opposing theories was found in the mechanism of espousal, whereby
the home State would espouse the investor’s claim, but only after all local
remedies of the host country had been exhausted. The disagreement
between the United States and Latin American countries over the MST
received an additional dimension in view of the Hull Rule, which
demanded ‘adequate, effective and prompt’ compensation for expropri-

1.1.2
Post-colonial Development in International Investment Law
Following World War II and until the end of the Cold War in 1989, the
development of IIL was shaped by decolonisation and the establishment
of a multilateral economic framework.8
The decolonisation of Africa and Asia reformulated the balance of
power in international relations. Former colonies, now newly independ-
ent States, believed that economic independence was an integral part of
their political self-determination and were determined to gain greater
control of their resources. Many embraced a socialist economic
agenda, which allowed them to nationalise key sectors of their economy,
expropriating existing foreign investment.9 Foreign capital and foreign
ownership and control over the means of production were seen as a new
form of colonisation, which could lead to foreign interference in domes-
tic affairs.10 With the balance of power in the UN General Assembly, and
supported by the Eastern Bloc, developing States pressed for the
Declaration on the New International Economic Order (NIEO) in
1974,11 which proclaimed the entitlement of each State to exercise
effective control over their natural resources and all economic activities,
‘including the right to nationalisation or transfer of ownership to its
nationals’, without specifying any obligation to pay compensation.12
Although by nature political, these UN instruments denounced the
Hull Rule as a rule of CIL, leaving the status of foreign investment in
the former colonies uncertain.
While developing States embraced a State-controlled economy with
the premise of nationalism, the West was heading towards economic
liberalisation. International consensus on trade liberalisation was reached

The ﬁrst bilateral investment treaty (BIT) was signed between West

consideration was nevertheless political. Pakistan’s internal instability
prompted Germany to gain reassurance that its economic interests in
Pakistan would be protected, while Pakistan differentiated itself from its
rival India.17 The United States kept FCNs with improved investment
focus until it established its own BIT programme in 1977.18

and developing countries. In theory, they had reciprocal obligations and
could be considered symmetrical in their legal effects. In practice, they
were asymmetrical in their political and economic effects with a one-
sided ﬂow of foreign capital from developed to developing countries. This
led to de facto asymmetrical legal effects with obligations borne by the
developing countries. Bilateral investment treaties formalised the Hull

1.1.3
International Investment Law in the Post–Cold War Period:
Towards International Investment Regulation
The end of the Cold War opened a new chapter in the development of
IIL. With the dissolution of the Soviet Bloc, the new global political and
economic order was to be based on democracy and market capitalism, as
neoliberalism became the dominant creed. International investment law
was to be developed on the same ideological foundation.
In the new order, the old differences – capital-exporting versus capital-
importing and free market versus State-controlled economies – would
diminish. The triumph of the Western political and economic idea
suggested that we might have reached ‘the end of history’ – with liberal
democracy and free market capitalism as the ﬁnal form of human
government.25 The role of the State was to fundamentally change, from
ownership of business enterprises to regulator with little involvement in
the market. There was a dramatic rise in the number of BITs concluded
because they signalled a country’s openness to foreign investment. The
World Bank promoted this practice, as the access to development funds
was made conditional upon signing BITs.
In parallel, a new generation of comprehensive bilateral and regional
trade agreements started to emerge, encompassing trade and investment
provisions to better reﬂect new economic realities, shaped by the eco-
nomic activity of multinational and transnational companies. These
global enterprises rose in numbers and size, but also in power, with the
ability to inﬂuence trade by controlling foreign direct investment (FDI)
ﬂows. Their economic activity had signiﬁcant impact on production,
sales, services and employment in many different locations.26 Their
effect, however, transcends a purely economic sphere. The concept of
globalisation advances the idea of a global system in which transnational
corporations are dominant forces. Ultimately, they necessitate legal
instruments to facilitate their cross-border operations.

New global economic governance meant closer economic integration

America, this integration was to happen in 1994 through the North
American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), a new regional trade agree-

was through the enlargement of the EU. In this global era of IIL, the
distinctions of the previous era seemed to disappear, with investment
agreements being concluded at all levels (bilateral, regional and plurilat-
eral) and between any States, regardless of their economic development
or geographic position. States were willing to enter into these agreements
in the expectation of increased levels of investment capital inﬂows.27 This
era marked a shift from nation-State-centred IIL towards the implemen-
tation of treaties through specialised bodies and their international
investment regulation.28
However, while seen as instruments for attracting foreign capital,
States were not fully aware of the policy implications of IIAs.29 Arbitral
proceedings under BITs were rare and there was no jurisprudence to
indicate their practical effects. Since then, ITA has risen to over 1,200
cases as per mid-2022.30

some cases is even more desirable than law. On the other hand, legal
disputes in investment arbitration are now seen as instruments of politics
in the hands of foreign investors and specialised legal experts.

the same ills. Heated political debate has prompted States to react,
through bilateral and multilateral ISDS reforms. Next, we explain sub-
stantive and procedural rules to provide a legal framework of contem-
porary IIR against which the reform process will be considered. The aim
is to demonstrate that issues of relevance for IIR are not exclusively
matters of politics and public perception but also matters of law.

1.2.1
Protection against Expropriation
International law duly protects foreign property against expropriation.
However, this concept has always been the subject of controversy. On the
one hand, expropriation of private property is an exercise of State
sovereignty. On the other hand, private property is an inalienable indi-
vidual right.36 As discussed, in the second half of the twentieth century,
the debate was centred on divergent views between developed and
developing countries regarding the standard of compensation for ‘direct’
expropriation of property that was nationalised. With the proliferation of
IIAs at the end of twentieth century, full compensation for expropriation
has become an accepted standard. The debate has since shifted toward
the issue of what constitutes expropriation, especially in situations other
than the classical category of nationalisation, and the impact of the
concept of expropriation on regulatory powers of the State for the
protection of health, safety, environment and other legitimate public
policy goals.
There are two types of expropriation. ‘Direct’ or de iure expropriation
results in the transfer of title to foreign property or its physical posses-
sion, with permanent deprivation of property. ‘Indirect’ or de facto
expropriation refers to government measures without formal transfer of
title, which interfere with exercise of property rights, leading to effective
loss of control or signiﬁcant depreciation of property value.37 Such
measures are also described as measures ‘tantamount’ or ‘equivalent’ to

1.2.3
Protection against Discrimination
Protection against discrimination in IIL is provided through national
treatment (NT) and most-favoured-nation treatment (MFN) and it is
also included in the concept of FET.94 Unlike FET, which is an absolute
standard, NT and MFN are relative standards, whose meaning is deﬁned
by reference to the treatment afforded to other investments. While the
principle of NT aims to accord to foreign investors the same treatment as
that granted to domestic investors, the MFN standard affords foreign
investors the treatment comparable to other foreign investors operating
in the host country.

1.2.4
Free Transfer of Capital
International investment agreements generally provide for free transfer
of capital in a separate, usually very speciﬁc clause.120 Free transfer
provisions usually contain a broad guarantee for free transfer of pay-
ments related to an investment, often listing in a non-exhaustive manner
types of funds which can be transferred,121 the currency and exchange
rate for the transfer122 and the time period in which free transfer is
guaranteed to investors.123 Usually, free transfer clauses will be restricted
by a number of exceptions, which allow States ‘equitable, non-
discriminatory, and good faith application of its laws and regulations’
generally aimed at prevention of abuses of justice.124
Provisions concerning the free transfer of capital have not had great
relevance in the practice of investment tribunals.125 The recent more
restrictive trend in drafting these clauses recognises the need of States to
adopt extraordinary regimes for extraordinary times, which is certainly a
well-learnt lesson of the GFC and the Euro crisis.126

1.3
Procedural Rules of International Investment Law
Most commonly, modern IIAs offer to investors the choice of ITA,
granting them speciﬁc procedural rights and remedies as the most
important component of IIAs.127 In international law the participation
of sovereign States in dispute resolution generally depends on their
consent, which in IIL is given through IIAs.128 Investment treaties
incorporate in their ISDS clauses an offer to arbitrate, extended by the
host State to an undeﬁned number of current and future investors of the
other Contracting Party for all future disputes arising out of investments
made in its territory. It is for the eligible investor to accept this standing
offer by correctly initiating the proceedings against the host State in
accordance with the requirements deﬁned in the treaty’s ISDS clause, at
which stage the arbitration contract is perfected. Through offer and
acceptance both parties express their mutual consent to arbitration,129
which is the precondition for jurisdiction of the arbitral tribunal.130 This
unusual arbitration contract in investment disputes, commissioned
through an international treaty, gives IIL its public-private nature.
However, ISDS is a rather generic term which encompasses a number
of different mechanisms.131 It is exactly with regard to the regulation of
the dispute settlement clauses that IIAs differ most. The following
sections provide an overview of procedural issues stemming from IIAs
and practice.132 The aim is to explain the procedural framework in which

1.3.1
Access to ISDS: ‘Investment’ and ‘Investor’
International investment agreements allow for a broad scope of claims
that can be subject to investment arbitration, sometimes expressly deﬁn-
ing the scope of disputes to breaches of substantive protections under the
treaty,133 and linking the claim to the loss or damage caused to the
investor by the breach.134 The disputed investment must be within the
deﬁnition of ‘investment’ provided in the IIA, which is usually set out in
broad terms, and sufﬁciently broad to include both direct and non-direct
types of foreign investment (portfolio investment).135 In some cases, the
scope of investment disputes can be limited by the scope of the treaty in
question,136 or IIAs can expressly limit the scope of ISDS clauses in
regard to the subject matter of claims.137 The German Model BIT and
EU investment agreements, unlike the US Model BIT, limit the jurisdic-
tion of arbitral tribunals to breaches of substantive protections in regard
to established investments, thus excluding the pre-establishment phase of
investment from the scope of ISDS clauses. The USMCA is an exception
from the previous US practice, which now provides for limited ISDS in
the post-establishment phase.138
Under the ICSID Convention, for a dispute to fall within the ICSID
jurisdiction, the dispute must be ‘legal’ and ‘arising directly out of an
investment’, which is decided on the facts of each particular case.139 The

‘Shopping’ in Investment Treaty Arbitration
Deﬁnitions of investors in IIAs, which in most cases focus on the formal
nationality criteria of incorporation in accordance with local laws, and
arbitral tribunals’ interpretations of such deﬁnitions, allow for the prac-
tice of treaty shopping. This involves investors structuring investments
through intermediate holding companies in order to take advantage of a
particular IIA which otherwise would not have been available to these
investors. Treaty shopping can take place in several ways, which
include:162 structuring an investment in the host State (subsidiary)
through an intermediate company established in the home State by an
investor (parent company) from a third country;163 structuring an invest-
ment in the host State (subsidiary) through an intermediate company in
the home State, which is effectively owned and controlled by an investor
(parent company) from the host State;164 and structuring an investment
in the host State (subsidiary) through an intermediate company in a third
country which is owned by an investor (parent company) from the home
State.165 The practice of treaty shopping can further be encouraged
through broadly worded MFN clauses.166
While treaty shopping disregards the origin of capital when determin-
ing the nationality of the claimant,167 and thus supports the neoliberal
vision of ﬂow of capital, it raises a number of policy issues.168 In
particular, the concerns include uncertainty of host States about the
extent of their legal obligations under IIAs, because a particular IIA,
instead of being a treaty with a particular Contracting Party becomes in
practice a treaty with an undeﬁned number of non-Contracting Parties.
Additionally, treaty shopping opens doors for domestic investors to
access investment arbitration, which otherwise would not be available

1.4
Conclusion
Looking at the past, the development of IIR can be described as a one-
way street. It was a mechanism developed by the need to protect the
interests of the capital-exporting countries in their capital-importing
counterparts. In the beginning it was the relationship between colonisers
and the either de iure or de facto colonised, which in the period after
decolonisation metamorphosed into the relationship between developed
and developing countries. The States, not fully aware of the real eco-
nomic, political and legal implications, were the main players in the
development
of
instruments
of
IIL,
acting
individually
rather
than collectively.
In the meantime, the one-way street has turned into a road with many
directions. In the multipolar economic order, the clear differentiation
between capital-exporting and capital-importing States has disappeared.
The power has diversiﬁed between economically powerful States acting
