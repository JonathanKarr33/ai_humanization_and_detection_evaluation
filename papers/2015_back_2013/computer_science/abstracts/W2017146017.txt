Today, the technology for video streaming over the Internet is converging\ntowards a paradigm named HTTP-based adaptive streaming (HAS). HAS comes with\ntwo unique flavors. First, by riding on top of HTTP/TCP, it leverages the\nnetwork-friendly TCP to achieve firewall/NATS traversal and bandwidth sharing.\nSecond, by pre-encoding and storing the video in a number of discrete bitrate\nlevels, it introduces video bitrate adaptivity in a scalable way that the video\nencoding is excluded from the closed-loop adaptation. A conventional wisdom is\nthat the TCP throughput observed by a HAS client indicates the available\nnetwork bandwidth, thus can be used as a reliable reference for the video\nbitrate selection.\n We argue that this no longer holds true when HAS becomes a substantial\nfraction of the Internet traffic. We show that when multiple HAS clients\ncompete at a network bottleneck, the presence of competing clients and the\ndiscrete nature of the video bitrates would together create confusion for a\nclient to correctly perceive its fair-share bandwidth. Through analysis and\nreal experiments, we demonstrate that this fundamental limitation would lead\nto, for example, video rate oscillation that negatively impacts the video\nwatching experiences. We therefore argue that it is necessary to implement at\nthe application layer a "probe-and-adapt" mechanism for HAS video rate\nadaptation, which is akin but orthogonal to the transport-layer network rate\nadaptation achieved by TCP. We present PANDA -- a client-side rate adaptation\nalgorithm for HAS -- as an embodiment of this idea. Our testbed results show\nthat compared to conventional algorithms, PANDA is able to reduce the\ninstability of video rate by 60%, at a given risk of buffer underrun.\n
