The growing popularity of location-based systems, allowing unknown/untrusted\nservers to easily collect huge amounts of information regarding users'\nlocation, has recently started raising serious privacy concerns. In this paper\nwe study geo-indistinguishability, a formal notion of privacy for\nlocation-based systems that protects the user's exact location, while allowing\napproximate information - typically needed to obtain a certain desired service\n- to be released. Our privacy definition formalizes the intuitive notion of\nprotecting the user's location within a radius r with a level of privacy that\ndepends on r, and corresponds to a generalized version of the well-known\nconcept of differential privacy. Furthermore, we present a perturbation\ntechnique for achieving geo-indistinguishability by adding controlled random\nnoise to the user's location. We demonstrate the applicability of our technique\non a LBS application. Finally, we compare our mechanism with other ones in the\nliterature. It turns our that our mechanism offers the best privacy guarantees,\nfor the same utility, among all those which do not depend on the prior.\n
