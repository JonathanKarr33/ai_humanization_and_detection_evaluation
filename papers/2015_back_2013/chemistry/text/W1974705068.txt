ABSTRACT 
21 
The cycling of soil organic matter (SOM) by microorganisms is a critical component of 
22 
the global carbon cycle but remains poorly understood. There is an emerging view that much of 
23 
SOM, and especially the dissolved fraction (DOM), is composed of small molecules of plant and 
24 
microbial origin resulting from lysed cells and released metabolites. Unfortunately, little is 
25 
known about the small molecule composition of soils and how these molecules are cycled (by 
26 
microbes or plants or by adsorption to mineral surfaces). The water-extractable organic matter 
27 
(WEOM) fraction is of particular interest given that this is presumably the most biologically-
28 
accessible component of SOM. Here we describe the development of a simple soil metabolomics 
29 
workflow and a novel spike recovery approach using 13C bacterial lysates to assess the types of 
30 
metabolites remaining in the WEOM fraction. Soil samples were extracted with multiple mass 
31 
spectrometry-compatible extraction buffers (water, 10 mM K2SO4 or NH4HCO3, 10-100% 
32 
methanol or isopropanol/methanol/water [3:3:2 v/v/v]) with and without prior chloroform vapor 
33 
fumigation. Profiling of derivatized extracts was performed using gas chromatography/ mass 
34 
spectrometry (GC/MS) with 55 metabolites identified by comparing fragmentation patterns and 
35 
retention times with authentic standards. As expected, fumigation, which is thought to lyse 
36 
microbial cells, significantly increased the range and abundance of metabolites relative to 
37 
unfumigated samples. To assess the types of microbial metabolites from lysed bacterial cells that 
38 
remain in the WEOM fraction, an extract was prepared from the soil bacterium Pseudomonas 
39 
stutzerii RCH2 grown on 13C acetate. This approach produced highly labeled metabolites that 
40 
were easily discriminated from the endogenous soil metabolites. Comparing the composition of 
41 
the fresh bacterial extract with what was recovered following a 15 min incubation with soil 
42 
revealed that only 27% of the metabolites showed >50% recovery in the WEOM. Many, 
43 

 
3
especially cations (polyamines) and anions showed <10% recovery. These represent metabolites 
44 
that may be inaccessible to microbes in this environment and would be most likely to accumulate 
45 
as SOM presumably due to binding with minerals and negatively-charged clay particles. This 
46 
study presents a simple untargeted metabolomics workflow for extractable organic matter and an 
47 
approach to estimate microbial metabolite availability in soils. These methods can be used to 
48 
further our understanding of SOM and DOM composition and examine the link between 
49 
metabolic pathways and microbial communities to terrestrial carbon cycling.

1. INTRODUCTION 
54 
Microbial products are an important source of soil organic matter 
55 
Over two-thirds of carbon in the terrestrial biosphere is stored as soil organic matter 
56 
(SOM) originating from plant, animal and microbial sources (Johnston et al., 2004). 
57 
Microorganisms are a critical component in soil as they are actively involved in the cycling of 
58 
SOM (i.e. production of microbial products and metabolic decomposition) (Schmidt et al., 2012). 
59 
The component of SOM that is accessible to microbial processing is the soluble fraction 
60 
(Gregorich et al., 2000; Blagadotsky et al., 2010), referred to here as dissolved organic matter 
61 
(DOM) and is physically defined as what can pass through a 0.45 μm filter. As with SOM, a 
62 
significant portion of this complex pool of metabolites is thought to be derived from soil 
63 
microbes with the flow of this carbon fraction mediating microbial biomass turnover (Baldock 
64 
and Nelson, 2000; Gregorich et al., 2000; Kalbitz et al., 2000).  
65 
DOM is in a constant state of flux by soil microbes (Schimel and Schaeffer, 2012; Xu et 
66 
al., 2014, Schmidt et al., 2012). However, microbial decomposition, and therefore the resultant 
67 
composition of DOM, depends on the availability of small molecule substrates in situ. A number 
68 
of biotic and abiotic factors affect microbial access to these molecules (such as leaching or 
69 
diffusion), but one of the most critical factors is adsorption to mineral surfaces (Kalbitz et al., 
70 
2000). The types of biologically accessible substrates likely depend on the specific interactions 
71 
between DOM components and mineral surfaces. In turn, the composition of the available 
72 
substrate pool will be a major determinant of microbial community structure and metabolic 
73 
activities (Judd et al., 2006). Hence, elucidation of the chemical composition of microbe-
74 
accessible substrates is a critical step toward understanding the complex dynamics of soil 
75 
nutrients and microbial communities.

77 
Traditional soil extraction methods and DOM analyses  
78 
To analyze DOM, a standard method is to obtain the water-extractable organic matter 
79 
(WEOM) fraction by using aqueous extractants, while microbial biomass is measured by 
80 
fumigating soil with chloroform vapors to release intracellular metabolites prior to extraction 
81 
(Brookes, et al., 1985; Vance et al., 1987). Comparisons are then made to an extraction of 
82 
unfumigated soil in order to estimate the labile or microbe-accessible fraction. Given technical 
83 
limitations on measuring its molecular composition, DOM has typically been quantified by 
84 
oxidizing or combusting the soil sample and analyzing its elemental composition to determine 
85 
dissolved organic (or inorganic) carbon or total dissolved nitrogen (Jones and Willett, 2006). In 
86 
some cases, DOM and SOM are resolved into more specific classes of metabolites such as 
87 
neutral sugars, amino sugars, amino acids, fatty acids and other biomolecules using colorimetric 
88 
methods or compound-specific derivatization followed by gas chromatography/ mass 
89 
spectrometry (GC/MS) (Amelung et al., 2008; Kakumanu et al., 2013). Such groupings into 
90 
broad classes of biomolecules essentially preclude linkage with microbial genomics since 
91 
biochemical specificity requires knowledge of molecular composition. 
92 
 
93 
The use of untargeted metabolomics to understand DOM composition and availability 
94 
 Untargeted metabolomics is a rapidly-growing and robust method that has become an 
95 
important approach in biomedical science by providing comprehensive data-driven metabolism 
96 
analyses of complex extracts (Fiehn, 2002; Garcia et al., 2008; Baran et al., 2009; 2013). As 
97 
mass spectrometry (MS) instruments such as Fourier Transform Ion Cyclotron Resonance 
98 
(FTICR/MS) (Hirai et al., 2004), capillary electrophoresis (CE/MS) (Soga et al., 2003; Edwards 
99 

 
6
et al., 2006), and liquid chromatography (LC/MS) (Baran et al., 2011; 2013) are commonly used, 
100 
they are not widely available. In contrast, GC/MS is a widely-used and generally more-
101 
accessible instrument to microbiologists and soil scientists due its low operational cost, 
102 
availability of curated metabolite spectral databases, broad analytical scope with good coverage 
103 
of metabolite classes (carbohydrates, alcohols, sterols, amino acids, fatty acids, etc), as well as 
104 
widespread application to phospholipid fatty acid analysis (Buyer and Sasser, 2012). It would 
105 
therefore be desirable to develop workflows for GC/MS-based soil metabolomics for 
106 
examination of extractable organic matter composition (Fiehn et al., 2000; Roessner et al., 2000; 
107 
Koek et al., 2006; Lee et al., 2012). 
108 
Metabolomics approaches are beginning to have a major impact on improving our 
109 
understanding of marine and freshwater communities and the significant role DOM plays in 
110 
these environments. While the majority of marine metabolomics studies have involved controlled 
111 
cultures, metabolite profiling has been successful with marine bacteria, microalgae, macroalgae 
112 
and animals (Mopper et al., 2007; Minor et al., 2014).  Metabolomics analyses of spent media 
113 
from these systems have shed light on unique chemical defense mechanisms and production of 
114 
secondary metabolites (Goulitquer et al., 2012). Marine metabolomics has also contributed to our 
115 
understanding of DOM lability and recalcitrance as a function of microbial carbon pumps in 
116 
aquatic systems (Jiao et al., 2010). Studies such as these are allowing us to grasp the value of 
117 
DOM in shaping microbial communities and to begin to extrapolate its role in global climate 
118 
change. Unfortunately, the field of soil metabolomics (and our understanding of SOM) lags 
119 
behind, but is emerging as an equally important area of study.  
120 
 
121 
Soils present many challenges to untargeted metabolomics methods 
122 

 
7
Recently a few papers have emerged relating to metabolomics of soils, many pertaining 
123 
to the production of osmolytes during drying and re-wetting conditions (Kakumanu et al., 2013; 
124 
Warren, 2013; 2014; Jones et al., 2014).  FTICR/MS is one of the most established methods for 
125 
DOM analysis and routinely resolves thousands of unique chemical formulas. However, these 
126 
methods are typically used to discriminate soils and soil treatments based on changes in 
127 
abundance and composition of these chemical formulas (Hockaday et al., 2006; Ohno et al., 
128 
2014). Understanding the soil biochemistry requires identifying the soil metabolites such that 
129 
they can be linked to enzymes and microorganisms.  Towards this goal, a recent pioneering study 
130 
by Warren describes important methods for identifying and quantifying soil metabolites using 
131 
CE/MS and GC/MS (Warren, 2014). Findings from this study provide key insights into the 
132 
drying response of soil bacteria in terms of osmolyte production and accumulation.  
133 
The comparatively sparse literature on metabolomics of soils versus marine systems is 
134 
likely attributable to the unique challenges soils present for untargeted metabolomics. To 
135 
evaluate the chemical composition of SOM or DOM, methods typically require the development 
136 
of analytical approaches similar to the classical soil extraction methods involving chloroform 
137 
fumigation followed by extraction with buffers containing high concentrations of salts (e.g. 500 
138 
mM K2SO4) (Brookes et al., 1985; Vance et al., 1987; Tate et al., 1988; Murage and Voroney, 
139 
2007; Makarov et al., 2013). Unfortunately the salt content of the resultant samples complicate 
140 
metabolite analysis because of salt crystal formation during dry-down and ion suppression during 
141 
electrospray ionization mass spectrometry analysis (Annesley, 2003). To overcome these 
142 
challenges and setbacks, optimizing detection of a large range of soil metabolites by GC/MS 
143 
requires careful consideration of numerous variables during method development including 
144 
extractant selection, extraction time, sample concentration and derivatization methods.  
145 

 
8
 
146 
Untargeted global metabolite profiling with GC/MS 
147 
As is evident, classical soil extraction methods involve long extraction times followed by 
148 
compound-specific analyses. However, as efforts aimed at understanding the effects of climate 
149 
change on soil will inevitably increase, there is a critical need to elucidate the small molecule 
150 
composition of soils, their dynamics and microbial substrate availability. Our initial goal here is 
151 
to establish a rapid and simple workflow to measure the small molecule composition of WEOM, 
152 
the most representative fraction of labile SOM (Ohno et al., 2014), using GC/MS. This 
153 
untargeted global soil profiling is a means to obtain unbiased investigations of soil metabolites in 
154 
a qualitative rather than quantitative manner. To develop a standardized method for extractable 
155 
organic matter, we used conventional soil extraction approaches and compared metabolite pools 
156 
that are extracted from unfumigated and chloroform-fumigated soils using water with and 
157 
without a series of additives: K2SO4, NH4HCO3, methanol or isopropanol. Another major focus 
158 
is to evaluate compound-specific soil adsorption (and therefore substrate accessibility) of 
159 
microbial metabolites. In attempt to extrapolate to in situ conditions, 13C-labeled lysates from a 
160 
soil bacterium were spiked into soil then analyzed for the components that remained in the 
161 
WEOM fraction. 
162 
Overall, this work lays a technical foundation for soil metabolomics including detailed 
163 
examination of the challenges that must be carefully considered when performing untargeted soil 
164 
metabolite profiling. Given the wide-spread availability of GC/MS instrumentation, this 
165 
workflow including stable isotope approaches has the potential to enable a wide range of 
166 
researchers to characterize metabolites in soils ultimately improving our understanding of 
167 
metabolic processes within soils.

180 
2.2. Soil collection, characterization and fumigation 
181 
Soil was collected from the Angelo Coast Range Reserve in Mendocino County, CA in 
182 
September 2013, prior to the first autumn rainfall. Dominant soils from this region are alfisols, 
183 
inceptisols, and ultisols with mixed minerology and a sandy loam texture as described earlier 
184 
(Cruz-Martínez et al., 2012). After removal of the residual litter layer, soil was sampled to 40 cm 
185 
depth and stored at -80°C in our laboratory until analysis. To determine the water content, a 
186 
subset of soil was oven-dried at 105°C until a constant weight. Soil pH (using a saturated paste) 
187 
and particle size (Sheldrick and Wang, 1993) were measured by the University of California 
188 
Davis Analytical Laboratory. Total dissolved organic carbon (TOC) was measured on a filtered 
189 
1:4 soil:water extract of unfumigated and fumigated soil (following the method of section 2.3) 
190 
(Shimadzu TOC-V CSH analyzer, Kyoto, Japan). Immediately prior to fumigation and 
191 

 
10
extractions, soil was sieved <2-mm using an ethanol-sterilized sieve (AS 200 sieve shaker, 
192 
Retsch, Haan, Germany) at 4°C. To measure total metabolites (defined here as microbial and 
193 
extracellular), soil was fumigated with ethanol-free chloroform for 24 h by the method of Vance 
194 
et al. (Vance et al., 1987).  
195 
 
196 
2.3. Soil extraction conditions 
197 
The following soil extraction method was based on a preliminary investigation of 
198 
optimizing experimental variables for soil metabolite extraction (see supplemental information). 
199 
Extractions were performed using either LC/MS-grade water (pH 7.4), 10 mM K2SO4 (pH 6.3), 
200 
10 mM NH4HCO3 (pH 8.5), isopropanol/methanol/water (3:3:2 v/v/v) or 10-100% methanol in 
201 
water. All extraction solutions were sterilized by filtration through 0.2 µm cellulose acetate 
202 
filters (Nalgene Rapid-Flow SFCA, Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA). Field-moist soil (2 g, 
203 
unfumigated or fumigated) was added to 50 mL polypropylene Falcon tubes and kept on ice. Ice-
204 
cold extraction solution (8 mL) was added followed by a spike with the internal standard, 
205 
ABMBA (5 µg). Samples were shaken on an orbital shaker (Orbital-Genie, Scientific Industries, 
206 
Bohemia, NY) at 200 rpm for 1 h at 4°C then centrifuged at 3220 x g for 15 min. The 
207 
supernatant was filtered through 0.45 µm syringe filters (Pall Acrodisc Supor membrane) into 15 
208 
mL tubes and dried in a Savant SpeedVac SPD111V (Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA) for 6-8 
209 
h. Dried residues were resuspended in 200 µl methanol, vortexed and sonicated for 5 min 
210 
followed by a final filtration through 0.22 µm centrifugal membranes (Ultrafree-MC-GV PVDF, 
211 
Millipore, Billerica, MA) and an aliquot (100 µl) dried for GC/MS derivatization. Soil 
212 
extractions were performed in quadruplicate.

2.4. Aqueous microbial metabolite spike-recovery 
215 
For the spike-recovery experiment, soil was sterilized in an oven at 105°C for 6 h and 
216 
checked for sterility by plating 100 µl aliquots (in triplicate) of centrifuged water extracts (2 g 
217 
soil + 8 mL water) on LB agar plates where no growth was observed. The soil isolate, 
218 
Pseudomonas stutzeri RCH2 was obtained from Romy Chakraborty, LBNL and grown at 37°C 
219 
in M9 minimal media (per liter: 6 g Na2HPO4, 3 g KH2PO4, 0.5 g NaCl, 1 g NH4Cl, 2 mM 
220 
MgSO4, 100 µM CaCl2). For unlabeled (12C) cultures, the minimal media was supplemented 
221 
with 0.2% sodium acetate as the carbon source, while the labeled (13C) cultures contained 13C2- 
222 
sodium acetate. Cultures were grown until an optical density of 0.5 (at 600 nm) was reached. 
223 
Cells were distributed into twelve 50 mL Falcon tubes and pelleted (by centrifuging at 3220 x g 
224 
for 10 min), washed with cold phosphate-buffered saline (pH 7.4) and re-pelleted. The 
225 
supernatant was discarded and pellets resuspended in 1 mL cold methanol. Cells were lysed by 
226 
sonicating for 2 x 20 s using a Q125 QSonica sonicator followed by 5 min in a sonicating water 
227 
bath (VWR symphony), centrifuged at 2348 x g for 5 min and the supernatant dried. Unlabeled 
228 
lysates were resuspended in 200 µl methanol and 100 µl were re-dried for GC/MS derivatization. 
229 
The 13C-labeled lysates were resuspended in 1 mL sterile LC/MS-grade water and pooled 
230 
together resulting in a total organic carbon content of 383 mg/L (Shimadzu TOC-V CSH 
231 
analyzer, Kyoto, Japan). Aliquots (1 mL) were incubated with sterile soil (2 g dry weight) in 50 
232 
mL Falcon tubes (or added to tubes not containing soil as a negative control) and shaken at 200 
233 
rpm at 4°C for 15 min. Extraction solution (8 mL, either LC/MS-grade H2O or 10 mM K2SO4) 
234 
was added to samples and shaken for an additional 1 h and extracted as in section 2.3. Three 
235 
biological replicates were performed for each condition. Dried extracts were resuspended in 200 
236 
µl methanol and 100 µl were re-dried and derivatized for GC/MS.

2.5. Sample Derivatization for GC/MS 
239 
Metabolite analysis was performed following methods described by Kind et al (2009). 
240 
Briefly, 10 µl of a solution containing 40 mg/mL of 98% methoxyamine hydrochloride in 
241 
pyridine was added and shaken for 90 min at 60°C. A mixture of internal retention time markers, 
242 
used to monitor retention time stability across runs, was prepared using fatty acid methyl esters 
243 
(FAMEs) of C8, C9, C10, C12, C14, C16, C18, C20, C22, C24, C26, C28 and C30 linear chain 
244 
length, dissolved in chloroform at a concentration of 0.8 mg/mL (C8-C16) and 0.4 mg/mL (C18-
245 
C30). The FAME mixture (20 µl) was added to 1 mL of MSTFA (containing 1% TMCS). To 
246 
each extract was then added 92 µl of MSTFA spiked with the FAME mixture followed by 
247 
mixing for 30 min at 37° C. 
248 
 
249 
2.6. GC/MS Data Acquisition 
250 
Derivatized samples were manipulated using a Gerstel automatic liner exchange MPS 
251 
system (Gerstel, Muehlheim, Germany) controlled by Maestro software to inject 0.5 µl of sample 
252 
into a Gerstel Cooled Injection System (CIS4). The injector was operated in splitless mode, and 
253 
the split vent was opened after 60 s. Samples were injected into the 50°C injector port which is 
254 
ramped to 270°C in a 12°C/s thermal gradient and held for 3 min. Volatilized metabolites were 
255 
separated using an Agilent 7890 gas chromatograph (Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA), 
256 
controlled by Agilent GC/MS MassHunter Acquisition software. The gas chromatograph was 
257 
equipped with a 30 m long, 0.25 mm ID Rtx5Sil-MS column (Restek, Bellefonte, PA), 0.25 mm 
258 
5% diphenyl film with a 10 m integrated guard column. Initial oven temperature was 50°C with 
259 
the following gradient applied: ramp at 5°C min-1 to 65°C, held for 0.2 min; ramp at 15°C min-1 
260 

 
13
to 80°C, held for 0.2 min; ramp at 15°C min-1 to 310°C, held for 12 min. Mass spectrometry was 
261 
performed by an Agilent 5977 single quadrupole mass spectrometer (MS) with 250°C transfer 
262 
line temperature, electron ionization at 70 eV and an ion source temperature of 230°C. Mass 
263 
spectra were acquired from 50–700 m/z at 8 spectra s-1. Blanks (prepared as in section 2.5, but 
264 
starting with an empty Eppendorf tube) were run between every 6 samples and a quality control 
265 
mix was run as five replicates before and after the sample set. 
266 
 
267 
2.7. GC/MS data analysis 
268 
Initial data analysis was done with Agilent MassHunter Qualitative Analysis software 
269 
(Agilent Technologies, Santa Clara, CA). Total ion chromatograms were integrated, with each 
270 
integrated peak was associated a cleaned spectra post background subtraction. Retention index 
271 
markers (FAMEs) were monitored across runs to ensure consistent retention time alignment and 
272 
instrument sensitivity. Compound identification was based on an orthogonal approach in 
273 
accordance with the Metabolomics Standards Initiative (Sumner et al., 2007). First, peak lists 
274 
were queried against the Agilent Fiehn Metabolomics mass spectral library of over 1,000 curated 
275 
metabolite spectra with a required minimum match score of 70%. Secondly, the identification of 
276 
these matches was confirmed by comparing retention times and spectra with authentic standards. 
277 
In the cases where matches could not be confirmed due to retention time discrepancies (± 10 sec 
278 
or more) with an authentic standard or unavailability of an authentic standard, the compound 
279 
identities are considered putatively characterized (based solely on spectral similarity with the 
280 
Fiehn library) as indicated in the figures and tables. Resultant compound lists were aligned by 
281 
retention times using custom software developed in-house using the Matlab (Mathworks, Natick, 
282 
MA) programming language followed by manual curation for verifying proper data alignment 
283 

 
14
and compound annotation. For retention times with more than one compound hit, the final 
284 
compound identification was determined based on manual investigation of the data (presence in 
285 
the largest number of samples and highest abundance). Significant differences between 
286 
unfumigated and fumigated samples were analyzed by Student’s t-test with a p value < 0.05 
287 
considered statistically significant.  
288 
For the spike-recovery experiment, spectra from unlabeled RCH2 (12C) were compared to 
289 
labeled RCH2 (13C) for the identification of m/z shifts corresponding to 13C labeling of 
290 
fragments. These 13C-m/z (target ions) originating from the labeled RCH2 were quantified in all 
291 
samples within the corresponding retention time window by subtracting the 13C-m/z contribution 
292 
from soil: 
293 
[13C-m/z peak area soil+lysates] - [13C-m/z peak area soil] = recovered 13C-m/z lysates 
294 
Percent recovery of labeled metabolites were calculated by: 
295 
[recovered 13C-m/z lysates  ∕  added 13C-m/z lysates, no soil] x 100 = % recovered  
296 
Compounds for the spike-recovery experiment were identified based on multiple 
297 
approaches: comparing spectra from the unlabeled RCH2 with the Fiehn and National Institute 
298 
of Standards and Technology mass spectral libraries, confirming retention times and spectra with 
299 
authentic standards, and based on 13C patterns in spectra (and comparing to 12C spectra) to 
300 
determine the number of carbons in the compound.

3.2. Detection of a wide range of soil metabolites using GC/MS 
309 
 
Our untargeted profiling soil extraction method (Fig. 1) and GC/MS analyses revealed a 
310 
wide range of metabolites (Fig. 2, Supp. Table 1) in the Angelo sandy loam soil. Initial analyses 
311 
led to the detection of approximately 300 molecular features at unique retention times. However, 
312 
many of these features were determined to be contaminants, derivatization artifacts and/or 
313 
unidentified metabolites. After further investigation, the list was narrowed down to 55 features 
314 
that were annotated to the Fiehn spectral metabolite database (Kind et al., 2009). Most 
315 
metabolites (80%) were verified with authentic standards in accordance with the guidelines of 
316 
the Metabolomics Standards Initiative (Sumner et al., 2007) and those that could not be 
317 
confirmed are considered putative and indicated by italics (Fig. 2). Metabolites that were 
318 
detected in all extracts and were among the most intense peaks include sugars (sucrose, glucose, 
319 
trehalose, fructose) and the sugar alcohol, mannitol. Nine amino acids and many amino acid 
320 
metabolites were also consistently detected. Other metabolites, more dependent on extraction 
321 
conditions, include other sugars and sugar alcohols, fatty acids, dicarboxylic acids, sterols and 
322 
nucleobases (Supp. Table 1). As is common in GC/MS, multiple peaks were detected for some 
323 
metabolites, which is especially problematic for sugars due to the many isomers and silylation 
324 
positions (Blau and Halket, 1993; Bertozzi and Rabuka, 2009; Ruiz-Matute et al., 2011). In these 
325 
cases, we report the most intense peak and in the few situations where we could not discriminate 
326 
between isomers they are presented as two identities (e.g. lyxose/ xylose).

3.3. Effects of extractant solutions on soil metabolite extraction 
329 
Extractant comparison included a small set of common additives to determine what, if 
330 
any, additional types of soil metabolites can be detected with our extraction and GC/MS 
331 
workflow. Additions included a classical soil extractant salt (K2SO4), a volatile salt (NH4HCO3), 
332 
and standard metabolomics organic solvents (methanol and isopropanol) commonly included to 
333 
induce cell lysis and/or to improve recovery of non-polar metabolites. Although 500 mM K2SO4 
334 
is commonly used, we used 10 mM since higher concentrations interfered with GC/MS 
335 
derivatization in our studies and low-salt concentrations have been reported as effective in earlier 
336 
studies (Gaillard et al., 1999, Haney et al., 2001, Zsolnay, 2003). Hierarchical clustering was 
337 
used to compare soil metabolite peak areas (normalized to the most intense peak for each 
338 
metabolite) across all 16 extraction conditions (eight extractants of unfumigated or fumigated 
339 
soil). This approach grouped compounds together with similar extraction patterns as shown in 
340 
Figure 2. Some metabolite peaks were more intense in the aqueous extractions (e.g. serine, 
341 
threonine, glycine, isoleucine, alanine, valine, 3-hydroxybutyric acid, malonic acid and many 
342 
sugars and sugar alcohols; lower half of Fig. 2) while others were only observed in soil extracted 
343 
with high organic solvent content (ergosterol, fucose, stigmasterol, oleic acid, linoleic acid, 
344 
palmitoleic acid; top of Fig. 2). The total number of compounds detected in each extraction 
345 
condition was surprisingly similar (Supp. Fig. 1). Notably, the addition of 10 mM K2SO4 or 10 
346 
mM NH4HCO3 did not increase the number of compounds compared to water alone.

3.5. Microbial metabolite adsorption and recovery 
361 
To explore the types of microbial metabolites that adsorb to soil and therefore may 
362 
represent those that are unavailable to microbes in situ, 13C-labeled RCH2 bacterial lysates were 
363 
incubated with soil and extracted with water (Fig. 3). K2SO4 (10 mM) was compared to water to 
364 
determine if this classical extractant was more efficient at metabolite recovery. The most intense 
365 
peaks from the unlabeled RCH2 lysates (grown with 12C-acetate) were selected for further 
366 
analysis in labeled samples (compound identification, 13C incorporation and recovery 
367 
calculations). Spectra from unlabeled RCH2 were compared to labeled cultures, which resulted 
368 
in the identification of 45 fully-labeled metabolites (Fig. 4, Supp. Table 3). The 13C target ion for 
369 
each metabolite was quantified for all samples and recovery was calculated. Only 27% of the 
370 
quantified metabolites showed >50% recovery in the WEOM, with the majority of those being 
371 
neutral metabolites. All of the polyamine cations identified in this experiment (putrescine, 
372 

 
18
cadaverine, lysine, spermine and spermidine) and most of the anionic metabolites were among 
373 
those that adsorbed rapidly to soil (Fig. 4). The inclusion of 10 mM K2SO4 as a recovery 
374 
extractant did not drastically improve or reduce recovery compared to water alone.  
375 
 
376 
4. DISCUSSION 
377 
GC/MS is a simple and suitable approach for untargeted soil metabolomics 
378 
GC/MS is a popular analytical technique for analyses of metabolites in a variety of 
379 
biological systems. In this study we aimed to develop a simple and robust GC/MS-based 
380 
metabolomics workflow to characterize the chemical composition of the extractable fraction of 
381 
soil, which consists of important microbial products, and to assess microbial metabolite recovery 
382 
from soil. These foundational studies present the ability to perform untargeted global profiling of 
383 
WEOM in soils and to detect and track microbial metabolites as a function of their soil-
384 
adsorptive properties (which can be expanded to other soil types and minerals).  
385 
 
386 
Detected metabolites play important roles in soil microbial communities 
387 
Numerous studies have shown that SOM composition has a major impact on the activity, 
388 
abundance and composition of microbial biomass in soils (Xu et al., 2014). Specifically, in 
389 
addition to microbial products, plant exudates (consisting mostly of sugars, amino acids and 
390 
organic acids) stimulate microbial activity in soils (Jones et al., 2004). Overall, our soil 
391 
extraction and metabolomics workflow detected a wide range of small molecules that have 
392 
critical roles in the biological activity of soils. Studies have shown that when glucose is 
393 
administered to soil it is rapidly assimilated into microbial biomass (Sugai and Schimel, 1993; 
394 
Kuzyakov and Jones, 2006) an indication that microbial communities will respond quickly to 
395 

 
19
changes in SOM composition resulting from fluctuating inputs from aboveground (e.g. plant 
396 
diversity and productivity) and belowground (e.g. root exudates, microbial population). 
397 
Production of small molecule osmolytes (e.g. proline, trehalose, fructose, sucrose) allow soil 
398 
microbes to respond to altered water conditions (Warren, 2014) and monitoring these small 
399 
molecules in DOM may provide insight into the physiological status of soil systems. Microbial 
400 
growth has also been shown to be mediated more by nitrogen rather than soil carbon content 
401 
(Eisenhauer et al., 2010) indicating that amino acids play a key role in regulating these microbial 
402 
communities.  
403 
 
404 
Addition of dilute salts has a weak effect on metabolite coverage 
405 
In order to demonstrate the suitability of an untargeted metabolite profiling approach for 
406 
detection of a range of compounds, we developed a rapid workflow to evaluate WEOM from soil 
407 
and compared a limited set of conditions. Water is an advantageous extractant in that it is simple 
408 
and may be more reflective of actual microbe-accessible DOM in soils (Blagodatsky et al., 
409 
2010). However, the addition of salts (typically K2SO4) is thought to be required to release 
410 
metabolites bound to mineral surfaces or clay particles somewhat analogous to ion exchange 
411 
chromatography. Our preliminary studies of more concentrated salt solutions showed that 
412 
concentrations greater than 10 mM were incompatible with GC/MS by forming excessive salt 
413 
crystals that likely trap metabolites and also retain water causing interference with derivatization. 
414 
The use of less than the standard salt concentration (500 mM) was still in agreement with earlier 
415 
soil extraction methods (Gregorich et al., 2000; Haney et al., 2001). However, even with 10 mM 
416 
K2SO4, salt crystals formed during dry-down which may have contributed to a slightly weaker 
417 
metabolite profile compared to water. Although we did not expand our experiments to include 
418 

 
20
buffers with a range of ionic strengths, it is likely that the efficiency of salt-based extractants 
419 
compared with water is dependent on the ionic strength of the solution (Gregorich et al., 2000; 
420 
Makarov et al., 2013) and on soil properties such as acidity and carbon/ nitrogen content (Haney 
421 
et al., 2001; Makarov et al., 2013). Specifically, in Haney et al (2001) and confirmed here, the 
422 
extractable yield is higher using water compared to K2SO4 for acidic soils such as the one 
423 
examined in this study. Overall, these studies show that 10 mM K2SO4 and 10 mM NH4HCO3 
424 
are compatible with mass spectrometry instrumentation, but are not more efficient at extracting 
425 
metabolites than water alone.  
426 
 
427 
Water is a suitable extractant for exometabolomics 
428 
Traditionally, another reason for including salts in extractants is to reduce osmotic shock 
429 
and prevent cell lysis or metabolite leakage during extraction (Gregorich et al., 2000) and this 
430 
may be another explanation for the richer metabolite profile in the water extracts. However, the 
431 
significant differences observed between the fumigated versus unfumigated treatments in the 
432 
water-extracted group suggest that lysis in the unfumigated samples is far from complete. This 
433 
result is consistent with the common interpretation of water-extractable metabolites as 
434 
extracellular metabolites (Gregorich et al., 2000), i.e. exometabolites, and it may be that soil 
435 
itself is sufficient for balancing salt concentrations.  In situations where it is necessary to include 
436 
a buffering reagent, NH4HCO3 may be the salt of choice when combining soil extractions with 
437 
mass spectrometry-based metabolomics since it can be removed by drying. In some cases, salt 
438 
concentrations can be decreased by using low molecular weight dialysis (Murage and Voroney, 
439 
2007), but was not used in our studies due to concerns about metabolite loss. 
440 

 
21
As expected, we found that aqueous solutions, due to their polar nature, were better at 
441 
extracting small, polar metabolites while organic solvents-based solutions extracted fatty acids 
442 
and sterols. Our results for 3:3:2 extractions support earlier studies in demonstrating a broad 
443 
coverage of metabolites (Lee et al., 2012), but extracted a similar number of compounds as water 
444 
and the other extractants (Supp. Fig. 1). While water appears to be an excellent soil extractant, if 
445 
the goal is to analyze fatty acids or sterols, higher concentrations of organic solvent are required.  
446 
 
447 
Fumigation prior to extraction allows detection of significantly more microbial metabolites  
448 
The original chloroform vapor fumigation method by Vance et al. (1987) was used rather 
449 
than direct extraction with chloroform since it would have interfered with comparing the 
450 
extraction efficiency of methanol and isopropanol due to miscibility. We found that fumigation 
451 
significantly increased the number and peak intensity of metabolites detected from soils. In fact, 
452 
hierarchical clustering based on metabolite patterns clustered fumigated samples with the 100% 
453 
methanol and 3:3:2 extracts of unfumigated soil (Supp. Fig. 2) supporting the notion that 
454 
chloroform vapor lyses microbial cells (just as organic solvent does when used as an extractant). 
455 
Many of the metabolites that increased after fumigation were also among the most recoverable 
456 
RCH2 bacterial metabolites detected in the spike-recovery experiments (e.g. alanine, valine, 
457 
isoleucine, glycine, threonine, serine) providing further evidence that these are microbial 
458 
metabolites. Compounds observed at higher abundances in unfumigated soils compared to 
459 
fumigated (trehalose, ergosterol and 5-hydroxy-tryptophan) could be the result of continued 
460 
metabolic activity and/ or increased enzymatic degradation of those metabolites during 24 h of 
461 
fumigation. The use of water as an extractant of fumigated soil is a suitable approach to examine 
462 
both (intracellular) microbial metabolites and exometabolites (Supp. Fig. 3), but some drawbacks 
463 

 
22
of fumigation need to be carefully considered (difficulty in fumigating dry soil, recolonization of 
464 
microbial communities, altered microbial enzymatic activity, chloroform exposure time 
465 
differences) (Brookes et al., 1985; Tate et al., 1988; Haubensak et al., 2002; Renella et al., 2002). 
466 
 
467 
Some microbial metabolites rapidly adsorb to soil 
468 
Soil physical properties such as mineral composition, surface area, shape and porosity 
469 
present a series of unique challenges to soil extraction and metabolomics approaches (Skopp, 
470 
2000). To gain insights into these confounding variables and to establish a method to estimate 
471 
what types of metabolites may be available to microbes in situ, we added bacterial extracts to 
472 
soil samples and determined the fractional recovery with water. Our results indicate that even 
473 
after a short incubation, potentially mimicking immediately after the onset of rainfall, many 
474 
metabolites are adsorbed to soil. Cationic and anionic metabolites were most affected, while the 
475 
majority of neutral metabolites were recovered. An important consideration is that the 
476 
metabolites not recovered represent molecules that are often overlooked in aqueous soil 
477 
extraction studies aimed at examining total SOM. Interestingly, the polyamines that were 
478 
detected in the bacterial lysates were among the least-recoverable from soil. The loss of these 
479 
compounds is unlikely due to microbial metabolism since the soil was sterilized by oven-heating 
480 
(105 °C). These findings are consistent with numerous reports of amines adsorbing to clay 
481 
material in soil (Zhang et al., 1993; Lee et al., 1997) as well as other metabolites, especially 
482 
carboxylic acids, potentially forming covalent bonds with organic matter or being chelated by 
483 
metals or other ions (originating from soil or from the extractant) (Jones et al., 2003; Fischer et 
484 
al., 2010). Therefore, cationic compounds such as polyamines may represent an inaccessible 
485 
biological class of substrates that are not present in DOM in situ. While we focused on RCH2 
486 

 
23
metabolite adsorption to a sandy loam soil, the system described could be further parameterized: 
487 
microbial metabolites (from other species), soil types, metabolite-soil contact time, incubation 
488 
temperature and recovery extractants (with ionic strength or pH similar to rainwater). This spike-
489 
recovery method holds great promise to further our understanding of how specific metabolites 
490 
will behave in response to future climatic changes (e.g. precipitation) thereby affecting substrate 
491 
availability and the structure of soil microbial communities. 
492 
 
493 
CONCLUSION 
494 
Here we report a GC/MS soil metabolomics workflow that was used to profile a diverse 
495 
set of metabolites in a sandy loam soil using a variety of extractants and to examine microbial 
496 
metabolite adsorption. We found that water is an excellent starting point to examine both 
497 
microbe-accessible extracellular soil nutrients (using unfumigated soil) and total extractable 
498 
organic matter (using fumigated soil) while the addition of organic solvent can expand lipid 
499 
coverage. Our spike-recovery studies revealed a set of metabolites (mostly cationic polyamines 
500 
and anions) that are not recovered using aqueous extractants and represent an important set of 
501 
biological compounds that may be inaccessible to microbes in the environment. Together this 
502 
work provides a rapid and simple foundation for GC/MS soil metabolite profiling methods that 
503 
are readily accessible to microbiologists and soil scientists as efforts continue in exploring the 
504 
chemical composition and dyamics of complex soil systems.
