21 The cycling of soil organic matter (SOM) by microorganisms is a critical component of 22 the global carbon cycle but remains poorly understood. There is an emerging view that much of 23 SOM, and especially the dissolved fraction (DOM), is composed of small molecules of plant and 24 microbial origin resulting from lysed cells and released metabolites. Unfortunately, little is 25 known about the small molecule composition of soils and how these molecules are cycled (by 26 microbes or plants or by adsorption to mineral surfaces). The water-extractable organic matter 27 (WEOM) fraction is of particular interest given that this is presumably the most biologically- 28 accessible component of SOM. Here we describe the development of a simple soil metabolomics 29 workflow and a novel spike recovery approach using 13C bacterial lysates to assess the types of 30 metabolites remaining in the WEOM fraction. Soil samples were extracted with multiple mass 31 spectrometry-compatible extraction buffers (water, 10 mM K2SO4 or NH4HCO3, 10-100% 32 methanol or isopropanol/methanol/water [3:3:2 v/v/v]) with and without prior chloroform vapor 33 fumigation. Profiling of derivatized extracts was performed using gas chromatography/ mass 34 spectrometry (GC/MS) with 55 metabolites identified by comparing fragmentation patterns and 35 retention times with authentic standards. As expected, fumigation, which is thought to lyse 36 microbial cells, significantly increased the range and abundance of metabolites relative to 37 unfumigated samples. To assess the types of microbial metabolites from lysed bacterial cells that 38 remain in the WEOM fraction, an extract was prepared from the soil bacterium Pseudomonas 39 stutzerii RCH2 grown on 13C acetate. This approach produced highly labeled metabolites that 40 were easily discriminated from the endogenous soil metabolites.
