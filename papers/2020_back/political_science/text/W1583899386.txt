 Long-term Trends in International Migration:  
An Analysis of the Six Main Receiving Countries 
In a global context of growing international mobility and rising 
education levels, emigration by highly skilled individuals is, not 
surprisingly, on the increase. To observe the brain drain,  an analysis 
by individual sending countries is more instructive than a general 
overview. In this article, Cécily Defoort uses an OECD database 
on immigrant stocks by skill level in the six main OECD receiving 
countries to reconstitute country-specific trends in emigration 
rates among highly skilled workers over the period 1975-2000. 
Small countries, like those of the Caribbean and Oceania, are 
especially affected. Their lack of university infrastructure makes 
them particularly vulnerable to the permanent loss of students who 
emigrate for study purposes and do not return home. Among larger 
countries, some traditional emigration flows continue throughout 
the study period, affecting countries like Kenya and Uganda, for 
example, though also the United Kingdom. The countries identified 
as badly affected in 2000, but not in 1975, include large countries 
of Africa and South Asia. On the other hand, although vast nations 
like China and India export large numbers of skilled migrants, their 
seemingly inexhaustible reserves of human capital mean that the 
local impact of these losses is slight.
International migration has increased considerably since the 1960s. 
According to the UN, the number of people living outside their country of 
birth rose from 75 million in 1960 to 175 million in 2000 (United Nations, 
2002). Because of their consequences for sending and receiving countries alike, 
the impact of these flows has always attracted attention from political decision-
makers. A key question, with far-reaching implications for development in the 
countries of origin, is that of migrant skill levels.
The literature on the impact of brain drain on sending countries began to 
expand during the 1960s. A first wave of research viewed migration by skilled 
workers in an optimistic light as being beneficial in the long run. Assuming

inflows, return migration and the development of networks could offset the 
negative short-term effects associated with loss of the most skilled individuals 
(Grubel and Scott, 1966; Johnson, 1967).(1) However, in a second wave of

authors emphasized the adverse consequences for the countries of origin, 
notably the net loss of the educational investment in their native populations

“brain drain tax”, dubbed the Bhagwati tax (Bhagwati, 1972), to be levied on 
emigrants living in developed countries with the aim of compensating the 
poorest countries for their human capital losses. Emigration was thus seen as 
threat to economic growth in sending countries, with the potential to curb 
their future development prospects.
A further change of direction in the research literature occurred in the 
1990s. Economists once again emphasized the potentially positive effects of 
brain drains, hitherto judged marginal. To the “classic” externalities of skilled 
migration were added the concepts of “migration perspectives” and “migrant 
selection” in developed countries. Economists demonstrated that emigration 
by highly skilled workers could, under certain conditions, have a beneficial 
impact for the countries of origin, in particular when individuals decide to 
acquire a skill with a view to emigrating at a later date but, for various reasons, 
are then unable to leave their country of origin. This turnaround is abundantly 
documented in the literature on skilled migration (Stark et al., 1997; Mountford, 
1997; Beine et al., 2001, 2008).
Empirical approaches, for their part, have been hampered by a lack of 
standardized and continuous data on international migration. Difficulties are 
linked notably to the problem of establishing an international definition of 
migrant status and to the emergence of migrant flows from new countries 
following the collapse of the Soviet regime. Important progress was made when 
Carrington and Detragiache (1998, 1999), two researchers at the IMF, estimated 
rates of emigration to the OECD countries from 61 developing countries for 
1990, distributed across three skill levels (using Barro and Lee’s (2000) data 
on capital human and those of the OECD for migration). This database marks 
the starting point for the empirical study of brain drains. Adams (2003) 
subsequently introduced the temporal dimension by supplying emigration 
data for 24 sending countries in 2000. Using census data for the OECD countries, 
Dumont and Lemaître (2005) calculated emigration rates by skill level for 
around 100 sending countries in 2000. In the same vein, Docquier and Marfouk 
(2005) produced new estimates of numbers and emigration rates by level of 
skill for 195 sending countries in 2000 and 175 countries in 1990. These data 
can be used to test empirically the theories of international migration (link

The main purpose of this article is to present a new database on international 
migration constructed using a method similar to that used by Carrington and 
Detragiache (1998, 1999) and Docquier and Marfouk (2005). We assembled 
data on resident migrants in the six main OECD receiving countries (United 
States, Canada, Australia, United Kingdom, Germany and France) and on the 
structure by skill level of emigrant and resident populations for all countries 
of the world between 1975 and 2000 (five-yearly observations), covering a 
longer period than was the case in previous studies. Since current migrant 
stocks depend on past and present migration decisions, a database organized 
on chronological lines is particularly useful for empirical research.
After describing the method used to construct the chronological database, 
we present the long-term trends in human capital and in migration of skills 
by world region. We then analyse the changes in migration rates of highly 
skilled individuals by country between 1975 and 2000. Last, we focus our 
attention on the countries where the impact from migration of the most skilled 
workers is greatest relative to population size.
I. Data and method
In this section we describe the data and the method used to estimate the 
stocks and emigration rates from all countries of the world since 1975. Stock 
variables are more appropriate for analysing the endogeneity and dynamics of 
migratory movements (the equilibrium values are always given as stocks, not 
flows). From a statistical viewpoint, migration flows are less reliable than 
stocks, since return migration (outflows) is impossible to quantify.
The method employed is in three stages. We begin by estimating immigrant 
numbers in the main OECD receiving countries by skill level and country of 
origin. In a second stage, this information is used to reconstruct stocks of 
emigrants by country of origin. Lastly, with these figures for emigrant numbers 
and the resident population size in the sending countries, we derive estimates 
of emigration rates for each sending country. These three stages involve a set 
of assumptions that must be described in detail.

1. Numbers of international migrants
To reconstruct emigrant numbers by skill level we first use information 
about the number of immigrants aged over 25 in the main receiving countries. 
As it is practically impossible to quantify emigration from every country in 
the world, we use the figures for immigrant numbers from the population 
censuses of the six main OECD receiving countries: Australia, Canada, United 
States, United Kingdom, France and Germany (OECD-6 hereafter). These are 
the six countries for which data are available for the entire period from 1975 
(including for Germany with its current borders). Our principal focus is on 
South-North migration.
Immigrant numbers are normally available by country of birth, except for 
Germany where the breakdown for 1980, 1990 and 2000 is by nationality. The 
information for Germany comes from the Mikrozensus of the Statistiches 
Bundesamt (Federal Institute of Statistics). Using citizenship rather than 
country of origin may lead to overestimating migrant skill levels: in principle, 
a German-born adult of foreign nationality (a Turk, for example) will on average 
have higher skills than a Turkish-born individual who moved to Germany as 
an adult. Furthermore, the number of individuals applying for German 
nationality rises with skill level.(3) This latter factor may lead to underestimating 
the stock of skilled migrants in the receiving country. Although it is hard to 
quantify the effect of each of these factors, the influence of the former appears 
to outweigh that of the latter. We can therefore assume an overestimation of 
the number of skilled immigrants in Germany that cannot be corrected using 
the available data.
Migrants living in the OECD-6 countries represent approximately 77% of 
aggregate immigrant populations in the OECD countries in 2000, so our estimates 
can be taken as representative of total South-North immigration. The potential 
bias introduced by this restriction will be discussed in the next section.
The years for which information is not available (between censuses) are 
handled by interpolation (1995 and 1985) or retropolation (1975). To perform 
the interpolations we use the OECD complementary data (SOPEMI, 2005). 
These data are generally less detailed than the censuses, but they give total 
immigrant numbers for 1980-1990 and for 1990-2000, and in some instances 
a distribution by region. Knowing the total immigrant stock for the intermediate 
years (1985 and 1995), we calculate the worldwide numbers according to skill 
level in each destination country by multiplying the total by the proportion 
of highly (and medium) skilled persons among the immigrants (hmt). This 
proportion is defined in relation to the proportions for the previous and

Our method has several limitations:
First, owing to non-availability of data, not every OECD receiving country 
is included in our database. Since we focus essentially on South-North migration, 
this introduces a slight bias into our estimates.(5)
Second, our database does not include illegal immigration. According to 
the US Immigration and Naturalization Service, the illegally resident population 
in the United States numbered 3.5 million in January 1990 and 7 million in 
January 2000. While the principal country of origin can be established for most 
of these migrants (nearly 70% came from Mexico in 2000), their skill level 
remains unknown. Data for the other OECD countries are non-existent or 
extremely unreliable,(6) and hence have not been included in our database. 
Assuming that the majority of illegal migrants are low skilled, it is likely that 
our database overestimates the proportion of highly skilled migrants.

Third, we have no information on age at entry to the host country. 
Consequently we cannot distinguish immigrants who were educated in their 
country of origin from those educated in their destination country. This is a 
major drawback, since it is difficult to see how an individual born in Côte d’Ivoire 
who comes to France to study can represent an example of brain drain. Restricting 
the database to individuals aged 25 and over (see below) does, however, exclude 
people who migrated solely for study purposes (because there was no higher 
education in their home country, for example, as is the case in many of the small 
islands badly affected by emigration). Once again, the data supplying age at entry 
to the host country are not available for the entire period under consideration. 
The only such available data come from Beine et al. (2007). They use a gravity 
model to estimate the age at entry of migrants, distinguishing migration after 
age 12, after age 18 and after age 22 for immigrants in the OECD countries for 
which this information is not available (23%). Age at entry is the basis for 
determining whether education was received in the sending or the receiving 
country. These estimates are available from 1990 and show a close correlation 
between the data by birth country and the data by country where skills were 
acquired: the countries hardest hit by brain drain identified from sending country 
data are on the whole the same as those identified from receiving country data. 
This result suggests that the bias will also be small in our database, which is 
built using the same methodology as that of Docquier and Marfouk (2005) and 
does not include the country where skills were acquired.
Fourth, we restrict attention to the population of working age (age 25 
or over). There are two reasons for this choice. First, when calculating emigration 
rates we use the stocks of resident population, which, as we shall see later, are 
only available for the over-25s. Another reason is that working on stocks of 
migrants aged 25 or over, rather than on total migrant numbers, removes from 
the database those individuals who migrate solely to study and who go straight 
back to their country of origin after completing their education. This decision 
limits the bias introduced by migrants being considered by their country of 
birth rather than by the country where they were educated (see above). Confining 
attention to the population aged 25 and over is one reason why our estimates 
are lower than those of the UN.(7) 
Fifth, a powerful critique can be made regarding comparability of skill 
levels worldwide. A diploma in medicine or financial management may have 
a particular value in the US, but it is uncertain that the same diploma can be 
treated as exactly equal when awarded in a country whose educational system 
is relatively under-developed. Coulombe et al. (2004) addressed this problem. 
Data from the 1994 International Adult Literacy Survey (IALS) was used to

2. Reliability of migrant stocks measured  
for the six main receiving countries
As noted at several points, figures for emigrant stocks by country of 
origin are derived from data on immigrant stocks in the six main OECD 
receiving countries. They do not represent the totality of migrant destination 
countries, however, since much international migration occurs between 
countries of the South. Migration to non-OECD countries generally involves 
unskilled migration, excepting for a few countries including South Africa 
(1.3 million immigrants in 2000), six Gulf states (9.6 million immigrants in 
Saudi Arabia, United Arab Emirates, Kuwait, Bahrain, Oman, and Qatar), 
plus a few countries in East Asia (4 million immigrants in Hong Kong and 
Singapore). However, according to Docquier and Marfouk (2005), skilled 
emigration to the OECD countries as a whole accounts for 90% of skilled 
migrants in the world. Since the six main receiving countries in our database 
attract 86% of skill migration, we therefore capture over 77% of global skilled 
emigration. Our database underestimates emigrant numbers for some twenty 
countries of origin (Egypt, Sudan, Jordan, Yemen, Pakistan, Namibia, 
Zimbabwe, Democratic Republic of Congo, Suriname, Mozambique, Angola, 
etc.), though with data from national statistical offices it is not possible to 
improve the base’s cross-sectional dimension while at the same time maintaining 
its longitudinal coverage.

emigrants to the OECD-6 countries accounting for a minimum of 75% of 
emigrants in OECD countries in 2000. This finding shows that the destination 
countries in our analysis receive proportionally more skilled migrants than 
the other OECD countries. 
Maps 1a and 1b and 2a to 2d show that the “least reliable” countries are 
randomly distributed. In other words, they are not necessarily the smallest or 
poorest countries. The countries with the lowest proportion of migrants in 
the six main OECD receiving countries are in Latin America (although rates 
of skilled emigration are over 70%  reliable for all the countries of South 
American except Brazil). The Democratic Republic of Congo, Angola and 
Namibia also have low levels of reliability, as have Morocco and Mauritania 
when total emigration rates are considered (the rate for skilled emigration from 
Morocco is 75.4% reliable).
Last, new receiving countries have emerged in southern Europe since 
the mid-1990s, chiefly Italy, Spain and Portugal. The rapid growth in migrant 
flows to these countries could be expected to generate a large bias in our 
database. In fact, however, the bias is relatively small for two reasons. First, 
as we are working on the period 1975-2000, the bias should only affect the 
last year of the database. Second, since we work on migrant stocks and not 
migrant flows, the large increase in flows to the southern Europe countries 
in the period 1995-2000 has only a limited impact on stocks in 2000. The 
flow of new migrants is largely “diluted” in the stock. The new international 
migration flows are included in the database of Docquier and Marfouk (2005), 
who treat all the OECD countries as receiving countries. But although the 
large increase in these flows is taken into account, the rates for skilled and 
total emigration remain extremely stable over the period 1990-2000, confirming 
the “dilution” of the increased flows in the emigration stocks of the sending 
countries.
In the remainder of this study, we consider the countries of which at least 
75% of emigrants to OECD countries reside in the six main receiving 
countries.

II. Trends in human capital and international migration, 
1975-2000
A database covering an extended period is of value chiefly for analysing 
long-term trends. Our database provides estimates of skill levels and emigration 
rates in the period 1975-2000 and can thus be used to analyse the brain drain 
during the last thirty years. The number of international migrants grew 
considerably between 1975 and 2000, more than doubling according to UN 
estimates. As noted earlier, unlike the UN, we consider only individuals aged 
25 or over, but the overall change observed is the same. According to our data, 
the total number of adult international migrants in the six main OECD receiving 
countries rose from nearly 20 million in 1975 to over 44 million in 2000, a 
more than two-fold increase. The growth was even stronger for migration by 
highly skilled persons, whose number increased four-fold between 1975 and 
2000 (nearly 4.3 million skilled international migrants in 1975 against nearly 
17.3 million in 2000, equivalent to annual growth of 5.7%). However, it is 
noteworthy that the number of skilled workers in resident populations was 
also increasing over the same period in most countries of the world. Table 3.1 
presents the change in the share of highly skilled workers in the total resident 
population by geographical sub-region.(9) At the global level, this proportion 
rose from 5.6% in 1975 to 11.4% in 2000. However, the increase affected different 
countries in different ways.
The regions with the lowest proportion of skilled workers are generally in 
Africa (to a lesser extent in North African countries), but also include certain 
Asian countries and Pacific Islands.(10) In these extremely poor regions, the 
proportion of skilled labour in the resident population was around 3% in 2000. 
The highest skill levels are observed in the most developed OECD countries, 
notably in North America, EU-15 and in Australia and New Zealand. In the 
other countries, the proportion of skilled labour in the resident population is 
between 5% and 8%. But are the countries with highest emigration rates of 
skilled workers those with the lowest share of skilled workers in the resident 
population?

III. Trends in skilled emigration rates by country 
If we focus on rates of skilled emigration for all the countries in the world, 
Figure 4a is particularly instructive. It represents the density of skilled emigration 
rates for all the countries in the study (for which the rates are considered 
reliable), comparing these distributions in 1975, 1990 and 2000. Figure 4b 
depicts the density of unskilled emigration rates.(13)
Since 1975, there has been an increase in the number of countries where 
the emigration rate for the highly skilled – the “brain drain rate”– is low (below 
1%) or comparatively high (between 1.5% and 30%), but a decrease in the 
number where it is over 30% (Figure 4a). At the same time, a fall is observed 
in the number of countries where the emigration rate of low-skilled adults is 
close to zero (below 0.2%), between 3% and 8%, or over 22% (Figure 4b). On 
the other hand, there was an increase in countries where the unskilled emigration 
rate is approximately between 0.2% and 3% or between 3% and 8%. This reflects 
the general rise in mobility levels throughout the world, made possible by ever 
lower migration costs and by the growth of migrant networks, since the 
existence of networks in destination countries tends to encourage migration 
by the low skilled (McKenzie and Rapoport, 2007).
Maps 3a and 3b represent country-level skilled emigration rates to OECD-6.

broadly the same in 2000 as in 1975. This applies specifically to the African 
countries (mainly sub-Saharan), the Caribbean, the Pacific Islands, and Central 
American countries. Africa and South America are shown in slightly lighter 
tones in Map 3b, suggesting a reduction in the brain drain in some countries 
despite an increase at regional level. This result reflects the widening disparities 
between countries in the African continent: since 1975, the situation for the 
worst-affected countries has deteriorated while for the others it has 
improved.

value of the skilled emigration rates is close to 13% for the 25 large countries

Conclusion
This article has presented a new database on international migration to 
the six main OECD receiving countries since 1975. It shows that skill levels 
at age 25 and over have risen across the world since 1975, with the proportion 
of skilled individuals in resident populations climbing from 5.6% to 11.4%. 
But this increase is very unevenly distributed, and skill levels in the poorest 
countries have remained particularly low. Concerning trends in skilled 
emigration rates between 1975 and 2000, our results show an increase in skilled 
emigration relative to unskilled emigration in many world regions (and in all 
regions during the decade 1990-2000). The brain drain is nonetheless taking 
place against a background of rising skill levels and increasing international 
mobility driven by globalization. Although the trend – upward or downward 
– was sizeable in thirty or so countries, emigration rates for a large number of 
countries show relative stability. However, this should not mask the fact that 
strong push factors are still operative in many countries, particularly those 
with small populations.
While past trends in international migration are helpful for understanding 
the difficulties currently facing sending countries, future trends will be decisive 
on several counts. First of all, demographic changes over the next fifty years 
will radically reshape the labour markets in origin and destination countries. 
Strong pressures will be exerted upon the economically active populations in 
the northern countries where the ageing process is most advanced, and where 
the temptation will be strong to recruit skilled labour from abroad. If increasingly 
selective policies are implemented in these countries, the impact on the countries 
of the South could be largely cushioned by their rapid population growth. But 
will they be able to meet the challenge of funding education for their native 
populations in response to demand from the North? In addition, the receiving 
countries will need to introduce effective integration policies for foreigners if 
they wish to avoid the problems now facing Europe, where governments are 
struggling to assimilate the second and third generation immigrants who still 
feel out of place in our societies.

This article proposes an estimation of international migration to the  six main receiving countries of the OECD 
between 1975 and 2000. The  analysis reveals a large increase in international migration during  this period, 
together with a substantial change in migrant skills. In  parallel with this phenomenon we note a significant 
rise in skill levels at world level. An analysis by country shows a high degree  of stability in the list of the 
countries most affected by the brain drain. While overall change in the migration of skills is limited, the list of 
countries with the largest losses of highly skilled workers in 1975 is largely unchanged twenty-five  years 
later.
