The efficiency with which renewable fuels and feedstocks are synthesized from 
electrical sources is limited at present by the sluggish oxygen evolution reaction 
(OER) in pH-neutral media. We took the view that generating transition metal sites 
with high valence at low applied bias should improve the activity of neutral OER 
catalysts. Using density functional theory, we find that the formation energy of 
desired NiP4+
P sites is systematically modulated by incorporating judicious 
combinations of Co, Fe and non-metal phosphorus. Here we synthesized 
NiCoFeP oxyhydroxides and probed their oxidation kinetics by employing in situ 
soft X-ray absorption (sXAS). In situ sXAS studies of neutral-pH OER catalysts 
indicate ready promotion of NiP4+
P under low overpotential conditions. NiCoFeP 
catalyst outperforms IrOR2
R and retains its performance following 100 hours of 
operation. We showcase NiCoFeP in a membrane-free COR2
R electroreduction 
system that achieves a 1.99 V cell voltage at 10 mA/cmP2
P, reducing COR2
R into CO 
and oxidizing HR2
RO to OR2
R with a 64% electricity-to-chemical-fuel efficiency. 
Electrochemical reduction of carbon dioxide to value-added fuels seeks to reduce 
the present-day dependence on legacy fossil fuels and thereby mitigate net COR2
R 
emissions P1-4
P. In electrochemical COR2
R reduction, the use of neutral-pH aqueous 
electrolyte is desired since it provides the highest COR2
R solubility while suppressing the 
undesired competing hydrogen evolution reaction.  
Unfortunately, in pH-neutral electrolytes, the overpotential of the anodic oxygen 
evolution reaction (OER) is, in the best catalysts reported to date, very high (> 460 mV 
at 10 mA/cmP2
P) P3, 5-7
P. This limits the electrical-to-chemical power conversion efficiency of 
the overall reaction. To improve the energy efficiency of COR2
R conversion, efforts have 

3 
 
been made to provide COR2
R reduction in neutral media while operating OER in alkaline 
electrolyte. However, the effective overpotential remains high (~ 430 mV) due to the 
additional energy needed to overcome the effect of pH gradients P3, 8, 9
P, i.e. the need also 
to overcome the membrane potential.  
Breakthroughs in low-overpotential, stable, and earth-abundant OER catalysts 
that operate optimally in neutral pH are therefore urgently needed to complement rapid 
advancements in COR2
R reduction electrocatalysts. 
High-valence transition metal (TM) ions incorporated into metal oxides are known 
to achieve a high reactivity towards water oxidationP5,10-15
P. However, as evidenced in 
their Pourbaix diagrams, TM ions prefer to take on low-valence oxidation states at 
neutral pHP5,11,16-18
P.  
We adopted a new strategy, one in which we would seek to minimize the energy 
(formation energy) required for active metal sites to reach the desired high oxidation 
state in neutral pHP5,12
P. We hypothesized that low active site formation energy would 
correlate well with a low overpotential to reach the high-turnover-frequency activated 
state.  
We focus in on the well-known NiP2+ 
PNiP4+
P earth-abundant catalystP5,12,18
P. We 
sought to challenge our posited correlation experimentally, pursuing in situ monitoring of 
the oxidation state of the OER catalyst. 
Multi-metal oxides based on Co, Fe, and Ni often outperform unary metal oxides 
due to modulation of their electronic properties and the resulting fine-tuning of the 
adsorption energies of the reaction intermediatesP10,19-25
P. Concomitantly, cobalt-

4 
 
phosphate compounds exhibit high activity at neutral pHP6,11,26
P. We reasoned that 
phosphate could help promote the transition metal to high valence states; and thus that 
the catalytic activity of promising multi-metal oxides (i.e. 3d metals) could be further 
enhanced if their 3d electronic structures were systematically and rationally modulated 
via incorporating multi-metal and non-metal modulatorsP19,25,26
P.  
Here we therefore explored combinations of elements incorporated into Ni oxides 
in an atomically intimate mannerP19
P, seeking the ready formation of NiP4+
P with the goal of 
improving the catalytic activity of the material. We synthesized NiCoFeP oxyhydroxides 
and probed their oxidation kinetics employing in situ soft X-ray absorption (sXAS). The 
new NiCoFeP catalysts exhibit exceptional OER performance, with a 330 mV 
overpotential at 10 mA/cmP2
P in COR2
R-saturated 0.5 M KHCOR3
R electrolyte. The new 
catalyst outperforms the leading precious metal oxide IrOR2
R and retains its performance 
following 100 hours of operation. We then combined the optimal multi-metal oxide with a 
gold nanoneedle COR2
R reduction catalyst to assemble an electrolyzer system which 
converts COR2
R and HR2
RO into CO and OR2
R. We found that the new system operated at a 
low cell voltage of 1.99 V and record electricity-to-chemical-fuel efficiency of 64%.  
Results and Discussion 
Theoretical studies. We began with computational studies aimed at understanding the 
effect of intimate metal and phosphorous doping on the stability of nickel structures with 
different valences. Density functional theory calculations with on-site Hubbard-like 
corrections (DFT+U) were performed on model Ni(OH)R2
R, β-NiOOH, and NiOR2
R structures 
(doped with Co, Fe, and P) to model NiP2+
P, NiP3+
P, and NiP4+
P oxidation states, respectively 
(see Supplementary Table 1 for optimized lattice constants). Experimentally, the NiOR2
R 

5 
 
phase is unstable and does not exist in the Ni Pourbaix diagram; simulations reproduce 
this observation, showing that NiOR2
R formation is unfavorable by 0.31 eV/formula unit (fu).  
However, once NiOR2
R is doped with phosphorus or other metals, the formation 
energies become negative, indicating a more stable phase (Supplementary Table 2). 
Thus, these simulations suggest that doping using Co, Fe, and P can lower the Gibbs 
formation energy of the compounds containing NiP4+
P that are otherwise unstable.

Oxidation-state changes monitored by in situ soft X-ray absorption. To challenge 
experimentally the concept that low formation energy could correlate with an early 
voltage onset of efficient OER, we sought a means to track the electronic states of 
metal sites in situ. Hard X-ray absorption (hXAS) has previously been used in situ to 
probe high-energy transitions between 1s and 4p electronic orbitals and is more 
relevant to local coordination environments. The electronically-relevant 3d final states is 
weakly evident in hXAS as a low-intensity pre-edge feature resulting from quadrupole 
transitionsP30
P. For this reason, the hXAS pre-edges do not enable an informative 
determination of the narrow band, TM 3d electronic structure. Realtime tracking of the x-
ray absorption near-edge region of the Ni K-edge was used to obtain initial information 
about their valence states and electronic configurationsP31
P.  
We turned instead to soft X-ray absorption spectroscopy (sXAS) at TM LR2,3
R-
edges, which accurately and efficiently quantifies TM oxidation states since these 
dipole-allowed 2p  3d optical transitions probe directly the 3d-states that are crucial to 
chemical activities. It provides detailed information on the local electronic structure and 
is sensitive to chemical bonding and oxidation statesP32-37
P. The sXAS measurements 
performed here were made using partial fluorescence yield intensity. These spectra are 

7 
 
not strictly proportional to the x-ray attenuation coefficient due to state dependent decay 
probabilitiesP32
P. These electron delocalization effects are known to enhance the 
fluorescence intensities of the higher energy transitions in a L-edge PFY spectrum.  We 
considered these effects in our interpretation of in situ measurements. We concluded 
that the magnitude of the bias-dependent changes we observed was much greater than 
the small effects that arise due to state-dependent decayP33
P. 
A challenge limiting the use of sXAS to study in situ operating OER catalysts 
arises from its limited probing depth. sXAS does not therefore reveal the (remote, far-
side) active surface behavior of an operating catalyst when excited and probed through 
a window and substrate (Fig. 2).  
We therefore synthesized highly nanoporous NiCoFeP samples into which we 
expected the electrolyte used in OER might substantially penetrate. We employed an in 
situ electrochemical flow cell that used an ultrathin SiR3
RNR4
R membrane window (100 nm 
thick) to separate the liquid medium inside the cell from the high-vacuum environment 
needed for soft X-ray measurements (Fig. 2a). The working electrode for loading 
catalysts consisted of a ~20-nm Au film evaporated on the internal side of the SiR3
RNR4
R 
membrane in contact with a flowing COR2
R saturated 0.5 M KHCOR3
R solution as electrolyte. 
Scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) images of NiCoFeP reveal a 
structure composed of nanosheets and nanopores (Fig. 2b). Energy-dispersive X-ray 
spectroscopy (EDS) mapping (Supplementary Fig. 7) and electron energy loss 
spectroscopy (EELS) elemental maps with sub-nm resolution (Supplementary Fig. 9) 
show a uniform, uncorrelated spatial distribution of Ni, Co, Fe, and P with 9:1:1:0.1

As a result of 2p core hole spin−orbit splitting, both experimental and theoretical

real-time spectra while applying constant anodic bias of +1.6 V, +1.8 V and +2.0 V vs. 
reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE), respectively.  
In order to understand the changes in the Ni L-edge, we carried out first-
principles calculations, solving the Bethe-Salpeter equation (BSE) for correlated 
electrons and holes with the goal of predicting theoretical sXAS spectra with the aid of 
the OCEAN simulation packageP38, 39
P.  The BSE is capable of reproducing the branching 
ratio of the L3/L2 edge correctlyP38

environments or oxidation statesP40
P. We use ground-state DFT+U Kohn-Sham orbitals as 
input for the BSE calculation, with U=6.45 eV, identical with our formation energy 
calculations above. Our BSE calculations, working within a basis of single electron-hole 
pair excitations, cannot describe atomic multiplet splitting. In particular, for the NiP2+
P 
system, the L3-edge is split into a main peak and a secondary feature at higher 
energies. To reproduce multiplet effects, we further perform an atomic configuration 

9 
 
calculation with the CTM4XAS codeP41,42

chosen to be 2.0 eV, which is estimated from the DFT+U calculation for the layered 
Ni(OH)R2
R structure. With the multiplet effects included, the simulated spectrum of NiP2+
P is 
in excellent agreement with experiments.

      Overall, the in situ studies agree with a physical picture in which Ni is the ionic 
species most susceptible to become oxidized to high valence in NiCoFeP; and that the 
incorporation of Co, Fe and P beneficially modulates the electronic structure of Ni metal 
oxides. It results in a reduced formation energy for NiP4+
P, a configuration favorable for 
water oxidation, consistent with the DFT studies in this work.  
We also used in situ XAS at the Fe and Co L-edges of NiCoFeP and relevant 
controls. In situ spectra of Fe L-edge indicate that FeP3+
P remains the dominant valence 
state before and during electrochemical operation (Supplementary Fig. 15), while the 
valence state of Co evolves from CoP2+
P under open circuit to CoP3+
P upon application of a 
potential, including only a modest admixture with CoP2+
P in all samples (Supplementary 
Fig. 16).  
Water oxidation electrocatalysis. We then proceeded to compare the catalytic 
performance of NiCoFeP with that of the reference samples NiP, NiCoP and IrOR2
R for the 
oxygen evolution reaction. To increase the conductivity of the electrode, we loaded our 
catalysts on a gold-plated nickel foam. Representative OER currents were measured in 
COR2
R-saturated 0.5 M KHCOR3
R aqueous electrolyte at a scan rate of 1 mV sP-1
P (Fig. 3a) 

11 
 
(currents are uncorrected and thus are already penalized by the effects of resistive 
losses incurred within the electrolyte).  
The NiCoFeP-on-gold-plated Ni foam requires an overpotential of only 330 mV at 
10 mA cmP-2
P (Table 1, all current densities based on projected geometric area), which is 
70 mV lower than that of the best commercial precious metal oxide IrOR2
R catalyst tested 
under identical conditions. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report of an 
earth-abundant catalyst that consistently outperforms precious metal oxide catalysts in 
a pH-neutral mediumP3, 5-7
P(Table 1). To evaluate the effect of surface area using an 
additional independent method, we analyzed the surface area via the Brunauer Emmett 
and Teller (BET) technique. This permitted us to report normalized kinetic current 
density (referred to as specific activity) as a function of potential versus RHE in order to 
assess the intrinsic activities of the catalysts. Using this metric, the intrinsic activity of 
NiCoFeP oxyhydroxide is confirmed to be notably higher than that of the controls 
(Supplementary Fig. 17). From BET, the high catalytic activity of NiCoFeP oxyhydroxide 
includes an appreciable intrinsic (not surface-area-related) contribution. 
To assess the kinetic barriers involved in OER, we studied the effect of 
temperature on the performance of the catalysts (Fig. 3b and Supplementary Fig. 19). 
OER proceeds more rapidly at elevated temperatures, with an exponential temperature 
dependence of the chemical rate constantP43
P. The electrochemical activation energies 
extracted from the slope of the Arrhenius plots are lower for the new catalyst than that 
of the precious metal oxide IrOR2
R (Table 1 and Supplementary Fig. 16). The most active 
NiCoFeP catalysts in this study exhibit the apparent barrier value of 27 kJ molP-1
P, 
indicating the high catalytic activity.  

12 
 
Our studies suggest that developing a TM oxyhydroxide having lower NiP4+
P 
formation energy provides an effective strategy and new guideline to enhance OER 
catalytic activity (Fig. 3c). To characterize performance stability, we operated the OER 
catalyst on gold-plated Ni foam under constant current density of 10 mA cmP-2
P 
continuously for 100 hours. We observed no appreciable increase in potential (Fig. 3d) 
over this time interval. To verify that the catalyst remained physically intact, we checked 
using ICP-AES for any observable leaching of the constituents into the electrolyte. We 
were able to detect no measurable elemental loss using this sensitive method. 
Consistently high activity throughout the entire duration of stability testing was further 
confirmed by measuring the rate of OR2
R evolution from the NiCoFeP-on-gold-plated Ni 
foam catalyst. We obtained quantitative (i.e. unity Faradaic efficiency) gas evolution of 
oxygen to within our available +/-5% experimental error. Our approach to minimize the 
formation energy required for active metal sites may also promote further avenues to 
other related oxidation reactions. 
We then assembled an electrolyzer that converts COR2
R and HR2
RO into CO and OR2
R 
based on NiCoFeP oxyhydroxides as OER catalysts and recently-reported Au 
nanoneedles as COR2
R reduction catalystsP44
P. We achieved a 1.99 V cell voltage at 10 
mA/cmP2
P, with an electricity-to-chemical-fuel efficiency of 64% (Figure 3e). This was 
accomplished in a membrane-free system that avoids membrane resistance. In contrast, 
when we use an H-cell electrolyzer with bipolar membranes, which enabled the use of 
state-of-art OER catalyst FeCoW in a favorable alkaline electrolyte, the total cell 
potential reached 2.37 V and achieved a 54% electricity-to-chemical-fuel efficiency (see 
Supplementary Table 3). The new neutral OER catalysts reported herein provide 

13 
 
efficient membrane-free electrolysis, and thus represent an overall enabling component 
in COR2
R reduction. 
Methods 
Energetics Calculations. Bulk energetics of metal and phosphorous doped Ni-oxides 
were calculated using density functional theory with the Hubbard-U framework (DFT+U) 
to account for the strongly localized d-electrons for Ni, Fe, and Co. The Perdew-Burke-
Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange-correlation functional and Blöchl’s all-electron, frozen-core, 
projector augmented (PAW) method with a plane-wave basis set cutoff value of 750 eV 
were used with the Vienna Ab-initio Simulation Package (VASP). The Gibbs free energy 
of formation, ΔG°f, was calculated using the framework of Persson et.alP16
P where the 
reference energies are taken from the most stable phase of each element. Full details 
are given in Supplementary Information. 
Synthesis of gelled NiCoFeP oxyhydroxides and controls. All gelled oxyhydroxides 
were synthesized using a modified aqueous sol-gel techniqueP16
P. 
In-situ X-ray absorption. X-ray absorption measurements at the nickel, Co and iron L-
edges were performed at the spherical grating monochromator (SGM) beamline 11ID-1 
at the Canadian Light Source. The window of the sample cells was mounted at an angle 
of roughly 45° with respect to both the incident beam and the detectors. The bodies of 
the sample cells were fabricated on an Object Connex500 printer by 3D printing with 
DurusWhite material. The catalysts were coated on the windows via drop-casting with a 
thickness of 400 nm. The gold-covered window was contacted by gold wires. Silver and 
platinum wires were used as reference and counter electrodes. All measurements were 
made at room temperature in the fluorescence mode using Amptek silicon drift 

14 
 
detectors (SDDs) with 1024 emission channels (energy resolution ∼120 eV). Four 
SDDs were employed simultaneously. The scanning energy ranges of Ni, Co and Fe L-
edges were set between 840 and 900 eV, 760 and 820 eV, and 690 and 750 eV, 
respectively. The partial fluorescence yield (PFY) was extracted from all SDDs by 
summation of the corresponding metal L emission lines. Full details can be found in 
Supplementary Information. 
L-edge simulation of XAS with the OCEAN code. The formalism of the Bethe-
Salpeter equation can be found in Ref (38).  The input DFT+U calculations are 
performed using norm-conserving pseudopotentials. Very large energy cutoff of 160 
Ryd is used for the plane-wave basis set of the Kohn-Sham wavefunctions. The key 
part of the electron-core-hole interactions is the screened Coulomb interactions, which 
is obtained within the random phase approximation. A large number of empty bands up 
to 50 eV above the Fermi level are included in the calculations. A 6x6x6 k-grid over the 
Brillouin zone is used to produce the sXAS spectra, which is convoluted with 
Lorentzians of 0.5 eV. 
Electrochemical Measurements. Electrochemical measurements were performed 
using a three-electrode system connected to an electrochemical workstation (Autolab 
PGSTAT302N). Cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements at 50 mV sP-1
P were performed 
for 3 cycles prior to the record of linear scan voltammetry (LSV) at 1 mV sP-1
P for each 
sample. The LSV performance of NiCoFeP, NiCoP, NiP and the corresponding controls 
with no phosphorus were also normalized by the BET surface area The apparent 
electrochemical activation energy (ERa
R) for water oxidation was determined using the 
Arrhenius relationship. The calibrations of Ag/AgCl reference electrode and Ag wire 

15 
 
reference electrode were conducted in the standard three-electrode system (the same 
system as that for performance measurements) as reference electrodes, using Pt foil as 
working and counter electrodes. Full details are given in Supplementary Information.
