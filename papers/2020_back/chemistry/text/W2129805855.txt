Abstract: Measurements of δ(13C) determined on CO2 with an isotope-ratio mass spectro m -
eter (IRMS) must be corrected for the amount of 17O in the CO2. For data consistency, this
must be done using identical methods by different laboratories. This report aims at unifying
data treatment for CO2 IRMS by proposing (i) a unified set of numerical values, and (ii) a
unified correction algorithm, based on a simple, linear approximation formula. Because the
oxygen of natural CO2 is derived mostly from the global water pool, it is recommended that
a value of 0.528 be employed for the factor λ, which relates differences in 17O and 18O abun-
dances. With the currently accepted N(13C)/N(12C) of 0.011180(28) in VPDB (Vienna
Peedee belemnite) reevaluation of data yields a value of 0.000393(1) for the oxygen isotope
ratio N(17O)/N(16O) of the evolved CO2. The ratio of these quantities, a ratio of isotope ra-
tios, is essential for the 17O abundance correction: [N(17O)/N(16O)]/[N(13C)/N(12C)] =
0.03516(8). 
The equation [δ(13C) ≈45δVPDB-CO2 + 2 17R/13R (45δVPDB-CO2 – λ46δVPDB-CO2)]
closely approximates δ(13C) values with less than 0.010 ‰ deviation for normal oxygen-
bearing materials and no more than 0.026 ‰ in extreme cases. Other materials containing
oxygen of non-mass-dependent isotope composition require a more specific data treatment.
A similar linear approximation is also suggested for δ(18O). The linear approximations are
easy to implement in a data spreadsheet, and also help in generating a simplified uncertainty
budget.

SELECTION OF LAMBDA
Equation 10 implies that the isotopic compositions of sample and reference material are linked expo-
nentially. The nature of this link can be one of the following cases [8,10]:
(1) 
Materials linked by a common fractionation process that started from the same material follow an
exponential relation characterized by λ. Luz and Barkan [8] demonstrated this for respiration,
both mathematically and by accurate measurements. Other examples include Rayleigh distillation
and diffusion. 
(2) 
Similar (or the same) fractionation processes starting from a single (or similar) materials give a
family of fractionation lines, being close in the three-isotope space (17O-18O-16O). This seems to
be the case for natural waters [5] (ocean and precipitation). In this case, the description by eq. 10
is a phenomenological approach [10]. 
(3) 
If the value for λ is calculated for two data points (e.g., sample and zero point of a scale) that are
not proven to be linked by a common fractionation process, then: λ does not characterize a
process; λ has a formal meaning only; and the value of λ may be confusing at best.
It has long been known that λ is not exactly 0.5 for any fractionation process. From theoretical
considerations [11,12], the exact relation between isotopic fractionation factors based on 17R and 18R
depends predominantly on the formation of the CO bond and on mass-separation processes, such as dif-
fusion or distillation (case 1 above). Thus, one cannot expect a constant value for different oxygen iso-
topic fractionation processes in nature. This has been confirmed experimentally for the major oxygen-
bearing pools in nature [4,5,7,8,11], and thus the 17O correction cannot simply follow a case 1 scenario.
However, most carbon-bearing materials analyzed as CO2 in IRMSs have their oxygen derived from the
global water pool, which has a constant λ of 0.528 [5] and which is now known with high precision to
be 0.5279 ± 0.0001 [7]. Analyses of numerous samples measured from the Vostok ice core (Antarctic),
covering the last 150000 years, also confirmed a value of 0.528 for λ [13]. Hence, for the global water
pool it can be concluded that λ is 0.528 (following a case 2 scenario).
When investigating the δ(17O)–δ(18O) relationship in carbonates by thermal decomposition,
Miller et al. [15] found a λ value of 0.5247 for the CO2 as well as for the associated residues (CaO and
MgO)h. The δ(18O) values in these experiments covered a range of about 30 ‰. Despite a small verti-
cal offset in the data, a value of 0.5247 appears to characterize carbonates—a value that is similar to
that found for the global water pool. The same value of 0.5247 was found for a large number of sili-
cates [4] where the 17O-18O partitioning involves high-temperature magmatic processes not directly re-
lated to marine carbonates or water.

Considering the findings above, and given that oxygen of natural CO2 is rapidly exchanged with
liquid water (except for some very specific cases like CO2 in the stratosphere and in some meteorites),
the water value of 0.528 for λ was proposed as the most probable value for the 17O-correction [16].
Equation 10 links the analyzed CO2 to respective isotope scales in use (VPDB-CO2 or VSMOW).
Hence, the use of 0.528 for λ in combination with VPDB-CO2 or VSMOW scales (both related to
water) is self-consistent. This is in agreement with the purpose of the 17O correction, which is to pro-
vide a “best” and most accurate estimate of the 17O-18O relationship without actually analyzing the 17O
abundance of CO2.j The choice of 0.528 for λ corresponds to the phenomenological approach, case 2
above.
We note two cases where deviations from the more general relation between δ(17O) and δ(18O)
in terrestrial waters have been documented. The oxygen isotopic composition of O2 in air is generated
mainly by photosynthesis and respiration. The latter has a considerable isotope fractionation effect char-
acterized by a λ value of 0.518 [8], resulting in O2 gas with a composition below the water fractiona-
tion line (the line with a λ value of 0.528 starting from VSMOW, where VSMOW by definition has
δ(17O) ≡0 and δ(18O) ≡0). In addition, 17O is removed in part by accumulation in stratospheric ozone,
giving a somewhat enhanced 17O depletion of air O2 relative to the water line. As a consequence, the
oxygen isotopic composition of O2 in air expressed on the VSMOW scale [7,8] can be characterized by
a (formal, case 3) value for λ of 0.509, representing the steady state among ocean water, photosynthe-
sis, and respiration with some 17O accumulation in stratospheric ozone.
Moreover, an increasing number of studies investigate the deviation of the 17O-18O relationship
from a mass-dependent fractionation in its general form Δ(17O); 17O anomaly or non-mass dependent
fractionationk. The materials usually originate from reactions of ozone in the stratosphere, which is ex-
tremely enriched in 17O. The correction in this case is starting at first with an approximation using the
water line (λ = 0.528). This usual 17O correction then needs to be refined by introducing Δ(17O) (scaled
to the VPDB-CO2 scale using λ = 0.528) into the correction equations [6,9,10,16]. We stress here that
using a value for λ other than the default value used for the 17O-correction, 0.528, is confusing, leads
to potential scale inconsistencies, and cannot be recommended. For example, λ = 0.509, which charac-
terizes air-O2 on the VSMOW scale, inflates to an artificial 0.555 on the VPDB-CO2 oxygen scale
[10,16] (case 3 considered above).
Summarizing, a value of 0.528 for λ appears to be the best estimate for general use in the 17O cor-
rection of CO2 originating from the relevant most abundant natural oxygen pools. The δ(13C) bias aris-
ing from atmospheric O2 with its negative Δ(17O) vs. the λ = 0.528 line is limited (and the same can be
expected for commercial oxygen produced from atmospheric O2), but can reach a maximum value of
0.032 ‰. While this value may seem large for some applications, biases will tend to cancel when ref-
erence material and sample material are treated using the same oxygen source. However, when CO2
samples with oxygen from different sources are compared to each other directly, as would be the case

SELECTION OF REFERENCE RATIOS
The second unknown in eq. 16 is K, which describes the relation of 17R and 18R in the reference mate-
rial. The value of 17R in CO2 evolved from international reference carbonates by treatment with 100 %
H3PO4 at 25 °C has been determined repeatedly with variable accuracy.l The original result adopted by
Craig [1] (0.0003800) was constructed from mass spectrometric measurements made by A. O. Nier on
oxygen from an O2 high-pressure cylinder gas with unknown origin that was calibrated using mixtures
of Ar isotopes. The 17R value of CO2 evolved from virtual VPDB carbonate derived by Santrock et al.
[2], from experiments where a known CO2 gas was equilibrated with water samples of varying isotopic
composition, marks the high end of the measurement values (0.00041085). It should be noted that the
difference between these two extreme cases is equivalent to a 17R span of about 80 ‰. The uncertainty
in the value of 17R is probably one of the largest sources of uncertainty in comparing 17O-corrected re-
sults of δ(13C) measurements from different laboratories. Li et al. [18] determined 17R by converting
O2 gases (O2 released from VSMOW and H2
16O) to CO2 and comparing the isotopic results before and
after the conversion step. They determined a value for 17RVSMOW of 0.0003799(8) and stressed the im-
portance of using a newly determined value for 13R [19]. However, Li et al. [18] did not report on tests
or on corrections for instrumental effects and have not considered use of 17R in the 17O correction. 
A careful redetermination of the 17R/13R relationship has been carried out by Assonov and
Brenninkmeijer [17]. By using essentially the same approach as that used by Li et al. [18] of convert-
ing O2 gases (a normal O2 and an O2 gas depleted in 17O and 18O) to CO2 and comparing the isotopic
results before and after the conversion step, including a thorough investigation of and correction for
known instrumental effects, they determined a value of 0.00039511(94) for the 17R value of CO2
evolved from virtual VPDB calcite at 25 °C with 100 % H3PO4 (VPDB-CO2). This value is about
halfway between the value adopted by Craig [1] and the value of Santrock et al. [2]. Because this ex-
periment only yields the ratio of R(17O) and R(13C) in the CO2 measurement, Assonov and
Brenninkmeijer [17] calculated the value above assuming the original 13R of Craig [1] (13RPDB =
0.011 2372). Using instead the revised value of 13RVPDB of 0.011 80(28), as determined from the meas-
urement of NBS 19 calcite by Chang and Li [19] and accepted as the reference value for x(13C) and
x(12C) by IUPAC [20], the assigned 17R value shifts to 0.00039310(94), which can be represented as
0.000393(1). It is important to recognize that any value assigned to 17R in VPDB-CO2 gas based on the
Assonov and Brenninkmeijer measurements [17] will change with a revised, more accurate determina-
tion of 13R. However, the 17O correction calculation does not require absolute values [9,16].
Quantitatively, it is sufficient to use a consistent 17R/13R value, such as 0.035 16(8), for the experiments
done by Assonov and Brenninkmeijer [17]. Thereafter, we propagate the uncertainty of the 17R/13R
ratio to the scale of 13R; for calculating δ(13C), the uncertainty of 13R does not need to be considered.

EXPRESSING 45δ AND 46δ ON THE VPDB-CO2 SCALE 
Equation 19 requires 45δVPDB-CO2 and 46δVPDB-CO2. Conceptually, VPDB-CO2 defines a virtual scale
realized via CO2 evolved from NBS 19 carbonate with 100 % H3PO4 at 25 °C. The VPDB scale is de-
fined for the virtual solid carbonate material by assigning a consensus δ(13C) value of +1.95 ‰ and as-
signing a δ(18O) value of –2.2 ‰ exactly to NBS 19 carbonate (not CO2 evolved from this carbonate)
[23]. By definition, the uncertainty of both of these consensus values is zero. 
For any sample analyzed, 45δ and 46δ values are obtained by comparison with a reference pre-
cisely calibrated for δ(13C)VPDB and δ(18O)VPDB-CO2. From these measurements, the composite
45δVPDB-CO2 and 46δVPDB-CO2 values have to be calculated in order to apply eq. 19 during correction
for the 17O contribution. This is essentially the same relation, applied in reverse order. It can be shown
that 
45δVPDB-CO2 = [δ(13C)VPDB + λ Q δ(18O)VPDB-CO2]/(Q + 1)
(21)
or
45δVPDB-CO2 = 0.9343 δ(13C)VPDB + 0.0347 δ(18O)VPDB-CO2
(22)
where Q = 2 (17R/13R) and is 0.07032 ± 0.00017 (following Assonov [17]), and λ = 0.528. Again, the
individual 17R and 13R values are not needed. Rather, their ratios enter the correction equation. 
It should be noted that eq. 21 and any correction for 17O are intimately linked and must be used
in a consistent fashion in order to avoid artefacts in the data. While this sounds simple, it often is diffi-
cult in practice, particularly when using secondary reference materials that have an uncertain history re-
garding the 17O correction applied during the initial assignments of δ(13C)VPDB and δ(18O)VPDB-CO2.
Similarly, 46δVPDB-CO2 can be calculated from eq. 20 by using the assigned δ(13C)VPDB and
δ(18O)VPDB-CO2 values
46δVPDB-CO2 ≈0.0021 δ(13C)VPDB + 0.99904 δ(18O)VPDB-CO2
(23)
Applying eqs. 22 and 23 to NBS 19 itself, we obtain
45δNBS19-CO2,VPDB-CO2 = 1.746 ‰
(24)
and
46δNBS19-CO2,VPDB-CO2 = –2.194 ‰.
(25)

δ(18O)NBS19,VPDB ≡–2.2 ‰ exactly. Both values by definition have an uncertainty of zero. The scale
is not defined in terms of 45δ and 46δ, although the values above could serve this purpose. One impor-
tant consequence is that the origin of the VPDB scale for δ(13C) cannot be fixed independently, and it
depends on the numerical parameters used for the 17O correction applied when CO2 is the measured
gas.n While this effect in general is not large, it cannot be ignored in applications where high precision

ACCURACY OF THE LINEARIZED 17O CORRECTION
By using a simplified uncertainty budget, one can demonstrate that the errors due to a linear approxi-
mation of the 17O correction are much less than or, in extreme cases, at least comparable to the analyt-
ical errors produced during measurement using the best IRMS instruments available. We start with the
singular point having zero uncertainty on the VPDB scale, and with the respective VPDB-CO2 scale an-
choro: NBS 19 calcite (for VPDB) and NBS 19-CO2 (for VPDB-CO2). Next, all measured values are
linked to the scale via a chain of measurements done on CO2 gases, each measurement having its own
uncertainty. For establishing a complete calibration or traceability chain, a simple equation can be de-
rived [3].
Using the general delta equation (eq. 1; δB,Sx = RB/RSx – 1) and using the relation RB/RS0 =
RB/RS1 × RS1/RS2 × RS2/RS3…× RSx/RS0 with B representing the sample and Sx referring to the vari-
ous levels of reference material in the chain, one can write
(δB,S0 + 1) = (δB,S1 + 1) × (δS1,S2 + 1) × (δS2,S3 + 1)… × (δSx,S0 + 1)
(26)
Equation 26 is valid for all delta expressions. The benefit of the linearized 17O correction de-
scribed in this work can be demonstrated by a simplified uncertainty budget. The combined uncertainty
of the 45δ values of a sample, Uc(45δ), then can be expressed as 
Uc(45δ) ≈{Σ [U(45δ)]2}0.5
(27)
where the U(45δ) are the uncertainties of the 45δ values. The same form is valid for determining Uc(46δ)
from values of U(46δ).
For the highest-accuracy IRMS instruments running in dual inlet mode, a typical analytical error
in 45δ is about ±0.01 ‰ (1σ). For three consecutive measurement steps (NBS 19-CO2 →working ref.
CO2 →sample), Uc(45δ) is ±0.017 ‰. Using eq. 19 and assuming Δ(17O) equals 0 and 46δVPDB-CO2 is
close to zero, the error in δ(13C) is ±0.019 ‰. This value is higher than any uncertainty (bias) intro-
duced by the linearized 17O correction (see Table 1). For the case when a secondary/tertiary-level ref-
erence material is distributed as combustible material, the error budget is more complicated. As a con-
sequence, the traceability chain to NBS 19-CO2 grows longer and errors will be enhanced.

SUMMARY AND PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION OF THE LINEARIZED 17O
CORRECTION
This report aims at unifying raw data treatment for CO2 IRMS by proposing (i) a unified set of numer-
ical values in use and (ii) a unified calculation algorithm, based on a simple linear approximation for-
mula. In this way, users can easily check calculations performed by mass-spectrometry software. In
practice, the approach aims at bringing users making CO2 isotope determinations by IRMS to the same
scale realization. As the value of 17R has been determined on the scale of the fixed 13R in current use;
only the ratio 17R/13R matters. Therefore, the error propagation does not include the error in the 13R de-
termination; the error of 17R/13R in use is sufficiently small not to affect δ(13C) determinations further.p
The calculation scheme includes measured (raw) 45δ and 46δ values only, and links a sample to a
primary reference material via a chain of relative/delta measurements. The scheme is based on a con-
sistent set of numerical values (13R, 17R, and 18R values and a general value for λ). In addition to the
data treatment unification, the approach helps getting transparent and simple estimation of uncertain-
ties. Errors introduced by the linearization formula are typically less than 0.008 ‰ as tabulated in
Table 1. If needed, users can apply numerical calculation methods to solve the whole set of equations
without approximation.
